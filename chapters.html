Physics Chapter Guide

Complete chapter-wise breakdown for Grade XI and XII — key concepts, important formulas, and direct links to interactive simulations.

GRADE XI Mechanics & Beyond

01 Kinematics

▼

Kinematics is the study of motion without considering its causes. This chapter lays the foundation for all of mechanics by introducing displacement, velocity, and acceleration in one and two dimensions, along with graphical analysis and relative motion.

  • Distance vs. Displacement; Speed vs. Velocity
  • Uniform and non-uniform motion in a straight line
  • Equations of motion (v = u + at, s = ut + ½at², v² = u² + 2as)
  • Motion under gravity — free fall
  • Projectile motion — time of flight, range, maximum height
  • Relative velocity in 1D and 2D
v = u + at   |   s = ut + ½at²   |   v² = u² + 2as
R = (u² sin2θ)/g   |   T = 2u sinθ/g   |   H = u² sin²θ/2g

02 Newton's Laws of Motion

▼

Newton’s three laws form the backbone of classical mechanics. This chapter explores force, inertia, momentum, and the techniques of free body diagrams to solve real-world problems involving friction, tension, and circular motion dynamics.

  • Newton’s First Law — inertia and equilibrium
  • Newton’s Second Law — F = ma, impulse and momentum
  • Newton’s Third Law — action-reaction pairs
  • Free body diagrams and constraint equations
  • Static and kinetic friction; angle of repose
  • Circular motion dynamics — centripetal force, banking of roads
F = ma   |   p = mv   |   J = FΔt = Δp
f = μN   |   Fc = mv²/r   |   tanθ = v²/rg

03 Work, Power & Energy

▼

Energy is the currency of the universe. This chapter introduces the scalar concepts of work and energy, the powerful work-energy theorem, conservation laws, and the mechanics of elastic and inelastic collisions.

  • Work done by a constant and variable force
  • Kinetic energy and the work-energy theorem
  • Potential energy — gravitational and elastic
  • Conservation of mechanical energy
  • Power — instantaneous and average
  • Elastic and inelastic collisions; coefficient of restitution
W = Fd cosθ   |   KE = ½mv²   |   PE = mgh
Wnet = ΔKE   |   P = F · v   |   e = (v₂ − v₁)/(u₁ − u₂)

04 Rotational Motion

▼

Rotational motion extends Newton’s laws into the angular domain. This chapter covers torque, moment of inertia, angular momentum conservation, and rolling motion.

  • Angular displacement, velocity, and acceleration
  • Torque (τ = Iα) and moment of inertia
  • Parallel and perpendicular axis theorems
  • Angular momentum and its conservation
  • Rolling motion without slipping
τ = Iα   |   L = Iω   |   KErot = ½Iω²
I = Icm + Md²   |   vcm = Rω

05 Gravitation

▼

Newton’s law of universal gravitation explains everything from falling apples to orbiting satellites. This chapter covers gravitational fields, potentials, Kepler’s laws, and the physics of escape velocity and orbital mechanics.

  • Newton’s law of gravitation — F = GMm/r²
  • Gravitational field and potential
  • Variation of g with height, depth, and latitude
  • Escape velocity and orbital velocity
  • Kepler’s laws of planetary motion
  • Satellites and geostationary orbits
F = GMm/r²   |   g = GM/R²   |   ve = √(2gR)
vo = √(gR)   |   T² ∝ r³   |   E = −GMm/2r

06 Mechanical Properties of Solids

▼

How materials deform under stress is central to engineering. This chapter introduces elasticity, stress-strain relationships, and the moduli that characterise different materials.

  • Stress (tensile, compressive, shear) and strain
  • Hooke’s law and the stress-strain curve
  • Young’s modulus, Bulk modulus, Shear modulus
  • Poisson’s ratio
  • Elastic potential energy
σ = F/A   |   ε = ΔL/L   |   Y = σ/ε
B = −VΔP/ΔV   |   U = ½σεV

07 Fluid Mechanics

▼

Fluids obey elegant principles that explain everything from hydraulic lifts to airplane wings. This chapter explores pressure, buoyancy, fluid flow, and viscosity.

  • Pressure and Pascal’s law; hydraulic machines
  • Archimedes’ principle and buoyancy
  • Equation of continuity and Bernoulli’s theorem
  • Viscosity, Stokes’ law, and terminal velocity
  • Surface tension and capillarity
P = P₀ + ρgh   |   Fb = ρVg

08 Heat & Thermodynamics

▼

Thermodynamics governs the flow of heat and the transformation of energy. From the microscopic world of gas molecules to the macroscopic laws of engines and refrigerators.

  • Thermal expansion — linear, area, and volume
  • Calorimetry and specific heat capacity
  • Kinetic Theory of Gases — RMS speed, degrees of freedom
  • First Law of Thermodynamics (ΔU = Q − W)
  • Thermodynamic processes — isothermal, adiabatic, isobaric, isochoric
  • Second Law — Carnot engine, entropy
  • Heat transfer — conduction, convection, radiation
PV = nRT   |   vrms = √(3RT/M)
ΔU = Q − W   |   ηCarnot = 1 − T₂/T₁

09 Simple Harmonic Motion

▼

Oscillations are ubiquitous in nature. This chapter covers the defining condition of SHM, energy in oscillating systems, spring-mass and pendulum systems, and resonance.

  • SHM — definition, characteristics, energy
  • Spring-mass system (T = 2π√(m/k))
  • Simple pendulum (T = 2π√(l/g))
  • Damped and forced oscillations; resonance
  • Superposition of SHM
x = A sin(ωt + φ)   |   ω = 2π/T
Tspring = 2π√(m/k)   |   Tpendulum = 2π√(l/g)   |   E = ½kA²

10 Waves

▼

Waves carry energy through space and matter. This chapter builds from the wave equation to superposition, standing waves, beats, and the Doppler effect for sound.

  • Transverse and longitudinal waves
  • Wave equation: y = A sin(ωt − kx)
  • Speed of waves in strings and air
  • Standing waves and harmonics
  • Beats (fbeat = |f₁ − f₂|)
  • Doppler effect for sound
v = fλ   |   v = √(T/μ) (string)
fbeat = |f₁ − f₂|   |   f' = f(v ± vo)/(v ∓ vs)

GRADE XII Electromagnetism, Optics & Modern Physics

01 Electrostatics & Capacitance

▼

Electrostatics is the study of charges at rest. From Coulomb’s inverse-square law to the elegant machinery of Gauss’s law, this chapter builds the concepts of electric field, potential, and capacitance that underpin all of electromagnetism.

  • Electric charge — properties, conservation, quantisation
  • Coulomb’s law and superposition principle
  • Electric field lines and field due to various charge distributions
  • Gauss’s law and its applications
  • Electric potential and potential energy
  • Equipotential surfaces
  • Capacitors — parallel plate, series/parallel combinations
  • Energy stored in a capacitor; effect of dielectrics
F = kq₁q₂/r²   |   E = kq/r²   |   V = kq/r
∮ E · dA = Qenc/ε₀ (Gauss)   |   C = ε₀A/d   |   U = ½CV²

02 Current Electricity

▼

When charges flow, we get electric current. This chapter covers the fundamental laws governing DC circuits — from Ohm’s law to Kirchhoff’s rules — and introduces practical instruments like the Wheatstone bridge and potentiometer.

  • Electric current, drift velocity, and mobility
  • Ohm’s law; resistivity and conductivity
  • Temperature dependence of resistance
  • Series and parallel combinations of resistors
  • Kirchhoff’s junction and loop rules
  • Wheatstone bridge and meter bridge
  • Potentiometer — principle and applications
  • EMF, terminal voltage, and internal resistance of cells
V = IR   |   R = ρl/A   |   P = VI = I²R = V²/R
ΣIin = ΣIout (KCL)   |   ΣV = 0 (KVL)   |   R₁/R₂ = R₃/R₄ (Wheatstone)

03 Moving Charges & Magnetism

▼

Moving charges create magnetic fields, and magnetic fields exert forces on moving charges. This beautiful interplay is the foundation of electromagnetism, from the Biot-Savart law to the cyclotron — a chapter that bridges electricity and magnetism.

  • Magnetic force on a moving charge (Lorentz force)
  • Motion of a charged particle in a magnetic field — circular and helical paths
  • Biot-Savart law — field due to a current-carrying wire, loop, solenoid
  • Ampere’s circuital law and its applications
  • Force between two parallel current-carrying conductors
  • Torque on a current loop in a magnetic field
  • Moving coil galvanometer; cyclotron
F = qv × B   |   r = mv/qB (circular motion)
dB = (μ₀/4π)(Idl × r̂)/r² (Biot-Savart)   |   ∮ B · dl = μ₀Ienc (Ampere)
τ = NIAB sinθ   |   F/l = μ₀I₁I₂/2πd

04 Electromagnetic Induction

▼

A changing magnetic flux induces an EMF — the revolutionary discovery by Faraday that gave us generators, transformers, and the entire electrical power grid. This chapter explores induction, Lenz’s law, inductance, and eddy currents.

  • Magnetic flux and Faraday’s law of electromagnetic induction
  • Lenz’s law and conservation of energy
  • Motional EMF — conducting rod in a magnetic field
  • Self-inductance (L) and mutual inductance (M)
  • Energy stored in an inductor (½LI²)
  • Eddy currents — applications and minimisation
  • AC generator — working principle
ε = −dΦB/dt (Faraday)   |   ΦB = B · A · cosθ
ε = Blv (motional EMF)   |   ε = −L(dI/dt)   |   U = ½LI²
M = μ₀N₁N₂A/l (mutual inductance for solenoids)

05 Alternating Current

▼

Alternating current powers the modern world. This chapter delves into AC circuit analysis using phasors, impedance, resonance in RLC circuits, the power factor, and the transformers that make efficient long-distance power transmission possible.

  • AC voltage and current — peak, RMS, and average values
  • AC through pure R, L, and C circuits
  • Phasor diagrams and impedance
  • Series LCR circuit and resonance
  • Quality factor (Q) and bandwidth
  • Power in AC circuits — power factor (cosφ)
  • Transformers — step-up and step-down
  • LC oscillations

XL = ωL   |   XC = 1/ωC   |   fres = 1/(2π√LC)
Pavg = VrmsIrmscosφ   |   V₁/V₂ = N₁/N₂ (transformer)

06 Electromagnetic Waves

▼

Maxwell unified electricity and magnetism, predicting electromagnetic waves that travel at the speed of light. This chapter explores Maxwell’s equations, the EM spectrum, and the properties that make radio waves, microwaves, light, and X-rays all part of one family.

  • Maxwell’s equations — displacement current and Ampere-Maxwell law
  • Electromagnetic waves — nature, speed, and transverse character
  • The electromagnetic spectrum — radio to gamma rays
  • Properties: energy, momentum, and intensity of EM waves
  • Applications of different regions of the EM spectrum

E₀/B₀ = c   |   I = ½ε₀cE₀²   |   p = U/c (momentum)

07 Ray Optics

▼

Ray optics treats light as straight-line rays, providing an elegant framework for understanding mirrors, lenses, prisms, and the optical instruments that extend human vision from microscopes to telescopes.

  • Reflection — laws, plane mirrors, spherical mirrors
  • Mirror formula (1/v + 1/u = 1/f) and magnification
  • Refraction — Snell’s law, total internal reflection
  • Lenses — thin lens formula, power, combinations
  • Prism — deviation, dispersion, and minimum deviation
  • Optical instruments — microscope, telescope, human eye
  • Defects of vision — myopia, hypermetropia
1/v + 1/u = 1/f (mirror)   |   n₁ sinθ₁ = n₂ sinθ₂ (Snell)
1/v − 1/u = 1/f (lens)   |   P = 1/f (dioptre)
δmin = (n − 1)A (thin prism)   |   sinθc = n₂/n₁ (TIR)

08 Wave Optics

▼

Wave optics reveals light’s true wave nature through phenomena that ray optics cannot explain. Interference fringes, diffraction patterns, and polarization provide stunning evidence of superposition and the transverse nature of light waves.

  • Huygens’ principle — wavefronts and secondary wavelets
  • Young’s double slit experiment — fringe width, conditions for maxima/minima
  • Coherent and incoherent sources
  • Diffraction — single slit, resolving power
  • Polarization — Malus’s law, Brewster’s angle
β = λD/d (fringe width, YDSE)   |   Path diff = d sinθ
Maxima: d sinθ = nλ   |   Minima: d sinθ = (n+½)λ
I = I₀ cos²θ (Malus)   |   tanθB = n (Brewster)

09 Modern Physics

▼

Modern physics broke the boundaries of classical mechanics, revealing the quantum nature of light, the structure of atoms, and the immense energy locked within the nucleus. From the photoelectric effect to nuclear fission, this chapter is the frontier of physics.

  • Photoelectric effect — Einstein’s equation, threshold frequency, stopping potential
  • de Broglie wavelength and wave-particle duality
  • Bohr’s model of the hydrogen atom — energy levels, spectral series
  • X-rays — production, properties, Moseley’s law
  • Nuclear structure — mass defect and binding energy
  • Radioactivity — α, β, γ decay; half-life; decay law
  • Nuclear fission and fusion
E = hf   |   KEmax = hf − φ (photoelectric)
λ = h/mv = h/p (de Broglie)   |   En = −13.6/n² eV (hydrogen)
N = N₀e−λt   |   t½ = 0.693/λ   |   E = Δmc²
