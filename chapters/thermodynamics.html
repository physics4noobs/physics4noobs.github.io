1. Kinetic Theory of Gases — Fundamental Assumptions

Fig 8.1

The kinetic theory of gases provides a microscopic model that explains the macroscopic properties of gases — pressure, temperature, and internal energy — in terms of the motion of individual molecules. It bridges the gap between molecular behaviour and the thermodynamic quantities we measure in the laboratory.

Postulates of Kinetic Theory

  • A gas consists of a very large number of identical molecules that are rigid, perfectly elastic spheres. The volume occupied by the molecules themselves is negligible compared to the volume of the container.
  • Molecules are in continuous random motion, moving in all directions with all possible velocities. There is no preferred direction of motion.
  • Molecules collide with one another and with the walls of the container. These collisions are perfectly elastic — kinetic energy is conserved before and after every collision.
  • No intermolecular forces of attraction or repulsion act between the molecules except during the brief instant of collision. The energy of the gas is purely kinetic.
  • Molecules travel in straight lines with uniform velocity between successive collisions. The average distance between two consecutive collisions is called the mean free path.
  • The time spent in a collision is negligible compared to the time between collisions.

A gas satisfying all these assumptions under all conditions of temperature and pressure is called an ideal gas (or perfect gas). Real gases deviate from ideal behaviour at low temperatures and high pressures, where intermolecular forces and molecular volume become significant. At high temperature and low pressure, real gases approximate ideal behaviour closely.

Ideal Gas Equation

The equation of state for an ideal gas relates its pressure, volume, temperature, and amount of substance:

$$PV = nRT$$

Here P is the pressure, V is the volume, n is the number of moles, T is the absolute temperature (in Kelvin), and R is the universal gas constant:

$$R = 8.314 \text{ J mol}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1} = 0.082 \text{ LÂ·atm mol}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1} = 2 \text{ cal mol}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1}$$

An alternate form in terms of density is PM = dRT, where M is the molar mass and d is the density of the gas. For N individual molecules, the equation becomes PV = NkBT, where kB = R/NA = 1.38 × 10−23 J/K is the Boltzmann constant.

Pressure from Molecular Motion

By considering the momentum transfer during elastic collisions of molecules with the walls of the container, we derive the pressure exerted by an ideal gas:

$$P = \frac{1}{3}\frac{m_0 N}{V} c_{\text{rms}}^2 = \frac{1}{3} \rho c_{\text{rms}}^2$$

Here m₀ is the mass of one molecule, N is the total number of molecules, ρ is the gas density, and crms is the root mean square speed. This result is one of the central achievements of the kinetic theory — it connects the microscopic quantity (molecular speed) to the macroscopic quantity (pressure).

Evidence for Molecular Motion

  • Diffusion: Gases spread spontaneously into available space (e.g., H₂ diffuses into CO₂ even against gravity).
  • Evaporation: Fast molecules escape from the liquid surface, causing cooling.
  • Brownian Motion: Irregular, zigzag motion of small suspended particles caused by random molecular impacts.

2. Molecular Speeds

Fig 8.2

The molecules of a gas do not all move at the same speed. The Maxwell–Boltzmann distribution describes how molecular speeds are distributed at a given temperature. Three characteristic speeds are defined, each providing a different statistical measure of the speed distribution.

Root Mean Square (RMS) Speed

The RMS speed is the square root of the mean of the squares of all molecular speeds. It is the most commonly used speed in kinetic theory because it relates directly to the kinetic energy of the gas.

$$v_{\text{rms}} = \sqrt{\frac{3RT}{M}} = \sqrt{\frac{3k_BT}{m_0}} = \sqrt{\frac{3P}{\rho}}$$

Average (Mean) Speed

The average speed is the arithmetic mean of the magnitudes of all molecular velocities:

$$v_{\text{avg}} = \sqrt{\frac{8RT}{\pi M}} = \sqrt{\frac{8k_BT}{\pi m_0}}$$

Most Probable Speed

The most probable speed is the speed possessed by the greatest number of molecules — it corresponds to the peak of the Maxwell–Boltzmann distribution curve:

$$v_{\text{mp}} = \sqrt{\frac{2RT}{M}} = \sqrt{\frac{2k_BT}{m_0}}$$

Speed Ratios

The three speeds are always ordered as vrms > vavg > vmp. Their ratio is:

$$v_{\text{mp}} : v_{\text{avg}} : v_{\text{rms}} = \sqrt{2} : \sqrt{\frac{8}{\pi}} : \sqrt{3} \approx 1.41 : 1.60 : 1.73$$

Important: At the same temperature, lighter molecules move faster (speed ∝ 1/√M). For the same gas at two different temperatures, vrms(T₁)/vrms(T₂) = √(T₁/T₂). For two different gases at different temperatures, vrms,1/vrms,2 = √(T₁M₂ / T₂M₁).

Example 1 — Argon RMS Speed

Calculate the root mean square speed of argon atoms at room temperature (25°C). At what temperature will the RMS speed be half that value? (MAr = 0.040 kg/mol, R = 8.3 J mol−1 K−1)

T = 273 + 25 = 298 K

vrms = √(3RT/M) = √(3 × 8.3 × 298 / 0.040)
vrms = √(185,115) ≈ 430.7 m/s

For the same gas, vrms ∝ √T. If v′rms = vrms/2:
v′/v = √(T′/T) ⇒ 1/2 = √(T′/298)
T′ = 298/4 = 74.5 K = −198.5°C

3. Kinetic Energy & Degrees of Freedom

Fig 8.3

Average Kinetic Energy

From the kinetic theory, the average translational kinetic energy of a single molecule is directly proportional to the absolute temperature and is independent of the nature of the gas (its mass or chemical composition):

$$KE_{\text{per molecule}} = \frac{1}{2}m_0 v_{\text{rms}}^2 = \frac{3}{2} k_B T$$

For one mole of gas, the total translational kinetic energy is:

$$KE_{\text{per mole}} = \frac{3}{2} RT = \frac{3}{2} PV$$

For n moles: KE = n(3/2)RT = (3/2)PV. This is the translational kinetic energy only. The total internal energy also includes rotational and vibrational contributions depending on the molecular structure.

Degrees of Freedom

The degrees of freedom (f) of a system is the total number of independent coordinates or quantities needed to completely specify the position and motion of the system. For gas molecules:

  • Monatomic gas (He, Ne, Ar): f = 3 (three translational DOF only). Total energy per mole = (3/2)RT.
  • Diatomic gas (H₂, N₂, O₂) at moderate temperatures: f = 5 (3 translational + 2 rotational DOF). Total energy per mole = (5/2)RT.
  • Triatomic / Polyatomic gas (CO₂, H₂O, SO₂): f = 6 (3 translational + 3 rotational DOF). Total energy per mole = 3RT.

Law of Equipartition of Energy

The law of equipartition of energy states that in thermal equilibrium, each degree of freedom contributes an equal amount of energy:

$$\text{Energy per DOF per molecule} = \frac{1}{2} k_B T$$ $$\text{Energy per DOF per mole} = \frac{1}{2} RT$$

Therefore, the total internal energy per mole is U = (f/2)RT. This theorem is fundamental because it directly determines the heat capacities Cv and Cp of ideal gases.

Key insight: At the same temperature, all ideal gas molecules have the same average translational KE = (3/2)kBT regardless of mass. However, the total KE depends on the molecular structure (mono/di/polyatomic) because it includes rotational energy. For a diatomic gas, total KE = (5/2)PV, not (3/2)PV.

4. Gas Laws

Fig 8.4

The gas laws describe the relationships between pressure, volume, and temperature of a fixed quantity of an ideal gas. Each law holds one variable constant while examining the relationship between the other two.

Boyle’s Law (Constant Temperature)

At constant temperature, the pressure of a given quantity of gas is inversely proportional to its volume. On a P–V diagram, isotherms appear as rectangular hyperbolas.

P ∝ 1/V   (at constant n, T)
PV = constant   |   P₁V₁ = P₂V₂

Charles’s Law (Constant Pressure)

At constant pressure, the volume of a given quantity of gas is directly proportional to its absolute temperature. The volume increases linearly with temperature on a V–T graph, and all lines extrapolate to zero volume at absolute zero (0 K).

V ∝ T   (at constant n, P)
V/T = constant   |   V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂

Gay-Lussac’s Law (Constant Volume)

At constant volume, the pressure of a given quantity of gas is directly proportional to its absolute temperature. This law is relevant to rigid containers where volume cannot change.

P ∝ T   (at constant n, V)
P/T = constant   |   P₁/T₁ = P₂/T₂

Avogadro’s Law

Equal volumes of all gases at the same temperature and pressure contain equal numbers of molecules: V₁/N₁ = V₂/N₂ at the same P and T. One mole of any ideal gas occupies 22.4 L at STP (273.15 K, 1 atm).

Dalton’s Law of Partial Pressures

The total pressure exerted by a mixture of non-reacting gases equals the sum of the partial pressures of each individual gas. The partial pressure of each gas is the pressure it would exert if it alone occupied the entire volume at the same temperature.

Ptotal = p₁ + p₂ + p₃ + …
Ptotal = (RT/V)(n₁ + n₂ + …)

Mixing gases at different temperatures: When two vessels at different temperatures are connected, total moles are conserved. The final equilibrium pressure is P = (P₁V₁ + P₂V₂)/(V₁ + V₂) and the equilibrium temperature is T = (n₁T₁ + n₂T₂)/(n₁ + n₂).

5. Specific Heat Capacities (Cp and Cv)

Fig 8.5

The specific heat of a gas depends on the conditions under which heat is supplied. Unlike solids and liquids, a gas can be heated at constant volume or constant pressure, leading to two distinct molar heat capacities.

Cv — Molar Heat Capacity at Constant Volume

Cv is the amount of heat required to raise the temperature of one mole of gas by 1 K when the volume is kept constant. Since W = 0 at constant volume, all the heat goes into increasing internal energy:

ΔQ = n Cv ΔT = ΔU   (at constant V)

Cp — Molar Heat Capacity at Constant Pressure

Cp is the amount of heat required to raise the temperature of one mole of gas by 1 K at constant pressure. At constant pressure, the gas expands and does work W = nRΔT, so the gas requires extra heat beyond what is needed to raise its internal energy:

ΔQ = n Cp ΔT   (at constant P)
Cp > Cv always

Mayer’s Relation

The difference between Cp and Cv for any ideal gas is exactly equal to R, the universal gas constant. This elegant result, known as Mayer’s relation, follows directly from the first law of thermodynamics:

Cp − Cv = R

Adiabatic Index (γ)

The ratio γ = Cp/Cv is called the adiabatic exponent or heat capacity ratio. From Cp − Cv = R and γ = Cp/Cv, we can express:

Cv = R/(γ − 1)   |   Cp = γR/(γ − 1)

Values for Different Gas Types

  • Monatomic (He, Ne, Ar): Cv = (3/2)R, Cp = (5/2)R, γ = 5/3 ≈ 1.67
  • Diatomic (H₂, N₂, O₂): Cv = (5/2)R, Cp = (7/2)R, γ = 7/5 = 1.4
  • Triatomic (CO₂, H₂O): Cv = 3R, Cp = 4R, γ = 4/3 ≈ 1.33

Polytropic Process Heat Capacity

For a general polytropic process PVx = constant, the molar heat capacity is:

C = Cv − R/(x − 1) = R/(γ − 1) − R/(x − 1)

Special cases: x = 0 gives C = Cp (isobaric), x = 1 gives C → ∞ (isothermal), x = γ gives C = 0 (adiabatic), and x → ∞ gives C = Cv (isochoric).

Mixture of Gases

For a mixture of ideal gases, the effective heat capacities and γ can be obtained from the individual components. If n₁ moles of gas 1 with Cv1 are mixed with n₂ moles of gas 2 with Cv2:

Cv,mix = (n₁Cv1 + n₂Cv2) / (n₁ + n₂)
Cp,mix = Cv,mix + R
γmix = Cp,mix / Cv,mix
Example 2 — Heating Oxygen Gas

One mole of oxygen (diatomic, γ = 7/5) is heated at constant pressure starting from 0°C. How much heat must be supplied to double the temperature? If the same amount of heat is supplied at constant volume instead, what will the final temperature be?

T₁ = 273 K. For temperature to double: T₂ = 546 K, so ΔT = 273 K.

At constant pressure:
ΔQ = nCpΔT = 1 × (7/2) × 8.3 × 273
Cp = (7/2)R = 3.5 × 8.3 = 29.05 J mol−1 K−1
ΔQ = 29.05 × 273 = 7930.6 J

At constant volume with the same heat:
ΔQ = nCvΔT′
Cv = R/(γ − 1) = 8.3/0.4 = 20.75 J mol−1 K−1
ΔT′ = 7930.6/20.75 = 382.2 K
Tfinal = 273 + 382.2 = 655.2 K

6. First Law of Thermodynamics

Fig 8.6

The first law of thermodynamics is a statement of the conservation of energy as applied to thermodynamic systems. If ΔQ is the heat supplied to the system, W is the work done by the system, and ΔU is the change in internal energy, then:

ΔQ = ΔU + W

In words: the heat supplied to a system goes partly into increasing the internal energy of the system and partly into doing external work against its surroundings.

Sign Convention

  • ΔQ > 0: heat is absorbed by the system; ΔQ < 0: heat is released.
  • W > 0: work is done by the system (expansion); W < 0: work is done on the system (compression).
  • ΔU > 0: internal energy increases (temperature rises for an ideal gas).

Internal Energy as a State Function

Internal energy U is a state function — its value depends only on the current state of the system (temperature, for an ideal gas), not on how the system reached that state. Therefore, ΔU between two states is the same regardless of the path taken. In contrast, both Q and W are path functions and depend on the specific process.

ΔU = nCvΔT = nRΔT/(γ − 1)   (valid for every process)

Work Done by a Gas

The work done by a gas during a volume change is:

W = ∫ P dV   (from V₁ to V₂)

Graphically, W equals the area under the P–V curve. Since W depends on the path, the same change of state can involve different amounts of work depending on the process.

Cyclic Processes

In a cyclic process the system returns to its initial state, so ΔU = 0. By the first law, ΔQ = W — the net heat absorbed equals the net work done in each complete cycle. The work done equals the area enclosed by the cycle on the P–V diagram.

Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics

The zeroth law establishes the concept of thermal equilibrium: if two bodies A and B are each in thermal equilibrium with a third body C, then A and B are in thermal equilibrium with each other. This law provides the logical foundation for temperature measurement using thermometers.

7. Thermodynamic Processes

Fig 8.7

A thermodynamic process is a change in the state of a system characterised by specific constraints on its variables (P, V, T). Below are the four fundamental processes and their associated formulas.

Isothermal Process (T = constant)

In an isothermal process, the temperature remains constant. The system must be in thermal contact with a large heat reservoir, and the process must be conducted slowly enough to maintain thermal equilibrium. Since T is constant for an ideal gas, ΔU = 0 and all the heat absorbed is converted into work: ΔQ = W.

PV = constant   (Boyle’s Law)
W = nRT ln(V₂/V₁) = 2.303 nRT log₁₀(V₂/V₁)
ΔU = 0   |   ΔQ = W

Adiabatic Process (ΔQ = 0)

In an adiabatic process, no heat enters or leaves the system. The system is either thermally insulated or the process occurs so rapidly that heat transfer is negligible (e.g., bursting of a tyre, sound propagation). All the work is done at the expense of internal energy: W = −ΔU. During adiabatic expansion the temperature falls; during adiabatic compression the temperature rises.

PVγ = constant
TVγ−1 = constant
Tγ P1−γ = constant
W = (P₁V₁ − P₂V₂)/(γ − 1) = nR(T₁ − T₂)/(γ − 1) = nCv(T₁ − T₂)

Important: On a P–V diagram, the adiabatic curve is always steeper than the isothermal curve at any given point, because it falls off as PVγ = constant (with γ > 1) rather than PV = constant.

Isobaric Process (P = constant)

At constant pressure, the gas obeys Charles’s law (V ∝ T). During isobaric expansion, both volume and temperature increase, and the gas does positive work. The heat supplied goes into both work and internal energy change:

W = P(V₂ − V₁) = nR(T₂ − T₁)
ΔQ = nCpΔT

Isochoric Process (V = constant)

At constant volume, no expansion or compression occurs, so the work done is zero. The pressure varies directly with temperature (Gay-Lussac’s law). All the heat supplied goes entirely into changing the internal energy:

W = 0   |   ΔQ = ΔU = nCvΔT
P₁/T₁ = P₂/T₂

Free Expansion

Free expansion occurs when a gas expands into a vacuum (external pressure = 0). Since the gas pushes against nothing, W = 0. If the container is insulated, ΔQ = 0 as well, so ΔU = 0 and the temperature remains unchanged for an ideal gas. Free expansion is an irreversible process.

Summary Table

Process   |   Condition   |   Work Done   |   Heat Exchange
Isothermal   |   T = const   |   nRT ln(V₂/V₁)   |   ΔQ = W
Adiabatic   |   ΔQ = 0   |   nR(T₁−T₂)/(γ−1)   |   ΔQ = 0
Isobaric   |   P = const   |   PΔV = nRΔT   |   nCpΔT
Isochoric   |   V = const   |   0   |   nCvΔT

For all processes, the internal energy change is ΔU = nCvΔT, since internal energy is a state function.

Example 3 — Isothermal vs Adiabatic Work

In a cylinder, 2.0 moles of an ideal monatomic gas (γ = 5/3) initially at 1.0 × 106 Pa and 300 K expands until its volume doubles. Compute the work done if the expansion is: (a) isothermal, (b) adiabatic, (c) isobaric.

Given: n = 2 mol, T₁ = 300 K, P₁ = 106 Pa, V₂ = 2V₁, γ = 5/3.
V₁ = nRT₁/P₁ = 2 × 8.3 × 300 / 106 = 4.98 × 10−3 m³

(a) Isothermal:
W = nRT ln(V₂/V₁) = 2.303 × 2 × 8.3 × 300 × log₁₀(2)
W = 2.303 × 4980 × 0.3010 ≈ 3452 J

(b) Adiabatic:
T₂ = T₁(V₁/V₂)γ−1 = 300 × (1/2)2/3
(1/2)2/3 = 1/20.667 ≈ 0.630
T₂ ≈ 300 × 0.630 = 189 K
W = nR(T₁ − T₂)/(γ − 1) = 2 × 8.3 × (300 − 189)/(5/3 − 1)
W = 2 × 8.3 × 111 / 0.667 ≈ 2764 J

(c) Isobaric:
W = PΔV = P₁ × V₁ = 106 × 4.98 × 10−3
W = 4980 J

Comparison: Wisobaric > Wisothermal > Wadiabatic for the same expansion ratio, which is a general result.

8. Carnot Engine & Second Law of Thermodynamics

Fig 8.8

The Carnot Cycle

The Carnot engine is an ideal, reversible heat engine operating between a hot reservoir (source) at temperature T₁ and a cold reservoir (sink) at temperature T₂. The cycle consists of four steps:

  • Step 1 — Isothermal Expansion at T₁: The gas absorbs heat Q₁ from the source and expands isothermally. Work is done by the gas.
  • Step 2 — Adiabatic Expansion: The gas continues to expand without heat exchange. Its temperature drops from T₁ to T₂.
  • Step 3 — Isothermal Compression at T₂: The gas is compressed isothermally, rejecting heat Q₂ to the sink.
  • Step 4 — Adiabatic Compression: The gas is compressed further without heat exchange. Its temperature rises from T₂ back to T₁, completing the cycle.

Carnot Efficiency

The efficiency of the Carnot engine depends only on the temperatures of the source and sink, not on the nature of the working substance. It represents the maximum possible efficiency for any heat engine operating between the same two temperatures.

ηCarnot = 1 − T₂/T₁ = (T₁ − T₂)/T₁
η = W/Q₁ = (Q₁ − Q₂)/Q₁
W = Q₁ − Q₂   |   Q₁/T₁ = Q₂/T₂

The efficiency is 100% only when T₂ = 0 K (absolute zero), which is physically impossible. No real engine can be more efficient than a Carnot engine operating between the same two temperatures.

Second Law of Thermodynamics

The second law of thermodynamics places fundamental restrictions on the direction of thermodynamic processes. It has two equivalent statements:

Kelvin–Planck Statement: It is impossible to construct a heat engine that operates in a cycle and converts all the heat absorbed from a single reservoir entirely into work — some heat must always be rejected to a cold reservoir. In other words, no perfect heat engine exists.

Clausius Statement: It is impossible for heat to flow spontaneously from a colder body to a hotter body without the input of external work. In other words, no perfect refrigerator exists.

Both statements are equivalent — violating one necessarily implies violating the other.

Refrigerator & Coefficient of Performance

A refrigerator is a heat engine operating in reverse. It extracts heat Q₂ from a cold reservoir and rejects heat Q₁ to a hot reservoir, consuming external work W = Q₁ − Q₂. Its performance is measured by the coefficient of performance (COP or β):

β = Q₂/W = Q₂/(Q₁ − Q₂) = T₂/(T₁ − T₂)   (for Carnot refrigerator)

Entropy

Entropy (S) is a thermodynamic state function that quantifies the disorder or randomness of a system. For a reversible process at temperature T:

ΔS = ΔQ / T   (reversible process)

The entropy of an isolated system never decreases: ΔStotal ≥ 0. For a reversible process, ΔStotal = 0; for an irreversible process, ΔStotal > 0. This is another way of stating the second law.

9. Heat Transfer

Fig 8.9

Heat can be transferred from one region to another by three mechanisms: conduction, convection, and radiation. Each mechanism dominates under different physical conditions.

Conduction

Heat conduction is the transfer of thermal energy through a material without any bulk motion of the material itself. It occurs due to molecular collisions and free electron drift, and is most effective in solids, especially metals. The rate of heat flow is governed by Fourier’s law:

dQ/dt = −KA (dT/dx)
For a uniform rod: dQ/dt = KA(T₁ − T₂)/L

Here K is the thermal conductivity of the material (W m−1 K−1), A is the cross-sectional area, dT/dx is the temperature gradient, and L is the length of the rod. The negative sign indicates that heat flows in the direction of decreasing temperature. Metals have high K (good conductors); materials like wood, air, and foam have low K (insulators).

For slabs in series (same heat current), the equivalent thermal resistance is Req = R₁ + R₂ + … where R = L/(KA). For slabs in parallel (same temperature difference), 1/Req = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + …

Convection

Convection is the transfer of heat by actual bulk movement of a fluid (liquid or gas). Natural convection occurs due to buoyancy forces arising from density differences caused by temperature variations — hot fluid rises and cool fluid sinks, creating convection currents. Forced convection involves an external agency (fan, pump) driving the fluid motion. Convection is the primary mode of heat transfer in fluids and is responsible for phenomena such as sea breezes, trade winds, and heating of rooms.

Radiation

Thermal radiation is the emission of electromagnetic waves by all bodies at temperatures above absolute zero. Unlike conduction and convection, radiation requires no medium and can travel through vacuum at the speed of light. The power radiated by a body is given by the Stefan–Boltzmann law:

P = σAT4   (for a perfect black body)
P = εσAT4   (for a grey body, 0 ≤ ε ≤ 1)
σ = 5.67 × 10−8 W m−2 K−4

Here ε is the emissivity of the surface (equal to its absorptivity by Kirchhoff’s law). A perfect black body has ε = 1.

The net rate of heat loss by a body at temperature T in surroundings at temperature Ts is:

Pnet = εσA(T4 − Ts4)

Newton’s Law of Cooling

When the temperature difference between a body and its surroundings is small, the rate of cooling is approximately proportional to that temperature difference. This is Newton’s law of cooling:

dT/dt = −k(T − Ts)
T(t) = Ts + (T₀ − Ts) e−kt

Here Ts is the surrounding temperature, T₀ is the initial body temperature, and k is a positive constant depending on the surface area, emissivity, and heat capacity of the body. This exponential decay formula is widely used in problems involving cooling of hot liquids.

10. Calorimetry & Thermal Expansion

Fig 8.10

Calorimetry

Calorimetry is the science of measuring heat exchange. When two bodies at different temperatures are brought into thermal contact within an insulated system, heat flows from the hotter body to the cooler one until thermal equilibrium is reached. The principle of calorimetry states:

Heat lost by hot body = Heat gained by cold body

Specific Heat Capacity

The specific heat capacity (c) of a substance is the amount of heat required to raise the temperature of 1 kg of that substance by 1 K (or 1°C). The heat absorbed or released during a temperature change is:

Q = mcΔT

The water equivalent of a body is the mass of water that would absorb or release the same amount of heat as the body for a given temperature change: W = mc/cwater.

Latent Heat & Phase Changes

During a phase change (solid → liquid or liquid → gas), the temperature remains constant even though heat is being supplied. All the energy goes into breaking or forming intermolecular bonds. The latent heat of fusion (Lf) is the heat per unit mass needed to convert a solid to liquid at the melting point. The latent heat of vaporisation (Lv) is for the liquid-to-gas transition at the boiling point.

Q = mL   (L = Lf for fusion, Lv for vaporisation)
Lf(ice) = 3.34 × 105 J/kg   |   Lv(water) = 2.26 × 106 J/kg

Thermal Expansion

When a solid is heated, its dimensions increase. The fractional change is proportional to the temperature change, governed by coefficients of thermal expansion. These coefficients are related as β ≈ 2α and γ ≈ 3α for isotropic materials.

Linear: ΔL = LαΔT   (α = coefficient of linear expansion)
Area: ΔA = 2α A ΔT = β A ΔT
Volume: ΔV = 3α V ΔT = γ V ΔT

For liquids, only volume expansion is meaningful. The apparent expansion of a liquid in a container is the difference between the actual expansion of the liquid and the expansion of the container: γapparent = γliquid − γcontainer.

Temperature Scales

Three principal temperature scales are used in physics. The Celsius scale sets 0°C at the freezing point of water and 100°C at its boiling point. The Kelvin scale is the SI unit of temperature, with 0 K at absolute zero. Conversions:

T(K) = T(°C) + 273.15
T(°F) = (9/5) T(°C) + 32   |   T(°C) = (5/9)[T(°F) − 32]
