Heat & Thermodynamics

Chapter 08 — Grade XI • Thermal Physics

1. Heat & Temperature

Heat is energy in transit due to a temperature difference, while temperature is a measure of the average kinetic energy of molecules. The Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics establishes the concept of thermal equilibrium: if two systems are each in thermal equilibrium with a third system, then they are in thermal equilibrium with each other. This law provides the foundation for temperature measurement using thermometers.

Temperature Scales

Three principal temperature scales are used in physics. The Celsius scale sets 0°C at the freezing point of water and 100°C at its boiling point. The Fahrenheit scale uses 32°F and 212°F for the same reference points. The Kelvin scale is the SI unit of temperature, with 0 K representing absolute zero — the lowest possible temperature where molecular motion ceases entirely.

T(K) = T(°C) + 273.15
T(°F) = (9/5) T(°C) + 32
T(°C) = (5/9) [T(°F) − 32]

Thermal Expansion

When a solid is heated, its dimensions increase. The fractional change is proportional to the temperature change, governed by the coefficient of thermal expansion. For linear expansion, the change in length is ΔL = LαΔT, where α is the coefficient of linear expansion. For area expansion, ΔA = 2αAΔT, and for volume expansion, ΔV = 3αVΔT (equivalently ΔV = γVΔT, where γ = 3α for isotropic solids).

ΔL = LαΔT   (linear expansion)
ΔA = 2α A ΔT   (area expansion)
ΔV = γ V ΔT   (volume expansion, γ ≈ 3α)

2. Calorimetry

Calorimetry is the science of measuring heat exchange. When two bodies at different temperatures are brought into thermal contact within an insulated system, heat flows from the hotter body to the cooler one until thermal equilibrium is reached. The principle of calorimetry states that heat lost by the hot body equals heat gained by the cold body.

Specific Heat Capacity

The specific heat capacity (c) of a substance is the amount of heat required to raise the temperature of 1 kg of that substance by 1 K (or 1°C). The heat absorbed or released during a temperature change is given by Q = mcΔT.

Latent Heat & Phase Changes

During a phase change (solid → liquid or liquid → gas), the temperature remains constant even though heat is being supplied. The latent heat of fusion (Lf) is the heat per unit mass required to change a solid into a liquid at its melting point, while the latent heat of vaporisation (Lv) applies to the liquid-gas transition. The heat involved is Q = mL.

The water equivalent of a body is the mass of water that would absorb or release the same amount of heat as the body for a given temperature change. It equals mc/cwater, where c is the specific heat of the body.

Q = mcΔT   (temperature change)
Q = mL   (phase change, L = latent heat)
Water equivalent W = mc / cwater
Solved Example 1

A 200 g copper block (c = 390 J/kg·K) at 150°C is dropped into 500 g of water (c = 4200 J/kg·K) at 20°C in a calorimeter. Find the final equilibrium temperature.

Heat lost by copper = Heat gained by water
mCu cCu (TCu − Tf) = mw cw (Tf − Tw)
0.2 × 390 × (150 − Tf) = 0.5 × 4200 × (Tf − 20)
78 (150 − Tf) = 2100 (Tf − 20)
11700 − 78 Tf = 2100 Tf − 42000
53700 = 2178 Tf
Tf ≈ 24.7°C

3. Kinetic Theory of Gases

The kinetic theory of gases models a gas as a large number of tiny molecules in constant random motion. It provides a microscopic explanation for macroscopic properties like pressure and temperature.

Ideal Gas Equation

An ideal gas obeys the equation PV = nRT, where P is pressure, V is volume, n is the number of moles, R = 8.314 J/(mol·K) is the universal gas constant, and T is the absolute temperature. For N molecules, PV = NkBT, where kB = R/NA is the Boltzmann constant.

Assumptions of Kinetic Theory

  • Gas consists of a very large number of identical molecules in random motion.
  • The size of molecules is negligible compared to the average intermolecular separation.
  • Molecules exert no forces on each other except during brief elastic collisions.
  • Collisions with the walls of the container are perfectly elastic.
  • The time of collision is negligible compared to the time between collisions.

Pressure from Molecular Motion

The pressure exerted by an ideal gas can be derived from the momentum transfer during molecular collisions with the walls. The result is P = (1/3)ρv²rms, where ρ is the gas density and vrms is the root mean square speed of the molecules.

Molecular Speeds

The Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution gives three characteristic speeds for gas molecules. The RMS speed is vrms = √(3RT/M), the mean speed is vavg = √(8RT/πM), and the most probable speed is vmp = √(2RT/M), where M is the molar mass. Their ratio is vmp : vavg : vrms = 1 : 1.128 : 1.224.

PV = nRT   |   P = ⅓ρv²rms
vrms = √(3RT/M)   |   vavg = √(8RT/πM)   |   vmp = √(2RT/M)

4. Degrees of Freedom & Equipartition of Energy

The degrees of freedom (f) of a gas molecule represent the number of independent ways in which the molecule can possess energy. By the law of equipartition of energy, each degree of freedom contributes ½kBT to the average energy of one molecule, or ½RT per mole.

Degrees of Freedom by Molecular Type

  • Monatomic (He, Ne, Ar): 3 translational DOF ⇒ f = 3
  • Diatomic (H₂, O₂, N₂) at moderate T: 3 translational + 2 rotational DOF ⇒ f = 5
  • Polyatomic / Non-linear (H₂O, CH₄): 3 translational + 3 rotational DOF ⇒ f = 6 (vibrational modes add at high T)

Molar Heat Capacities

Using the equipartition theorem, the molar heat capacity at constant volume is Cv = (f/2)R and at constant pressure is Cp = Cv + R = ((f+2)/2)R. The heat capacity ratio (adiabatic index) is γ = Cp/Cv = (f+2)/f.

Cv = (f/2)R   |   Cp = Cv + R = ((f+2)/2)R
γ = Cp/Cv = (f+2)/f
Monatomic: Cv = 3R/2, γ = 5/3 ≈ 1.67
Diatomic: Cv = 5R/2, γ = 7/5 = 1.4
Polyatomic: Cv = 3R, γ = 4/3 ≈ 1.33

5. First Law of Thermodynamics

The first law of thermodynamics is a statement of energy conservation applied to thermal systems. It states that the change in internal energy of a system equals the heat supplied to it minus the work done by the system on its surroundings:

ΔU = Q − W

Here ΔU is the change in internal energy, Q is the heat added to the system, and W is the work done by the system. Internal energy is a state function — it depends only on the current state (temperature, for an ideal gas) and not on how the system reached that state.

Sign Convention

  • Q > 0: heat is absorbed by the system; Q < 0: heat is released.
  • W > 0: work is done by the system (expansion); W < 0: work is done on the system (compression).
  • ΔU > 0: internal energy (and temperature, for an ideal gas) increases.

For an ideal gas, the internal energy depends only on temperature: U = nCvT, and therefore ΔU = nCvΔT for any process.

6. Thermodynamic Processes

Isothermal Process (T = constant)

In an isothermal process the temperature stays constant, so ΔU = 0 and therefore Q = W. For an ideal gas, PV = constant (Boyle’s law). The work done is:

W = nRT ln(V₂/V₁)   (isothermal)

Adiabatic Process (Q = 0)

In an adiabatic process no heat enters or leaves the system. All the work done comes at the expense of internal energy: ΔU = −W. The relations governing an adiabatic process for an ideal gas are:

PVγ = constant
TVγ−1 = constant
Tγ P1−γ = constant
W = (P₁V₁ − P₂V₂) / (γ − 1) = nCv(T₁ − T₂)

Isobaric Process (P = constant)

At constant pressure, the work done by the gas is simply W = PΔV = P(V₂ − V₁). The heat supplied is Q = nCpΔT.

W = PΔV   |   Q = nCpΔT   (isobaric)

Isochoric Process (V = constant)

At constant volume, no expansion or compression occurs, so W = 0. All the heat supplied goes into changing the internal energy: Q = ΔU = nCvΔT.

W = 0   |   Q = ΔU = nCvΔT   (isochoric)
Solved Example 2

Two moles of an ideal diatomic gas (γ = 7/5) at 300 K undergo an adiabatic expansion where the volume doubles. Find the final temperature.

For an adiabatic process: TVγ−1 = constant
T₁ V₁γ−1 = T₂ V₂γ−1
T₂ = T₁ (V₁/V₂)γ−1
T₂ = 300 × (1/2)0.4
(1/2)0.4 = 2−0.4 ≈ 0.7579
T₂ ≈ 300 × 0.758 ≈ 227.4 K

7. Second Law of Thermodynamics & Carnot Engine

Kelvin-Planck Statement

It is impossible to construct a heat engine that operates in a cycle and converts all the heat absorbed from a single reservoir entirely into work — some heat must always be rejected to a cold reservoir.

Clausius Statement

It is impossible for heat to flow spontaneously from a colder body to a hotter body without the expenditure of external work.

Carnot Cycle & Efficiency

The Carnot engine is an ideal reversible engine operating between two temperature reservoirs. It consists of four steps: isothermal expansion (absorb Q₁ at T₁), adiabatic expansion (cool from T₁ to T₂), isothermal compression (reject Q₂ at T₂), and adiabatic compression (heat from T₂ back to T₁). The Carnot efficiency sets an upper bound for all real engines operating between the same two temperatures:

ηCarnot = 1 − T₂/T₁ = (T₁ − T₂)/T₁
η = W/Q₁ = (Q₁ − Q₂)/Q₁

Refrigerator & Coefficient of Performance

A refrigerator extracts heat Q₂ from a cold reservoir and rejects Q₁ to a hot reservoir, consuming work W = Q₁ − Q₂. Its coefficient of performance (COP) is:

COP = Q₂/W = Q₂/(Q₁ − Q₂) = T₂/(T₁ − T₂)   (Carnot refrigerator)
Solved Example 3

A Carnot engine operates between a source at 600 K and a sink at 300 K. If the engine absorbs 1000 J of heat per cycle, find the efficiency and work output per cycle.

η = 1 − T₂/T₁ = 1 − 300/600 = 0.5 = 50%
W = η × Q₁ = 0.5 × 1000 = 500 J
Q₂ = Q₁ − W = 1000 − 500 = 500 J (rejected to sink)

8. Heat Transfer

Conduction

Heat conduction is the transfer of thermal energy through a material without any bulk motion of the material itself. It occurs due to molecular collisions and is most effective in solids, especially metals. The rate of heat flow through a uniform rod is given by Fourier’s law:

dQ/dt = KA (dT/dx)
For a uniform rod: dQ/dt = KA (T₁ − T₂) / L

Here K is the thermal conductivity of the material, A is the cross-sectional area, dT/dx is the temperature gradient, and L is the length of the rod.

Convection

Convection is the transfer of heat by actual bulk movement of a fluid (liquid or gas). Natural convection occurs due to buoyancy forces from density differences caused by temperature variations. Forced convection involves an external agency (fan, pump) driving the fluid flow. Convection is the primary mode of heat transfer in fluids.

Radiation

Thermal radiation is the emission of electromagnetic waves by all bodies at any temperature above absolute zero. Unlike conduction and convection, radiation does not require any medium and can travel through a vacuum.

P = σAT4   (Stefan-Boltzmann law, for a black body)
P = σAεT4   (for a grey body, ε = emissivity)
σ = 5.67 × 10−8 W m−2 K−4

Newton’s Law of Cooling

For small temperature differences between a body and its surroundings, the rate of cooling is approximately proportional to the temperature difference:

dT/dt = −k (T − Ts)
T(t) = Ts + (T₀ − Ts) e−kt

Here Ts is the surrounding temperature, T₀ is the initial temperature of the body, and k is a positive constant that depends on the surface area, emissivity, and heat capacity.

Key Formulas Summary

PV = nRT   |   vrms = √(3RT/M)
ΔU = Q − W   |   ηCarnot = 1 − T₂/T₁
ΔL = LαΔT   |   Q = mcΔT   |   Q = mL
Cv = (f/2)R   |   Cp = Cv + R   |   γ = Cp/Cv
P = σAT4   |   dQ/dt = KA(dT/dx)
