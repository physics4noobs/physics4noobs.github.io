Wave Optics

Grade XII — Chapter 08
Huygens' Principle Interference Diffraction Polarization

1. Huygens' Principle

Wavefronts

A wavefront is the locus of all points in a medium that are in the same phase of oscillation at any given instant. Wavefronts can be:

  • Spherical wavefronts: Produced by a point source. The wavefronts are concentric spheres expanding outward from the source.
  • Cylindrical wavefronts: Produced by a linear source (e.g., a slit illuminated by a point source).
  • Plane wavefronts: At very large distances from the source, a small portion of a spherical wavefront can be treated as a plane. Parallel rays produce plane wavefronts.

The direction of propagation of a wave is always perpendicular to the wavefront. Rays are perpendicular to wavefronts.

Huygens' Construction

Huygens proposed that every point on a given wavefront acts as a source of secondary wavelets, which spread out in all directions with the speed of the wave in that medium. The new wavefront at a later time is the forward envelope (tangent surface) of all these secondary wavelets.

  • Each point on the existing wavefront emits a spherical secondary wavelet.
  • After time Δt, each wavelet has radius vΔt (where v is the wave speed).
  • The new wavefront is the surface tangent to all the forward-propagating wavelets.
  • The backward-propagating wavelets are ignored (this was a weakness of the original theory, later resolved by Kirchhoff's mathematical treatment).

Derivation of Laws of Reflection

Using Huygens' principle, consider a plane wavefront incident on a flat reflecting surface. When one edge of the wavefront hits the surface, it starts emitting secondary wavelets. By the time the other edge reaches the surface, the first wavelet has expanded to a radius equal to the distance the incident wavefront travelled. Constructing the reflected wavefront as the tangent to all these wavelets yields the result that the angle of incidence equals the angle of reflection, and all rays lie in the same plane.

Derivation of Laws of Refraction (Snell's Law)

Consider a plane wavefront passing from medium 1 (speed v1) to medium 2 (speed v2). When one edge of the wavefront enters the second medium, it slows down (if v2 < v1). The wavelet in medium 2 has a smaller radius than it would have in medium 1. Constructing the refracted wavefront as the tangent to the wavelets in medium 2 gives:

sin θ1 / sin θ2 = v1 / v2 = n2 / n1

This is Snell's law: n1 sin θ1 = n2 sin θ2

This derivation also shows that light travels slower in a denser medium (higher refractive index), which is consistent with the wave theory. This was a key prediction that distinguished the wave theory from Newton's corpuscular theory, which required light to travel faster in a denser medium.

2. Interference of Light

Coherent Sources

Two sources are said to be coherent if they emit light waves of the same frequency with a constant phase difference. Ordinary light sources (bulbs, candles) are incoherent because they emit light in random bursts with random phases. To obtain coherent sources, we must derive two sources from a single source. Methods include:

  • Young's double slit (division of wavefront)
  • Lloyd's mirror
  • Fresnel's biprism
  • Thin film interference (division of amplitude)

Principle of Superposition

When two or more waves meet at a point, the resultant displacement at that point is the algebraic sum of the individual displacements. This leads to the phenomenon of interference: when two coherent waves superpose, the intensity at each point depends on the phase difference between the waves at that point.

Resultant intensity: I = I1 + I2 + 2√(I1 I2) cos φ

For equal intensities I1 = I2 = I0:
I = 4I0 cos²(φ/2)

where φ = phase difference between the two waves at the point

Young's Double Slit Experiment (YDSE)

Thomas Young's experiment (1801) provided the first conclusive evidence for the wave nature of light. A monochromatic source illuminates two narrow slits S1 and S2 separated by distance d. The interference pattern is observed on a screen placed at distance D from the slits (D >> d).

Path Difference and Fringe Conditions

At a point P on the screen at distance y from the central axis, the path difference between waves from S1 and S2 is approximately:

Path difference: Δx = yd / D

Phase difference: φ = 2π Δx / λ = 2π yd / (λD)

Conditions for Maxima and Minima

Constructive interference (bright fringes):
Δx = nλ   (n = 0, ±1, ±2, ...)
yn = nλD / d

Destructive interference (dark fringes):
Δx = (2n − 1)λ/2   (n = 1, 2, 3, ...)
yn = (2n − 1)λD / (2d)

Fringe Width

The fringe width is the distance between two consecutive bright (or dark) fringes. It is uniform across the screen (for small angles).

Fringe width: β = λD / d

Key observations:

  • β increases with wavelength λ (red fringes are wider than violet).
  • β increases with screen distance D.
  • β decreases with slit separation d.
  • The central fringe (n = 0) is always bright and is equidistant from both slits.
  • All bright fringes have the same maximum intensity Imax = 4I0.
  • All dark fringes have zero intensity (for perfectly coherent, equal-intensity sources).
  • The total energy is conserved — energy is redistributed from minima to maxima.
Solved Example 1

In a Young's double slit experiment, the slit separation is 0.5 mm, the screen is 1.5 m away, and monochromatic light of wavelength 600 nm is used. Calculate the fringe width and the distance of the 5th bright fringe from the central maximum.

Solution:
d = 0.5 mm = 5 × 10−4 m, D = 1.5 m, λ = 600 nm = 6 × 10−7 m

Fringe width: β = λD/d = (6 × 10−7 × 1.5) / (5 × 10−4)
β = 9 × 10−7 / 5 × 10−4 = 1.8 × 10−3 m = 1.8 mm

Distance of 5th bright fringe: y5 = 5β = 5 × 1.8 = 9 mm

Solved Example 2

In YDSE, two slits are illuminated by a monochromatic source. The intensity at the central maximum is I0. What is the intensity at a point where the path difference equals λ/3?

Solution:
At the central maximum, φ = 0, so I0 = 4I (where I is the intensity from each slit).

Path difference = λ/3, so phase difference φ = 2π/3

I = 4I cos²(φ/2) = 4I cos²(π/3) = 4I × (1/2)² = 4I × 1/4 = I

Since I0 = 4I, we get I = I0/4.
The intensity at the given point is I0/4.

3. Diffraction of Light

What is Diffraction?

Diffraction is the bending of light waves around obstacles or through narrow openings. It is a characteristic property of all waves and becomes significant when the size of the obstacle or aperture is comparable to the wavelength of the wave. Diffraction provides strong evidence for the wave nature of light.

Single Slit Diffraction

When monochromatic light passes through a single narrow slit of width a, a diffraction pattern is observed on a distant screen. The pattern consists of a bright central maximum flanked by alternating dark and bright fringes of decreasing intensity.

Conditions for Minima

The dark fringes (minima) occur at angles where:

a sin θ = nλ   (n = ±1, ±2, ±3, ...)

Position of n-th minimum on screen: yn = nλD/a

(where D = distance from slit to screen, and small angle approximation sin θ ≈ tan θ = y/D)

Note: n = 0 does NOT correspond to a minimum — it is the central maximum.

Central Maximum

The central maximum extends from the first minimum on one side to the first minimum on the other side. Its angular width and linear width are:

Angular half-width of central maximum: sin θ = λ/a   (first minimum)
Full angular width: 2θ = 2λ/a (for small angles)

Linear width of central maximum: W = 2λD/a

The central maximum is twice as wide as any other secondary maximum and contains about 84% of the total light intensity. As the slit width a decreases, the central maximum broadens (more spreading). As a increases, the pattern narrows and approaches geometric optics.

Conditions for Secondary Maxima

The secondary maxima occur approximately at:

a sin θ ≈ (2n + 1)λ/2   (n = 1, 2, 3, ...)

Intensity ratio: I1 : I2 : I3 ... ≈ 1 : 1/22 : 1/61 ... (secondary maxima are much dimmer than the central maximum)

Resolving Power

The resolving power of an optical instrument is its ability to distinguish between two closely spaced objects. According to Rayleigh's criterion, two point sources are just resolved when the central maximum of the diffraction pattern of one falls on the first minimum of the other.

Limit of resolution of a telescope: Δθ = 1.22 λ/D
(where D = diameter of the objective lens/mirror)

Resolving power = 1/Δθ = D/(1.22λ)

Limit of resolution of a microscope: dmin = 1.22λ/(2n sinβ) = 0.61λ/NA
(where NA = n sinβ is the numerical aperture)

A larger aperture (larger D) gives better resolution (smaller Δθ). Shorter wavelengths also improve resolution, which is why electron microscopes (using de Broglie wavelength of electrons) have much higher resolving power than optical microscopes.

Solved Example 3

Light of wavelength 500 nm falls on a slit of width 0.2 mm. The screen is placed 2 m from the slit. Find the width of the central maximum and the position of the first secondary maximum.

Solution:
λ = 500 nm = 5 × 10−7 m, a = 0.2 mm = 2 × 10−4 m, D = 2 m

Width of central maximum: W = 2λD/a = 2 × 5 × 10−7 × 2 / (2 × 10−4)
W = 2 × 10−6 / 2 × 10−4 = 1 × 10−2 m = 10 mm

Position of first secondary maximum (approximately at 3λ/2 path difference):
a sin θ ≈ 3λ/2, so sin θ ≈ 3λ/(2a)
y = D sin θ ≈ 3λD/(2a) = 3 × 5 × 10−7 × 2 / (2 × 2 × 10−4)
y = 3 × 10−6 / 4 × 10−4 = 7.5 × 10−3 m = 7.5 mm from the central maximum

4. Polarization of Light

Polarized vs Unpolarized Light

In an ordinary light wave, the electric field vector vibrates in all possible directions perpendicular to the direction of propagation. Such light is called unpolarized light. When the electric field vector is restricted to vibrate in a single plane, the light is called plane-polarized (or linearly polarized) light. The plane containing the electric field vector and the direction of propagation is called the plane of polarization.

Since polarization involves the direction of the electric field oscillation, it is a property exclusively of transverse waves. Longitudinal waves (like sound) cannot be polarized. The observation of polarization of light was a key piece of evidence proving that light is a transverse wave.

Methods of Polarization

  • By selective absorption (polaroids): Certain materials preferentially absorb one component of the electric field while transmitting the perpendicular component.
  • By reflection: When unpolarized light is reflected from a surface at the Brewster angle, the reflected light is completely polarized.
  • By refraction: Partial polarization occurs during refraction.
  • By scattering: Sunlight scattered by atmospheric molecules is partially polarized (this is why the sky appears polarized when viewed at 90° to the sun).
  • By double refraction: Certain crystals (calcite, quartz) split an incident ray into two polarized rays with perpendicular polarizations (ordinary and extraordinary rays).

Malus's Law

When plane-polarized light of intensity I0 passes through a second polaroid (called the analyser) whose transmission axis makes an angle θ with the polarization direction of the incident light, the transmitted intensity is:

I = I0 cos²θ   (Malus's Law)

When θ = 0°: I = I0 (maximum transmission)
When θ = 90°: I = 0 (no transmission — crossed polaroids)
When θ = 45°: I = I0/2

If unpolarized light of intensity I passes through a single polaroid, the transmitted intensity is I/2 (half the incident intensity), since on average, cos²θ averages to 1/2 over all angles.

Brewster's Angle

When unpolarized light strikes a transparent surface, the reflected light is partially polarized. At a particular angle of incidence called Brewster's angle (θB), the reflected light is completely polarized (with the electric field vector parallel to the surface). At Brewster's angle, the reflected and refracted rays are perpendicular to each other.

tan θB = n   (Brewster's Law)

where n = refractive index of the reflecting medium relative to the incident medium.

At Brewster's angle: θB + θr = 90° (reflected and refracted rays are perpendicular)

For glass (n = 1.5): θB = tan−1(1.5) ≈ 56.3°. For water (n = 1.33): θB ≈ 53.1°.

Polaroids — Applications

  • Sunglasses: Polaroid sunglasses reduce glare by blocking horizontally polarized light reflected from surfaces like roads and water.
  • LCD screens: Liquid crystal displays use two polaroids with a liquid crystal layer in between to control light transmission.
  • Photography: Polarizing filters reduce reflections and enhance contrast.
  • 3D movies: Different polarizations are used for left and right eye images.
  • Stress analysis: Photoelasticity uses polarized light to visualize stress patterns in transparent materials.
Solved Example 4

Unpolarized light of intensity 32 W/m² passes through three polaroids. The transmission axis of the first polaroid is vertical. The second makes an angle of 30° with the first, and the third makes an angle of 60° with the first (i.e., 30° with the second). Find the intensity of light emerging from the system.

Solution:
After the first polaroid (unpolarized to polarized):
I1 = I/2 = 32/2 = 16 W/m²

After the second polaroid (angle 30° with the first):
I2 = I1 cos²30° = 16 × (3/4) = 12 W/m²

After the third polaroid (angle 30° with the second):
I3 = I2 cos²30° = 12 × (3/4) = 9 W/m²

The emerging intensity is 9 W/m².
