1. Electric Charge

Fig 11.1

What is Electric Charge?

Electric charge is a fundamental intrinsic property of matter that causes it to experience a force when placed near other electrically charged matter. There are exactly two types of electric charge: positive and negative. Like charges repel each other while unlike charges attract. The SI unit of charge is the coulomb (C). Charge can be produced through three primary mechanisms: friction (electrons transfer between rubbed bodies), electrostatic induction (charges redistribute in conductors near a charged body), and conduction (direct contact between a charged and an uncharged body).

Fundamental Properties of Electric Charge

  • Additivity: Charges are scalars and add algebraically. The total charge of a system is the algebraic sum of all individual charges: Q = q1 + q2 + q3 + ...
  • Conservation: The total electric charge of an isolated system is always conserved. Charge can neither be created nor destroyed — it can only be transferred from one body to another.
  • Quantization: Charge always occurs in integral multiples of the elementary charge e = 1.6 × 10−19 C. Thus, q = ne where n is an integer (positive or negative).
  • Relativistic invariance: The charge on a body does not change with its speed. It is a Lorentz scalar.

Conductors and Insulators

Conductors are materials that allow electric charge to flow freely through them due to the presence of free electrons (e.g., metals, electrolytic solutions). Insulators (dielectrics) do not allow free flow of charge because their electrons are tightly bound to their atoms (e.g., glass, rubber, plastic). This distinction plays a central role in electrostatic phenomena and in the design of capacitors.

Methods of Charging

  • Friction: When two dissimilar materials are rubbed together, electrons transfer from one to the other. The body that loses electrons becomes positively charged and the one that gains becomes negatively charged.
  • Conduction: When a charged body is brought into direct contact with an uncharged conductor, charge flows until both reach the same potential.
  • Induction: A charged body brought near (but not touching) a conductor causes redistribution of charges. By grounding the conductor while the charged body is nearby and then removing the ground, a net charge opposite to the inducing charge remains.
$$q = ne \quad (n = 0, \pm 1, \pm 2, \ldots)$$ $$e = 1.6 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}$$ $$-e = -1.6 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}$$ $$+e = +1.6 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}$$

2. Coulomb's Law & Superposition

Fig 11.2

Statement of Coulomb's Law

Coulomb's law states that the electrostatic force between two stationary point charges is directly proportional to the product of the magnitudes of the charges and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them. The force acts along the line joining the two charges and obeys Newton's third law.

$$F = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \cdot \frac{|q_1 q_2|}{r^2}$$ $$\frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} = k = 9 \times 10^9 \text{ N m}^2 \text{ C}^{-2}$$ $$\epsilon_0 = 8.854 \times 10^{-12} \text{ C}^2 \text{ N}^{-1} \text{ m}^{-2}$$

Vector Form of Coulomb's Law

The force on charge q1 due to charge q2 in vector form is:

$$F_{12} = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \cdot \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2} \quad \hat{r}_{12}$$

Here r̂12 is the unit vector from q2 to q1. If both charges have the same sign, the force is repulsive (directed outward); if opposite signs, the force is attractive.

Comparison with Gravitational Force

Coulomb's law is structurally similar to Newton's law of gravitation. Both are inverse-square laws. However, there is a key difference: gravitational charge (mass) is always positive and the force is always attractive, whereas electric charge comes in two varieties and the force can be either attractive or repulsive.

Superposition Principle

The total force on a charge due to several other charges is the vector sum of the individual forces exerted by each charge independently. The force between any two charges is unaffected by the presence of other charges. This powerful principle allows us to handle any charge configuration by summing pairwise interactions.

$$F_{\text{net}} = F_1 + F_2 + F_3 + \ldots = \sum \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \cdot \frac{q q_i}{r_i^2} \quad \hat{r}_i$$

Effect of Dielectric Medium

When charges are placed in a medium with dielectric constant (relative permittivity) κ, the electrostatic force is reduced by a factor of κ:

$$F_{\text{medium}} = \frac{F_{\text{vacuum}}}{\kappa} = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0\kappa} \cdot \frac{|q_1 q_2|}{r^2}$$ $$\kappa = 1 \text{ (vacuum/air)}; \quad \kappa > 1 \text{ (all other media)}$$
Example 1 — Coulomb's Law and Superposition

Two point charges q1 = +3 μC and q2 = −4 μC are placed 30 cm apart in vacuum. Find the magnitude of the electrostatic force between them. Also find the force if the medium has a dielectric constant κ = 2.

Solution:
Given: q1 = 3 × 10−6 C, q2 = 4 × 10−6 C, r = 0.30 m

Using Coulomb's law:
F = k|q1q2| / r² = (9 × 109)(3 × 10−6)(4 × 10−6) / (0.30)²
F = (9 × 109)(12 × 10−12) / 0.09
F = 0.108 / 0.09 = 1.2 N (attractive, since charges are opposite)

In medium with κ = 2:
Fmedium = F / κ = 1.2 / 2 = 0.6 N

3. Electric Field

Fig 11.3

Definition of Electric Field

The electric field at a point in space is defined as the electrostatic force experienced by a unit positive test charge placed at that point. The test charge must be vanishingly small so that it does not disturb the source charge distribution. The electric field is a vector quantity that describes the strength and direction of the electric influence at every point in space.

$$E = \frac{F}{q_0}$$ $$\text{SI unit: N/C or V/m}$$ $$F = qE$$

Electric Field Due to a Point Charge

The electric field at a distance r from a point charge Q is directed radially outward (for positive Q) or radially inward (for negative Q). The field magnitude decreases as the inverse square of the distance.

$$E = \frac{Q}{4\pi\epsilon_0 r^2} = \frac{kQ}{r^2}$$ $$\vec{E} = \frac{Q}{4\pi\epsilon_0 r^2} \hat{r}$$ $$\text{Direction: away from } +Q, \text{ toward } -Q$$

Charged Particle Motion in a Uniform Electric Field

A charged particle in a uniform electric field experiences a constant force and hence undergoes constant acceleration a = qE/m. If the initial velocity is parallel to the field (or zero), the motion is along a straight line. If the initial velocity is perpendicular to the field, the trajectory becomes parabolic, analogous to projectile motion under gravity.

$$a = \frac{qE}{m}$$ $$x = ut, \quad y = \frac{1}{2}\frac{qE}{m}t^2$$ $$y = \frac{qE}{2mu^2} x^2 \quad \text{(parabola)}$$

4. Electric Field Lines

Fig 11.4

Concept of Field Lines

Electric field lines (or lines of force) are imaginary curves whose tangent at any point gives the direction of the electric field at that point. They were introduced by Michael Faraday as a visual tool to represent the electric field pattern. The density of field lines (number per unit cross-sectional area) is proportional to the field strength.

Properties of Electric Field Lines

  • Lines originate from positive charges and terminate on negative charges
  • The tangent to a field line at any point gives the direction of E at that point
  • Field lines never intersect each other (otherwise the field would have two directions at the same point)
  • Lines are always perpendicular to the surface of a conductor
  • Closer spacing indicates stronger field; wider spacing indicates weaker field
  • In a charge-free region, field lines are continuous curves
  • For two equal like charges, a null point exists at the midpoint where E = 0
  • For two opposite charges (+q and −q), field lines go directly from positive to negative
$$\vec{E} = \vec{E}_1 + \vec{E}_2 + \vec{E}_3 + \ldots \quad \text{(vector sum)}$$ $$\text{Units: N/C or V/m}$$

5. Electric Dipole

Fig 11.5

Definition and Dipole Moment

An electric dipole consists of two equal and opposite charges +q and −q separated by a small distance 2ℓ. The dipole moment p is a vector quantity of magnitude p = q × 2ℓ, directed from the negative charge to the positive charge. The dipole is the simplest system that produces a non-zero electric field at large distances while having zero net charge.

$$p = q \times 2\ell \quad \text{(vector from } -q \text{ to } +q \text{)}$$ $$\text{SI unit: C} \cdot \text{m}$$

Electric Field Due to a Dipole

The electric field of a dipole varies with both position and angle. For a short dipole (r >> ℓ), the field at key locations is:

$$E_{\text{axial}} = \frac{2p}{4\pi\epsilon_0 r^3} = \frac{2kp}{r^3}$$ $$E_{\text{eq}} = \frac{p}{4\pi\epsilon_0 r^3} = \frac{kp}{r^3}$$ $$E = \frac{p}{4\pi\epsilon_0 r^3} \sqrt{1 + 3\cos^2\theta}$$ $$E_{\text{axial}} = 2 \times E_{\text{equatorial}}$$

Dipole in a Uniform External Electric Field

When an electric dipole is placed in a uniform external field E, it experiences a torque that tends to align it with the field. There is no net translational force in a uniform field. In a non-uniform field, there is also a net force on the dipole.

$$\tau = pE\sin\theta \quad \text{or} \quad \vec{\tau} = \vec{p} \times \vec{E}$$ $$U = -pE\cos\theta = -\vec{p} \cdot \vec{E}$$ $$\theta = \text{angle between } \vec{p} \text{ and } \vec{E}$$
  • θ = 0°: Stable equilibrium — minimum energy U = −pE, zero torque
  • θ = 180°: Unstable equilibrium — maximum energy U = +pE, zero torque
  • θ = 90°: Maximum torque τmax = pE
Example 2 — Dipole in External Field

A dipole with moment p = 6.2 × 10−30 C·m is placed in an external field of 1 × 105 N/C. Find the maximum torque on the dipole and the work required to rotate it from θ = 0° to θ = 90°.

Solution:
Given: p = 6.2 × 10−30 C·m, E = 1 × 105 N/C

Maximum torque (at θ = 90°):
τmax = pE sin90° = pE = (6.2 × 10−30)(105)
τmax = 6.2 × 10−25 N·m

Work done to rotate from 0° to 90°:
W = ΔU = U(90°) − U(0°) = (−pE cos90°) − (−pE cos0°)
W = 0 − (−pE) = pE = 6.2 × 10−25 J

6. Continuous Charge Distributions

Fig 11.6

Types of Charge Distributions

When charge is spread over a body rather than concentrated at discrete points, we describe it using charge densities. The total field is obtained by dividing the charge into infinitesimal elements dq, treating each as a point charge, and integrating.

$$\lambda = \frac{dq}{d\ell} \quad \text{(C/m)}, \quad dq = \lambda \, dx$$ $$\sigma = \frac{dq}{dA} \quad \text{(C/m}^2\text{)}, \quad dq = \sigma \, dA$$ $$\rho = \frac{dq}{dV} \quad \text{(C/m}^3\text{)}, \quad dq = \rho \, dV$$

General Method of Calculation

For any continuous distribution, the field at a point is found by integrating the contributions from all elements:

$$dE = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \cdot \frac{dq}{r^2} \hat{r}$$ $$\vec{E} = \int d\vec{E} \quad \text{(vector integral)}$$

The key technique is to exploit symmetry: components of dE perpendicular to the symmetry axis cancel in pairs, and only the component along the axis survives. This dramatically simplifies the integration.

Important Results for Symmetric Distributions

$$E = \frac{\lambda}{2\pi\epsilon_0 r} \quad \text{(infinite line)}$$ $$E = \frac{\sigma}{2\epsilon_0} \quad \text{(infinite sheet)}$$ $$E = \frac{Qx}{4\pi\epsilon_0(x^2 + R^2)^{3/2}} \quad \text{(charged ring)}$$ $$E_{\text{max}} \text{ at } x = \frac{R}{\sqrt{2}}$$ $$E = \frac{\sigma}{2\epsilon_0}\left[1 - \frac{x}{\sqrt{x^2 + R^2}}\right] \quad \text{(disc)}$$

7. Electric Flux & Gauss's Law

Fig 11.7

Electric Flux

Electric flux is a scalar quantity that measures the "flow" of electric field through a surface. Physically, it is proportional to the number of field lines passing through the surface. For a uniform field E and a flat surface of area A (with outward normal):

$$\Phi_E = \vec{E} \cdot \vec{A} = EA\cos\theta$$ $$\Phi_E = \int \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{A}$$ $$\text{SI unit: N} \cdot \text{m}^2/\text{C (or V} \cdot \text{m)}$$

For a closed surface, outward flux is taken as positive and inward flux as negative. The net flux through a closed surface depends only on the total charge enclosed.

Statement of Gauss's Law

Gauss's law states that the total electric flux through any closed surface (Gaussian surface) is equal to 1/ε0 times the total charge enclosed within that surface. It is one of the four Maxwell's equations of electromagnetism and is entirely equivalent to Coulomb's law for static charges.

$$\oint \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{A} = \frac{Q_{\text{enc}}}{\epsilon_0}$$ $$\oint \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{A} = \frac{1}{\epsilon_0} \int \rho \, dV$$

Key Points about Gauss's Law

  • The E in the surface integral is due to all charges (inside and outside), but the net flux depends only on the enclosed charge
  • Gauss's law holds for any closed surface, but is computationally useful only when the charge distribution has high symmetry (spherical, cylindrical, or planar)
  • Charges outside the Gaussian surface contribute zero net flux through it
  • The Gaussian surface is an imaginary mathematical surface chosen for convenience — it need not coincide with any physical boundary
  • Flux is a scalar quantity and can be positive, negative, or zero

8. Applications of Gauss's Law

Fig 11.8

Infinite Line Charge

For a long straight wire carrying uniform linear charge density λ, choose a coaxial cylindrical Gaussian surface of radius r and length ℓ. By symmetry, E is radial and constant on the curved surface; the flat end-caps contribute no flux.

$$E(2\pi r \ell) = \frac{\lambda \ell}{\epsilon_0}$$ $$E = \frac{\lambda}{2\pi\epsilon_0 r} \quad \text{(radially outward for } +\lambda \text{)}$$

Infinite Plane Sheet of Charge

For an infinite plane with uniform surface charge density σ, use a cylindrical Gaussian surface (pillbox) straddling the sheet. By symmetry, E is perpendicular to the sheet on both sides.

$$E = \frac{\sigma}{2\epsilon_0} \quad \text{(uniform, independent of distance)}$$ $$\text{Between two sheets with } \pm\sigma:$$ $$\text{Inside: } E = \frac{\sigma}{\epsilon_0}, \quad \text{Outside: } E = 0$$

Uniformly Charged Conducting Sphere

All charge resides on the surface. Using a spherical Gaussian surface:

$$E = \frac{Q}{4\pi\epsilon_0 r^2} \quad (r > R)$$ $$E = \frac{Q}{4\pi\epsilon_0 R^2} = \frac{\sigma}{\epsilon_0} \quad (r = R)$$ $$E = 0 \quad (r < R)$$

Uniformly Charged Non-Conducting Sphere

For a solid sphere of radius R with uniform volume charge density ρ = 3Q/(4πR³):

$$E = \frac{Q}{4\pi\epsilon_0 r^2} \quad (r > R)$$ $$E = \frac{Qr}{4\pi\epsilon_0 R^3} = \frac{\rho r}{3\epsilon_0} \quad (r < R)$$ $$\text{Maximum field at the surface} (r = R)$$
Example 3 — Gauss's Law for Uniformly Charged Sphere

A uniformly charged solid sphere of radius R = 10 cm has total charge Q = 50 nC. Find the electric field at (a) r = 5 cm (inside) and (b) r = 20 cm (outside).

Solution:
Given: Q = 50 × 10−9 C, R = 0.10 m, k = 9 × 109 N·m²/C²

(a) Inside (r = 0.05 m < R):
E = kQr / R³ = (9 × 109)(50 × 10−9)(0.05) / (0.10)³
E = 22.5 / 10−3 = 2.25 × 104 N/C

(b) Outside (r = 0.20 m > R):
E = kQ / r² = (9 × 109)(50 × 10−9) / (0.20)²
E = 450 / 0.04 = 1.125 × 104 N/C

9. Electric Potential

Fig 11.9

Definition of Electric Potential

The electric potential at a point is defined as the work done per unit positive charge by an external agent in bringing a test charge from infinity to that point without acceleration (quasi-statically). Unlike the electric field which is a vector, the potential is a scalar quantity, making it much simpler to compute for complex charge distributions.

$$V = \frac{W}{q_0} = \frac{kQ}{r}$$ $$\text{SI unit: Volt (V) = J/C}$$ $$V(\infty) = 0$$

Potential Difference and Path Independence

The potential difference between two points A and B equals the negative line integral of the electric field. Because the electrostatic force is conservative, this integral is independent of the path taken between the two points.

$$V_A - V_B = -\int_B^A \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{l}$$ $$W = q(V_B - V_A)$$

Relation Between Electric Field and Potential

The electric field is the negative gradient of the potential. In one dimension, E = −dV/dr. This means the electric field points in the direction of decreasing potential and is stronger where the potential changes more rapidly.

$$E = -\frac{dV}{dr} \quad \text{(1D)}$$ $$\vec{E} = -\nabla V = -\left(\frac{\partial V}{\partial x}\hat{i} + \frac{\partial V}{\partial y}\hat{j} + \frac{\partial V}{\partial z}\hat{k}\right) \quad \text{(3D)}$$

Potential Due to Various Charge Configurations

$$V = \frac{kQ}{r} \quad \text{(point charge)}$$ $$V = \sum \frac{kq_i}{r_i} \quad \text{(superposition)}$$ $$V = \frac{kp\cos\theta}{r^2} \quad \text{(dipole)}$$ $$V = \frac{kp}{r^2} \quad (\theta = 0Â°), \quad V = 0 \quad (\theta = 90Â°)$$ $$V = \int \frac{dq}{4\pi\epsilon_0 r} \quad \text{(continuous)}$$ $$V = \frac{kQ}{r} \quad (r > R), \quad V = \frac{kQ(3R^2 - r^2)}{2R^3} \quad (r < R)$$

10. Equipotential Surfaces

Fig 11.10

Definition and Properties

An equipotential surface is a surface on which the electric potential has the same value at every point. Since the potential difference between any two points on such a surface is zero, no work is done in moving a charge along an equipotential surface.

Key Properties

  • Perpendicularity: Electric field lines are always perpendicular to equipotential surfaces. If they were not, the field would have a component along the surface, which would mean work is done moving a charge along it — contradicting the definition.
  • Non-intersection: Two equipotential surfaces never intersect (otherwise the potential would have two values at the intersection point).
  • Point charge: Equipotential surfaces are concentric spheres centred on the charge.
  • Uniform field: Equipotential surfaces are planes perpendicular to the field direction.
  • Dipole: Equipotential surfaces are more complex shapes; the equatorial plane is at V = 0.
  • Field strength: Closer spacing of equipotential surfaces indicates a stronger electric field.
$$W = q\Delta V = 0$$ $$\vec{E} \perp \text{equipotential surface}$$

11. Electrostatic Potential Energy

Fig 11.11

Potential Energy of a System of Charges

The electrostatic potential energy of a system of charges equals the total work done by an external agent in assembling the configuration by bringing each charge from infinity. For a conservative force, this energy depends only on the final configuration, not on the order or path of assembly.

$$U = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \cdot \frac{q_1 q_2}{r}$$ $$U = k\left(\frac{q_1 q_2}{r_{12}} + \frac{q_2 q_3}{r_{23}} + \frac{q_1 q_3}{r_{13}}\right)$$ $$U = \frac{1}{2}\sum_i \sum_{j \neq i} \frac{kq_i q_j}{r_{ij}}$$

Energy Conservation in Electrostatics

When a charged particle moves in an electric field, total mechanical energy is conserved. As potential energy decreases, kinetic energy increases by the same amount, and vice versa.

$$KE_A + PE_A = KE_B + PE_B$$ $$W_{\text{field}} = q(V_A - V_B) = -\Delta PE$$ $$W_{\text{ext}} = q(V_B - V_A) = \Delta PE$$

Energy Density of the Electric Field

The energy stored in an electric field can be expressed as an energy density (energy per unit volume) distributed throughout space. This concept is especially useful for capacitors.

$$u = \frac{1}{2}\epsilon_0 E^2 \quad \text{(vacuum)}$$ $$u = \frac{1}{2}\epsilon_0\kappa E^2 \quad \text{(dielectric)}$$ $$U = \int u \, dV = \int \frac{1}{2}\epsilon_0\kappa E^2 \, dV$$

12. Conductors in Electrostatic Equilibrium

Fig 11.12

Key Properties

When a conductor is in electrostatic equilibrium (no current flowing), the free charges have rearranged themselves so that several remarkable properties hold:

  • Zero internal field: The electric field inside the bulk of a conductor is exactly zero. Free charges rearrange until the internal field vanishes.
  • Surface charge: All excess charge resides on the surface of the conductor. A Gaussian surface just inside the conductor surface encloses no net charge.
  • Equipotential body: The entire conductor (surface and interior) is at the same potential.
  • Normal field at surface: The electric field just outside the conductor surface is perpendicular to the surface and has magnitude σ/ε0.
  • Charge concentration: Surface charge density is highest at regions of greatest curvature (sharp points). This is the principle behind the lightning conductor.
$$E = 0 \quad \text{(inside)}$$ $$E = \frac{\sigma}{\epsilon_0} \quad \text{(just outside)}$$ $$V = \text{constant} \quad \text{(inside)}$$

Electrostatic Shielding

A hollow conductor shields its interior from external electric fields. Regardless of the external field, the electric field inside a hollow conducting shell is zero (provided no charge is placed inside). This principle is used in Faraday cages for protection of sensitive electronic equipment.

13. Dielectrics & Polarization

Fig 11.13

Dielectric Constant

A dielectric is an insulating material that, when placed in an electric field, becomes polarized: the positive and negative charges within each molecule shift slightly in opposite directions. The dielectric constant κ (relative permittivity) of the material is defined as the ratio of the capacitance with the dielectric to the capacitance without it.

$$\kappa = \frac{C}{C_0} = \frac{\epsilon}{\epsilon_0}$$ $$\text{Vacuum: } \kappa=1, \quad \text{Air: } \kappa \approx 1.00054$$ $$\text{Paper: } \kappa \approx 3.7, \quad \text{Glass: } \kappa \approx 6$$ $$\text{Water: } \kappa \approx 80$$

Effect of Dielectric on Electric Field

The polarization of the dielectric creates bound surface charges that produce an opposing field, thereby reducing the net field inside the dielectric by a factor of κ:

$$E_{\text{dielectric}} = \frac{E_0}{\kappa}$$ $$V_{\text{dielectric}} = \frac{V_0}{\kappa}$$ $$C = \kappa C_0$$

Dielectric Insertion: Two Important Scenarios

Case 1: Battery remains connected (V = constant)

  • Capacitance increases: C = κC0
  • Charge increases: Q = κQ0
  • Energy increases: U = κU0
  • Electric field remains the same: E = V/d (unchanged)

Case 2: Battery disconnected (Q = constant)

  • Capacitance increases: C = κC0
  • Charge remains the same: Q = Q0
  • Voltage decreases: V = V0/κ
  • Electric field decreases: E = E0/κ
  • Energy decreases: U = U0/κ

Multiple Dielectric Slabs in Series

When slabs of different dielectrics fill the gap in series (perpendicular to the plates), the equivalent capacitance is found by treating them as capacitors in series:

$$C = \frac{\epsilon_0 A}{\sum\left(\frac{d_i}{\kappa_i}\right)}$$ $$\kappa_1 E_1 = \kappa_2 E_2 \quad \text{(D continuous)}$$

Partially Inserted Dielectric (Parallel Configuration)

If a dielectric slab covers only part of the plate area, it creates two capacitors in parallel:

$$C = \frac{\kappa\epsilon_0 A_1}{d} + \frac{\epsilon_0 A_2}{d}$$ $$A_1 + A_2 = A$$

14. Capacitance & Capacitor Types

Fig 11.14

Definition of Capacitance

A capacitor is an arrangement of two conductors carrying equal and opposite charges, separated by an insulating medium. The capacitance is defined as the ratio of the magnitude of charge on either plate to the potential difference between them. Capacitance depends only on the geometry and the dielectric material — not on the voltage or charge applied.

$$C = \frac{Q}{V}$$ $$\text{SI unit: Farad (F) = C/V}$$ $$1 \, \mu\text{F} = 10^{-6} \text{ F}, \quad 1 \, \text{nF} = 10^{-9} \text{ F}, \quad 1 \, \text{pF} = 10^{-12} \text{ F}$$

Isolated Conducting Sphere

A single conducting sphere of radius R can be treated as a capacitor with the second conductor at infinity (earth). Its capacitance depends only on its radius.

$$V = \frac{Q}{4\pi\epsilon_0 R}$$ $$C = 4\pi\epsilon_0 R$$ $$\text{For } R = 1 \text{ m: } C \approx 111 \text{ pF}$$

Spherical Capacitor

Two concentric conducting spheres of radii a (inner) and b (outer) form a spherical capacitor. The field exists only between the spheres and varies as 1/r².

$$C = \frac{4\pi\epsilon_0 ab}{b - a}$$ $$C = \frac{4\pi\kappa\epsilon_0 ab}{b - a} \quad \text{(with dielectric)}$$ $$\lim_{b \to \infty} C = 4\pi\epsilon_0 a$$

Cylindrical Capacitor

Two coaxial conducting cylinders of radii a (inner) and b (outer) and length ℓ form a cylindrical capacitor. The field is radial and varies as 1/r.

$$C = \frac{2\pi\epsilon_0 \ell}{\ln(b/a)}$$ $$C = \frac{2\pi\kappa\epsilon_0 \ell}{\ln(b/a)} \quad \text{(with dielectric)}$$ $$C/\ell = \frac{2\pi\epsilon_0}{\ln(b/a)}$$

15. Parallel Plate Capacitor

Fig 11.15

Basic Theory

The parallel plate capacitor is the most common and most important capacitor geometry. It consists of two large parallel conducting plates, each of area A, separated by a distance d. When d is much smaller than the plate dimensions, the field between the plates is practically uniform and the fringing fields at the edges can be neglected.

$$E = \frac{\sigma}{\epsilon_0} = \frac{Q}{\epsilon_0 A}$$ $$V = Ed = \frac{Qd}{\epsilon_0 A}$$ $$C = \frac{\epsilon_0 A}{d}$$ $$C = \frac{\kappa\epsilon_0 A}{d} = \kappa C_0 \quad \text{(with dielectric)}$$ $$E = \frac{\sigma}{\kappa\epsilon_0}$$

Key Observations

  • Capacitance is proportional to plate area A and inversely proportional to separation d
  • The field outside the plates is essentially zero (fields from the two plates cancel)
  • Inserting a dielectric increases C by the factor κ
  • The net charge on the capacitor as a whole is zero; "charge on a capacitor" refers to the magnitude Q on each plate
Example 4 — Parallel Plate Capacitor with Dielectric

A parallel plate capacitor has plates of area 200 cm² separated by 2 mm. It is charged to 100 V and then disconnected from the battery. A dielectric slab (κ = 5) fully filling the gap is inserted. Find the capacitance, charge, voltage, and energy before and after insertion.

Solution:
Given: A = 2 × 10−2 m², d = 2 × 10−3 m, V0 = 100 V, κ = 5

Before insertion:
C0 = ε0A/d = (8.854 × 10−12)(2 × 10−2) / (2 × 10−3) = 88.54 pF
Q0 = C0V0 = 88.54 × 10−12 × 100 = 8.854 nC
U0 = ½C0V0² = ½(88.54 × 10−12)(104) = 4.43 × 10−7 J

After insertion (battery disconnected, Q constant):
C = κC0 = 5 × 88.54 = 442.7 pF
Q = 8.854 nC (unchanged)
V = Q/C = 8.854 × 10−9 / 442.7 × 10−12 = 20 V
U = ½QV = ½(8.854 × 10−9)(20) = 8.85 × 10−8 J

Voltage and energy both decrease by a factor of κ = 5. The lost energy goes into pulling the dielectric slab into the gap (the slab is attracted inward).

16. Capacitors in Series & Parallel

Fig 11.16

Capacitors in Series

When capacitors are connected in series (end-to-end), the charge is the same on every capacitor, but the voltage divides across them. The equivalent capacitance is always less than the smallest individual capacitance.

$$\frac{1}{C_{\text{eq}}} = \frac{1}{C_1} + \frac{1}{C_2} + \frac{1}{C_3} + \ldots$$ $$C_{\text{eq}} = \frac{C_1 C_2}{C_1 + C_2} \quad \text{(two capacitors)}$$ $$V_1 = \frac{C_2}{C_1 + C_2} V, \quad V_2 = \frac{C_1}{C_1 + C_2} V$$

Capacitors in Parallel

When capacitors are connected in parallel (same two nodes), the voltage is the same across all capacitors, but the charge divides in proportion to their capacitances.

$$C_{\text{eq}} = C_1 + C_2 + C_3 + \ldots$$ $$Q_i = C_i V$$

Summary: Series vs. Parallel

  • Series: Same Q on all; voltage divides; Ceq < smallest Ci
  • Parallel: Same V across all; charge divides; Ceq > largest Ci
  • Series is analogous to resistors in parallel; parallel is analogous to resistors in series (opposite rules)

17. Energy Stored in Capacitors

Fig 11.17

Energy Storage Formulas

The energy stored in a charged capacitor is equal to the work done in charging it. As charge accumulates, the voltage builds from 0 to V, so the average voltage during charging is V/2 and the total work is ½QV.

$$U = \frac{1}{2}CV^2 = \frac{1}{2}QV = \frac{Q^2}{2C}$$

All three forms are equivalent. Use the form that matches the quantities held constant in your problem.

Energy Density in the Electric Field

The energy is stored in the electric field between the plates. The energy per unit volume (energy density) is:

$$u = \frac{1}{2}\epsilon_0 E^2 \quad \text{(vacuum)}$$ $$u = \frac{1}{2}\epsilon_0\kappa E^2 \quad \text{(dielectric)}$$ $$U = \int u \, dV = \frac{1}{2}\epsilon_0\kappa E^2 \cdot Ad$$

Force Between Capacitor Plates

The two plates attract each other with a force that can be calculated from the energy method:

$$F = \frac{Q^2}{2\epsilon_0 A} = \frac{1}{2}\epsilon_0 E^2 A$$ $$p = \frac{\sigma^2}{2\epsilon_0} = \frac{1}{2}\epsilon_0 E^2$$

Energy Budget During RC Charging

When a capacitor is charged from a battery of EMF ϵ through a resistor:

  • Energy supplied by battery: Wbattery = Qϵ = Cϵ²
  • Energy stored in capacitor: U = ½Cϵ²
  • Heat dissipated in resistor: H = ½Cϵ²
  • Key result: Exactly half the energy from the battery is lost as heat in the resistance, regardless of the value of R

18. Van de Graaff Generator

Fig 11.18

Principle

The Van de Graaff generator is a device that produces very high electrostatic potentials (up to several million volts) by continuously transferring charge to a large hollow metallic sphere. It exploits two key electrostatic principles:

  • Corona discharge: Sharp points on a conductor have very high surface charge density, producing intense electric fields that ionize nearby air and spray charge onto a moving belt.
  • Charge on a hollow conductor: Any charge placed on the inner surface of a hollow conductor immediately migrates to the outer surface. This means charge can be continuously added to the sphere without limit (until electrical breakdown of the surrounding air occurs).

Working

A moving insulating belt transports charge from a lower spray comb (connected to a high-voltage source) to an upper collector comb inside the large metallic dome. The charge deposited on the inner surface of the dome rapidly moves to the outer surface, keeping the inner surface charge-free and ready to accept more charge. This process continues, building up enormous potential on the dome.

$$V_{\text{max}} = E_{\text{breakdown}} \times R$$ $$E_{\text{breakdown}} \approx 3 \times 10^6 \text{ V/m (air)}$$ $$V_{\text{max}} \approx 3 \times 10^6 \text{ V} = 3 \text{ MV} \quad (R = 1 \text{ m})$$

Applications

  • Accelerating charged particles to high energies for nuclear physics experiments
  • Generating high voltages for research and testing of electrical insulation
  • The charged particles can be used to induce nuclear reactions and study atomic structure
