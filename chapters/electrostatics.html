1. Electric Charge

What is Electric Charge?

Electric charge is an intrinsic property of matter that causes it to experience a force when placed near other electrically charged matter. There are exactly two types of electric charge: positive and negative. Like charges repel and unlike charges attract. The SI unit of charge is the coulomb (C).

Properties of Electric Charge

  • Additivity: Charges are scalars and add algebraically. The total charge of a system is the algebraic sum of all individual charges: Q = q1 + q2 + q3 + ...
  • Conservation: The total electric charge of an isolated system is always conserved. Charge can neither be created nor destroyed — it can only be transferred from one body to another.
  • Quantization: Charge always occurs in integral multiples of the elementary charge e = 1.6 × 10−19 C. Thus, q = ne where n is an integer (positive or negative).
  • Charge is invariant: The charge on a body does not change with its speed. It is a relativistic invariant.

Methods of Charging

There are three primary methods by which a body can acquire electric charge:

  • Friction: When two dissimilar materials are rubbed together, electrons transfer from one to the other. The body that loses electrons becomes positively charged and the one that gains becomes negatively charged. Example: rubbing a glass rod with silk.
  • Conduction: When a charged body is brought into direct contact with an uncharged conductor, charge flows from the charged body to the uncharged one until both reach the same potential.
  • Induction: A charged body brought near (but not touching) a conductor causes a redistribution of charges in the conductor. By grounding the conductor while the charged body is nearby and then removing the ground, a net charge opposite to the inducing charge is left on the conductor.
Quantization of charge: q = ne   (n = 0, ±1, ±2, ...)
Elementary charge: e = 1.6 × 10−19 C
Charge of electron: −e = −1.6 × 10−19 C
Charge of proton: +e = +1.6 × 10−19 C

2. Coulomb's Law

Statement

Coulomb's law states that the electrostatic force between two point charges is directly proportional to the product of the magnitudes of the charges and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them. The force acts along the line joining the two charges.

F = k q1q2 / r²

where k = 1/(4πε0) = 9 × 109 N m² C−2
ε0 = 8.854 × 10−12 C² N−1 m−2 (permittivity of free space)

Vector Form

The force on charge q1 due to charge q2 in vector form is:

F12 = k q1q2 / r²   r̂12

Here r̂12 is the unit vector pointing from q2 to q1. If both charges have the same sign, the force is repulsive (along r̂12); if opposite signs, the force is attractive (opposite to r̂12).

Superposition Principle

The total force on a charge due to several other charges is the vector sum of the individual forces exerted by each charge independently. The force between any two charges is unaffected by the presence of other charges.

Fnet = F1 + F2 + F3 + ... = ∑ k q qi / ri²   r̂i

Effect of Medium

When charges are placed in a medium with dielectric constant (relative permittivity) κ, the force is reduced by a factor of κ:

Fmedium = Fvacuum / κ = k q1q2 / (κ r²)
Solved Example 1

Two point charges q1 = +3 μC and q2 = −4 μC are placed 30 cm apart in vacuum. Find the magnitude of the electrostatic force between them. Also find the force if the medium has a dielectric constant κ = 2.

Solution:
q1 = 3 × 10−6 C, q2 = 4 × 10−6 C, r = 0.30 m

F = k q1q2 / r² = (9 × 109)(3 × 10−6)(4 × 10−6) / (0.30)²
F = (9 × 109)(12 × 10−12) / 0.09
F = 108 × 10−3 / 0.09 = 1.2 N (attractive)

In medium: Fmedium = F / κ = 1.2 / 2 = 0.6 N

3. Electric Field

Definition

The electric field at a point in space is defined as the electrostatic force experienced by a unit positive test charge placed at that point. The test charge must be vanishingly small so that it does not disturb the source charge distribution.

E = F / q0

SI unit: N/C or V/m

Electric Field Due to a Point Charge

The electric field at a distance r from a point charge Q is directed radially outward (for positive Q) or radially inward (for negative Q).

E = kQ / r² = Q / (4πε0r²)

Electric Field Due to an Electric Dipole

An electric dipole consists of two equal and opposite charges +q and −q separated by a small distance 2a. The dipole moment is p = q × 2a, directed from the negative charge to the positive charge.

Axial point (on the axis, distance r from centre, r >> a):
Eaxial = 2kp / r³ = p / (2πε0r³)

Equatorial point (on perpendicular bisector, distance r from centre, r >> a):
Eequatorial = kp / r³ = p / (4πε0r³)

Note: Eaxial = 2 Eequatorial

Electric Field Due to a Uniformly Charged Ring

For a uniformly charged ring of total charge Q and radius R, the electric field at a point on the axis at distance x from the centre is:

E = kQx / (R² + x²)3/2

At the centre (x = 0): E = 0
Maximum E occurs at x = R / √2

Electric Field Due to an Infinite Plane Sheet of Charge

For an infinite plane sheet with uniform surface charge density σ, the electric field is uniform and perpendicular to the sheet:

E = σ / (2ε0)   (on each side of the sheet)

For two parallel sheets with charges +σ and −σ:
Between the sheets: E = σ / ε0
Outside: E = 0

Electric Field Due to a Uniformly Charged Sphere

Conducting sphere (charge Q, radius R):
Outside (r > R): E = kQ / r²
On the surface (r = R): E = kQ / R² = σ / ε0
Inside (r < R): E = 0

Non-conducting sphere (uniform volume charge density ρ):
Outside (r > R): E = kQ / r²
Inside (r < R): E = kQr / R³ = ρr / (3ε0)

4. Gauss's Law

Electric Flux

Electric flux through a surface is a measure of the number of electric field lines passing through that surface. For a uniform field E making an angle θ with the area vector A:

ΦE = E · A = EA cosθ
SI unit: N m² C−1 (or V m)

Statement of Gauss's Law

Gauss's law states that the total electric flux through any closed surface (Gaussian surface) is equal to 1/ε0 times the total charge enclosed within that surface.

∮ E · dA = Qenc / ε0

Important points about Gauss's law:

  • The electric field E on the Gaussian surface is due to all charges (inside and outside), but the flux depends only on the enclosed charge.
  • Gauss's law is valid for any closed surface, but it is most useful when there is sufficient symmetry (spherical, cylindrical, or planar) to simplify the integral.
  • Charges outside the Gaussian surface contribute zero net flux through it.

Applications of Gauss's Law

1. Infinite Line Charge (linear charge density λ):

Using a cylindrical Gaussian surface of radius r and length l coaxial with the line charge:

E = λ / (2πε0r) = 2kλ / r
(radially outward for +λ)

2. Infinite Plane Sheet (surface charge density σ):

Using a cylindrical Gaussian surface (pillbox) straddling the sheet:

E = σ / (2ε0)
(perpendicular to the sheet, away from it)

3. Uniformly Charged Sphere (total charge Q, radius R):

Outside (r > R): E = Q / (4πε0r²) = kQ / r²
On surface (r = R): E = Q / (4πε0R²)
Inside conducting sphere: E = 0
Inside non-conducting sphere (r < R): E = Qr / (4πε0R³)
Solved Example 2

A uniformly charged solid sphere of radius R = 10 cm has a total charge Q = 50 nC. Find the electric field at (a) r = 5 cm (inside) and (b) r = 20 cm (outside).

Solution:
Q = 50 × 10−9 C, R = 0.10 m

(a) Inside (r = 0.05 m):
E = kQr / R³ = (9 × 109)(50 × 10−9)(0.05) / (0.10)³
E = (9 × 109)(2.5 × 10−9) / 10−3
E = 22.5 / 10−3 = 22,500 N/C = 2.25 × 104 N/C

(b) Outside (r = 0.20 m):
E = kQ / r² = (9 × 109)(50 × 10−9) / (0.20)²
E = 450 / 0.04 = 11,250 N/C = 1.125 × 104 N/C

5. Electric Potential

Definition

The electric potential at a point is defined as the work done by an external agent in bringing a unit positive charge from infinity to that point (against the electric field) without any acceleration.

V = W / q = kQ / r   (for a point charge Q at distance r)
SI unit: Volt (V) = J/C

Potential Difference

The potential difference between two points A and B is the work done per unit positive charge in moving the charge from B to A:

VA − VB = WB→A / q = − ∫BA E · dl

Relation Between Electric Field and Potential

The electric field is the negative gradient of the electric potential. In one dimension:

E = −dV/dr

In three dimensions: E = −∇V = −(∂V/∂x i + ∂V/∂y j + ∂V/∂z k)

This means the electric field points in the direction of decreasing potential and is stronger where the potential changes more rapidly.

Equipotential Surfaces

An equipotential surface is a surface on which the electric potential is the same at every point. Key properties:

  • No work is done in moving a charge along an equipotential surface.
  • Electric field lines are always perpendicular to equipotential surfaces.
  • Equipotential surfaces never intersect each other.
  • For a point charge, equipotential surfaces are concentric spheres.
  • For a uniform field, they are planes perpendicular to the field.
  • Closer spacing of equipotential surfaces indicates a stronger electric field.

Potential Due to a Dipole

V = kp cosθ / r²   (at distance r, angle θ from dipole axis, r >> a)

On the axis (θ = 0): V = kp / r²
On the equatorial line (θ = 90°): V = 0

Potential Energy of a System of Charges

For two charges: U = kq1q2 / r
For three charges: U = k(q1q2/r12 + q2q3/r23 + q1q3/r13)

6. Capacitance

Definition

A capacitor is a device that stores electric charge and energy. The capacitance of a capacitor is defined as the ratio of the charge on one of its plates to the potential difference between them.

C = Q / V
SI unit: Farad (F) = C/V

Parallel Plate Capacitor

A parallel plate capacitor consists of two large parallel conducting plates, each of area A, separated by a distance d. The electric field between the plates is uniform: E = σ/ε0 = Q/(ε0A).

C = ε0A / d

With dielectric of constant κ: C = κε0A / d

Capacitors in Series

When capacitors are connected in series, the charge on each capacitor is the same, but the voltage divides. The equivalent capacitance is always less than the smallest individual capacitance.

1/Ceq = 1/C1 + 1/C2 + 1/C3 + ...

For two capacitors: Ceq = C1C2 / (C1 + C2)

Capacitors in Parallel

When capacitors are connected in parallel, the voltage across each capacitor is the same, but the charge divides. The equivalent capacitance is the sum of individual capacitances.

Ceq = C1 + C2 + C3 + ...

Capacitance of Spherical and Cylindrical Capacitors

Isolated sphere of radius R: C = 4πε0R

Spherical capacitor (radii a and b, b > a): C = 4πε0ab / (b − a)

Cylindrical capacitor (radii a and b, length l): C = 2πε0l / ln(b/a)

7. Energy Stored in a Capacitor

Energy Formulae

The energy stored in a charged capacitor is equal to the work done in charging it. Since the voltage builds up from 0 to V as charge accumulates from 0 to Q, the average voltage during charging is V/2:

U = ½CV² = ½QV = Q² / (2C)

Energy Density

The energy is stored in the electric field between the plates. The energy per unit volume (energy density) is:

u = ½ε0E²

With dielectric: u = ½κε0E²

Effect of Dielectrics

When a dielectric slab of constant κ is inserted between the plates of a capacitor:

  • Battery connected (V constant): C increases by factor κ, Q increases by factor κ, energy U increases by factor κ.
  • Battery disconnected (Q constant): C increases by factor κ, V decreases by factor κ, energy U decreases by factor κ.

Force Between Capacitor Plates

The two plates of a parallel plate capacitor attract each other. The force per unit area (electrostatic pressure) is:

Force per unit area = σ² / (2ε0) = ½ε0E²

Total force: F = Q² / (2ε0A)
Solved Example 3

A parallel plate capacitor has plates of area 200 cm² separated by 2 mm. It is charged to 100 V and then disconnected from the battery. A dielectric slab of thickness 2 mm and dielectric constant κ = 5 is then inserted between the plates. Find the capacitance, charge, voltage, and energy before and after inserting the dielectric.

Solution:
A = 200 × 10−4 m² = 2 × 10−2 m², d = 2 × 10−3 m, V0 = 100 V

Before dielectric:
C0 = ε0A/d = (8.854 × 10−12)(2 × 10−2) / (2 × 10−3) = 8.854 × 10−11 F ≈ 88.54 pF
Q = C0V0 = (88.54 × 10−12)(100) = 8.854 nC
U0 = ½C0V0² = ½(88.54 × 10−12)(104) = 4.427 × 10−7 J

After dielectric (battery disconnected, Q constant):
C = κC0 = 5 × 88.54 = 442.7 pF
Q remains = 8.854 nC
V = Q/C = 8.854 × 10−9 / 442.7 × 10−12 = 20 V
U = ½QV = ½(8.854 × 10−9)(20) = 8.854 × 10−8 J

Energy decreased by factor 5 (energy goes into pulling the dielectric in).
