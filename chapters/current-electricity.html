1. Electric Current & Resistance

Fig 12.1

What is Electric Current?

Electric current is the rate of flow of electric charge through any cross-section of a conductor. When a potential difference is maintained across the ends of a conductor, free charges experience a net force and begin drifting in a definite direction, constituting an electric current. The SI unit of current is the ampere (A), defined as one coulomb of charge flowing per second.

By convention, the direction of electric current is taken as the direction of flow of positive charges. In metallic conductors the actual charge carriers are electrons, so conventional current flows opposite to the electron drift. In electrolytes, both positive ions (cations) and negative ions (anions) contribute to the current.

$$I = \frac{dQ}{dt}$$ $$1 \text{ A} = 1 \text{ C/s}, \quad 1 \text{ mA} = 10^{-3} \text{ A}, \quad 1 \, \mu\text{A} = 10^{-6} \text{ A}$$

Charge Transported

If the current varies with time, the total charge flowing through a cross-section between times t1 and t2 is obtained by integration. For a steady (constant) current, this simplifies to Q = It.

$$Q = \int_{t_1}^{t_2} I \, dt$$

Current Density

Current density j is the current per unit cross-sectional area of the conductor, directed along the flow of positive charge. It is a vector quantity, and for a uniform conductor carrying current I through area A:

$$j = \frac{I}{A} \quad \text{(A/m}^2)$$

Resistance and Resistivity

Resistance (R) is the opposition a conductor offers to the flow of charge. It arises because moving electrons undergo repeated collisions with the fixed ions of the crystal lattice. The resistance of a conductor depends on its material, length, cross-sectional area, and temperature.

$$R = \rho \frac{l}{A}$$ $$\rho = \text{resistivity} \, (\Omega \cdot \text{m})$$ $$l = \text{length (m)}, \quad A = \text{area} \, (\text{m}^2)$$

Resistivity (ρ) is an intrinsic property of the material, independent of the shape or dimensions of the conductor. It is defined as the resistance of a specimen of unit length and unit cross-sectional area. The SI unit is Ω·m. Conductivity (σ) is the reciprocal of resistivity: σ = 1/ρ, with SI unit S/m (siemens per metre).

Definition of One Ohm

One ohm (Ω) is the resistance of a conductor that permits a current of 1 A when a potential difference of 1 V is maintained across its ends: 1 Ω = 1 V/A.

2. Drift Velocity & Mobility

Fig 12.2

Random Thermal Motion of Electrons

In a metal at any temperature above absolute zero, free electrons are in ceaseless random thermal motion, much like molecules in an ideal gas. Their thermal speeds are of the order of 105–106 m/s, but because the motion is completely random in all directions, the average velocity is zero. Therefore, no net current flows in the absence of an applied electric field.

Drift Velocity

When a potential difference is applied across a conductor, an electric field E is established inside it. Each free electron experiences a force F = eE opposite to the field (since the electron charge is negative). Between successive collisions with lattice ions, an electron accelerates and gains a small additional velocity in the direction of the force. This additional velocity is lost at each collision, but on average a slow, steady velocity develops along the field direction. This average velocity is called the drift velocity (vd).

$$v_d = \frac{eE\tau}{m}$$ $$e = 1.6 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}, \quad m = 9.11 \times 10^{-31} \text{ kg}$$ $$\tau = \text{relaxation time}, \quad E = \text{electric field (V/m)}$$

Typical drift velocities are extremely small — of the order of 10−4 to 10−5 m/s — yet the electric signal propagates at nearly the speed of light because the electric field is established almost instantaneously throughout the conductor.

Relation Between Current and Drift Velocity

Consider a conductor of cross-sectional area A with n free electrons per unit volume, each carrying charge e and drifting with velocity vd. In a small time dt, the charge crossing any cross-section is dQ = nAevd dt, giving:

$$I = nAev_d$$ $$j = nev_d$$

This shows that for a given current, a thinner conductor requires a higher drift velocity. It also provides a microscopic derivation of Ohm's law: since vd ∝ E and I ∝ vd, we get I ∝ V (because E = V/l).

Mobility

The mobility (μ) of a charge carrier is defined as the magnitude of drift velocity per unit electric field. It characterises how easily a charge carrier moves through the material under an applied field.

$$\mu = \frac{v_d}{E} = \frac{e\tau}{m}$$ $$\text{SI unit: m}^2 \text{ V}^{-1} \text{ s}^{-1}$$

Relaxation Time

The relaxation time τ is the average time interval between two successive collisions of a free electron with the lattice ions. It can be expressed in terms of measurable quantities:

$$\tau = \frac{m}{ne^2\rho}$$

Since resistivity ρ increases with temperature (for metals), τ decreases with temperature, meaning electrons collide more frequently at higher temperatures.

Example 1 — Drift Velocity of Conduction Electrons

A copper wire of cross-sectional area 4 × 10−6 m² carries a current of 20 A. If the free-electron density of copper is 8 × 1028 m−3, calculate the drift velocity.

Solution:
Given: n = 8 × 1028 m−3, A = 4 × 10−6 m², I = 20 A, e = 1.6 × 10−19 C

Using I = nAevd:
vd = I/(nAe)
vd = 20 / (8 × 1028 × 4 × 10−6 × 1.6 × 10−19)
vd = 20 / (5.12 × 104)
vd = 3.9 × 10−4 m/s

3. Ohm's Law

Fig 12.3

Statement

Ohm's law states that the current (I) flowing through a conductor is directly proportional to the potential difference (V) across its ends, provided the temperature and other physical conditions remain unchanged. The constant of proportionality is the resistance R of the conductor.

Macroscopic Form

$$V = IR$$ $$V = \text{potential difference (V)}, \quad I = \text{current (A)}, \quad R = \text{resistance} \, (\Omega)$$

Microscopic (Point) Form

At the microscopic level, Ohm's law relates the electric field E inside the conductor to the current density j. This form is more fundamental because it applies at every point inside the material, independent of geometry:

$$j = \sigma E \quad \text{or} \quad E = \rho j$$ $$\sigma = \text{conductivity (S/m)}, \quad \rho = \text{resistivity} \, (\Omega \cdot \text{m})$$

From E = V/l and j = I/A, the microscopic form directly yields the macroscopic relation V = IR with R = ρl/A.

I-V Characteristics

For an ohmic conductor, the I-V graph is a straight line through the origin with slope 1/R. Materials that obey Ohm's law over a wide range of voltages are called ohmic conductors (e.g., metallic wires at constant temperature).

Limitations and Non-Ohmic Devices

  • Ohm's law is not a fundamental law of nature; it is an empirical relation valid only for ohmic materials.
  • Semiconductor devices (diodes, transistors), gas discharge tubes, and electrolytes are non-ohmic — their I-V curves are nonlinear.
  • Even metallic conductors deviate from Ohm's law at very high current densities where Joule heating raises the temperature appreciably.
Example 2 — Current from Electric Field

A conductor of length 12 m and resistance 0.072 Ω has a uniform electric field of 0.12 V/m established inside it. Find the total current through the conductor.

Solution:
V = E × l = 0.12 × 12 = 1.44 V

Using Ohm's law:
I = V/R = 1.44/0.072 = 20 A

4. Resistivity & Temperature Dependence

Fig 12.4

Classification of Materials by Resistivity

Materials are broadly classified based on their resistivity values:

  • Conductors (metals): ρ ∼ 10−8 to 10−6 Ω·m. Examples: copper, aluminium, silver.
  • Semiconductors: ρ ∼ 10−5 to 106 Ω·m. Examples: silicon, germanium.
  • Insulators: ρ ∼ 108 to 1016 Ω·m. Examples: glass, rubber, mica.

Temperature Dependence in Metals

In metals, resistivity increases with temperature. As temperature rises, lattice ions vibrate more vigorously, increasing the frequency of collisions between conduction electrons and ions. This reduces the relaxation time τ and increases ρ. For moderate temperature changes, the variation is approximately linear:

$$\rho_T = \rho_0(1 + \alpha_\rho \Delta T)$$ $$R_T = R_0(1 + \alpha_R \Delta T)$$ $$\Delta T = T - T_0$$

The relationship between the two coefficients is αR = αρ − α (where α is the coefficient of linear expansion of the material). Since α ≪ αρ for metals, we have αR ≈ αρ to a very good approximation.

Temperature Dependence in Semiconductors and Insulators

In semiconductors and insulators, resistivity decreases with increasing temperature. Higher temperatures provide more thermal energy, which excites additional electrons from the valence band to the conduction band, increasing the number of charge carriers. The increase in carrier concentration far outweighs the increased collision rate, so net resistance falls.

Special Alloys

  • Manganin (Cu-Mn-Ni alloy): Has a very small and nearly zero temperature coefficient. Ideal for constructing standard resistors and precision instruments.
  • Constantan (Cu-Ni alloy): Similar low temperature coefficient; used in thermocouples and strain gauges.
  • Nichrome (Ni-Cr alloy): High resistivity and good oxidation resistance at high temperatures; used in heating elements.
Example 3 — Temperature of a Heating Element

A toaster uses a Nichrome heating element and operates on 120 V. When switched on at 0°C, it draws an initial current of 1.5 A. After reaching steady state, the current becomes 1.33 A. The temperature coefficient of Nichrome is 0.00045 /°C. Find the steady-state temperature.

Solution:
R0 = V/I0 = 120/1.5 = 80 Ω (at 0°C)
RT = V/IT = 120/1.33 = 90.23 Ω (at T°C)

Using RT = R0(1 + αT):
90.23 = 80(1 + 0.00045 × T)
1 + 0.00045T = 90.23/80 = 1.1279
0.00045T = 0.1279
T = 0.1279/0.00045 = 284°C

5. Colour Code of Carbon Resistors

Fig 12.5

Reading the Colour Bands

Carbon resistors are tiny components whose resistance values cannot be printed numerically on their bodies. Instead, a system of coloured bands is used. A standard four-band resistor has two significant digits, a multiplier, and a tolerance band.

Colour Table

  • Black = 0, Brown = 1, Red = 2, Orange = 3, Yellow = 4
  • Green = 5, Blue = 6, Violet = 7, Grey = 8, White = 9

A popular mnemonic to remember the order is: "B B ROY of Great Britain has a Very Good Wife."

How to Read a Four-Band Resistor

$$\text{Band 1: First digit}$$ $$\text{Band 2: Second digit}$$ $$\text{Band 3: Multiplier} \, (10^n)$$ $$\text{Band 4: Gold} \pm 5\%, \quad \text{Silver} \pm 10\%, \quad \text{None} \pm 20\%$$

Example: Yellow – Violet – Orange – Gold = 47 × 103 Ω = 47 kΩ ± 5%.

Five-Band and SMD Resistors

Precision resistors use five bands: three significant digits, a multiplier, and a tolerance band. Surface-mount (SMD) resistors use a numeric code printed directly on the component (e.g., "473" means 47 × 103 = 47 kΩ).

JEE Tip

Colour-code questions are straightforward marks in competitive exams. Remember the mnemonic and practice converting colours to resistance values (and vice versa) quickly. Pay special attention to the tolerance band, which is read from the opposite end of the resistor.

6. Combinations of Resistors

Fig 12.6

Series Combination

When resistors are connected end-to-end so that the same current flows through each one, they are said to be in series. The total voltage across the combination equals the sum of the individual voltage drops. The equivalent resistance is always greater than the largest individual resistance.

$$R_{\text{eq}} = R_1 + R_2 + R_3 + \ldots + R_n$$ $$I_1 = I_2 = \ldots = I$$ $$V = V_1 + V_2 + \ldots + V_n$$

Voltage Divider Rule (Series)

For two resistors R1 and R2 in series connected across a total voltage V:

$$V_1 = V \times \frac{R_1}{R_1 + R_2}$$ $$V_2 = V \times \frac{R_2}{R_1 + R_2}$$

Parallel Combination

When resistors are connected across the same pair of nodes so that each has the same potential difference across it, they are said to be in parallel. The total current is the sum of the branch currents. The equivalent resistance is always less than the smallest individual resistance.

$$\frac{1}{R_{\text{eq}}} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} + \ldots + \frac{1}{R_n}$$ $$R_{\text{eq}} = \frac{R_1 R_2}{R_1 + R_2}$$ $$V_1 = V_2 = \ldots = V$$ $$I = I_1 + I_2 + \ldots + I_n$$

Current Divider Rule (Parallel)

For two resistors R1 and R2 in parallel with total current I entering the combination:

$$I_1 = I \times \frac{R_2}{R_1 + R_2}$$ $$I_2 = I \times \frac{R_1}{R_1 + R_2}$$

Note that the larger current flows through the smaller resistance.

Mixed Combinations

In complex circuits, resistors may be connected in a mixture of series and parallel. The strategy is to identify groups that are purely series or purely parallel, replace each group by its equivalent, and repeat until a single equivalent resistance remains. For networks that cannot be reduced this way (e.g., the Wheatstone bridge), Kirchhoff's laws or star-delta transformations are needed.

Key Properties

  • Series connection increases the effective resistance; used in voltage dividers and current limiters.
  • Parallel connection decreases the effective resistance; used to distribute current and increase current-handling capacity.
  • For n identical resistors of resistance R each: series gives nR; parallel gives R/n.
Example 4 — Parallel Resistors

Two resistances of 4 Ω and 12 Ω are connected in parallel across a 24 V supply. Find the equivalent resistance, total current, and current through each resistor.

Solution:
1/Req = 1/4 + 1/12 = 3/12 + 1/12 = 4/12
Req = 12/4 = 3 Ω

Total current: I = V/Req = 24/3 = 8 A

Current through 4 Ω: I1 = V/R1 = 24/4 = 6 A
(or by divider rule: I1 = 8 × 12/(4 + 12) = 6 A)

Current through 12 Ω: I2 = V/R2 = 24/12 = 2 A

Verification: I1 + I2 = 6 + 2 = 8 A = I   ✓

7. EMF & Internal Resistance

Fig 12.7

Electromotive Force (EMF)

An electric cell converts chemical energy into electrical energy. The electromotive force (EMF, denoted E or ε) of a cell is the work done per unit positive charge by the non-electrostatic (chemical) force in taking the charge from the negative terminal to the positive terminal inside the cell. Despite its name, EMF is not a force — it has units of volts and represents the energy supplied per coulomb.

EMF equals the potential difference across the terminals of the cell only when no current is being drawn (open-circuit condition).

Internal Resistance

Every real cell has an internal resistance r due to the finite conductivity of its electrolyte and electrodes. When the cell delivers current, some energy is dissipated inside the cell itself as heat (I²r), reducing the voltage available at the external terminals.

Terminal Voltage

$$V = E - Ir \quad \text{(discharging)}$$ $$V = E + Ir \quad \text{(charging)}$$

Key observations: (i) when I = 0, terminal voltage = E; (ii) terminal voltage decreases as current increases during discharge; (iii) during charging, terminal voltage exceeds the EMF.

Current in a Simple Circuit

For a cell of EMF E and internal resistance r connected to an external resistance R, applying Kirchhoff's voltage law around the loop gives:

$$I = \frac{E}{R + r}$$

Cells in Series

When n cells are connected in series (same polarity order), EMFs add while internal resistances also add:

Enet = E1 + E2 + … + En
rnet = r1 + r2 + … + rn

If cells oppose: Enet = |E1 − E2|
(current direction determined by the cell with larger EMF)

Cells in Parallel

When n identical cells (each EMF E, internal resistance r) are connected in parallel across an external resistance R:

Enet = E   (same as one cell)
rnet = r/n

I = E/(R + r/n)

Parallel grouping is advantageous when the external resistance is small compared to the internal resistance of each cell. Series grouping is better when R is large compared to r.

Mixed Grouping

For m rows in parallel, each row containing n cells in series:

I = mnE/(mR + nr)

Maximum current when: R = nr/m (external resistance = total internal resistance)

8. Kirchhoff's Laws

Fig 12.8

Why Kirchhoff's Laws?

Simple series-parallel reduction cannot solve all circuits. Networks with multiple loops and batteries require a systematic approach. Gustav Kirchhoff formulated two laws — one based on conservation of charge and the other on conservation of energy — that together allow us to analyse any electrical network, no matter how complex.

Kirchhoff's Current Law (KCL) — Junction Rule

At any junction (node) in a circuit, the algebraic sum of all currents is zero. In other words, the total current entering a junction equals the total current leaving it. This is a direct consequence of the conservation of charge: charge cannot accumulate at a node in a steady-state circuit.

Σ Iin = Σ Iout

Or equivalently: Σ I = 0   (at any junction, taking a sign convention)

Kirchhoff's Voltage Law (KVL) — Loop Rule

Around any closed loop in a circuit, the algebraic sum of all potential differences (including EMFs and IR drops) is zero. This follows from the conservative nature of the electrostatic field: a charge returning to its starting point must have the same potential energy.

Σ ΔV = 0   (around any closed loop)

Σ E = Σ IR

Sign Conventions for Traversing a Loop

  • Choose a direction to traverse the loop (clockwise or anticlockwise).
  • Resistor: Traversing in the direction of current → potential change = −IR. Against current → +IR.
  • Battery: Traversing from − to + terminal → potential change = +E. From + to − terminal → −E.

Systematic Approach to Circuit Analysis

  • Step 1: Assign current variables (I1, I2, …) with assumed directions to each branch.
  • Step 2: Apply KCL at each independent node. Number of independent KCL equations = (number of nodes − 1).
  • Step 3: Apply KVL to each independent loop. Number of independent loops = (number of branches − number of nodes + 1).
  • Step 4: Solve the simultaneous equations. A negative value for any current means the actual direction is opposite to the assumed one.
Example 5 — Two-Loop Circuit with Kirchhoff's Laws

Two cells E1 = 10 V (r1 = 1 Ω) and E2 = 40 V (r2 = 2 Ω) are connected with an external resistance R = 15 Ω. E2 drives current through the circuit and E1 opposes it. Find the current through R and the terminal voltage of each cell.

Solution:
Net EMF: Enet = E2 − E1 = 40 − 10 = 30 V
Total resistance: Rtotal = R + r1 + r2 = 15 + 1 + 2 = 18 Ω

Current: I = Enet/Rtotal = 30/18 = 5/3 A ≈ 1.67 A

Terminal voltage of E1 (being charged):
V1 = E1 + Ir1 = 10 + (5/3)(1) = 35/3 V ≈ 11.67 V

Terminal voltage of E2 (discharging):
V2 = E2 − Ir2 = 40 − (5/3)(2) = 110/3 V ≈ 36.67 V

9. Wheatstone Bridge & Meter Bridge

Fig 12.9

The Wheatstone Bridge

The Wheatstone bridge is a network of four resistances P, Q, R, and S arranged in a quadrilateral (diamond) configuration. A galvanometer G is connected across one diagonal (say between junctions B and D), and a battery is connected across the other diagonal (between junctions A and C). The bridge is used to measure an unknown resistance with high precision.

Balanced Condition

The bridge is said to be balanced when no current flows through the galvanometer, meaning points B and D are at the same potential. Applying Kirchhoff's laws to the balanced bridge gives:

P/Q = R/S

Equivalently: P × S = Q × R (product of opposite arms are equal)

At balance, the galvanometer branch can be removed without affecting the circuit. The equivalent resistance of the balanced bridge is then (P + R) in parallel with (Q + S).

Key Properties

  • The balance condition is independent of the battery EMF and the galvanometer resistance.
  • Maximum sensitivity occurs when all four resistances are of comparable magnitude (ideally P ≈ Q ≈ R ≈ S).
  • The bridge is widely used in strain gauges, temperature sensors, and laboratory measurements.

The Meter Bridge (Slide Wire Bridge)

The meter bridge is a practical realisation of the Wheatstone bridge principle. It uses a uniform resistance wire (typically 100 cm long, made of constantan or nichrome) stretched along a metre scale. A standard (known) resistance R is placed in one gap, and the unknown resistance X is placed in the other. A jockey is slid along the wire to locate the null point where the galvanometer shows zero deflection.

At balance: X/R = l1/(100 − l1)

Therefore: X = R × l1/(100 − l1)

where l1 = length of wire on the side of X (cm)

Sources of Error in Meter Bridge

  • End corrections due to contact resistance at the wire junctions.
  • Non-uniformity of the wire along its length.
  • Heating of the wire by the current flowing through it.
  • The null point should be obtained near the centre of the wire (l ≈ 50 cm) for maximum sensitivity and minimum percentage error.
Example 6 — Meter Bridge with Parallel Resistance

Two unknown resistances X and Y are placed in the left and right gaps of a meter bridge. The null point is found at 80 cm from the left. When a 100 Ω resistance is connected in parallel across X, the null point shifts 20 cm to the left. Find X and Y.

Solution:
From first null point: X/Y = 80/20 = 4   … (i)

With 100 Ω in parallel with X:
Equivalent resistance = 100X/(100 + X)
New null point at 60 cm from left:
[100X/(100 + X)] / Y = 60/40 = 3/2   … (ii)

From (i): X = 4Y. Substituting into (ii):
[100(4Y)/(100 + 4Y)] / Y = 3/2
400/(100 + 4Y) = 3/2
800 = 300 + 12Y
Y = 500/12 = 125/3 Ω ≈ 41.67 Ω
X = 4Y = 500/3 Ω ≈ 166.67 Ω

10. Potentiometer

Fig 12.10

Principle

The potentiometer is an instrument that measures potential difference (or EMF) without drawing any current from the source under test. It works on the principle that when a steady current flows through a wire of uniform cross-section and material, the potential drop across any length of the wire is directly proportional to that length.

This makes the potentiometer a null method instrument — at balance, the galvanometer reads zero, so no current is drawn from the cell being measured. This gives it a significant advantage over a voltmeter, which always draws some current and therefore measures the terminal voltage rather than the true EMF.

Potential Gradient

Potential gradient: k = VAB/L   (volt per metre)

Potential difference across length x: V = kx = (VAB/L) × x

where VAB is the potential difference across the full length L of the potentiometer wire, maintained by an external battery (called the driver cell).

Comparison of EMFs of Two Cells

Connect cell 1 (EMF E1) and find balancing length l1. Then connect cell 2 (EMF E2) and find balancing length l2. Since E = kl at balance:

E1/E2 = l1/l2

Measurement of Internal Resistance of a Cell

First, find the balancing length l1 with the test cell in open circuit (giving E). Then connect a known resistance R across the cell and find the new balancing length l2 (giving the terminal voltage V = IR). Since E = I(R + r) and V = IR:

E/V = l1/l2

r = R(l1/l2 − 1)

Conditions for Successful Balancing

  • The EMF of the driver cell must be greater than the EMF being measured; otherwise no balance point exists.
  • The positive terminals of both the driver cell and the test cell must be connected to the same end of the wire.
  • The potentiometer wire should have uniform cross-section and resistivity along its entire length.

Advantages of Potentiometer over Voltmeter

  • Measures true EMF (no current drawn at balance).
  • Theoretically infinite input resistance at the null point.
  • Null method eliminates errors due to instrument calibration.
  • Can compare EMFs with high precision.

Sensitivity of Potentiometer

The sensitivity of a potentiometer increases when the potential gradient k is decreased. This can be achieved by: (i) using a longer wire, (ii) reducing the current through the wire by adding a series resistance (rheostat) in the driver circuit. A smaller k means a larger balancing length for the same EMF, which improves the precision of length measurement.

11. Heating Effect of Current & Electrical Power

Fig 12.11

Joule's Law of Heating

When current passes through a resistor, the kinetic energy gained by the charge carriers between collisions is transferred to the lattice ions at each collision. This converts electrical energy into thermal energy (Joule heating). The heat produced is given by Joule's law:

$$H = I^2 Rt \quad \text{(in joules)}$$ $$H = VIt = \frac{V^2 t}{R}$$ $$H_{\text{cal}} = \frac{I^2 Rt}{4.186}$$

Joule heating is the principle behind electric heaters, toasters, electric irons, fuses, and incandescent bulbs. In a bulb, only about 5% of the electrical energy is converted into visible light; the rest is dissipated as heat.

Electric Power

Power is the rate at which electrical energy is delivered or consumed. The SI unit is the watt (W), equal to one joule per second.

$$P = VI = I^2 R = \frac{V^2}{R}$$ $$1 \text{ W} = 1 \text{ J/s} = 1 \text{ V} \times 1 \text{ A}$$

Electrical Energy and the Commercial Unit

Energy = Power × Time
W = Pt = VIt

1 kilowatt-hour (kWh) = 1000 W × 3600 s = 3.6 × 106 J
(1 kWh is also called 1 "unit" of electricity)

Power Rating of Appliances

Every electrical appliance has a power rating (e.g., "100 W, 220 V") which specifies the power it consumes when operated at its rated voltage. The resistance of the appliance is:

$$R = \frac{V_{\text{rated}}^2}{P_{\text{rated}}}$$

If the actual supply voltage differs from the rated voltage, the actual power consumed changes (assuming resistance remains constant): Pactual = Vactual² / R.

Maximum Power Transfer Theorem

For a battery of EMF E and internal resistance r connected to external resistance R, the power delivered to R is P = I²R = E²R/(R + r)². This power is maximum when R = r:

$$P_{\max} = \frac{E^2}{4r}$$

At maximum power transfer, the efficiency is only 50% (half the power is lost in internal resistance). This theorem is important in electronics and communication circuits.

Example 7 — Power and Energy of an Electric Kettle

An electric kettle has a resistance of 20 Ω and is connected to a 220 V supply. Calculate the power consumed, the current, and the energy consumed in 5 minutes.

Solution:
Power: P = V²/R = (220)²/20 = 48400/20 = 2420 W = 2.42 kW

Current: I = V/R = 220/20 = 11 A

Energy in 5 min: W = Pt = 2420 × (5 × 60) = 2420 × 300
W = 726,000 J = 726 kJ = 0.202 kWh

12. Thermoelectricity

Fig 12.12

The Seebeck Effect

When two dissimilar metals are joined to form a closed loop (a thermocouple) and the two junctions are maintained at different temperatures, an EMF is developed in the circuit and a current flows. This phenomenon, discovered by Thomas Seebeck in 1821, is called the Seebeck effect. The EMF produced is called thermo-EMF.

The magnitude and direction of the thermo-EMF depend on the pair of metals used and the temperature difference between the junctions. For small temperature differences, the thermo-EMF is approximately proportional to the temperature difference.

e = αΔT + ½β(ΔT)²

where α and β are constants depending on the pair of metals,
ΔT = Thot − Tcold

Thermoelectric Series

Metals can be arranged in a thermoelectric series such that a metal earlier in the series develops a positive potential at the cold junction when paired with a metal later in the series. The series (partial): Bi, Ni, Co, Pd, Pt, Cu, Mn, Hg, Pb, Sn, Au, Ag, Zn, Cd, Fe, Sb, Te. The farther apart two metals are in the series, the greater the thermo-EMF produced.

Neutral Temperature and Inversion Temperature

As the temperature of the hot junction is gradually raised (keeping the cold junction fixed), the thermo-EMF first increases, reaches a maximum at the neutral temperature (Tn), and then decreases, becoming zero and eventually reversing direction at the inversion temperature (Ti).

Tn = (Tcold + Ti)/2

The neutral temperature Tn is the arithmetic mean of the cold junction
temperature and the inversion temperature.

The Peltier Effect

The Peltier effect is the reverse of the Seebeck effect. When a current is passed through a thermocouple, one junction is heated and the other is cooled. The heat absorbed or released at a junction per unit charge is called the Peltier coefficient (π). The Peltier effect is used in thermoelectric coolers and small refrigeration devices.

Peltier heat: Q = π × I × t

Peltier EMF: π = dQ/(I · dt)   (volts)

The Thomson Effect

William Thomson (Lord Kelvin) predicted that when a current flows through a single conductor with a temperature gradient along it, heat is absorbed or evolved (in addition to the Joule heating). This is the Thomson effect. The Thomson coefficient (σT) is the heat absorbed or released per unit current per unit temperature difference per unit time.

Thomson heat: dQ = σT × I × dT × dt

Lead (Pb) has a Thomson coefficient of nearly zero and is often used as a reference metal for thermoelectric measurements.

Applications of Thermoelectricity

  • Thermocouples: Used for temperature measurement, especially at extreme temperatures where mercury thermometers fail.
  • Thermoelectric generators (TEG): Convert waste heat into electrical energy using the Seebeck effect.
  • Peltier coolers: Solid-state cooling devices used in portable refrigerators, CPU coolers, and scientific instruments.
  • Thermopile: Multiple thermocouples connected in series for detecting weak thermal radiation.

13. Key Formulas Summary

Current and Charge

$$I = \frac{dQ}{dt} \quad Q = \int I \, dt \quad j = \frac{I}{A} \quad 1 \text{ A} = 1 \text{ C/s}$$

Drift Velocity and Mobility

$$v_d = \frac{eE\tau}{m} \quad I = nAev_d \quad j = nev_d$$ $$\mu = \frac{v_d}{E} = \frac{e\tau}{m} \quad \tau = \frac{m}{ne^2\rho}$$

Ohm's Law (Macroscopic and Microscopic)

$$V = IR \quad j = \sigma E \quad E = \rho j \quad \sigma = \frac{1}{\rho}$$

Resistance and Resistivity

$$R = \frac{\rho l}{A} \quad \rho_T = \rho_0(1 + \alpha \Delta T) \quad R_T = R_0(1 + \alpha \Delta T)$$

Series and Parallel Combinations

$$R_{\text{eq}} = R_1 + R_2 + \ldots$$ $$\frac{1}{R_{\text{eq}}} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} + \ldots$$ $$R_{\text{eq}} = \frac{R_1 R_2}{R_1 + R_2}$$

EMF and Internal Resistance

$$V = E - Ir \quad \text{(discharge)} \quad V = E + Ir \quad \text{(charge)}$$ $$I = \frac{E}{R + r} \quad \text{Series: } E_{\text{net}} = \sum E, \quad r_{\text{net}} = \sum r$$

Kirchhoff's Laws

$$\sum I_{\text{in}} = \sum I_{\text{out}} \quad \text{(KCL)}$$ $$\sum \Delta V = 0 \quad \text{(KVL, closed loop)}$$

Wheatstone Bridge and Meter Bridge

$$\frac{P}{Q} = \frac{R}{S} \quad \text{(Balance condition)}$$ $$\frac{X}{R} = \frac{l}{100 - l} \quad \text{(Meter Bridge)}$$

Potentiometer

$$k = \frac{V_{AB}}{L} \quad E = kl \quad \frac{E_1}{E_2} = \frac{l_1}{l_2}$$ $$r = R\left(\frac{l_1}{l_2} - 1\right)$$

Heating Effect and Power

$$H = I^2 Rt = VIt = \frac{V^2 t}{R}$$ $$P = VI = I^2 R = \frac{V^2}{R} \quad 1 \text{ kWh} = 3.6 \times 10^6 \text{ J}$$ $$P_{\max} = \frac{E^2}{4r} \quad \text{when } R = r$$

Thermoelectricity

$$e = \alpha \Delta T + \frac{1}{2}\beta(\Delta T)^2$$ $$T_n = \frac{T_{\text{cold}} + T_i}{2} \quad \text{or} \quad Q = \pi I t$$

Ammeter and Voltmeter Conversion

Shunt (ammeter): S = IgG/(I − Ig)
Multiplier (voltmeter): R = V/Ig − G

RC Circuits

τ = RC   |   Charging: q = Q(1 − e−t/τ),   i = (E/R)e−t/τ
Discharging: q = Q0e−t/τ,   i = (V0/R)e−t/τ
