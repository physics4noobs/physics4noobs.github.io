Electric Current Ohm's Law Series & Parallel Kirchhoff's Laws Wheatstone Bridge Potentiometer Energy & Power

1. Electric Current

Definition

Electric current is the rate of flow of electric charge through a cross-section of a conductor. By convention, the direction of current is taken as the direction in which positive charges would flow, which is opposite to the direction of electron flow.

I = dQ / dt

For steady current: I = Q / t
SI unit: Ampere (A) = C/s

Drift Velocity

In the absence of an electric field, free electrons in a conductor move randomly with high thermal velocities (of the order of 105 m/s) but with zero average displacement. When an electric field is applied, the electrons acquire a small systematic velocity superimposed on their random motion. This systematic velocity is called the drift velocity vd.

vd = eEτ / m

where e = electron charge, E = electric field,
τ = relaxation time (average time between successive collisions),
m = electron mass

Typical drift velocities are of the order of 10−4 m/s, which is much smaller than the random thermal speed. Despite this slow drift, the electric signal travels at nearly the speed of light because the electric field is set up almost instantaneously throughout the conductor.

Current Density

Current density J is a vector quantity that gives the current per unit cross-sectional area. It is directed along the direction of current flow.

J = I / A = nevd

where n = number density of free electrons (electrons per unit volume)

Mobility

Mobility (μ) of a charge carrier is defined as the drift velocity per unit electric field:

μ = vd / E = eτ / m
SI unit: m² V−1 s−1

2. Ohm's Law

Statement

Ohm's law states that the current through a conductor between two points is directly proportional to the potential difference across the two points, provided the physical conditions (temperature, mechanical strain, etc.) remain constant.

V = IR

where R = resistance of the conductor (unit: Ohm, Ω)

Resistance

Resistance is the opposition offered by a conductor to the flow of current. It depends on the material, length, cross-sectional area, and temperature of the conductor.

R = ρl / A

where ρ = resistivity of the material (unit: Ω m),
l = length of the conductor, A = cross-sectional area

Resistivity and Conductivity

Resistivity (ρ) is an intrinsic property of the material, independent of the shape or size of the conductor. It is the resistance offered by a unit cube of the material. Conductivity (σ) is the reciprocal of resistivity.

σ = 1 / ρ
SI unit of conductivity: S/m (siemens per metre)

In terms of microscopic quantities:
ρ = m / (ne²τ)   and   σ = ne²τ / m

Temperature Dependence of Resistance

For most metals, resistance increases with temperature. For small temperature changes, the variation is approximately linear:

RT = R0(1 + αΔT)
ρT = ρ0(1 + αΔT)

where α = temperature coefficient of resistance (unit: °C−1 or K−1)
  • Metals: α is positive (resistance increases with temperature).
  • Semiconductors and insulators: α is negative (resistance decreases with temperature).
  • Alloys like manganin and constantan have very small α, making them suitable for standard resistors.
Solved Example 1

A copper wire of length 2 m and cross-sectional area 1 mm² carries a current of 1 A. If the number density of free electrons in copper is n = 8.5 × 1028 m−3, find the drift velocity and the time taken by an electron to traverse the wire.

Solution:
I = 1 A, A = 1 × 10−6 m², n = 8.5 × 1028 m−3, e = 1.6 × 10−19 C

Using I = nAevd:
vd = I / (nAe) = 1 / [(8.5 × 1028)(1 × 10−6)(1.6 × 10−19)]
vd = 1 / (8.5 × 1028 × 1.6 × 10−25)
vd = 1 / (1.36 × 104) = 7.35 × 10−5 m/s

Time to traverse the wire:
t = l / vd = 2 / (7.35 × 10−5) = 2.72 × 104 s ≈ 7.55 hours

3. Resistors in Series & Parallel

Series Combination

When resistors are connected in series, the same current flows through each resistor. The total voltage is the sum of the individual voltage drops.

Req = R1 + R2 + R3 + ...

V = V1 + V2 + V3 + ...   (I same through all)

Parallel Combination

When resistors are connected in parallel, the voltage across each resistor is the same. The total current divides among the branches.

1/Req = 1/R1 + 1/R2 + 1/R3 + ...

For two resistors: Req = R1R2 / (R1 + R2)

I = I1 + I2 + I3 + ...   (V same across all)

Voltage Divider

For two resistors R1 and R2 in series across a voltage V:

V1 = V × R1 / (R1 + R2)
V2 = V × R2 / (R1 + R2)

Current Divider

For two resistors R1 and R2 in parallel with total current I:

I1 = I × R2 / (R1 + R2)
I2 = I × R1 / (R1 + R2)

Notice in the current divider that more current flows through the smaller resistance.

4. Kirchhoff's Laws

Kirchhoff's Current Law (KCL) — Junction Rule

At any junction (node) in an electrical circuit, the algebraic sum of currents entering the junction equals the algebraic sum of currents leaving it. Equivalently, the total current entering a junction equals the total current leaving it. This is a consequence of the conservation of charge.

∑ Iin = ∑ Iout

Or equivalently: ∑ I = 0   (at any junction)

Kirchhoff's Voltage Law (KVL) — Loop Rule

In any closed loop of an electrical circuit, the algebraic sum of all the potential differences (including EMFs and voltage drops across resistors) is zero. This is a consequence of the conservation of energy.

∑ V = 0   (around any closed loop)

∑ ε = ∑ IR   (EMFs = sum of voltage drops)

Sign Conventions for KVL

  • Choose a direction to traverse the loop (clockwise or anticlockwise).
  • When traversing a resistor in the direction of assumed current: voltage drop = −IR.
  • When traversing a resistor against the assumed current: voltage rise = +IR.
  • When traversing an EMF source from − to + terminal: voltage rise = +ε.
  • When traversing an EMF source from + to − terminal: voltage drop = −ε.

Solving Circuits with Kirchhoff's Laws

For a circuit with n unknowns, you need n independent equations. Use as many junction equations (KCL) as needed, and supplement with loop equations (KVL). For a circuit with b branches and n nodes, you need (n − 1) KCL equations and (b − n + 1) KVL equations.

Solved Example 2

In the circuit shown, two cells of EMF ε1 = 10 V (internal resistance r1 = 1 Ω) and ε2 = 4 V (internal resistance r2 = 2 Ω) are connected in parallel across an external resistance R = 10 Ω. Find the current through R.

Solution:
Let I1 be the current from ε1, I2 from ε2, and I through R.
By KCL at the junction: I = I1 + I2   ...(i)

Loop 1 (through ε1 and R):
ε1 − I1r1 − IR = 0
10 − I1 − 10I = 0   ...(ii)

Loop 2 (through ε2 and R):
ε2 − I2r2 − IR = 0
4 − 2I2 − 10I = 0   ...(iii)

From (ii): I1 = 10 − 10I
From (iii): I2 = (4 − 10I) / 2 = 2 − 5I
Substituting in (i): I = (10 − 10I) + (2 − 5I)
I = 12 − 15I
16I = 12
I = 0.75 A

5. Wheatstone Bridge

Principle

A Wheatstone bridge is an arrangement of four resistances P, Q, R, and S connected in a diamond (quadrilateral) configuration. A galvanometer is connected between the junctions of P-Q and R-S. The bridge is said to be balanced when no current flows through the galvanometer.

Balanced Condition

P / Q = R / S

When balanced, VB = VD   (points B and D are at equal potential)

At balance, the galvanometer can be effectively removed from the circuit without affecting the currents in the branches. This principle is widely used in measurement of unknown resistances.

Meter Bridge (Slide Wire Bridge)

The meter bridge is a practical form of the Wheatstone bridge. It uses a uniform wire of length 100 cm as two of the resistances. A standard resistance S is placed in one gap and the unknown resistance R in the other. A jockey is moved along the wire to find the balance point at length l from one end.

At balance: R / S = l / (100 − l)
Therefore: R = S × l / (100 − l)

Sensitivity of Wheatstone Bridge

The sensitivity of a Wheatstone bridge is highest when all four resistances are of comparable magnitude. The bridge is most sensitive when P = Q = R = S. High sensitivity means even a small imbalance produces a noticeable deflection in the galvanometer.

6. Potentiometer

Principle

A potentiometer is a device used to measure the EMF of a cell or potential difference without drawing any current from the source being measured. It works on the principle that when a constant current flows through a wire of uniform cross-section and material, the potential drop across any length of the wire is proportional to that length.

V ∝ l   (for a uniform wire carrying constant current)
V = (Iρ/A) × l = kl
where k = potential gradient (V/m)

Comparison of EMFs

Two cells with EMFs ε1 and ε2 are connected one at a time and their balance lengths l1 and l2 are found:

ε1 / ε2 = l1 / l2

Internal Resistance of a Cell

A potentiometer can be used to find the internal resistance of a cell. First find the balance length l1 with the cell in open circuit (EMF = ε). Then connect a known resistance R across the cell and find the new balance length l2 (terminal voltage = V):

ε / V = l1 / l2
Since ε = I(R + r) and V = IR:
r = R(l1 − l2) / l2

Advantages of Potentiometer over Voltmeter

  • At the balance point, no current is drawn from the cell being measured, so the EMF is measured accurately (no voltage drop across internal resistance).
  • A potentiometer has theoretically infinite resistance at balance.
  • It can compare EMFs with high precision.
  • It is a null method, which eliminates calibration errors.

7. Electrical Energy & Power

Electrical Energy

When a current I flows through a resistor R for a time t, the electrical energy dissipated as heat is given by Joule's law:

W = VIt = I²Rt = V²t / R

Electrical Power

Power is the rate at which electrical energy is dissipated or consumed:

P = VI = I²R = V² / R
SI unit: Watt (W) = J/s

Maximum Power Transfer Theorem

When a cell of EMF ε and internal resistance r is connected to an external load resistance R, the power delivered to the external resistance is:

P = ε²R / (R + r)²

To find the value of R for maximum power, we differentiate P with respect to R and set it to zero. This gives:

For maximum power transfer: R = r

Pmax = ε² / (4r)

The maximum power is transferred to the external load when the load resistance equals the internal resistance of the source. At this condition, the efficiency is 50% (half the total power is dissipated internally).

Combination of Bulbs

  • Series: The bulb with higher resistance (lower wattage rating) glows brighter because P = I²R and I is the same.
  • Parallel: The bulb with lower resistance (higher wattage rating) glows brighter because P = V²/R and V is the same.
Solved Example 3

A battery of EMF 12 V and internal resistance 2 Ω is connected to a variable external resistance R. Find the value of R for maximum power transfer, the maximum power delivered to R, and the efficiency at this condition.

Solution:
ε = 12 V, r = 2 Ω

For maximum power: R = r = 2 Ω

Current: I = ε / (R + r) = 12 / (2 + 2) = 3 A

Power to R: PR = I²R = (3)²(2) = 18 W
(Alternatively: Pmax = ε²/(4r) = 144/8 = 18 W)

Total power from battery: Ptotal = εI = 12 × 3 = 36 W
Power dissipated internally: Pr = I²r = 9 × 2 = 18 W

Efficiency = PR / Ptotal = 18/36 = 50%
