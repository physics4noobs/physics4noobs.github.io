Rotational Motion

GRADE XI | CHAPTER 04

1. Angular Kinematics

Rotational motion is the motion of a body about a fixed axis. Just as linear kinematics describes translational motion using displacement, velocity, and acceleration, angular kinematics uses their rotational counterparts: angular displacement, angular velocity, and angular acceleration.

Angular Displacement (θ)

Angular displacement is the angle swept by the radius vector of a rotating body. It is measured in radians (rad). The relationship between linear and angular displacement is: s = rθ, where s is the arc length and r is the radius of the circular path.

Angular Velocity (ω)

Angular velocity is the rate of change of angular displacement:

ω = dθ/dt   (instantaneous)
ωavg = Δθ/Δt
Relation to linear velocity: v = rω   (tangential velocity)

The direction of ω is along the axis of rotation, given by the right-hand rule: curl the fingers of the right hand in the direction of rotation, and the thumb points in the direction of ω.

Angular Acceleration (α)

α = dω/dt = d²θ/dt²
Relation to linear (tangential) acceleration: at = rα

Equations of Rotational Motion (constant α)

Analogous to the three kinematic equations for constant linear acceleration:

ω = ω0 + αt   (cf. v = u + at)
θ = ω0t + ½αt²   (cf. s = ut + ½at²)
ω² = ω0² + 2αθ   (cf. v² = u² + 2as)

2. Torque

Torque (or moment of force) is the rotational analogue of force. It is the tendency of a force to cause or change the rotational motion of a body. Torque is defined as the cross product of the position vector and the force vector:

τ = r × F
|τ| = rF sinθ = r⊥F = rF⊥
SI unit: Newton-metre (N·m)

where θ is the angle between r and F, r⊥ = r sinθ is the moment arm (perpendicular distance from the axis to the line of action of the force), and F⊥ = F sinθ is the component of force perpendicular to r.

Couple

A couple consists of two equal and opposite parallel forces whose lines of action do not coincide. A couple produces pure rotation (no translation). The torque of a couple is:

τcouple = F × d   (where d is the perpendicular distance between the two forces)

The torque of a couple is the same about any point in the plane.

Conditions for Equilibrium

For a rigid body to be in complete equilibrium, two conditions must be simultaneously satisfied:

  • Translational equilibrium: ΣF = 0 (net force is zero — no linear acceleration).
  • Rotational equilibrium: Στ = 0 about any point (net torque is zero — no angular acceleration).

3. Moment of Inertia

The moment of inertia (I) is the rotational analogue of mass. It measures the resistance of a body to angular acceleration, just as mass measures resistance to linear acceleration. Newton’s second law for rotation is:

τnet = Iα   (analogous to F = ma)

For Discrete Particles

For a system of point masses m1, m2, …, mn at perpendicular distances r1, r2, …, rn from the axis of rotation:

I = Σ miri² = m1r1² + m2r2² + … + mnrn²

For Continuous Bodies

For a continuous body, the summation is replaced by integration: I = ∫ r² dm, where dm is an infinitesimal mass element at distance r from the axis.

Standard Moments of Inertia

Thin rod (about center, perpendicular): I = ML²/12
Thin rod (about end, perpendicular): I = ML²/3
Ring (about axis through center): I = MR²
Ring (about diameter): I = MR²/2
Solid disc/cylinder (about axis): I = MR²/2
Solid disc (about diameter): I = MR²/4
Solid sphere (about diameter): I = 2MR²/5
Hollow sphere (about diameter): I = 2MR²/3
Hollow cylinder (about axis): I = MR² (thin-walled) or M(R1² + R2²)/2 (thick-walled)

The radius of gyration k is defined by I = Mk², so k = √(I/M). It represents the effective distance from the axis at which the entire mass may be considered concentrated.

4. Parallel and Perpendicular Axis Theorems

Parallel Axis Theorem

The moment of inertia of a body about any axis is equal to its moment of inertia about a parallel axis through the centre of mass, plus the product of the total mass and the square of the perpendicular distance between the two axes:

I = Icm + Md²

where Icm is the moment of inertia about the centre-of-mass axis, M is the total mass, and d is the distance between the two parallel axes. This theorem is valid for any body and any pair of parallel axes. The moment of inertia is minimum about the axis through the centre of mass.

Application: Moment of inertia of a rod about one end = ML²/12 + M(L/2)² = ML²/3.

Perpendicular Axis Theorem

For a planar (2D/laminar) body, the moment of inertia about an axis perpendicular to the plane is equal to the sum of the moments of inertia about two mutually perpendicular axes in the plane that intersect at the point where the perpendicular axis passes through:

Iz = Ix + Iy

This theorem applies only to flat (planar) bodies like discs, rings, and flat plates. It does NOT apply to 3D bodies like spheres or cylinders (about their length).

Application: For a disc, Iaxis = MR²/2. By symmetry, the two diameters are equivalent: Id1 = Id2. Using the perpendicular axis theorem: MR²/2 = 2Idiameter, so Idiameter = MR²/4.

5. Angular Momentum

Angular momentum is the rotational analogue of linear momentum. For a rigid body rotating about a fixed axis with moment of inertia I and angular velocity ω:

L = Iω   (for rotation about a fixed axis)
L = r × p = r × mv   (for a particle)
|L| = mvr sinθ = mvr⊥

The relation between torque and angular momentum (analogous to F = dp/dt):

τnet = dL/dt

Conservation of Angular Momentum

If the net external torque on a system is zero, the total angular momentum of the system remains constant:

If τext = 0, then L = Iω = constant
I1ω1 = I2ω2

The Ice Skater Effect

When an ice skater spins with arms outstretched and then pulls their arms in, their moment of inertia decreases (mass moves closer to the axis). Since no external torque acts (ideally), angular momentum is conserved, and the angular velocity increases dramatically. This is why figure skaters spin faster with arms tucked in and slow down when arms are extended.

Other examples: a diver tucking during a somersault, a spinning neutron star (pulsar) contracting and spinning faster, a cat twisting to land on its feet.

6. Rotational Kinetic Energy

A rotating body possesses kinetic energy due to its rotational motion. For a body rotating about a fixed axis with moment of inertia I and angular velocity ω:

KErot = ½Iω²

This is analogous to translational KE = ½mv². The rotational kinetic energy can also be expressed in terms of angular momentum: KErot = L²/2I.

The work-energy theorem for rotation states that the net work done by torques equals the change in rotational kinetic energy:

W = ∫ τ dθ = ΔKErot = ½Iωf² − ½Iωi²

Power in rotational motion: P = τω (analogous to P = Fv).

7. Rolling Motion

Rolling is a combination of translational and rotational motion. A body that rolls without slipping has a special constraint that connects its translational and rotational quantities.

Condition for Rolling Without Slipping

vcm = Rω   (no-slip condition)
acm = Rα   (differentiating the above)

At the contact point with the ground, the velocity is zero (vcontact = vcm − Rω = 0). This is the instantaneous axis of rotation.

Total Kinetic Energy of a Rolling Body

The total kinetic energy is the sum of translational and rotational kinetic energies:

KEtotal = KEtrans + KErot = ½mvcm² + ½Icmω²
Using vcm = Rω and Icm = Mk²:
KEtotal = ½mvcm²(1 + k²/R²)

where k is the radius of gyration. The ratio KErot/KEtotal = k²/(R² + k²) and KEtrans/KEtotal = R²/(R² + k²).

Rolling on an Incline

For a body of mass M, radius R, and radius of gyration k rolling without slipping down an incline of angle θ:

Acceleration: a = g sinθ / (1 + k²/R²)
Velocity at bottom (from height h): v = √[2gh / (1 + k²/R²)]
Time to reach bottom (from rest, length L): t = √[2L(1 + k²/R²) / (g sinθ)]

The friction required for rolling (not sliding): f = Mg sinθ / (1 + R²/k²). For rolling to occur without slipping, this friction must not exceed μsN = μsMg cosθ, which gives the condition: tanθ ≤ μs(1 + R²/k²).

Comparison of Rolling Bodies on an Incline

Since acceleration a = g sinθ / (1 + k²/R²), the body with the smallest k²/R² has the largest acceleration and reaches the bottom first. Ranking (fastest to slowest):

  • Solid sphere: k²/R² = 2/5,   a = 5g sinθ/7   (fastest)
  • Solid cylinder / disc: k²/R² = 1/2,   a = 2g sinθ/3
  • Hollow sphere: k²/R² = 2/3,   a = 3g sinθ/5
  • Hollow cylinder / ring: k²/R² = 1,   a = g sinθ/2   (slowest)

This ranking is independent of mass and radius — it depends only on the shape (mass distribution). A small solid sphere beats a large hollow cylinder every time.

Solved Examples

Example 1 — Moment of Inertia using Parallel Axis Theorem

A uniform solid disc of mass 5 kg and radius 0.2 m rotates about an axis tangent to its rim and perpendicular to its plane. Find the moment of inertia about this axis.

Solution: The moment of inertia of the disc about an axis through the centre and perpendicular to its plane is:

Icm = MR²/2 = 5 × (0.2)²/2 = 5 × 0.04/2 = 0.1 kg·m²

The tangent axis is at distance d = R = 0.2 m from the centre-of-mass axis. By the parallel axis theorem:

I = Icm + Md² = 0.1 + 5 × (0.2)² = 0.1 + 5 × 0.04 = 0.1 + 0.2 = 0.3 kg·m²

Example 2 — Conservation of Angular Momentum

A turntable of moment of inertia 4 kg·m² rotates at 2 rev/s. A disc of moment of inertia 1 kg·m² is dropped coaxially onto the turntable. Find the common angular velocity after the disc is placed.

Solution: No external torque acts on the system (the internal friction between the disc and turntable is an internal force). Angular momentum is conserved:

I1ω1 = (I1 + I2)ωf
4 × 2 = (4 + 1) × ωf
8 = 5ωf
ωf = 8/5 = 1.6 rev/s

KE lost = ½I1ω1² − ½(I1 + I2)ωf² = ½ × 4 × (2 × 2π)² − ½ × 5 × (1.6 × 2π)². This energy is dissipated as heat due to friction between the surfaces.

Example 3 — Rolling on an Incline

A solid sphere, a solid cylinder, and a hollow sphere, all having the same mass and radius, are released simultaneously from the top of a frictionless incline of height 3 m. They all roll without slipping. Find the speed of each at the bottom. Which reaches first? (g = 10 m/s²)

Solution: Using energy conservation for rolling: mgh = ½mv²(1 + k²/R²)

v = √[2gh / (1 + k²/R²)]

Solid sphere (k²/R² = 2/5):
v = √[2 × 10 × 3 / (1 + 2/5)] = √[60/1.4] = √42.86 ≈ 6.55 m/s

Solid cylinder (k²/R² = 1/2):
v = √[60 / 1.5] = √40 ≈ 6.32 m/s

Hollow sphere (k²/R² = 2/3):
v = √[60 / (5/3)] = √36 = 6.0 m/s

The solid sphere has the highest speed and the greatest acceleration, so it reaches first. Order: solid sphere, then solid cylinder, then hollow sphere. This ranking is independent of mass and radius — only the shape (mass distribution) matters.

Key Formulas Summary

ω = ω0 + αt   |   θ = ω0t + ½αt²   |   ω² = ω0² + 2αθ
τ = r × F = Iα   |   L = Iω   |   τ = dL/dt
KErot = ½Iω²   |   P = τω
I = Icm + Md² (parallel axis)   |   Iz = Ix + Iy (perpendicular axis, planar bodies)
vcm = Rω (rolling)   |   KEtotal = ½mv²(1 + k²/R²)
aincline = g sinθ / (1 + k²/R²)
