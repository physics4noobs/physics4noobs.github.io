1. Rotational Kinematics

Rotational motion is the motion of a body about a fixed axis. Just as linear kinematics describes translational motion using displacement, velocity, and acceleration, angular kinematics uses their rotational counterparts: angular displacement, angular velocity, and angular acceleration. All points on a rotating body share the same angular velocity and angular acceleration, but have different tangential speeds and accelerations depending on their distance from the rotation axis.

Angular Displacement, Velocity, and Acceleration

Angular displacement (θ) is the angle swept by the radius vector of a rotating body, measured in radians. The relationship between linear and angular displacement is s = rθ, where s is arc length.

Angular velocity (ω) is the rate of change of angular displacement: ω = dθ/dt. For a body rotating with angular velocity ω, a point at distance r from the axis has tangential velocity v = rω. The period and frequency relationships are: ω = 2π/T = 2πf.

Angular acceleration (α) is the rate of change of angular velocity: α = dω/dt. A point at distance r has tangential acceleration at = rα and radial (centripetal) acceleration ar = ω²r = v²/r.

ω = 2π/T = 2πf   |   v = rω
at = rα   |   ar = ω²r
Diagram: Rotating disc with point at distance r, showing angular velocity vector, tangential velocity vector, centripetal acceleration toward center, and tangential acceleration

When angular acceleration is constant, the rotational equations are analogous to the linear kinematic equations with θ replacing s, ω replacing v, and α replacing a:

ωf = ωi + αt
θ = ωit + ½αt²
ωf² = ωi² + 2αθ

Non-Constant Angular Acceleration

When α is not constant, use calculus integration: ω = ∫ α dt and θ = ∫ ω dt. These allow solving problems where acceleration varies with time or angular position.

Example 1 — Stopping Wheel

A wheel is rotating at 50 rev/min in the anticlockwise direction. What angular acceleration is needed to stop it in 8 seconds? How many revolutions does it cover before stopping?

Solution:

Step 1: ωi = 2π(50/60) = 5π/3 rad/s, ωf = 0

Step 2: α = (ωf - ωi)/t = (0 - 5π/3)/8 = -5π/24 rad/s² (clockwise)

Step 3: θ = (ωf² - ωi²)/(2α) = (0 - (5π/3)²) / (2(-5π/24)) = 20π/3 rad

Step 4: Number of revolutions = θ/(2π) = (20π/3)/(2π) = 10/3 revolutions (3.33 revolutions)

2. Moment of Inertia

The moment of inertia (I) is the rotational analogue of mass. It measures the resistance of a body to angular acceleration, just as mass measures resistance to linear acceleration. For a system of point masses or a continuous body rotating about a fixed axis, moment of inertia is the sum (or integral) of mass elements weighted by the square of their distance from the axis.

Definition and Calculation

For discrete masses: I = Σ miri²
For continuous bodies: I = ∫ r² dm

Moment of inertia is related to rotational kinetic energy: KErot = ½Iω², analogous to translational kinetic energy KE = ½mv².

Diagram: Several point masses at different distances from rotation axis, with r₁, r₂, r₃ labeled, showing how I depends on distance squared

Radius of Gyration

The radius of gyration (k) is a distance such that if all mass were concentrated there, the moment of inertia would be the same. It is defined by I = Mk², so k = √(I/M). This provides a convenient comparison of mass distributions across different bodies.

Standard Moments of Inertia

For common shapes about standard axes:

Thin Rod (about center, perpendicular): I = ML²/12
Thin Rod (about end, perpendicular): I = ML²/3
Circular Ring (about axis through center): I = MR²
Circular Ring (about diameter): I = MR²/2
Solid Disc/Cylinder (about axis): I = MR²/2
Solid Disc (about diameter): I = MR²/4
Solid Sphere (about diameter): I = 2MR²/5
Hollow Sphere (about diameter): I = 2MR²/3
Hollow Cylinder (about axis): I = MR²

3. Theorems for Moment of Inertia

Parallel Axis Theorem

The moment of inertia of a body about any axis equals its moment of inertia about a parallel axis through the centre of mass, plus the product of the mass and the square of the distance between axes. This is one of the most powerful tools for calculating moments of inertia.

IP = ICM + Md²

where ICM is the moment of inertia about the centre-of-mass axis, M is total mass, and d is the perpendicular distance between axes. The moment of inertia is minimum about the centre-of-mass axis.

Diagram: Rigid body with two parallel axes: one through center of mass and one at distance d away, showing how parallel axis theorem applies

Perpendicular Axis Theorem

For planar (2D) bodies only (like discs, rings, flat plates), the moment of inertia about an axis perpendicular to the plane equals the sum of moments of inertia about two mutually perpendicular axes in the plane that intersect at the same point.

Iz = Ix + Iy

This theorem does not apply to 3D bodies like spheres or solid cylinders. It is particularly useful for finding moments of inertia of planar bodies about diameters.

Example 2 — Radius of Gyration of Sphere

The radius of gyration of a solid sphere of radius R about a certain axis is R. Find the distance of this axis from the centre of the sphere.

Solution:

Step 1: Given k = R, so I = Mk² = MR²

Step 2: By parallel axis theorem: I = ICM + Md² = (2/5)MR² + Md²

Step 3: MR² = (2/5)MR² + Md²

Step 4: d² = R² - (2/5)R² = (3/5)R², so d = √0.6 · R ≈ 0.775R

4. Torque

Torque (or moment of force) is the rotational analogue of force. It measures the ability of a force to cause or change rotational motion about a fixed axis. Torque is the cross product of the position vector and force vector.

τ = r × F
|τ| = rF sinθ = r⊥F = rF⊥
SI unit: Newton-metre (N·m)

where θ is the angle between r and F, r⊥ = r sinθ is the moment arm (perpendicular distance from the axis to the line of action of the force), and F⊥ = F sinθ is the perpendicular component of the force.

Diagram: Position vector r from pivot to point of force application, force F at angle, moment arm r_perp shown perpendicular to F, torque direction by right-hand rule

Couple

A couple consists of two equal and opposite forces acting along parallel lines that do not coincide. A couple produces pure rotation without translation. The torque of a couple is independent of the point about which it is calculated and is given by:

τcouple = F × d
where d is the perpendicular distance between the lines of action of the two forces

Conditions for Equilibrium

For a rigid body to be in complete equilibrium, two conditions must be simultaneously satisfied:

  • Translational equilibrium: ΣF = 0 (net force is zero)
  • Rotational equilibrium: Στ = 0 about any point (net torque is zero)

5. Work & Power in Rotational Motion

Just as work in translational motion is W = F · s, work in rotational motion is the product of torque and angular displacement. This work-energy relationship is fundamental to solving rotational problems.

W = τ Δθ   (for constant torque)
W = ∫ τ dθ   (for variable torque)
P = dW/dt = τ ω
W = ΔKErot = ½Iωf² − ½Iωi²

Rotational power P = τω is analogous to translational power P = Fv. The work-energy theorem states that the net work done by torques equals the change in rotational kinetic energy.

6. Newton's Second Law for Rotation

The fundamental equation governing rotational dynamics relates net torque to angular acceleration. Just as F = ma in translational motion, we have τnet = Iα in rotational motion. This equation is the cornerstone of solving rotational dynamics problems.

τnet = Iα
Also: τnet = dL/dt (rate of change of angular momentum)

This law shows that torque plays the role of force, moment of inertia plays the role of mass, and angular acceleration plays the role of linear acceleration.

Example 3 — Grindstone Acceleration

A grindstone (solid cylinder) has radius 0.5 m and mass 50 kg. What torque brings it from rest to 300 rev/min in 10 seconds? What is its rotational kinetic energy at 300 rev/min?

Solution:

Step 1: ωf = 2π(300/60) = 10π rad/s, ωi = 0

Step 2: α = (ωf - ωi)/t = (10π - 0)/10 = π rad/s²

Step 3: I = ½MR² = ½ × 50 × (0.5)² = 6.25 kg·m²

Step 4: τ = Iα = 6.25 × π = 19.6 N·m

Step 5: KE = ½Iω² = ½ × 6.25 × (10π)² = 3084 J

7. Atwood Machine with Massive Pulley

When a pulley has mass and moment of inertia I, the tensions on the two sides of the string are not equal. The net torque on the pulley (τ = (T2 - T1)R = Iα) causes it to accelerate angularly. This coupling between the translational motion of the blocks and rotational motion of the pulley makes the problem more complex than an ideal massless pulley.

For blocks of mass m1 and m2 (m2 > m1) with disc pulley of mass M:
a = (m2 - m1)g / (m1 + m2 + M/2)
Constraint: a = Rα (linear acceleration of blocks = tangential acceleration of pulley rim)

The acceleration is less than that of a massless pulley because some of the gravitational potential energy goes into rotating the pulley. The factor M/2 for a disc pulley reflects the relationship between its moment of inertia and mass.

8. Angular Momentum

Angular momentum is the rotational analogue of linear momentum. For a rigid body rotating about a fixed axis, angular momentum L is simply the product of moment of inertia and angular velocity. For a particle moving in space, angular momentum is the cross product of position and linear momentum vectors.

For rigid body about fixed axis: L = Iω
For a particle: L = r × (mv) = r × p
Magnitude: L = mvr sinθ = mvr⊥
Relation to torque: τnet = dL/dt

The direction of angular momentum follows the right-hand rule. The relationship between torque and angular momentum (τ = dL/dt) is analogous to the relationship between force and linear momentum (F = dp/dt).

Example 4 — Angular Momentum of Particle

A particle of mass 1 kg moves along the line y = x + 2 with speed 2 m/s. Find the angular momentum about the origin.

Solution:

Step 1: L = mvr⊥ where r⊥ is the perpendicular distance from origin to the line of motion

Step 2: The line y = x + 2 can be written as x - y + 2 = 0

Step 3: r⊥ = |0 - 0 + 2|/√(1² + 1²) = 2/√2 = √2 m

Step 4: L = 1 × 2 × √2 = 2√2 kg·m²/s ≈ 2.83 kg·m²/s

9. Conservation of Angular Momentum

If the net external torque on a system is zero, the total angular momentum remains constant. This is one of the most powerful conservation principles in physics. It applies to isolated systems and to systems where the net torque about a specific point is zero.

If τext = 0, then Linitial = Lfinal
I1ω1 = I2ω2

Classic examples: An ice skater pulling in their arms increases angular velocity because moment of inertia decreases while angular momentum remains constant. A cat twisting to land on its feet uses sequential rotation of different body parts, conserving angular momentum about the centre of mass. A diver performing a somersault spins faster when tucked.

Example 5 — Turntable and Disc

A turntable of moment of inertia 4 kg·m² rotates at 2 rev/s. A disc of moment of inertia 1 kg·m² is dropped coaxially onto the turntable. Find the common angular velocity after the disc is placed.

Solution:

Step 1: No external torque acts (internal friction is not external). Angular momentum is conserved.

Step 2: I1ω1 = (I1 + I2)ωf

Step 3: 4 × 2 = (4 + 1) × ωf

Step 4: 8 = 5ωf, so ωf = 1.6 rev/s

Energy is dissipated as heat due to friction between the surfaces during the process.

10. Angular Impulse

Angular impulse is the rotational analogue of linear impulse. Just as linear impulse equals the change in linear momentum, angular impulse equals the change in angular momentum. This is particularly useful for problems involving collisions and impulsive forces.

J = ∫ τ dt = ΔL = Lf - Li
For point force: J = Ilinear × r⊥
where Ilinear is the linear impulse and r⊥ is the perpendicular distance

Compare the relation τ = Fr⊥ (torque) with J = Ir⊥ (angular impulse). Angular impulse is typically calculated about the centre of mass or about a fixed pivot point.

11. General Rigid Body Motion

The most general motion of a rigid body is a combination of translation of the centre of mass and rotation about the centre of mass. This principle applies to objects that move through space while rotating, such as rolling balls, tumbling bodies, and projectiles spinning.

Total kinetic energy: Ktotal = Ktrans + Krot = ½MVCM² + ½ICMω²

Velocity of Any Point on the Rigid Body

The velocity of any point A on the rigid body is the vector sum of the velocity of the centre of mass and the velocity of point A relative to the centre of mass (which is purely rotational about the CM).

VA = VCM + Vrelative
where Vrelative = ω × rrelative (tangential relative to CM)

12. Rolling Without Slipping

Rolling is a combination of translational and rotational motion with a special constraint: the point of contact with the surface has zero velocity. This no-slip condition provides the fundamental constraint that relates the linear velocity of the centre of mass to the angular velocity.

The Rolling Condition

VCM = Rω   (no-slip condition)
ACM = Rα   (differentiating the above)
Vcontact = 0   (instantaneous axis of rotation at contact point)
Diagram: Rolling wheel with contact point at ground, center velocity V_CM pointing forward, angular velocity omega, top point velocity 2V_CM shown

On a rolling wheel, the contact point has zero velocity, the centre has velocity VCM, and the top has velocity 2VCM. For a general point at angle θ from the bottom contact point:

Vtop = 2VCM
Vcontact = 0
Vpoint at angle θ = 2VCM sin(θ/2)

Kinetic Energy of Rolling Bodies

The total kinetic energy is the sum of translational and rotational parts:

KEtotal = ½mVCM² + ½ICMω²
Using VCM = Rω and ICM = mk²:
KEtotal = ½mVCM²(1 + k²/R²)
Ratio: KEtrans/KErot = R²/k²

For a solid sphere (k²/R² = 2/5), 71% of the energy is translational and 29% is rotational. For a ring (k²/R² = 1), exactly half is translational and half is rotational.

13. Rolling on an Inclined Plane

When a body rolls without slipping down an incline, static friction provides the torque needed for rotation while also affecting the translational acceleration. The acceleration depends on the mass distribution (shape) of the body.

Acceleration and Friction

Linear acceleration: ACM = g sinθ / (1 + k²/R²)
Friction force: f = mg sinθ / (1 + R²/k²)
Speed at bottom (height h): VCM = √[2gh / (1 + k²/R²)]
Condition for pure rolling: μs ≥ tanθ / (1 + R²/k²)

Ranking of Rolling Bodies

Bodies with smaller k²/R² accelerate faster and reach the bottom first. The ranking is independent of mass and radius—it depends only on the shape:

  • Solid sphere: k²/R² = 2/5, a = 5g sinθ/7 (fastest)
  • Solid cylinder/disc: k²/R² = 1/2, a = 2g sinθ/3
  • Hollow sphere: k²/R² = 2/3, a = 3g sinθ/5
  • Hollow cylinder/ring: k²/R² = 1, a = g sinθ/2 (slowest)
Example 6 — Comparing Rolling Bodies on an Incline

A solid sphere, solid cylinder, and hollow sphere, all with mass 1 kg and radius 0.1 m, roll down a frictionless incline from height 3 m. Find their speeds at the bottom and rank them.

Solution:

Using energy conservation: mgh = ½mV²(1 + k²/R²), so V = √[2gh / (1 + k²/R²)]

Solid sphere (k²/R² = 2/5):
V = √[2 × 10 × 3 / 1.4] = √42.86 = 6.55 m/s

Solid cylinder (k²/R² = 1/2):
V = √[60 / 1.5] = √40 = 6.32 m/s

Hollow sphere (k²/R² = 2/3):
V = √[60 / (5/3)] = √36 = 6.0 m/s

Ranking (fastest to slowest): Solid sphere > Solid cylinder > Hollow sphere

14. Transition from Sliding to Rolling

When a sphere (or other body) is given an initial translational velocity on a rough surface with no initial spin, kinetic friction decelerates the centre of mass while simultaneously accelerating the rotation. The body transitions from pure sliding to pure rolling when the rolling condition VCM = Rω is satisfied.

General Transition Formula

Time to reach pure rolling: t = (V0k²) / ((k² + R²)μg)
Final rolling velocity: Vfinal = V0R² / (k² + R²)

Special Case: Solid Sphere

For a solid sphere (I = 2/5 mR²), starting with initial velocity V0:

Time to rolling: t = 2V0 / (7μg)
Final velocity: Vfinal = 5V0/7
Energy lost to friction: ΔE = ½mV0²/7

The energy loss during transition is dissipated due to sliding friction at the contact point. Once pure rolling is established, friction becomes static (or zero) and there is no further energy dissipation on a horizontal surface.

Example 7 — Billiard Ball Struck at Height h

A billiard ball (solid sphere) is struck by a cue at height h above the centre. What must h be for the ball to roll without slipping immediately after impact?

Solution:

For immediate pure rolling: VCM = Rω

Linear impulse: I = mVCM

Angular impulse about CM: I × h = ICMω = (2/5)mR² × ω

Dividing: h = (2/5)mR² × ω / mVCM = (2/5)R² ω / (Rω) = 2R/5

So the cue must strike at height 2R/5 above the centre for immediate pure rolling. For other shapes, h = k²/R.

15. Collisions Involving Rotation

When a particle collides with a rotating body (or a rotating body collides with another), both linear momentum and angular momentum must be conserved (in the absence of external impulses). The point of application of the impulse affects both the linear and angular motion.

Impulse on a Rod

Linear impulse: J = M × VCM
Angular impulse about CM: J × d = ICMω
where d is the distance from the CM to the point of impulse application

For a pivoted rod struck at distance x from the pivot, angular momentum is conserved about the pivot. For a free rod on a smooth surface struck by a particle, both linear and angular momentum are conserved.

Example 8 — Particle Colliding with Meter Stick

A meter stick (mass M, length L) lies on a frictionless table. A hockey puck (mass m, velocity v) collides elastically with one end. Find the puck's velocity after impact, and the CM velocity and angular velocity of the stick.

Solution:

Step 1: Conservation of linear momentum: mv = mv1 + MVCM

Step 2: Conservation of angular momentum about CM: mv(L/2) = mv1(L/2) + ICMω

Step 3: Elastic collision at contact: VCM + (L/2)ω - v1 = v

Step 4: Solving the three equations:
v1 = v(4m - M)/(4m + M)
VCM = 2mv/(4m + M)
ω = 12mv/((4m + M)L)

16. Equilibrium & Special Topics

Toppling vs Sliding

When a block with square base (side a) and height h is placed on an incline of angle θ, it may either topple (rotate about the edge) or slide depending on the friction coefficient and angle. The block topples if the torque about the contact edge exceeds the restoring torque.

Condition for toppling before sliding: μ > a/h
Condition for sliding before toppling: μ < a/h
Critical angle for toppling: tanθ = a/h

Equilibrium of Rigid Bodies

For a rigid body in static equilibrium, both translational and rotational equilibrium conditions must be satisfied:

ΣFx = 0, ΣFy = 0 (no translation)
Στ = 0 about any point (no rotation)

Cyclist on a Horizontal Curve

A cyclist going around a horizontal curve of radius r at speed v must lean at angle θ from vertical to maintain balance. The friction provides the centripetal force, and torque equilibrium about the centre of gravity determines the lean angle.

Lean angle: tanθ = v²/(rg)
Maximum speed without skidding: v = √(μrg)

Cylinder Rolling off an Edge

When a solid cylinder is placed on a horizontal edge and given a slight push to roll, it follows the curved edge and eventually loses contact when the normal force becomes zero. Using energy conservation and the centripetal force requirement, the cylinder leaves contact at a specific angle.

Angle at separation: θ = cos-1(4/7)   (for solid cylinder)
Speed at separation: VCM = √(4Rg/7)
Contact loss condition: mg cosθ = mVCM²/R

Rolling on a Track with Loop

A sphere rolling on a track enters a circular loop of radius R. Energy conservation and force balance determine the normal force at any point on the loop. For the sphere to maintain contact at the top, the normal force must be non-negative.

Normal force at bottom: N = mg + mVCM²/(R - r)
Normal force at top: N = mVCM²/(R - r) - mg
Energy conservation: mgh = ½mV²(1 + k²/R²)

Tension in a Rotating Ring

A thin ring of radius r rotating with angular velocity ω experiences tension throughout due to the centripetal acceleration. Consider a small element of the ring; the tension provides the centripetal force for that element.

Tension in ring: T = mω²r²
where m is the mass per unit length of the ring
