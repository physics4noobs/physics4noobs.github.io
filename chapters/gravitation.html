05 — Gravitation

Universal Gravitation Field & Potential Acceleration due to g Gravitational PE Escape Velocity Orbital Motion & Kepler Satellites Key Formulas Solved Examples

1. Newton’s Law of Universal Gravitation

Fig 5.1

Every particle in the universe attracts every other particle with a force that is directly proportional to the product of their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them. This is Newton’s Law of Universal Gravitation, one of the most fundamental laws of classical physics.

$$F = \frac{GMm}{r^2}$$

Here, G is the universal gravitational constant with the value G = 6.674 × 10−11 N m² kg−2. The force is always attractive and acts along the line joining the centres of the two masses.

Vector Form

In vector notation, the gravitational force exerted by mass M on mass m is:

$$\vec{F} = -\frac{GMm}{r^2} \hat{r}$$

The negative sign indicates that the force is attractive — it is directed from mass m towards mass M, opposite to the unit vector r̂ pointing from M to m. Gravitation obeys the principle of superposition: the net force on a particle is the vector sum of the individual gravitational forces due to all other particles.

2. Gravitational Field & Potential

Fig 5.2

Gravitational Field Intensity

The gravitational field intensity (or gravitational field strength) at a point is the force experienced per unit mass placed at that point. For a point mass M at distance r:

$$g = \frac{GM}{r^2}$$

The field is a vector quantity directed radially inward towards the mass creating it. Inside a uniform spherical shell, the gravitational field is zero (Shell Theorem). Outside the shell, the field behaves as though all the mass were concentrated at the centre.

Gravitational Potential

Gravitational potential at a point is defined as the work done per unit mass in bringing a test mass from infinity to that point. For a point mass M at distance r:

$$V = -\frac{GM}{r}$$

The potential is a scalar quantity and is always negative (since work is done by the gravitational field when bringing the mass from infinity). The reference point is at infinity where V = 0.

Relation Between Field and Potential

$$E = -\frac{dV}{dr}$$

The gravitational field intensity is the negative gradient of the gravitational potential. This means the field points in the direction of decreasing potential, and equipotential surfaces are always perpendicular to the field lines.

3. Acceleration due to Gravity

Fig 5.3

At the Surface of the Earth

The acceleration due to gravity at the surface of the Earth is obtained by applying Newton’s law to an object at the surface:

$$g = \frac{GM}{R^2}$$

where M is the mass of the Earth and R is its radius. The standard value is approximately g = 9.8 m/s².

Variation with Height

At a height h above the Earth’s surface, the acceleration due to gravity decreases. The exact expression is g' = GM/(R + h)². For h « R, the approximate formula is:

$$g' = g\left(1 - \frac{2h}{R}\right) \quad (h \ll R)$$

Variation with Depth

At a depth d below the Earth’s surface (assuming uniform density), only the mass of the sphere of radius (R − d) contributes to gravity:

$$g' = g\left(1 - \frac{d}{R}\right)$$

At the centre of the Earth (d = R), the acceleration due to gravity becomes zero.

Variation with Latitude

Due to the Earth’s rotation, the effective acceleration due to gravity varies with latitude λ. The apparent weight is reduced at the equator compared to the poles:

$$g' = g - \omega^2 R \cos^2\lambda$$

At the poles (λ = 90°), g' = g (maximum). At the equator (λ = 0°), g' = g − ω²R (minimum). The Earth’s oblate shape also contributes to this variation since the poles are closer to the centre.

4. Gravitational Potential Energy

Fig 5.4

The gravitational potential energy of a system of two masses M and m separated by a distance r is defined as the work done in assembling the system by bringing the masses from infinity to their current positions:

$$U = -\frac{GMm}{r}$$

The reference point is at infinity where U = 0. The negative sign signifies that the system is bound — energy must be supplied to separate the masses to infinity. As the masses come closer, the potential energy decreases (becomes more negative), and this loss appears as kinetic energy.

Near the Earth’s surface, for small heights h compared to R, the familiar expression U = mgh is an approximation of the general formula.

5. Escape Velocity

Fig 5.5

Escape velocity is the minimum velocity that must be given to an object at the surface of a planet so that it can escape the gravitational pull of the planet and reach infinity with zero velocity. Using conservation of energy:

$$\frac{1}{2}m v_e^2 - \frac{GMm}{R} = 0$$ $$v_e = \sqrt{\frac{2GM}{R}} = \sqrt{2gR}$$

For Earth, ve ≈ 11.2 km/s. The escape velocity is independent of the mass of the escaping object and of the direction of projection. It depends only on the mass and radius of the planet (or equivalently, on g and R).

  • Escape velocity does not depend on the mass or direction of the projected body
  • For Earth: ve ≈ 11.2 km/s
  • If v < ve, the object will return back (bound trajectory)
  • If v = ve, the object just escapes (parabolic trajectory)
  • If v > ve, the object escapes with residual KE (hyperbolic trajectory)

6. Orbital Motion & Kepler’s Laws

Fig 5.6

Orbital Velocity

For a satellite in a circular orbit of radius r around the Earth, the gravitational force provides the centripetal acceleration. Equating these:

$$\frac{GMm}{r^2} = \frac{m v_o^2}{r}$$ $$v_o = \sqrt{\frac{GM}{r}} = \sqrt{\frac{gR^2}{r}}$$

For an orbit close to the Earth’s surface (r ≈ R), vo = √(gR) ≈ 7.9 km/s. Note that ve = √2 × vo.

Time Period of Orbit

$$T = \frac{2\pi r}{v_o} = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{r^3}{GM}}$$

Kepler’s Laws of Planetary Motion

  • First Law (Law of Orbits): All planets move in elliptical orbits with the Sun at one focus.
  • Second Law (Law of Areas): The line joining a planet and the Sun sweeps out equal areas in equal intervals of time (conservation of angular momentum).
  • Third Law (Law of Periods): The square of the orbital period is proportional to the cube of the semi-major axis of the orbit: T² ∝ r³.
$$T^2 = \frac{4\pi^2}{GM} r^3 \quad \Rightarrow \quad T^2 \propto r^3$$

7. Satellites

Fig 5.7

Geostationary Orbits

A geostationary satellite orbits the Earth in the equatorial plane with the same angular velocity as the Earth’s rotation. Its period is exactly 24 hours, and it appears stationary relative to the Earth’s surface. The orbital radius is approximately 42,164 km from the Earth’s centre (about 35,786 km above the surface).

Energy of a Satellite

For a satellite of mass m in a circular orbit of radius r around Earth (mass M):

  • Kinetic Energy: KE = GMm / 2r
  • Potential Energy: PE = −GMm / r
  • Total Energy: E = KE + PE = −GMm / 2r
$$E = -\frac{GMm}{2r}$$

The total energy is negative, indicating that the satellite is in a bound state. The magnitude of total energy equals the kinetic energy, and |PE| = 2 × KE.

Binding Energy

The binding energy of a satellite is the minimum energy required to free it from its orbit (i.e., take it to infinity with zero velocity). It equals the magnitude of the total energy:

$$\text{Binding Energy} = \frac{GMm}{2r}$$

Key Formulas Summary

$$F = \frac{GMm}{r^2} \quad | \quad g = \frac{GM}{R^2} \quad | \quad V = -\frac{GM}{r}$$ $$g' = g\left(1 - \frac{2h}{R}\right) \quad | \quad g' = g\left(1 - \frac{d}{R}\right)$$ $$v_e = \sqrt{2gR} \quad | \quad v_o = \sqrt{gR}$$ $$T^2 \propto r^3 \quad | \quad E = -\frac{GMm}{2r}$$

Solved Examples

Example 1 — Escape Velocity from Earth

Calculate the escape velocity from the surface of the Earth. Given: g = 9.8 m/s², R = 6.4 × 10³ km.

Solution:
Escape velocity: ve = √(2gR)

ve = √(2 × 9.8 × 6.4 × 10&sup6;)
ve = √(2 × 9.8 × 6,400,000)
ve = √(125,440,000)
ve = 11,200 m/s ≈ 11.2 km/s

Example 2 — Satellite at Height h = R

A satellite orbits the Earth at a height equal to the radius of the Earth (h = R). Find its orbital velocity and time period. Given: g = 9.8 m/s², R = 6400 km.

Solution:
Orbital radius: r = R + h = R + R = 2R

Orbital velocity: vo = √(GM/r) = √(gR²/2R) = √(gR/2)
vo = √(9.8 × 6.4 × 10&sup6; / 2)
vo = √(31,360,000) ≈ 5,600 m/s ≈ 5.6 km/s

Time period: T = 2πr / vo = 2π × 2 × 6.4 × 10&sup6; / 5600
T = 2π × 12,800,000 / 5600 ≈ 14,360 s ≈ 4.0 hours

Example 3 — Depth for 75% Surface Gravity

At what depth below the surface of the Earth is the acceleration due to gravity reduced to 75% of its value at the surface? Given: R = 6400 km.

Solution:
Using g' = g(1 − d/R), set g' = 0.75g:

0.75g = g(1 − d/R)
0.75 = 1 − d/R
d/R = 0.25
d = 0.25 × 6400 = 1600 km

The acceleration due to gravity is reduced to 75% of its surface value at a depth of 1600 km below the surface.
