Mechanical Properties of Solids

Chapter 06 — Grade XI — Properties of Matter

1. Elasticity & Plasticity

When an external force (called a deforming force) is applied to a body, the body undergoes a change in shape or size. The property by virtue of which a body tends to regain its original shape and size upon removal of the deforming force is called elasticity. The property by which a body does not regain its original shape and retains the deformed state permanently is called plasticity.

No material is perfectly elastic or perfectly plastic. Steel is close to being perfectly elastic (for small deformations), while putty and clay are nearly perfectly plastic. The behaviour of a material under deformation is best described by its stress-strain curve.

  • Elastic behaviour: Body returns to original shape after force is removed
  • Plastic behaviour: Body retains the deformed shape permanently
  • At the atomic level, elasticity arises from interatomic restoring forces when atoms are displaced from equilibrium positions

2. Stress & Strain

Stress

Stress is the restoring force per unit area developed inside a body when it is deformed. It is measured in Pascals (Pa) or N/m².

σ = F / A

Types of Stress

  • Tensile Stress: When the body is stretched along its length by equal and opposite forces applied normally to its cross-section.
  • Compressive Stress: When the body is compressed by equal and opposite forces applied normally, reducing its length.
  • Shear Stress (Tangential Stress): When equal and opposite forces are applied tangentially to the opposite faces, causing angular deformation.

Strain

Strain is the ratio of change in dimension to the original dimension. It is a dimensionless quantity.

Types of Strain

  • Longitudinal Strain: ε = ΔL / L (change in length per unit original length)
  • Volumetric Strain: εv = ΔV / V (change in volume per unit original volume)
  • Shear Strain: γ = Δx / L = tanθ ≈ θ (angular displacement, for small deformations)
ε = ΔL / L   |   εv = ΔV / V   |   γ = tanθ ≈ θ

3. Hooke’s Law

Hooke’s Law states that within the elastic limit, stress is directly proportional to strain. The constant of proportionality is called the modulus of elasticity (or elastic modulus).

Stress ∝ Strain   (within elastic limit)

Stress = E × Strain

where E is the modulus of elasticity. This linear relationship holds only up to the proportional limit. Beyond the elastic limit, the material undergoes permanent (plastic) deformation, and Hooke’s Law no longer applies.

The modulus of elasticity has the same dimensions as stress (since strain is dimensionless) and is measured in Pascals (Pa). A higher modulus means the material is stiffer and resists deformation more effectively.

4. Elastic Moduli

Young’s Modulus (Y)

Young’s modulus is the ratio of longitudinal (tensile or compressive) stress to longitudinal strain. It measures a material’s resistance to being stretched or compressed.

Y = σ / ε = (F/A) / (ΔL/L) = FL / AΔL

Steel has a very high Young’s modulus (~200 GPa), making it extremely rigid. Rubber has a very low Young’s modulus, making it highly deformable.

Bulk Modulus (B)

Bulk modulus is the ratio of volumetric (hydraulic) stress to volumetric strain. It measures a material’s resistance to uniform compression.

B = −V ΔP / ΔV = −ΔP / (ΔV/V)

The negative sign ensures that B is positive, since an increase in pressure (ΔP > 0) causes a decrease in volume (ΔV < 0). The reciprocal of bulk modulus is called compressibility: K = 1/B.

Shear Modulus / Modulus of Rigidity (G)

Shear modulus is the ratio of shear stress to shear strain. It measures a material’s resistance to angular deformation.

G = Shear Stress / Shear Strain = (F/A) / θ

Fluids have zero shear modulus since they cannot sustain shear stress at rest. For most solids, G is typically about one-third to one-half of Y.

5. Poisson’s Ratio

When a rod is stretched along its length, it contracts in the lateral (perpendicular) direction. Poisson’s ratio is defined as the ratio of lateral strain to longitudinal strain, with a negative sign to make it positive for normal materials:

σP = −(Lateral Strain) / (Longitudinal Strain) = −(Δd/d) / (ΔL/L)

The theoretical range for Poisson’s ratio is −1 to 0.5, but for most real materials it lies between 0 and 0.5:

  • σP = 0: Material does not contract laterally when stretched (e.g., cork, approximately)
  • σP = 0.5: Material is perfectly incompressible (volume remains constant during deformation)
  • Most metals: σP ≈ 0.25 to 0.35
  • Rubber: σP ≈ 0.5 (nearly incompressible)

Relation Between Elastic Constants

Y = 2G(1 + σP)   |   Y = 3B(1 − 2σP)

6. Elastic Potential Energy

When a body is deformed within the elastic limit, work is done by the deforming force and this work is stored as elastic potential energy in the body. The energy is recovered when the deforming force is removed.

The work done per unit volume in deforming the body equals the area under the stress-strain curve. For a linear (Hookean) material:

Energy stored per unit volume (u) = ½ × Stress × Strain = ½ × σ × ε

Total Energy: U = ½ × σ × ε × V

Using Hooke’s Law (σ = Yε), this can be rewritten as:

U = ½ × Y × ε² × V   =   σ²V / (2Y)

This energy density concept is critical in engineering design — it determines how much energy a material can absorb before yielding (resilience) or fracturing (toughness).

7. Stress-Strain Curve

The stress-strain curve is the most informative representation of a material’s mechanical behaviour. For a typical ductile material (like mild steel), the curve exhibits several important regions:

  • Proportional Limit (A): Up to this point, stress is directly proportional to strain (Hooke’s Law holds exactly). The curve is a straight line.
  • Elastic Limit (B): The maximum stress up to which the material returns to its original shape upon unloading. Beyond this, permanent deformation sets in.
  • Yield Point (C): The point where the material begins to deform plastically with little or no increase in stress. Many materials exhibit an upper yield point and a lower yield point.
  • Ultimate Tensile Strength (D): The maximum stress the material can withstand. Beyond this, necking (localised constriction) begins.
  • Fracture Point (E): The point at which the material breaks. The stress at this point is called the breaking stress or fracture stress.

Ductile vs. Brittle Materials

  • Ductile materials (e.g., copper, steel, aluminium): Show a large plastic deformation region before fracture. They can be drawn into wires. The stress-strain curve has a long plateau after the yield point.
  • Brittle materials (e.g., glass, cast iron, ceramics): Fracture soon after the elastic limit with negligible plastic deformation. Their stress-strain curve shows little or no yielding region.

The area under the entire stress-strain curve up to fracture represents the toughness of the material (total energy absorbed per unit volume before breaking). The area under the curve up to the elastic limit represents the resilience (energy recovered upon unloading).

Key Formulas Summary

σ = F/A   |   ε = ΔL/L   |   Stress = E × Strain
Y = σ/ε = FL/AΔL   |   B = −VΔP/ΔV   |   G = (F/A)/θ
U = ½ × σ × ε × V = ½ × Y × ε² × V
Y = 2G(1 + σP)   |   Y = 3B(1 − 2σP)

Solved Examples

Example 1

A steel wire of length 2 m and cross-sectional area 1 mm² is stretched by a force of 400 N. Find the elongation produced if Young’s modulus of steel is Y = 2 × 1011 Pa.

Solution:
Given: L = 2 m, A = 1 mm² = 1 × 10−6 m², F = 400 N, Y = 2 × 1011 Pa

Using Y = FL / AΔL:
ΔL = FL / (AY)
ΔL = (400 × 2) / (1 × 10−6 × 2 × 1011)
ΔL = 800 / (2 × 105)
ΔL = 4 × 10−3 m = 4 mm

Example 2

A copper cube of side 10 cm is subjected to a uniform pressure of 7 × 106 Pa. Find the change in volume if the bulk modulus of copper is B = 140 × 109 Pa.

Solution:
Given: Side = 10 cm = 0.1 m, so V = (0.1)³ = 10−3 m³
ΔP = 7 × 106 Pa, B = 140 × 109 Pa

Using B = −VΔP / ΔV:
ΔV = −VΔP / B
ΔV = −(10−3 × 7 × 106) / (140 × 109)
ΔV = −7 × 103 / (140 × 109)
ΔV = −5 × 10−8 m³ = −0.05 mm³

The volume decreases by 5 × 10−8 m³ (the negative sign indicates compression).
