06 — Mechanical Properties of Solids

Elasticity Stress & Strain Hooke's Law Elastic Moduli Poisson's Ratio Elastic PE Applications Torsion Thermal Expansion Thermal Applications Stress-Strain Curve

1. Elasticity & Plasticity

Fig 6.1

When an external force (called a deforming force) is applied to a body, the body undergoes a change in shape or size. The property by virtue of which a body tends to regain its original shape and size upon removal of the deforming force is called elasticity. The property by which a body does not regain its original shape and retains the deformed state permanently is called plasticity.

No material is perfectly elastic or perfectly plastic. Steel is close to being perfectly elastic (for small deformations), while putty and clay are nearly perfectly plastic. The behaviour of a material under deformation is best described by its stress-strain curve.

  • Elastic behaviour: Body returns to original shape after force is removed
  • Plastic behaviour: Body retains the deformed shape permanently
  • At the atomic level, elasticity arises from interatomic restoring forces when atoms are displaced from equilibrium positions

2. Stress & Strain

Fig 6.2

Stress

Stress is the restoring force per unit area developed inside a body when it is deformed. It is measured in Pascals (Pa) or N/m².

$$\sigma = \frac{F}{A}$$

Types of Stress

  • Tensile Stress: When the body is stretched along its length by equal and opposite forces applied normally to its cross-section.
  • Compressive Stress: When the body is compressed by equal and opposite forces applied normally, reducing its length.
  • Shear Stress (Tangential Stress): When equal and opposite forces are applied tangentially to the opposite faces, causing angular deformation.

Strain

Strain is the ratio of change in dimension to the original dimension. It is a dimensionless quantity.

Types of Strain

  • Longitudinal Strain: ε = ΔL / L (change in length per unit original length)
  • Volumetric Strain: εv = ΔV / V (change in volume per unit original volume)
  • Shear Strain: γ = Δx / L = tanθ ≈ θ (angular displacement, for small deformations)
$$\epsilon = \frac{\Delta L}{L} \quad | \quad \epsilon_v = \frac{\Delta V}{V} \quad | \quad \gamma = \tan\theta \approx \theta$$

3. Hooke’s Law

Fig 6.3

Hooke’s Law states that within the elastic limit, stress is directly proportional to strain. The constant of proportionality is called the modulus of elasticity (or elastic modulus).

$$\text{Stress} \propto \text{Strain} \quad \text{(within elastic limit)}$$ $$\text{Stress} = E \times \text{Strain}$$

where E is the modulus of elasticity. This linear relationship holds only up to the proportional limit. Beyond the elastic limit, the material undergoes permanent (plastic) deformation, and Hooke’s Law no longer applies.

The modulus of elasticity has the same dimensions as stress (since strain is dimensionless) and is measured in Pascals (Pa). A higher modulus means the material is stiffer and resists deformation more effectively.

4. Elastic Moduli

Fig 6.4

Young’s Modulus (Y)

Young’s modulus is the ratio of longitudinal (tensile or compressive) stress to longitudinal strain. It measures a material’s resistance to being stretched or compressed.

$$Y = \frac{\sigma}{\epsilon} = \frac{F/A}{\Delta L/L} = \frac{FL}{A\Delta L}$$

Steel has a very high Young’s modulus (~200 GPa), making it extremely rigid. Rubber has a very low Young’s modulus, making it highly deformable.

Bulk Modulus (B)

Bulk modulus is the ratio of volumetric (hydraulic) stress to volumetric strain. It measures a material’s resistance to uniform compression.

$$B = -\frac{V \Delta P}{\Delta V} = -\frac{\Delta P}{\Delta V/V}$$

The negative sign ensures that B is positive, since an increase in pressure (ΔP > 0) causes a decrease in volume (ΔV < 0). The reciprocal of bulk modulus is called compressibility: K = 1/B.

Shear Modulus / Modulus of Rigidity (G)

Shear modulus is the ratio of shear stress to shear strain. It measures a material’s resistance to angular deformation.

$$G = \frac{\text{Shear Stress}}{\text{Shear Strain}} = \frac{F/A}{\theta}$$

Fluids have zero shear modulus since they cannot sustain shear stress at rest. For most solids, G is typically about one-third to one-half of Y.

5. Poisson’s Ratio

Fig 6.5

When a rod is stretched along its length, it contracts in the lateral (perpendicular) direction. Poisson’s ratio is defined as the ratio of lateral strain to longitudinal strain, with a negative sign to make it positive for normal materials:

$$\sigma_P = -\frac{\text{Lateral Strain}}{\text{Longitudinal Strain}} = -\frac{\Delta d/d}{\Delta L/L}$$

The theoretical range for Poisson’s ratio is −1 to 0.5, but for most real materials it lies between 0 and 0.5:

  • σP = 0: Material does not contract laterally when stretched (e.g., cork, approximately)
  • σP = 0.5: Material is perfectly incompressible (volume remains constant during deformation)
  • Most metals: σP ≈ 0.25 to 0.35
  • Rubber: σP ≈ 0.5 (nearly incompressible)

Relation Between Elastic Constants

$$Y = 2G(1 + \sigma_P) \quad | \quad Y = 3B(1 - 2\sigma_P)$$

6. Elastic Potential Energy

Fig 6.6

When a body is deformed within the elastic limit, work is done by the deforming force and this work is stored as elastic potential energy in the body. The energy is recovered when the deforming force is removed.

The work done per unit volume in deforming the body equals the area under the stress-strain curve. For a linear (Hookean) material:

$$u = \frac{1}{2} \times \text{Stress} \times \text{Strain} = \frac{1}{2} \sigma \epsilon$$ $$U = \frac{1}{2} \sigma \epsilon V$$

Using Hooke’s Law (σ = Yε), this can be rewritten as:

$$U = \frac{1}{2} Y \epsilon^2 V = \frac{\sigma^2 V}{2Y}$$

This energy density concept is critical in engineering design — it determines how much energy a material can absorb before yielding (resilience) or fracturing (toughness).

7. Applications of Elasticity

Fig 6.7

Elongation of a Wire Under Its Own Weight

For a uniform wire or rod of mass M, length l, cross-sectional area A, and Young’s modulus Y hanging vertically, the stress varies linearly from Mg/A at the support to zero at the free end. This is equivalent to a concentrated load of M/2 acting at the center.

$$\Delta l = \frac{Mgl}{2AY} = \frac{\rho g l^2}{2Y}$$

where ρ is the density of the material. The elongation is proportional to the square of the length.

Thermal Stress in Constrained Rods

When a rod is clamped between two rigid supports and the temperature changes by ΔT, the rod tries to expand or contract but cannot. A thermal stress is developed that opposes the thermal deformation.

$$\sigma_{\text{thermal}} = Y \times \alpha \times \Delta T$$ $$F_{\text{thermal}} = Y \times A \times \alpha \times \Delta T$$

If temperature increases, the stress is compressive (the material tries to expand but is prevented). If temperature decreases, the stress is tensile (the material tries to contract but is prevented).

Wire Supporting a Load at Angle

When a wire of length 2l and radius r is stretched taut between two fixed points and a mass m is hung from the midpoint, producing a vertical depression d:

$$d = l \times \left(\frac{mg}{\pi r^2 Y}\right)^{1/3}$$

Natural Length from Two Tension Measurements

If a wire has length l1 at tension T1 and length l2 at tension T2, the natural (unstretched) length can be determined:

$$l_0 = \frac{l_1 T_2 - l_2 T_1}{T_2 - T_1}$$

Two Wires Supporting a Rod

When a rod is supported by two wires of different materials:

  • Equal stress condition: σ1 = σ2, giving F1/A1 = F2/A2
  • Equal strain condition: ε1 = ε2, giving F1/(A1Y1) = F2/(A2Y2)

8. Torsion of a Cylinder

Fig 6.8

When a cylinder (or wire) is fixed at one end and twisted at the other end by an angle θ, it exerts a restoring torque opposing the twist. This restoring torque is directly proportional to the angle of twist and is analogous to the restoring force in a spring.

Restoring Torque

$$\tau = \frac{\pi \eta r^4}{2l} \times \theta = C \times \theta$$

where:

  • η = modulus of rigidity (shear modulus)
  • r = radius of the cylinder
  • l = length of the cylinder
  • θ = angle of twist (in radians)
  • C = (π η r&sup4;) / (2l) is the torsional rigidity

Hollow vs. Solid Shaft

A hollow shaft is stronger than a solid shaft of the same mass and material. For a hollow shaft with outer radius r2 and inner radius r1:

$$\tau = \frac{\pi \eta (r_2^4 - r_1^4)}{2l} \times \theta$$

For equal volume of material, the hollow shaft requires more torque to produce the same twist, making it more resistant to torsional deformation and more efficient for engineering applications.

Worked Example: Cylinder Twist

Example: A cylinder of length 1.5 m and diameter 4 cm is fixed at one end. A tangential force of 4 × 10&sup5; N is applied at the other end (perpendicular to a radius). Calculate the twist if the modulus of rigidity is 6 × 10¹0; N/m².

Solution:
Applied torque τ = F × r = 4 × 10&sup5; × 0.02 = 8 × 10³ N·m

Equating to restoring torque:
θ = (2l F) / (π η r³)
θ = (2 × 1.5 × 4 × 10&sup5;) / (π × 6 × 10¹0; × (0.02)³)
θ = (1.2 × 10&sup6;) / (1.508 × 10&sup6;) ≈ 0.796 radians (≈ 45.6°)

9. Thermal Expansion

Fig 6.9

When a solid is heated, its length, surface area, and volume increase due to increased oscillation of atoms about their equilibrium positions. Three coefficients describe this expansion: α (linear), β (superficial/areal), and γ (volumetric/cubical).

Linear Expansion

$$l_2 = l_1(1 + \alpha \Delta T)$$

where α is the coefficient of linear expansion and ΔT = T2 − T1.

Superficial (Areal) Expansion

$$A_2 = A_1(1 + \beta \Delta T)$$

where β is the coefficient of superficial expansion.

Volume (Cubical) Expansion

$$V_2 = V_1(1 + \gamma \Delta T)$$

where γ is the coefficient of cubical (volumetric) expansion.

Relation Between Coefficients

$$\beta = 2\alpha \quad | \quad \gamma = 3\alpha \quad | \quad 6\alpha = 3\beta = 2\gamma$$

These relations hold for isotropic materials where expansion is uniform in all directions.

Density Change with Temperature

As temperature increases, volume increases but mass remains constant, so density decreases:

$$d_2 = \frac{d_1}{1 + \gamma \Delta T}$$

For small values of γ ΔT: d2 ≈ d1 (1 − γ ΔT)

Anomalous Expansion of Water

Water exhibits anomalous expansion: it contracts from 0°C to 4°C and expands above 4°C. The maximum density of water occurs at 4°C. This unusual behavior has important ecological consequences, allowing aquatic life to survive under ice in winter.

Coefficients Are Small But Important

Typical values: α ∼ 10−5 to 10−6 / °C for solids. Though small, these coefficients have significant engineering implications, especially in precision instruments, structural design, and thermometer calibration.

10. Thermal Expansion Applications

Fig 6.10

Thermal expansion creates practical challenges and opportunities in engineering and everyday life. Understanding these applications is essential for proper design and troubleshooting.

Heated Metallic Scale Calibration

When a metallic scale is heated, all markings expand proportionally. Each division becomes larger than the true unit. This must be corrected when taking measurements with a heated scale:

$$\text{Actual length} = \text{Reading} \times (1 + \alpha \Delta T)$$

A heated scale underestimates true length (reads less), while a cooled scale overestimates (reads more).

Constant Length Difference Between Two Rods

For the difference in lengths of two rods to remain constant at all temperatures, their expansions must compensate each other:

$$l_1 \times \alpha_1 = l_2 \times \alpha_2$$

This principle is used in designing composite structures where different materials must maintain precise dimensional relationships.

Pendulum Clock Temperature Effects

The time period of a simple pendulum is T = 2π√(l/g). When temperature rises by ΔT, the pendulum length increases, so the time period increases and the clock runs slow (loses time):

$$\Delta t = \frac{1}{2} \times \alpha \times \Delta T \times 86400 \text{ seconds (per day)}$$

If temperature rises: clock loses time (runs slow)
If temperature falls: clock gains time (runs fast)
This is why precision pendulum clocks require temperature compensation.

Bimetallic Strip Thermostats

A bimetallic strip consists of two strips of different metals (different α values) bonded together. On heating, the strip bends toward the metal with the smaller α (since the other metal expands more and forms the outer arc of the bend). This property is used in:

  • Automatic thermostats that turn heating/cooling on and off
  • Temperature-sensing devices
  • Thermal circuit breakers

Expansion of Liquids

Liquids expand only in volume (no linear or areal expansion). Two types of expansion coefficients are defined:

$$\gamma_{\text{apparent}} = \gamma_{\text{liquid}} - \gamma_{\text{vessel}}$$ $$\gamma_{\text{real}} = \gamma_{\text{apparent}} + \gamma_{\text{vessel}}$$

The apparent expansion is what we observe (the liquid level rise in the container), while the real expansion is the actual expansion of the liquid itself. The vessel expands too, reducing the observed volume change.

Liquid Overflow from Heated Container

When a container full of liquid is heated by ΔT, some liquid will overflow if the container cannot expand enough to accommodate it:

$$\text{Volume overflow} = V \times (\gamma_{\text{liquid}} - \gamma_{\text{vessel}}) \times \Delta T$$

Worked Example: Mercury Overflow from Glass Flask

Example: A glass flask of volume 1000 cm³ at 0°C is filled completely with mercury. When heated to 100°C, 15.2 cm³ of mercury overflows. If γHg = 1.82 × 10−4/°C, find the coefficient of linear expansion of glass.

Solution:
Mercury overflow = V (γHg − γglass) ΔT
15.2 = 1000 (1.82 × 10−4 − γglass) × 100

15.2 / (1000 × 100) = 1.82 × 10−4 − γglass
1.52 × 10−4 = 1.82 × 10−4 − γglass
γglass = 3.0 × 10−5 / °C

Since γ = 3α:
αglass = γ / 3 = 1.0 × 10−5 / °C

11. Stress-Strain Curve

Fig 6.11

The stress-strain curve is the most informative representation of a material’s mechanical behaviour. For a typical ductile material (like mild steel), the curve exhibits several important regions:

  • Proportional Limit (A): Up to this point, stress is directly proportional to strain (Hooke’s Law holds exactly). The curve is a straight line.
  • Elastic Limit (B): The maximum stress up to which the material returns to its original shape upon unloading. Beyond this, permanent deformation sets in.
  • Yield Point (C): The point where the material begins to deform plastically with little or no increase in stress. Many materials exhibit an upper yield point and a lower yield point.
  • Ultimate Tensile Strength (D): The maximum stress the material can withstand. Beyond this, necking (localised constriction) begins.
  • Fracture Point (E): The point at which the material breaks. The stress at this point is called the breaking stress or fracture stress.

Ductile vs. Brittle Materials

  • Ductile materials (e.g., copper, steel, aluminium): Show a large plastic deformation region before fracture. They can be drawn into wires. The stress-strain curve has a long plateau after the yield point.
  • Brittle materials (e.g., glass, cast iron, ceramics): Fracture soon after the elastic limit with negligible plastic deformation. Their stress-strain curve shows little or no yielding region.

The area under the entire stress-strain curve up to fracture represents the toughness of the material (total energy absorbed per unit volume before breaking). The area under the curve up to the elastic limit represents the resilience (energy recovered upon unloading).

Example 2

A copper cube of side 10 cm is subjected to a uniform pressure of 7 × 106 Pa. Find the change in volume if the bulk modulus of copper is B = 140 × 109 Pa.

Solution:
Given: Side = 10 cm = 0.1 m, so V = (0.1)³ = 10−3 m³
ΔP = 7 × 106 Pa, B = 140 × 109 Pa

Using B = −VΔP / ΔV:
ΔV = −VΔP / B
ΔV = −(10−3 × 7 × 106) / (140 × 109)
ΔV = −7 × 103 / (140 × 109)
ΔV = −5 × 10−8 m³ = −0.05 mm³

The volume decreases by 5 × 10−8 m³ (the negative sign indicates compression).

Example 3

An iron rod (Y = 2 × 1011 Pa, α = 12 × 10−6 / °C) of length 1 m and cross-sectional area 1 cm² is heated from 20°C to 120°C while being held rigid. Calculate (i) the thermal stress, and (ii) the compressive force.

Solution:
Given: Y = 2 × 1011 Pa, α = 12 × 10−6 / °C, ΔT = 100°C, A = 1 cm² = 10−4 m²

(i) Thermal stress:
σ = Y × α × ΔT
σ = 2 × 1011 × 12 × 10−6 × 100
σ = 2.4 × 108 Pa

(ii) Compressive force:
F = σ × A = 2.4 × 108 × 10−4
F = 2.4 × 104 N = 24 kN

Example 4

A brass pendulum clock has a time period of 2 seconds at 20°C. The coefficient of linear expansion for brass is α = 18 × 10−6 / °C. How much time does the clock lose in one day if the temperature increases to 40°C?

Solution:
Given: T = 2 s at 20°C, α = 18 × 10−6 / °C, ΔT = 20°C

Time lost per day:
Δt = ½ × α × ΔT × 86400
Δt = ½ × 18 × 10−6 × 20 × 86400
Δt = ½ × 18 × 10−6 × 1.728 × 106
Δt = 15.55 seconds

The clock loses approximately 15.55 seconds per day (runs slow).
