1. Cathode Rays and the e/m Ratio

Fig 19.1

Discovery of Cathode Rays

Cathode rays are beams of fast-moving electrons produced when a high voltage is applied between two electrodes sealed inside a low-pressure glass tube. They were first studied systematically by Sir William Crookes using his famous Crookes tube. The rays always travel from cathode (negative electrode) to anode (positive electrode), regardless of the position of the anode or the material of the electrodes.

  • Cathode rays travel in straight lines and carry negative charge.
  • They are deflected by both electric and magnetic fields.
  • They produce heat, cause fluorescence, and carry mechanical energy.
  • The specific charge (e/m) is identical for all gases — establishing the electron as a universal particle.

Thomson's Determination of e/m

J.J. Thomson (1897) determined the charge-to-mass ratio of the electron by applying perpendicular electric and magnetic fields to the cathode ray beam inside a deflection tube. When the fields are adjusted so that the beam passes straight through (no deflection), the electric force exactly balances the magnetic force:

$$eE = evB \quad \Rightarrow \quad v = \frac{E}{B}$$ $$evB = \frac{mv^2}{r} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \frac{e}{m} = \frac{v}{Br} = \frac{E}{B^2r}$$ $$\frac{e}{m} = \frac{E^2}{2VB^2}$$ $$\frac{e}{m} = 1.758 \times 10^{11} \text{ C/kg}$$

Properties of Cathode Rays (Summary)

  • They are material particles with mass and charge (electrons).
  • They ionize gases through which they pass.
  • They produce X-rays when they strike a heavy metal target.
  • Charge is always negative; e/m is the same regardless of cathode material or residual gas.

2. Millikan Oil Drop Experiment

Fig 19.2

Objective and Setup

Robert Millikan's oil drop experiment (1909–1913) measured the elementary charge on an electron with great precision. Tiny oil droplets are sprayed between two horizontal parallel plates. As they fall through the air, they pick up charge by friction or ionizing radiation. By applying a voltage across the plates, the electric force on a charged droplet can be made to balance gravity.

Analysis of Forces

For a droplet of radius r and density ρ falling with terminal velocity vg under gravity alone (field off), the viscous drag equals the net downward force:

$$6\pi\eta rv_g = \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3(\rho - \rho_{\text{air}})g$$ $$qE = 6\pi\eta r(v_e + v_g)$$ $$q = \frac{6\pi\eta r(v_e + v_g)}{E}$$

Quantization of Charge

Millikan found that the charge on any oil drop is always an integral multiple of a minimum value, establishing that charge is quantized:

$$q = ne \quad (n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots)$$ $$e = 1.602 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}$$

This was direct proof that electric charge is not continuous but comes in discrete packets of size e.

3. Photoelectric Effect

Fig 19.3

The Phenomenon

The photoelectric effect is the emission of electrons from a metal surface when light of sufficiently high frequency falls on it. The emitted electrons are called photoelectrons. This phenomenon was first observed by Heinrich Hertz in 1887 and was explained by Albert Einstein in 1905, for which he received the Nobel Prize.

Experimental Observations

  • For a given metal, there exists a minimum frequency of light below which no photoelectrons are emitted, regardless of the intensity of light. This is called the threshold frequency (f0).
  • The maximum kinetic energy of the emitted photoelectrons depends on the frequency of incident light, not on its intensity.
  • Increasing the intensity of light (above the threshold frequency) increases the number of photoelectrons (photocurrent) but not their maximum kinetic energy.
  • The photoelectric emission is instantaneous — there is no observable time lag (less than 10−9 s).
  • Photoelectric current is directly proportional to light intensity for a fixed frequency.
  • Stopping potential is independent of light intensity for the same frequency.

Einstein's Photoelectric Equation

Einstein explained the photoelectric effect using the quantum (photon) theory of light. Each photon has energy E = hf, where h is Planck's constant. When a photon strikes a metal surface, it can transfer all its energy to a single electron. Part of this energy is used to overcome the binding energy of the electron (called the work function, φ), and the rest appears as kinetic energy of the emitted electron.

$$KE_{\max} = hf - \phi$$ $$h = 6.626 \times 10^{-34} \text{ JÂ·s}$$ $$KE_{\max} = h(f - f_0)$$ $$f_0 = \frac{\phi}{h}$$ $$\lambda_{\max} = \frac{hc}{\phi}$$ $$E \text{ (eV)} = \frac{12400}{\lambda \text{ (Ã…)}}$$

Stopping Potential

The stopping potential (V0) is the minimum negative potential applied to the collector plate that just stops the most energetic photoelectrons.

$$eV_0 = KE_{\max} = hf - \phi$$ $$V_0 = \frac{h}{e}f - \frac{\phi}{e}$$

Radiation Pressure

$$P = \frac{I}{c} \quad \text{(absorption)}$$ $$P = \frac{2I}{c} \quad \text{(reflection)}$$

Important Graphs

V0 vs f graph: A straight line with slope h/e and x-intercept at f0 (threshold frequency). The y-intercept is −φ/e. For different metals, the lines are parallel (same slope h/e) but have different intercepts (different work functions).

Photocurrent (I) vs voltage (V) graph: At zero voltage, there is some photocurrent. As the collector voltage increases (positive), the current increases and saturates at the saturation current (all emitted electrons reach the collector). As the voltage becomes negative (retarding potential), the current decreases and becomes zero at V = −V0. Increasing intensity increases the saturation current but does not change V0.

Example 1 — Stopping potential from wavelength

Light of frequency 6 × 1014 Hz is incident on a metal surface whose work function is 2.0 eV. Find the maximum kinetic energy and the stopping potential of the emitted photoelectrons. (h = 6.63 × 10−34 J·s, 1 eV = 1.6 × 10−19 J)

Solution:
Energy of incident photon:
E = hf = 6.63 × 10−34 × 6 × 1014 = 3.978 × 10−19 J
E = 3.978 × 10−19 / 1.6 × 10−19 = 2.486 eV ≈ 2.49 eV

Maximum kinetic energy:
KEmax = hf − φ = 2.49 − 2.0 = 0.49 eV

Stopping potential:
eV0 = KEmax
V0 = KEmax/e = 0.49 eV / e = 0.49 V

4. Matter Waves and de Broglie Wavelength

Fig 19.4

de Broglie Hypothesis

In 1924, Louis de Broglie proposed that just as light (a wave) exhibits particle behaviour (photons), all material particles must also have a wave nature. The wavelength of this matter wave is inversely proportional to the momentum of the particle:

$$\lambda = \frac{h}{p} = \frac{h}{mv} = \frac{h}{\sqrt{2mKE}}$$ $$\lambda_e = \frac{12.27}{\sqrt{V}} \text{ Ã…}$$ $$\lambda_p = \frac{0.286}{\sqrt{V}} \text{ Ã…}$$ $$\lambda = \frac{h}{\sqrt{3mkT}}$$

Key Features of Matter Waves

  • Matter waves are not electromagnetic waves; they are quantum mechanical probability waves.
  • The de Broglie wavelength is significant only for microscopic particles. For macroscopic bodies, λ is negligibly small and wave behaviour is unobservable.
  • Heavier and faster particles have shorter wavelengths.
  • The wavelength of a particle at rest is infinite (zero momentum ⇒ no defined wave).
  • Matter waves explain the quantization of electron orbits in Bohr's model — only orbits where standing waves fit an integer number of wavelengths are allowed: 2πr = nλ.
Example 2 — de Broglie wavelength of accelerated electron

Find the de Broglie wavelength of an electron accelerated through a potential difference of 100 V.

Solution:
Using the formula for an electron:
λ = 12.27 / √V = 12.27 / √100 = 12.27 / 10 = 1.227 Å

This wavelength is of the order of the spacing between atoms in a crystal lattice, which is why electron diffraction can probe crystal structures.

5. Wave-Particle Duality

Fig 19.5

Dual Nature of Radiation and Matter

Nature exhibits a fundamental duality: light behaves as a wave in phenomena like interference, diffraction and polarisation, yet it behaves as a stream of particles (photons) in the photoelectric effect and Compton scattering. De Broglie extended this idea — all matter also has a wave nature, making duality a universal principle of quantum physics.

Davisson-Germer Experiment

In 1927, Clinton Davisson and Lester Germer experimentally confirmed de Broglie's hypothesis by demonstrating the diffraction of electrons. They directed a beam of electrons at a nickel crystal and observed that the reflected electrons showed an intensity pattern consistent with diffraction — a phenomenon exclusive to waves. The measured wavelength matched the de Broglie prediction.

  • Electrons accelerated through 54 V produced a diffraction peak at 50°.
  • The measured wavelength was 1.65 Å, closely matching the de Broglie prediction of λ = 12.27/√54 ≈ 1.67 Å.
  • This experiment proved that particles like electrons exhibit wave behaviour, establishing wave-particle duality as a fundamental principle of quantum mechanics.

Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle

A consequence of wave-particle duality is that it is impossible to simultaneously know both the exact position and exact momentum of a particle:

$$\Delta x \cdot \Delta p \geq \frac{h}{4\pi}$$ $$\Delta E \cdot \Delta t \geq \frac{h}{4\pi}$$

This is not a limitation of measurement technology but a fundamental feature of quantum reality.

Key Points about Matter Waves

  • Matter waves are not electromagnetic waves. They are probability waves (quantum mechanical waves).
  • The de Broglie wavelength is significant only for microscopic particles (electrons, protons, etc.). For macroscopic objects, λ is negligibly small.
  • Heavier and faster particles have shorter de Broglie wavelengths.
  • The wave nature of electrons is used in electron microscopes, which have much higher resolution than optical microscopes.

6. Bohr's Atomic Model

Fig 19.6

Postulates

Niels Bohr proposed his model of the hydrogen atom in 1913 to explain the discrete line spectrum of hydrogen. The key postulates are:

  • Quantized orbits: Electrons revolve around the nucleus in certain definite circular orbits called stationary orbits (or allowed orbits) without radiating energy.
  • Angular momentum quantization: The angular momentum of the electron in a stationary orbit is an integral multiple of h/(2π): L = mvr = nh/(2π), where n = 1, 2, 3, … is the principal quantum number.
  • Energy transitions: An electron can jump from one orbit to another. When it jumps from a higher energy orbit (n2) to a lower energy orbit (n1), it emits a photon of energy hf = En2 − En1. When it absorbs a photon of the right energy, it jumps to a higher orbit.

Results for Hydrogen-like Atoms

For a hydrogen-like atom with atomic number Z (single electron orbiting a nucleus of charge Ze):

$$r_n = \frac{n^2 a_0}{Z}, \quad a_0 = 0.529 \text{ Ã…}$$ $$v_n = \frac{v_1 Z}{n}, \quad v_1 = 2.18 \times 10^6 \text{ m/s}$$ $$E_n = -\frac{13.6 Z^2}{n^2} \text{ eV}$$ $$KE_n = \frac{13.6 Z^2}{n^2} \text{ eV}$$ $$PE_n = -\frac{27.2 Z^2}{n^2} \text{ eV}$$ $$BE = \frac{13.6 Z^2}{n^2} \text{ eV}$$ $$IE = 13.6 Z^2 \text{ eV}$$

Spectral Series of Hydrogen

When an electron transitions from orbit n2 to n1, it emits a photon with wavelength given by the Rydberg formula:

$$\frac{1}{\lambda} = RZ^2\left[\frac{1}{n_1^2} - \frac{1}{n_2^2}\right]$$ $$R = 1.097 \times 10^7 \text{ m}^{-1}$$

The different series are named after their discoverers:

  • Lyman series: n1 = 1, n2 = 2, 3, 4, … (ultraviolet region)
  • Balmer series: n1 = 2, n2 = 3, 4, 5, … (visible region, partially UV)
  • Paschen series: n1 = 3, n2 = 4, 5, 6, … (infrared region)
  • Brackett series: n1 = 4, n2 = 5, 6, 7, … (infrared region)
  • Pfund series: n1 = 5, n2 = 6, 7, 8, … (far infrared region)

The number of spectral lines emitted when an electron falls from level n to the ground state is n(n−1)/2.

Example 3 — Balmer series wavelength

Calculate the wavelength of the first line of the Balmer series of hydrogen. (R = 1.097 × 107 m−1)

Solution:
For the Balmer series: n1 = 2. The first line corresponds to n2 = 3.

1/λ = R [1/n1² − 1/n2²] = 1.097 × 107 [1/4 − 1/9]
1/λ = 1.097 × 107 × (9 − 4)/36
1/λ = 1.097 × 107 × 5/36
1/λ = 1.524 × 106 m−1

λ = 1 / (1.524 × 106) = 6.56 × 10−7 m = 656 nm

This is the H-alpha line, which lies in the red part of the visible spectrum.

7. Atomic Line Spectrum and Energy Levels

Fig 19.7

Origin of Discrete Spectra

Each element produces a unique set of discrete spectral lines — its atomic fingerprint. These lines arise because electrons can only exist in specific quantized energy levels. An emission spectrum results from electrons transitioning to lower energy levels and releasing photons. An absorption spectrum results from electrons absorbing specific photon energies to jump to higher levels.

Spectral Series Limits

Each series has a series limit corresponding to the transition from n = ∞ to the lower level n1. At the series limit, the electron is just barely bound (from infinity), giving the maximum frequency (minimum wavelength) of the series.

$$\Delta E = 13.6 Z^2\left(\frac{1}{n_1^2} - \frac{1}{n_2^2}\right) \text{ eV}$$ $$\lambda = \frac{12400}{\Delta E \text{ (eV)}} \text{ Ã…}$$ $$\lambda_{\text{limit}} = \frac{n_1^2}{RZ^2}$$ $$N = \frac{(n_i - n_f)(n_i + n_f - 1)}{2}$$ $$N = \frac{n(n - 1)}{2}$$

Spectral Series Regions

  • Lyman series (n1 = 1): UV region. λ range: 91.2 nm to 121.6 nm.
  • Balmer series (n1 = 2): Visible/UV region. λ range: 365 nm to 656 nm.
  • Paschen series (n1 = 3): Near-infrared. λ range: 820 nm to 1875 nm.
  • Brackett series (n1 = 4): Infrared. λ range: 1458 nm to 4051 nm.
  • Pfund series (n1 = 5): Far-infrared.

8. X-Rays

Fig 19.8

Production of X-Rays

X-rays are produced when high-energy electrons (accelerated through a high voltage V, typically 30–100 kV) strike a heavy metal target (such as tungsten, molybdenum, or copper) in a Coolidge tube. The Coolidge tube consists of:

  • A heated filament (cathode) that emits electrons by thermionic emission.
  • A high potential difference between cathode and anode to accelerate the electrons.
  • A heavy metal target (anode) that the electrons strike.
  • A vacuum enclosure to prevent electron scattering by air molecules.

Only 1–10% of the electron kinetic energy is converted to X-rays; the rest becomes heat. X-rays are classified as hard X-rays (0.1–1 Å, high energy, high penetration) and soft X-rays (1–100 Å, lower energy, lower penetration).

Continuous X-Ray Spectrum (Bremsstrahlung)

When a fast-moving electron is decelerated by the electric field of a target nucleus, it loses kinetic energy, which is emitted as X-ray photons. This produces a continuous spectrum of X-rays with all wavelengths above a certain minimum value. The minimum wavelength (maximum frequency) corresponds to the case where the electron loses all its kinetic energy in a single collision.

$$\lambda_{\min} = \frac{hc}{eV}$$ $$\lambda_{\min} \text{ (Ã…)} = \frac{12400}{V \text{ (volts)}}$$

The minimum wavelength depends only on the accelerating voltage, not on the target material.

Characteristic X-Ray Spectrum

In addition to the continuous spectrum, sharp intensity peaks appear at specific wavelengths that depend on the target material. These are called characteristic X-rays. They are produced when an incident electron knocks out an inner-shell electron from a target atom, and an electron from a higher shell fills the vacancy, emitting an X-ray photon of a definite energy.

  • K-series: Vacancy in the K-shell (n=1) is filled by electrons from L (Kα), M (Kβ), N (Kγ) shells.
  • L-series: Vacancy in the L-shell (n=2) is filled by electrons from M (Lα), N (Lβ) shells.

Moseley's Law

Henry Moseley (1913) discovered that the square root of the frequency of characteristic X-rays varies linearly with the atomic number Z of the target element:

$$\sqrt{f} = a(Z - b)$$ $$b \approx 1 \quad \text{(K}_\alpha\text{)}, \quad b \approx 7.4 \quad \text{(L}_\alpha\text{)}$$

Moseley's law established the importance of atomic number (rather than atomic weight) as the fundamental property of an element and helped correct the ordering of elements in the periodic table.

Bragg's Law (X-Ray Diffraction)

$$2d\sin\theta = n\lambda$$

9. Compton Effect

Fig 19.9

The Phenomenon

The Compton effect (1923) is the inelastic scattering of a photon by a free (or loosely bound) electron. Unlike classical wave theory predictions (Thomson scattering where wavelength is unchanged), the scattered photon has a longer wavelength than the incident photon. This is because the photon transfers part of its energy and momentum to the recoiling electron. The Compton effect is direct evidence of the particle nature of light.

Compton Shift Formula

$$\Delta\lambda = \lambda' - \lambda = \frac{h}{m_e c}(1 - \cos\theta)$$ $$\lambda_c = \frac{h}{m_e c} = 2.43 \times 10^{-12} \text{ m} = 0.0243 \text{ Ã…}$$ $$E' = \frac{E}{1 + \frac{E}{m_e c^2}(1 - \cos\theta)}$$ $$KE_e = E - E'$$

Important Features

  • At θ = 0° (forward scattering): Δλ = 0 (no shift, no energy transfer).
  • At θ = 90°: Δλ = λc = 0.0243 Å.
  • At θ = 180° (backscattering): Δλ = 2λc = 0.0486 Å (maximum shift, maximum energy transfer to electron).
  • The shift Δλ is independent of the incident wavelength but depends only on the scattering angle.
  • The Compton effect is significant for X-rays and γ-rays, not for visible light (because λc is much smaller than visible wavelengths).

10. Pair Production and Annihilation

Fig 19.10

Pair Production

Pair production is the process by which a high-energy photon spontaneously converts into an electron–positron pair in the presence of a nucleus (which is needed to conserve momentum). The positron (e+) is the antiparticle of the electron — same mass but opposite charge. This process demonstrates complete conversion of electromagnetic energy into matter, illustrating E = mc².

$$E_{\gamma} \geq 2m_e c^2 = 1.022 \text{ MeV}$$ $$m_e c^2 = 0.511 \text{ MeV}$$ $$E_{\gamma} = 1.022 \text{ MeV} + KE_{e^-} + KE_{e^+}$$ $$\gamma \to e^- + e^+$$

Why a Nucleus is Required

Pair production cannot occur in free space because it would violate conservation of momentum. The nucleus (or any massive particle) recoils slightly to absorb excess momentum, making the process energetically possible.

Pair Annihilation

The reverse process — pair annihilation — occurs when an electron and positron meet and their mass is completely converted into two gamma-ray photons emitted in opposite directions (to conserve momentum):

$$e^- + e^+ \to 2\gamma$$ $$E = m_e c^2 = 0.511 \text{ MeV}$$

Pair production creates matter from pure energy; pair annihilation converts matter entirely into energy. Both are direct verifications of mass-energy equivalence.

11. Nucleus and Nuclear Properties

Fig 19.11

Composition of the Nucleus

The nucleus of an atom contains protons (positively charged) and neutrons (electrically neutral), collectively called nucleons. The nucleus is described by:

  • Atomic number (Z): Number of protons in the nucleus.
  • Mass number (A): Total number of nucleons (protons + neutrons). A = Z + N, where N is the number of neutrons.
  • Isotopes: Same Z, different A (same element, different mass).
  • Isobars: Same A, different Z (different elements, same mass number).
  • Isotones: Same N (same number of neutrons).

Nuclear Size and Density

$$R = R_0 A^{1/3}, \quad R_0 = 1.2 \times 10^{-15} \text{ m} = 1.2 \text{ fm}$$ $$V \propto A$$ $$\rho \approx 2.3 \times 10^{17} \text{ kg/m}^3$$ $$1 \text{ u} = 1.66 \times 10^{-27} \text{ kg} = 931.5 \text{ MeV/c}^2$$

The nuclear density is enormous — about 1014 times the density of ordinary matter — because almost all atomic mass is concentrated in a nucleus whose radius is only about 1/100,000th the size of the atom.

Rest Mass Energies

$$m_p = 1.00727 \text{ u} = 938.3 \text{ MeV}$$ $$m_n = 1.00866 \text{ u} = 939.6 \text{ MeV}$$ $$m_e = 0.000549 \text{ u} = 0.511 \text{ MeV}$$

12. Mass Defect and Binding Energy

Fig 19.12

Mass Defect

The mass of a nucleus is always less than the sum of the masses of its constituent free nucleons. This deficit is called the mass defect (Δm), and it represents the mass equivalent of the energy released when the nucleus formed:

$$\Delta m = [Z m_p + (A - Z)m_n] - M_{\text{nucleus}}$$

Binding Energy

The mass defect corresponds to energy (by Einstein's mass-energy equivalence) that was released when the nucleons came together to form the nucleus. This energy is called the binding energy — it is the minimum energy required to completely separate all the nucleons.

$$BE = \Delta m \times c^2$$ $$BE \text{ (MeV)} = \Delta m \text{ (u)} \times 931.5$$ $$\frac{BE}{A}$$

Binding Energy per Nucleon Curve

The binding energy per nucleon (BE/A) is a measure of nuclear stability. The BE/A vs A curve reveals:

  • BE/A is low for very light nuclei (e.g., deuterium: ∼1.1 MeV).
  • It rises steeply for light nuclei, with peaks at He-4, C-12, O-16 (especially stable, "magic numbers").
  • It reaches a maximum of about 8.75 MeV per nucleon for iron-56 (Fe-56) and nickel-62, making them the most stable nuclei.
  • It gradually decreases for heavier nuclei (e.g., U-238: ∼7.6 MeV per nucleon).
  • This curve explains why fusion of light nuclei and fission of heavy nuclei both release energy — in both cases the products have higher BE/A than the reactants.
Example 4 — Binding energy of oxygen-16

Calculate the binding energy and BE/A for oxygen-16. Given: mp = 1.00727 u, mn = 1.00866 u, m(O-16) = 15.99493 u.

Solution:
O-16 has Z = 8 protons and N = 8 neutrons.

Expected mass of free nucleons:
Mfree = 8(1.00727) + 8(1.00866) = 8.05816 + 8.06928 = 16.12744 u

Mass defect: Δm = 16.12744 − 15.99493 = 0.13251 u

Binding energy: BE = 0.13251 × 931.5 = 123.4 MeV

Binding energy per nucleon: BE/A = 123.4 / 16 = 7.71 MeV/nucleon

13. Nuclear Forces

Fig 19.13

Nature of Nuclear Forces

The strong nuclear force holds the nucleus together against the mutual electrostatic repulsion between protons. It has the following distinctive properties:

  • Short range: It operates only at distances of the order of 1–3 fm (10−15 m). Beyond ∼3 fm it drops to essentially zero.
  • Strongest known fundamental force: About 100 times stronger than the electromagnetic force at nuclear distances.
  • Charge independence: The nuclear force between two protons is the same as between two neutrons or a proton and a neutron (when in the same spin state).
  • Attractive at normal nuclear distances but becomes repulsive at very short distances (< 0.5 fm), preventing nuclear collapse.
  • Saturation: Each nucleon interacts only with its nearest neighbours, so the binding energy is proportional to A (not A²).

Comparison of Fundamental Forces

$$1 : 10^{-2} : 10^{-13} : 10^{-38}$$

Q-Value of a Nuclear Reaction

$$Q = (m_{\text{initial}} - m_{\text{final}}) \times 931.5 \text{ MeV}$$ $$Q > 0: \text{ exoergic (spontaneous)}$$ $$Q < 0: \text{ endoergic (threshold)}$$

14. Nuclear Stability

Fig 19.14

Neutron-to-Proton Ratio

The stability of a nucleus is governed primarily by the neutron-to-proton ratio (N/Z). Stable nuclei occupy a narrow "stability belt" on a plot of N vs Z:

  • For light nuclei (Z < 20): N/Z ≈ 1 (roughly equal protons and neutrons).
  • For heavy nuclei: N/Z > 1, increasing up to ≈ 1.5–1.6 for the heaviest stable nuclei. Extra neutrons dilute the Coulomb repulsion between protons without adding to it.
  • No stable nucleus exists for Z > 82 (bismuth is the heaviest stable element).

Even-Odd Rules

  • Even Z, Even N (even-even): Most stable — about 160 stable nuclei.
  • Even Z, Odd N or Odd Z, Even N: Moderately stable — about 56 and 52 stable nuclei respectively.
  • Odd Z, Odd N (odd-odd): Very rarely stable — only 4 stable nuclei: H-2, He-3 (actually Li-6), Li-6, B-10, N-14.

Decay Modes and the Stability Belt

$$n \to p + e^- + \bar{\nu}_e \quad \text{(Î²}^-\text{ decay)}$$ $$p \to n + e^+ + \nu_e \quad \text{(Î²}^+\text{ decay)}$$ $$\alpha \text{ decay (Z > 82)}$$

Magic Numbers

Nuclei with proton or neutron numbers equal to 2, 8, 20, 28, 50, 82, or 126 are exceptionally stable. These are called magic numbers, analogous to closed electron shells in atoms (noble gases). Doubly magic nuclei (magic N and magic Z) like He-4, O-16, Ca-40, and Pb-208 are especially stable.

15. Radioactivity and Nuclear Decay

Fig 19.15

Types of Radioactive Decay

Radioactivity is the spontaneous disintegration of unstable nuclei with the emission of particles and/or radiation. It is a random, statistical process independent of temperature, pressure, or chemical state. There are five main decay modes:

Alpha (α) decay: The nucleus emits a helium-4 nucleus (2 protons + 2 neutrons). The parent nucleus loses 2 protons and 2 neutrons.

$$^A_Z X \to ^{A-4}_{Z-2} Y + ^4_2 \text{He}$$ $$^{238}_{92} \text{U} \to ^{234}_{90} \text{Th} + ^4_2 \text{He}$$

Beta (β−) decay: A neutron in the nucleus converts to a proton, emitting an electron and an antineutrino.

$$^A_Z X \to ^A_{Z+1} Y + e^- + \bar{\nu}_e$$ $$^{14}_6 \text{C} \to ^{14}_7 \text{N} + e^- + \bar{\nu}_e$$

Beta-plus (β+) decay: A proton converts to a neutron, emitting a positron and a neutrino.

$$^A_Z X \to ^A_{Z-1} Y + e^+ + \nu_e$$ $$(Q > 1.022 \text{ MeV})$$

Electron capture: An orbital electron is absorbed by the nucleus.

$$e^- + ^A_Z X \to ^A_{Z-1} Y + \nu_e$$

Gamma (γ) decay: An excited nucleus transitions to a lower energy state by emitting a high-energy photon (gamma ray). Neither Z nor A changes.

$$^A_Z X^* \to ^A_Z X + \gamma$$

Properties Comparison

  • α particles: Charge +2e, mass 4u, low penetration (stopped by paper), high ionizing power, deflected in electric/magnetic fields.
  • β particles: Charge −e, negligible mass, moderate penetration (stopped by aluminium sheet), moderate ionizing power, strongly deflected in fields.
  • γ rays: No charge, no mass, high penetration (attenuated by thick lead), low ionizing power, not deflected by fields.

Radioactive Decay Law

The number of radioactive atoms that decay per unit time is proportional to the number of radioactive atoms present at that instant. This leads to an exponential decay:

$$\frac{dN}{dt} = -\lambda N$$ $$N(t) = N_0 e^{-\lambda t}$$

Half-Life and Mean Life

$$t_{1/2} = \frac{\ln 2}{\lambda} = \frac{0.693}{\lambda}$$ $$N = \frac{N_0}{2^n}$$ $$\tau = \frac{1}{\lambda} = \frac{t_{1/2}}{0.693} \approx 1.44 \, t_{1/2}$$

Activity

The activity (or rate of decay) of a radioactive sample is the number of disintegrations per unit time:

$$A = \lambda N = \lambda N_0 e^{-\lambda t} = A_0 e^{-\lambda t}$$ $$1 \text{ Ci} = 3.7 \times 10^{10} \text{ Bq}$$ $$\lambda_{\text{total}} = \lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \ldots$$
Example 5 — Activity after multiple half-lives

A radioactive sample has an initial activity of 1000 Bq and a half-life of 10 days. Find (a) the activity after 30 days, and (b) the time for 75% of the sample to decay.

Solution (a):
Number of half-lives in 30 days = 30/10 = 3
A = A0 × (1/2)3 = 1000 / 8 = 125 Bq

Solution (b):
If 75% has decayed, 25% = N0/4 remains.
N0/4 = N0 × (1/2)n ⇒ n = 2 half-lives
t = 2 × 10 = 20 days

16. Nuclear Fission

Fig 19.16

The Fission Process

Nuclear fission is the splitting of a heavy nucleus into two lighter nuclei (of comparable masses) with the release of a large amount of energy and typically 2–3 neutrons. The most important fission reaction is induced by slow (thermal) neutrons striking U-235:

$$^{235}_{92}\text{U} + ^1_0 n \to ^{141}_{56}\text{Ba} + ^{92}_{36}\text{Kr} + 3 \, ^1_0 n + Q$$ $$Q \approx 200 \text{ MeV}$$

Fissile materials include U-235, Pu-239, and U-233. Fast neutrons must be slowed down (moderated) by heavy water or graphite to increase the probability of inducing fission.

Chain Reaction

The neutrons released in one fission event can induce further fissions, leading to a chain reaction. The multiplication factor k (average number of fissions caused by one fission) determines the reaction behaviour:

  • k < 1: Sub-critical — reaction dies out.
  • k = 1: Critical — steady, self-sustaining chain reaction (nuclear reactor).
  • k > 1: Super-critical — exponentially growing reaction (nuclear bomb).

The minimum mass of fissile material needed to sustain a chain reaction is called the critical mass.

Nuclear Reactor

  • Fuel: Enriched uranium (U-235) or plutonium.
  • Moderator: Heavy water (D2O) or graphite — slows fast neutrons to thermal energies.
  • Control rods: Cadmium or boron — absorb excess neutrons to regulate k ≈ 1.
  • Coolant: Water, heavy water, or liquid metal — carries heat away to generate steam.
  • Shielding: Thick concrete walls to contain radiation.

17. Nuclear Fusion

Fig 19.17

The Fusion Process

Nuclear fusion is the combining of two light nuclei to form a heavier nucleus with the release of energy. Fusion requires extremely high temperatures (millions to hundreds of millions of Kelvin) to give the nuclei enough kinetic energy to overcome the Coulomb barrier. Such reactions are called thermonuclear reactions. Fusion is the energy source of stars and the Sun.

$$4 \, ^1_1\text{H} \to ^4_2\text{He} + 2e^+ + 2\nu_e + 26.7 \text{ MeV}$$ $$^2_1\text{H} + ^3_1\text{H} \to ^4_2\text{He} + ^1_0 n + 17.6 \text{ MeV}$$ $$^2_1\text{H} + ^2_1\text{H} \to ^3_2\text{He} + ^1_0 n + 3.27 \text{ MeV}$$ $$n \times \tau > 5 \times 10^{14} \text{ s/cm}^3$$

Why Fusion is Difficult on Earth

  • Temperatures required: 100–300 million Kelvin (much higher than the solar core).
  • At such temperatures matter exists as a plasma; no solid container can hold it.
  • Confinement approaches: magnetic confinement (tokamak) and inertial confinement (laser-driven implosion).
  • The D-T reaction is favoured because it has the largest cross-section and highest energy release among practical fusion reactions.

Fission vs Fusion

  • Fission splits heavy nuclei; fusion combines light nuclei. Both release energy because the products have higher binding energy per nucleon.
  • Energy per nucleon released in fusion (∼7 MeV) is much greater than in fission (∼0.85 MeV).
  • Fusion produces far less radioactive waste and uses abundant fuel (hydrogen isotopes).
  • Uncontrolled fusion is used in thermonuclear (hydrogen) bombs; controlled fusion for power generation remains an active research goal.
Example 6 — Energy released in D-D fusion

Calculate the energy released in the reaction: 21H + 21H → 32He + 10n.
Given: m(21H) = 2.01410 u, m(32He) = 3.01603 u, m(10n) = 1.00866 u.

Solution:
Mass of reactants = 2 × 2.01410 = 4.02820 u
Mass of products = 3.01603 + 1.00866 = 4.02469 u

Mass defect: Δm = 4.02820 − 4.02469 = 0.00351 u

Energy released: Q = 0.00351 × 931.5 = 3.27 MeV

18. Nuclear Reactions and Q-Value Analysis

Fig 19.18

Mass-Energy Equivalence

Einstein's famous equation E = mc² establishes the equivalence of mass and energy. In nuclear reactions, a measurable amount of mass is converted into energy (or vice versa). The energy released (or absorbed) in a nuclear reaction is called the Q-value:

$$a + X \to Y + b$$ $$Q = (m_a + m_X - m_Y - m_b) \times 931.5 \text{ MeV}$$ $$Q > 0: \text{exoergic}, \quad Q < 0: \text{endoergic}$$

Threshold Kinetic Energy

For an endoergic reaction (Q < 0) with a stationary target X, the bombarding particle a must have a minimum kinetic energy (threshold) to initiate the reaction:

$$KE_{\text{threshold}} = |Q| \times \frac{m_a + m_X + m_Y + m_b}{2m_X}$$

Conservation Laws in Nuclear Reactions

  • Mass number (A): Total A is conserved.
  • Charge (Z): Total proton number is conserved.
  • Energy: Total relativistic energy (including rest mass energy) is conserved.
  • Momentum: Total linear momentum is conserved.
  • Angular momentum and parity are also conserved in strong interactions.
Example 7 — Q-value of a proton-induced reaction

Find the Q-value for the reaction p + 15N → 12C + 4He. The atomic masses are: H = 1.00783 u, 15N = 15.00011 u, 12C = 12.00000 u, 4He = 4.00260 u.

Solution:
Mass of reactants = 1.00783 + 15.00011 = 16.00794 u
Mass of products = 12.00000 + 4.00260 = 16.00260 u

Δm = 16.00794 − 16.00260 = 0.00534 u

Q = 0.00534 × 931.5 = +4.97 MeV (exoergic reaction)
