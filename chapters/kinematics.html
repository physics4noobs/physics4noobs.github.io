1. Position, Distance & Displacement

Position Vector

The position of a particle in space is described by a position vector, which is a vector drawn from the origin of a chosen reference frame to the location of the particle. In one dimension, we simply use a coordinate x measured along a straight line from the origin. In two dimensions the position vector is r = x i + y j, and in three dimensions r = x i + y j + z k.

The choice of origin and axis orientation is arbitrary but must remain consistent throughout a problem. Once a coordinate system is fixed, every point in space has a unique position vector.

Distance vs Displacement

Distance is the total length of the path travelled by an object, regardless of direction. It is a scalar quantity and is always non-negative. Displacement is the change in the position vector of the object — it is the straight-line vector from the initial position to the final position. Displacement is a vector quantity.

Key distinctions:

  • Distance is always greater than or equal to the magnitude of displacement: d ≥ |Δr|.
  • Distance can never be zero if the object has moved and returned to its starting point, but displacement will be zero in that case.
  • For motion along a straight line without change of direction, distance equals the magnitude of displacement.
  • Displacement depends only on the initial and final positions, not on the path taken.
Displacement: Δr = rf − ri
In 1-D: Δx = xf − xi
In 2-D: Δr = (xf − xi) i + (yf − yi) j
|Δr| = √[(Δx)² + (Δy)²]

Path Length vs Displacement

Path length is another term for distance. Consider a particle that moves from point A to point B along a curved path and then from B to C. The path length is the sum of the arc lengths AB and BC. The displacement, however, is the vector from A to C directly. In circular motion, after completing one full revolution, the path length equals the circumference 2πr but the displacement is zero.

Solved Example 1

A particle moves along the x-axis from x = 2 m to x = 8 m, then reverses direction and moves to x = 3 m. Find the distance travelled and the displacement.

Solution:
Distance from x = 2 to x = 8: |8 − 2| = 6 m
Distance from x = 8 to x = 3: |3 − 8| = 5 m
Total distance = 6 + 5 = 11 m

Displacement = xf − xi = 3 − 2 = +1 m (in the positive x-direction)
Magnitude of displacement = 1 m.

Solved Example 2

A car moves 3 km due North, then 4 km due East. Find the magnitude of displacement and the total distance covered.

Solution:
Distance = 3 + 4 = 7 km

The displacement is the hypotenuse of a right triangle with sides 3 km and 4 km.
|Δr| = √(3² + 4²) = √(9 + 16) = √25 = 5 km
Direction: tan θ = 4/3, so θ = 53.13° East of North.

2. Speed & Velocity

Average Speed

Average speed is defined as the total distance travelled divided by the total time taken. It is a scalar quantity and is always positive.

Average Speed = Total Distance / Total Time
vavg = d / t

If a body covers distances d1, d2, d3, ... with speeds v1, v2, v3, ... respectively, then the average speed is:

vavg = (d1 + d2 + d3 + ...) / (d1/v1 + d2/v2 + d3/v3 + ...)

Special case — Equal distances: vavg = 2v1v2 / (v1 + v2)   (Harmonic mean)
Special case — Equal times: vavg = (v1 + v2) / 2   (Arithmetic mean)

Average Velocity

Average velocity is defined as the displacement divided by the time interval. Unlike average speed, average velocity is a vector quantity and can be positive, negative, or zero.

vavg = Δr / Δt = (rf − ri) / (tf − ti)

Instantaneous Velocity

Instantaneous velocity is the velocity of a particle at a specific instant of time. Mathematically, it is the limiting value of the average velocity as the time interval approaches zero.

v = lim (Δt → 0) Δr / Δt = dr/dt

In 1-D: v = dx/dt

The magnitude of instantaneous velocity is the instantaneous speed. The direction of instantaneous velocity is always tangent to the path of the particle at that point.

Speed vs Velocity

  • Speed is a scalar; velocity is a vector.
  • Speed is always non-negative; velocity can be positive, negative, or zero.
  • Average speed ≥ |average velocity| for any motion.
  • Instantaneous speed = |instantaneous velocity| always.
  • A body can have varying velocity but constant speed (e.g., uniform circular motion).
Solved Example 3

A car travels the first half of a distance at speed 40 km/h and the second half at speed 60 km/h. Find the average speed for the entire journey.

Solution:
Since equal distances are covered, we use the harmonic mean formula:
vavg = 2v1v2 / (v1 + v2)
vavg = 2 × 40 × 60 / (40 + 60)
vavg = 4800 / 100 = 48 km/h

Solved Example 4

The position of a particle is given by x(t) = 3t² − 2t + 5 (in metres, t in seconds). Find the instantaneous velocity at t = 2 s and the average velocity between t = 1 s and t = 3 s.

Solution:
v = dx/dt = 6t − 2
At t = 2 s: v = 6(2) − 2 = 10 m/s

For average velocity:
x(1) = 3(1) − 2(1) + 5 = 6 m
x(3) = 3(9) − 2(3) + 5 = 27 − 6 + 5 = 26 m
vavg = (26 − 6) / (3 − 1) = 20/2 = 10 m/s

3. Acceleration

Average Acceleration

Average acceleration is defined as the change in velocity divided by the time interval over which the change occurs. It is a vector quantity.

aavg = Δv / Δt = (vf − vi) / (tf − ti)

Instantaneous Acceleration

Instantaneous acceleration is the acceleration at a specific instant of time. It is the time derivative of velocity, or equivalently, the second derivative of position.

a = dv/dt = d²r/dt²

In 1-D: a = dv/dt = d²x/dt²

Important points about acceleration:

  • If velocity and acceleration are in the same direction, the object speeds up (accelerating).
  • If velocity and acceleration are in opposite directions, the object slows down (decelerating or retarding).
  • An object can have zero velocity but non-zero acceleration (e.g., a ball thrown vertically upward at its highest point).
  • Acceleration can also be written as: a = v (dv/dx), which is useful when velocity is given as a function of position.

Uniform Acceleration

When acceleration remains constant in both magnitude and direction throughout the motion, we call it uniformly accelerated motion. In this case, the average acceleration equals the instantaneous acceleration at every point. All three kinematic equations (discussed in the next section) apply strictly only to uniformly accelerated motion.

Motion Under Gravity (Free Fall)

Near the surface of the Earth, all objects experience a constant downward acceleration due to gravity, denoted by g ≈ 9.8 m/s² (often approximated as 10 m/s²). When air resistance is negligible, this motion is called free fall. Taking the upward direction as positive:

  • For a body thrown vertically upward: a = −g
  • For a body falling freely downward from rest: a = +g (if downward is taken positive)
  • The time to reach the highest point equals the time to fall back from the highest point.
  • The speed at which the object returns to the point of projection equals the speed of projection.
Free fall from rest: v = gt,   h = ½gt²,   v² = 2gh
Object thrown upward with speed u: v = u − gt,   h = ut − ½gt²,   v² = u² − 2gh
Time to reach max height: t = u/g
Maximum height: H = u²/(2g)
Total time of flight: T = 2u/g
Solved Example 5

A ball is thrown vertically upward with a speed of 20 m/s from the top of a 60 m tall building. Find the time it takes to hit the ground and the speed with which it hits. Take g = 10 m/s².

Solution:
Taking upward as positive and origin at the top of the building:
u = +20 m/s, a = −10 m/s², s = −60 m (ground is below origin)

Using s = ut + ½at²:
−60 = 20t − ½(10)t²
−60 = 20t − 5t²
5t² − 20t − 60 = 0
t² − 4t − 12 = 0
(t − 6)(t + 2) = 0
t = 6 s   (rejecting t = −2 s)

Speed at impact: v = u + at = 20 + (−10)(6) = 20 − 60 = −40 m/s
Speed = |v| = 40 m/s (downward)

Solved Example 6

The velocity of a particle is given by v = (3t² − 6t) m/s. Find the acceleration at t = 2 s and the instant when the particle has zero acceleration.

Solution:
a = dv/dt = 6t − 6
At t = 2 s: a = 6(2) − 6 = 6 m/s²

For zero acceleration: 6t − 6 = 0, so t = 1 s

4. Equations of Motion

The three kinematic equations relate displacement (s), initial velocity (u), final velocity (v), acceleration (a), and time (t) for uniformly accelerated motion in a straight line. These equations are valid only when acceleration is constant.

First Equation of Motion

Starting from the definition of acceleration: a = (v − u)/t, rearranging gives us the velocity-time relation.

v = u + at

Derivation: Since a = dv/dt for constant acceleration, integrating both sides from 0 to t: ∫dv = a ∫dt gives v − u = at, hence v = u + at.

Second Equation of Motion

This gives the displacement-time relation. Since v = ds/dt = u + at, integrating both sides from 0 to t yields the displacement.

s = ut + ½at²

Derivation: ds = v dt = (u + at) dt. Integrating: s = ut + ½at². Note that s here is the displacement, not the distance.

Third Equation of Motion

This is the velocity-displacement relation, obtained by eliminating time from the first two equations.

v² = u² + 2as

Derivation: From v = u + at, we get t = (v − u)/a. Substituting in s = ut + ½at² and simplifying: s = u(v − u)/a + ½a[(v − u)/a]² = (v − u)(v + u)/(2a), which gives v² − u² = 2as.

Distance Travelled in the n-th Second

A frequently asked formula in competitive exams gives the displacement during the n-th second of motion (not the first n seconds, but only the n-th one).

sn = u + a(2n − 1)/2

Derivation: sn = s(n) − s(n−1) = [un + ½an²] − [u(n−1) + ½a(n−1)²] = u + ½a(2n − 1). This formula is extremely useful for problems asking about distance in a specific second of motion.

Important notes on these equations:

  • All quantities are vectors in 1-D; use sign conventions carefully (positive direction = positive values).
  • When a body decelerates, a is negative (opposite to velocity direction).
  • These equations do not apply if acceleration is variable — use calculus methods instead.
  • For problems with multiple phases of motion (e.g., accelerate then decelerate), apply equations separately for each phase.
Solved Example 7

A car starts from rest and accelerates uniformly at 2 m/s² for 10 s. It then moves with constant velocity for 20 s and finally decelerates uniformly to rest in 5 s. Find the total distance covered.

Solution:
Phase 1 (acceleration): u = 0, a = 2 m/s², t = 10 s
v = u + at = 0 + 2(10) = 20 m/s
s1 = ut + ½at² = 0 + ½(2)(100) = 100 m

Phase 2 (constant velocity): v = 20 m/s, t = 20 s
s2 = vt = 20 × 20 = 400 m

Phase 3 (deceleration): u = 20 m/s, v = 0, t = 5 s
s3 = (u + v)t/2 = (20 + 0)(5)/2 = 50 m

Total distance = 100 + 400 + 50 = 550 m

Solved Example 8

A body starts from rest with uniform acceleration. The distance covered by the body in the 5th second is 9 m. Find the acceleration.

Solution:
Using the n-th second formula: sn = u + a(2n − 1)/2
Here u = 0, n = 5, s5 = 9 m
9 = 0 + a(2 × 5 − 1)/2
9 = a(9)/2
a = 18/9 = 2 m/s²

5. Graphical Analysis of Motion

Position-Time (x-t) Graph

The position-time graph plots the position of a particle on the y-axis against time on the x-axis. Key principles:

  • Slope of the x-t graph = instantaneous velocity. A steeper slope means higher speed.
  • A straight line with positive slope represents uniform velocity in the positive direction.
  • A horizontal line represents the particle at rest (v = 0).
  • A parabolic curve opening upward represents uniformly accelerated motion (constant positive acceleration).
  • A parabolic curve opening downward represents uniformly decelerated motion.
  • A straight line with negative slope represents uniform velocity in the negative direction.

Velocity-Time (v-t) Graph

The velocity-time graph is arguably the most important graph in kinematics. It encodes both velocity and displacement information.

  • Slope of the v-t graph = instantaneous acceleration.
  • Area under the v-t curve = displacement. Area above the time axis is positive displacement; area below is negative displacement.
  • Total area (considering signs) = net displacement. Total area (ignoring signs) = total distance.
  • A horizontal line indicates zero acceleration (constant velocity).
  • A straight line with positive slope indicates constant positive acceleration.
  • A straight line passing through the origin indicates a = constant with u = 0.

Acceleration-Time (a-t) Graph

The acceleration-time graph gives information about how the acceleration changes with time.

  • Area under the a-t curve = change in velocity (Δv).
  • A horizontal line represents constant acceleration (uniformly accelerated motion).
  • The a-t graph alone does not directly give displacement; you need the initial velocity as well.

Extracting Information from Graphs

A common competitive-exam strategy is to convert between graphs. For example, given a v-t graph, you can construct the corresponding x-t and a-t graphs. From the v-t graph, compute the slope at each point for the a-t graph, and compute the area under the curve for changes in position to construct the x-t graph.

x-t graph: Slope = v (velocity)
v-t graph: Slope = a (acceleration),   Area = Δx (displacement)
a-t graph: Area = Δv (change in velocity)
Solved Example 9

The velocity-time graph of a particle is a straight line from (0, 10) to (4, −6) where units are in (seconds, m/s). Find the displacement and the total distance covered in 4 seconds.

Solution:
The v-t graph is a straight line: v = 10 + [(−6 − 10)/4]t = 10 − 4t
Acceleration = slope = −4 m/s²

Velocity becomes zero when 10 − 4t = 0, i.e., t = 2.5 s

Displacement: Area under v-t curve (with signs)
Area from t = 0 to 2.5 s (positive triangle) = ½ × 2.5 × 10 = 12.5 m
Area from t = 2.5 to 4 s (negative triangle) = ½ × 1.5 × (−6) = −4.5 m
Net displacement = 12.5 + (−4.5) = 8 m

Distance: Sum of absolute areas = 12.5 + 4.5 = 17 m

Solved Example 10

The position of a particle varies as x = 2t² − 8t + 6 (in metres, t in seconds). At what time does the particle change direction? What is the minimum position coordinate?

Solution:
v = dx/dt = 4t − 8
The particle changes direction when v = 0:
4t − 8 = 0 ⇒ t = 2 s

At t = 2 s: x = 2(4) − 8(2) + 6 = 8 − 16 + 6 = −2 m
Since a = d²x/dt² = 4 > 0, the x-t graph is a parabola opening upward, confirming this is a minimum.

6. Non-Uniform Acceleration

When the acceleration of a particle is not constant, the standard kinematic equations (v = u + at, s = ut + ½at², v² = u² + 2as) are not valid. Instead, we must use calculus — differentiation and integration — to relate acceleration, velocity, and position.

Case 1: Acceleration as a Function of Time — a = f(t)

Integrate successively to obtain velocity and then position.

Given: a(t) = f(t)

Velocity: v(t) = v0 + ∫0t a(t′) dt′

Position: x(t) = x0 + ∫0t v(t′) dt′

Case 2: Acceleration as a Function of Position — a = f(x)

Use the relation a = v (dv/dx) to convert to a separable differential equation.

Given: a(x) = f(x)

v dv = a(x) dx

½(v² − v0²) = ∫x0x f(x′) dx′

Case 3: Acceleration as a Function of Velocity — a = f(v)

Separate variables depending on what is needed.

Given: a(v) = f(v)

To find v vs t:   t = ∫v0v dv′ / f(v′)

To find v vs x:   x − x0 = ∫v0v v′ dv′ / f(v′)

This case commonly arises in problems involving resistive forces (drag), where the retarding force depends on speed.

Solved Example 11

A particle starts from rest at the origin. Its acceleration is a(t) = (2t − 2) m/s². Find v(t), x(t), and the displacement from t = 2 s to t = 4 s.

Solution:
Given: a(t) = 2t − 2, v(0) = 0, x(0) = 0

Finding v(t):
v(t) = ∫(2t − 2) dt = t² − 2t + C
v(0) = 0 ⇒ C = 0, so v(t) = t² − 2t m/s

Finding x(t):
x(t) = ∫(t² − 2t) dt = t³/3 − t² + C′
x(0) = 0 ⇒ C′ = 0, so x(t) = t³/3 − t² m

Displacement from t = 2 to t = 4:
x(4) = 64/3 − 16 = 16/3 m
x(2) = 8/3 − 4 = −4/3 m
Δx = 16/3 − (−4/3) = 20/3 ≈ 6.67 m

7. Projectile Motion

A projectile is any object that is given an initial velocity and then moves under the influence of gravity alone (neglecting air resistance). The path followed by a projectile is called its trajectory, and it is always a parabola.

Consider a projectile launched from the origin with initial speed u at angle θ with the horizontal. We resolve the motion into two independent components:

  • Horizontal (x-axis): No acceleration → ax = 0, constant velocity u cosθ
  • Vertical (y-axis): Acceleration due to gravity → ay = −g (taking upward as positive)

Equations of Motion for a Projectile

Horizontal:
x = (u cosθ) t
vx = u cosθ (constant)

Vertical:
y = (u sinθ) t − ½gt²
vy = u sinθ − gt

Time of Flight (T)

The projectile returns to the same vertical level (y = 0) when:

T = 2u sinθ / g

The time to reach maximum height is exactly half the total time of flight: tpeak = T/2 = u sinθ / g.

Maximum Height (H)

At the highest point, vy = 0. Using vy² = uy² − 2gH:

H = u² sin²θ / (2g)

Horizontal Range (R)

The horizontal distance covered during the total time of flight:

R = u² sin 2θ / g

Maximum range occurs when sin 2θ = 1, i.e., θ = 45°, giving Rmax = u²/g.

Equation of Trajectory

Eliminating time from x = u cosθ · t and y = u sinθ · t − ½gt²:

y = x tanθ − gx² / (2u² cos²θ)
or equivalently: y = x tanθ (1 − x/R)

This is the equation of a parabola opening downward.

Complementary Angles of Projection

Since sin 2θ = sin(180° − 2θ) = sin 2(90° − θ), two complementary angles θ and (90° − θ) give the same range but different maximum heights and times of flight.

  • For θ and (90° − θ): R1 = R2
  • H1 + H2 = u²/(2g)
  • T1/T2 = tanθ
  • R = 4√(H1 H2)
Solved Example 12

A ball is projected with an initial speed of 20 m/s at an angle of 60° with the horizontal. Find the time of flight, maximum height, and horizontal range. (Take g = 10 m/s²)

Given: u = 20 m/s, θ = 60°, g = 10 m/s²

Time of Flight:
T = 2u sinθ / g = 2(20) sin 60° / 10 = 40 × (√3/2) / 10 = 2√3 ≈ 3.46 s

Maximum Height:
H = u² sin²θ / (2g) = (400 × 3/4) / 20 = 300/20 = 15 m

Horizontal Range:
R = u² sin 2θ / g = 400 × sin 120° / 10 = 400 × (√3/2) / 10 = 20√3 ≈ 34.64 m

8. Horizontal Projectile

A special case of projectile motion occurs when an object is launched horizontally from a certain height h above the ground. Common examples include a ball rolling off the edge of a table, a stone thrown horizontally from a cliff, or a package dropped from a moving airplane.

In this case, the initial conditions are:

  • Initial horizontal velocity: vx = u (constant throughout)
  • Initial vertical velocity: vy0 = 0
  • Horizontal acceleration: ax = 0
  • Vertical acceleration: ay = g (downward)

Key Formulas

Time to reach ground: t = √(2h/g)
Horizontal distance (Range): x = u√(2h/g)
Final velocity at impact: v = √(u² + 2gh)
Angle below horizontal at impact: tanα = √(2gh) / u

Derivation

Vertical motion: Starting from rest vertically, h = ½gt² → t = √(2h/g)

Horizontal motion: x = ut = u√(2h/g)

Final velocity components: vx = u,   vy = gt = √(2gh)

Resultant: v = √(vx² + vy²) = √(u² + 2gh)

Trajectory equation: y = gx²/(2u²) — a parabola (with y-axis pointing down from launch point).

Solved Example 13

A stone is thrown horizontally from the top of an 80 m high tower with a velocity of 15 m/s. Find the time taken to reach the ground, the horizontal distance from the base, and the velocity at impact. (g = 10 m/s²)

Given: h = 80 m, u = 15 m/s, g = 10 m/s²

Time: t = √(2h/g) = √(160/10) = √16 = 4 s

Range: x = ut = 15 × 4 = 60 m

Velocity at impact:
vy = gt = 10 × 4 = 40 m/s
v = √(15² + 40²) = √(225 + 1600) = √1825 ≈ 42.72 m/s
Angle: tanα = 40/15 = 8/3, α ≈ 69.44° below horizontal

9. Projectile on Inclined Plane

When a projectile is launched from the surface of an inclined plane making angle β with the horizontal, it is convenient to analyze the motion using axes along and perpendicular to the incline.

Let the projectile be launched with speed u at angle α from the horizontal (so the angle from the incline surface is α − β).

Acceleration Components

Along incline (x′-axis): ax′ = −g sinβ
Perpendicular to incline (y′-axis): ay′ = −g cosβ

Key Formulas

Time of Flight: T = 2u sin(α − β) / (g cosβ)
Range on Incline: R = 2u² sin(α − β) cosα / (g cos²β)
Angle for Maximum Range: α = 45° + β/2
Maximum Range: Rmax = u² / [g(1 + sinβ)]

Important Notes

  • When β = 0, all formulas reduce to the standard projectile motion equations.
  • α is measured from the horizontal, not from the incline surface.
  • For maximum range, the launch angle from the incline surface is α − β = 45° − β/2.
  • For a projectile launched down the incline, replace β with −β.
Solved Example 14

A projectile is launched at 60° from the horizontal on an inclined plane that makes 30° with the horizontal. If the initial speed is 20 m/s, find the time of flight and range on the incline. (g = 10 m/s²)

Given: α = 60°, β = 30°, u = 20 m/s, g = 10 m/s²

Time of Flight:
T = 2u sin(α − β) / (g cosβ) = 2(20) sin 30° / (10 cos 30°)
T = 40 × 0.5 / (10 × √3/2) = 20 / (5√3) = 4/√3 ≈ 2.31 s

Range on Incline:
R = 2u² sin(α − β) cosα / (g cos²β)
R = 2(400) × sin 30° × cos 60° / (10 × cos² 30°)
R = 800 × 0.5 × 0.5 / (10 × 0.75) = 200 / 7.5 ≈ 26.67 m

10. Circular Motion

Circular motion is the motion of a body along a circular path at a constant distance from a fixed point called the center. The velocity is always tangent to the circle.

In Uniform Circular Motion (UCM), speed remains constant but velocity direction changes continuously. This means the particle is accelerated even though its speed is constant. The acceleration is directed toward the center — centripetal acceleration.

Fundamental Quantities

Angular velocity: ω = dθ/dt = v/r  (rad/s)
Period: T = 2π/ω = 2πr/v
Frequency: f = 1/T = ω/(2π)  (Hz)
Linear velocity: v = rω
Centripetal acceleration: ac = v²/r = ω²r  (toward center)

Position and Velocity in UCM

Position: r(t) = r cos(ωt) î + r sin(ωt) ĵ
Velocity: v(t) = −rω sin(ωt) î + rω cos(ωt) ĵ
Speed: |v| = rω (constant)
Acceleration: a(t) = −ω²r(t)  (toward center)

Important Points for JEE

  • In UCM, speed is constant but velocity changes direction — the motion is accelerated.
  • v² = u² + 2as cannot be used directly for circular motion (acceleration direction keeps changing).
  • Centripetal acceleration is always perpendicular to velocity — it changes direction, not magnitude.
  • Angular displacement in time t: θ = ωt (for uniform circular motion).
Solved Example 15

A particle moves in a circle of radius 2 m with a constant angular velocity of 3 rad/s. Find the linear speed, centripetal acceleration, period, and frequency.

Given: r = 2 m, ω = 3 rad/s

Linear speed: v = rω = 2 × 3 = 6 m/s

Centripetal acceleration: ac = v²/r = 36/2 = 18 m/s²
(Also: ac = ω²r = 9 × 2 = 18 m/s² ✓)

Period: T = 2π/ω = 2π/3 ≈ 2.09 s

Frequency: f = ω/(2π) = 3/(2π) ≈ 0.48 Hz

11. Non-Uniform Circular Motion

When a particle moves along a circular path with changing speed, both the magnitude and direction of velocity change, resulting in two components of acceleration:

  • Radial (centripetal) acceleration ar: directed toward the center, changes the direction of velocity.
  • Tangential acceleration at: along the tangent, changes the speed.

Key Formulas

Radial acceleration: ar = v²/r  (toward center)
Tangential acceleration: at = dv/dt  (along tangent)
Net acceleration: a = √(ar² + at²)
Angle of net acceleration with radius: tanα = at/ar

Important Points

  • Speed increasing: at is in the direction of velocity.
  • Speed decreasing: at is opposite to velocity.
  • Centripetal acceleration is always present and always toward the center.
  • Tangential acceleration is present only when speed changes.
  • At max/min speed: at = 0, only centripetal remains.
  • Since ar ⊥ at, net acceleration uses the Pythagorean theorem.
Solved Example 16

A particle moves in a circle of radius 4 m. At a certain instant, its speed is 8 m/s and is increasing at 6 m/s². Find ar, at, net acceleration, and the angle of the net acceleration with the radius.

Given: r = 4 m, v = 8 m/s, dv/dt = 6 m/s²

Radial acceleration: ar = v²/r = 64/4 = 16 m/s²

Tangential acceleration: at = 6 m/s²

Net acceleration: a = √(16² + 6²) = √(256 + 36) = √292 ≈ 17.09 m/s²

Angle with radius: tanα = 6/16 = 3/8, α = tan−1(3/8) ≈ 20.56°

12. Relative Motion

The velocity of an object is always measured with respect to a frame of reference. The relative velocity of object A with respect to object B is:

vAB = vA − vB
Property: vAB = −vBA

Relative Velocity in 1-D

If two objects A and B move along the same straight line with velocities vA and vB:

Same direction: |vAB| = |vA − vB|
Opposite directions: |vAB| = |vA| + |vB|  (approach speed)

Relative Velocity in 2-D

|vAB| = √(vA² + vB² − 2 vA vB cosθ)
where θ is the angle between vA and vB

Rain-Man Problem

Rain falls vertically with velocity vR and a man moves horizontally with velocity vM:

vRM = vR − vM
|vRM| = √(vR² + vM²)
Angle with vertical: tanα = vM / vR

The man must tilt his umbrella at angle α from the vertical (in the direction of his motion) to stay dry.

River-Boat Problem

River of width d, current velocity vr, boat speed vb in still water:

Case 1: Shortest Time — Point the boat straight across.

Time = d / vb
Drift downstream = vr × (d / vb)

Case 2: Shortest Path (straight across) — Aim upstream. Requires vb > vr.

sinθ = vr/vb  (angle upstream)
Effective speed = √(vb² − vr²)
Time = d / √(vb² − vr²)
Solved Example 17

Rain is falling vertically downward at 30 m/s. A man walks horizontally at 10 m/s. At what angle should he hold his umbrella?

Given: vR = 30 m/s, vM = 10 m/s

|vRM| = √(900 + 100) = √1000 = 10√10 ≈ 31.62 m/s

tanθ = vM/vR = 10/30 = 1/3
θ = tan−1(1/3) ≈ 18.43° from the vertical (toward his direction of motion)

Solved Example 18

A river is 400 m wide with a current of 3 m/s. A boat can travel at 5 m/s in still water. Find: (a) the shortest time to cross and the drift, (b) the time to cross by the shortest path.

Given: d = 400 m, vr = 3 m/s, vb = 5 m/s

(a) Shortest time:
t = d/vb = 400/5 = 80 s
Drift = vr × t = 3 × 80 = 240 m

(b) Shortest path:
veff = √(vb² − vr²) = √(25 − 9) = √16 = 4 m/s
t = d/veff = 400/4 = 100 s
