Newton’s Laws of Motion

GRADE XI | CHAPTER 02

1. Newton’s First Law — The Law of Inertia

Newton’s First Law states: Every body continues in its state of rest or of uniform motion in a straight line unless compelled to change that state by an external unbalanced force. This seemingly simple statement establishes two profound ideas — the concept of inertia and the existence of inertial reference frames.

Inertia

Inertia is the tendency of a body to resist any change in its state of motion. It is a qualitative measure directly related to mass — the greater the mass, the greater the inertia. Inertia manifests in three forms:

  • Inertia of rest: A body at rest tends to remain at rest. Example: passengers jerk forward when a bus suddenly brakes.
  • Inertia of motion: A body in motion tends to continue moving with the same velocity. Example: a ball thrown inside a moving train continues forward even after release.
  • Inertia of direction: A body tends to maintain its direction of motion. Example: mud flies tangentially off a rotating wheel.

Equilibrium

When the net external force on a body is zero (ΣF = 0), the body is said to be in equilibrium. If the body is at rest, it is in static equilibrium; if moving with constant velocity, it is in dynamic equilibrium. In both cases, the acceleration is zero.

Inertial Frames of Reference

An inertial frame is one in which Newton’s first law holds true — i.e., an unaccelerated frame. Any frame moving with constant velocity relative to an inertial frame is also inertial. The surface of the Earth is approximately inertial for most practical problems, although strictly it is non-inertial due to rotation and revolution.

2. Newton’s Second Law — F = ma

Newton’s Second Law provides the quantitative relationship between force and motion: The rate of change of linear momentum of a body is directly proportional to the net external force applied and takes place in the direction of the applied force.

F = dp/dt = d(mv)/dt
For constant mass: F = ma
For variable mass systems: Fext = m(dv/dt) + vrel(dm/dt)

Linear Momentum

The linear momentum of a particle is defined as p = mv, where m is the mass and v is the velocity. Momentum is a vector quantity with the same direction as velocity. For a system of particles, the total momentum is the vector sum of individual momenta: P = Σmivi.

Impulse

Impulse is the product of force and the time interval over which it acts. It equals the change in momentum of the body:

J = F · Δt = Δp = pf − pi
For variable force: J = ∫ F dt (from t1 to t2)

The impulse-momentum theorem is particularly useful for collision problems and for situations where forces act for very short durations (e.g., a bat hitting a ball). The area under the F-t graph gives the impulse.

Variable Mass Systems

In systems where the mass changes with time (e.g., a rocket ejecting fuel), Newton’s second law must be applied in its general form. For a rocket of mass m at time t, ejecting gas at a relative velocity vrel, the thrust is given by Fthrust = vrel(dm/dt). The rocket equation yields: v = v0 + vrel ln(m0/m), where m0 is the initial mass.

3. Newton’s Third Law — Action and Reaction

Newton’s Third Law states: To every action, there is always an equal and opposite reaction. If body A exerts a force FAB on body B, then body B simultaneously exerts a force FBA = −FAB on body A.

Key points about action-reaction pairs:

  • Action and reaction forces are always equal in magnitude and opposite in direction.
  • They act on different bodies — never on the same body. This is why they do not cancel each other.
  • They are of the same type (both gravitational, both electromagnetic, etc.).
  • They exist simultaneously — there is no time delay between action and reaction.

Examples: When you walk, your foot pushes the ground backward (action), and the ground pushes your foot forward (reaction). A swimmer pushes water backward, and the water pushes the swimmer forward. A book resting on a table pushes the table downward (weight transferred), and the table pushes the book upward (normal force). Note: the weight of the book (Earth pulling the book) and the normal force from the table are not an action-reaction pair — they act on the same body.

4. Free Body Diagrams (FBD)

A Free Body Diagram is a diagram of a single body isolated from its surroundings, showing all the external forces acting on it. Drawing correct FBDs is the most critical skill in solving mechanics problems for JEE and NEET.

Steps to Draw an FBD

  • Identify the body of interest and isolate it mentally from all other objects.
  • Draw the body as a simple shape (usually a dot or rectangle).
  • Identify all forces: weight (mg, always downward), normal force (perpendicular to the contact surface), tension (along the string, away from the body), friction (along the surface, opposing relative motion or tendency of motion), applied forces.
  • Draw each force as an arrow with correct direction and label. Do not include internal forces or forces the body exerts on other objects.
  • Choose a convenient coordinate system (often along and perpendicular to the surface) and resolve forces into components.

Constraint Equations

When objects in a system are connected by strings, pulleys, or surfaces, their motions are not independent. Constraint equations relate the accelerations (or velocities) of different parts of the system. For an inextensible string passing over a pulley, if one end moves down by x, the other end moves up by x — hence their accelerations are equal in magnitude.

For a string of fixed length L connecting masses via a pulley: x1 + x2 = L (constant) ⇒ a1 = −a2. For more complex pulley systems, differentiate the string length equation twice with respect to time.

Tension

Tension is a pulling force transmitted through a string, rope, or cable when pulled taut by forces from opposite ends. For a massless, inextensible string, the tension is the same throughout the string. If the string has mass m and acceleration a, the tension varies along its length.

Normal Force

The normal reaction is the contact force exerted by a surface on a body, perpendicular to the surface. It is a self-adjusting force — it adjusts its magnitude to prevent interpenetration of surfaces. For a body on a horizontal surface: N = mg. For a body on an inclined plane of angle θ: N = mg cosθ.

5. Friction

Friction is a contact force that opposes the relative motion (or the tendency of relative motion) between two surfaces in contact. It arises from the electromagnetic interactions between the surface molecules.

Static Friction (fs)

Static friction acts when there is no relative motion between surfaces. It is a self-adjusting force: it matches the applied force up to a maximum value called the limiting friction.

fs ≤ μsN   (self-adjusting)
fs,max = μsN   (limiting value)

Kinetic Friction (fk)

Once the body starts sliding, kinetic friction takes over. It has a constant magnitude (for a given pair of surfaces) and is always less than the maximum static friction:

fk = μkN   where μk < μs

Angle of Repose

The angle of repose is the maximum angle of an inclined plane at which a body placed on it just begins to slide. At this angle, the component of gravity along the plane equals the maximum static friction:

mg sinα = μs mg cosα
tanα = μs
α = tan−1(μs)

Friction on Inclines

For a body on an inclined plane of angle θ: if θ < α (angle of repose), the body remains stationary with fs = mg sinθ. If θ = α, the body is on the verge of sliding. If θ > α, the body accelerates down the incline with a = g(sinθ − μk cosθ). For a body pushed up the incline, friction acts downward along the plane, and a = g(sinθ + μk cosθ) in the deceleration direction.

6. Circular Motion Dynamics

For an object moving in a circle of radius r with speed v, there must be a net inward force called the centripetal force directed toward the center of the circle. This is not a new type of force — it is provided by existing forces (tension, gravity, friction, normal force, etc.).

Fc = mv²/r = mω²r
ac = v²/r = ω²r   (centripetal acceleration, directed toward center)

Banking of Roads

On a banked curved road of angle θ and radius r, the normal force has a horizontal component that provides the centripetal force. For the ideal speed (no friction needed):

tanθ = v²/rg   ⇒   v = √(rg tanθ)
With friction: vmax = √[rg(tanθ + μs)/(1 − μs tanθ)]
With friction: vmin = √[rg(tanθ − μs)/(1 + μs tanθ)]

Conical Pendulum

A conical pendulum consists of a mass m attached to a string of length L, revolving in a horizontal circle while the string traces a cone. The tension has a vertical component (balancing mg) and a horizontal component (providing centripetal force):

T cosθ = mg      T sinθ = mv²/r
tanθ = v²/rg      Time period: Tperiod = 2π√(L cosθ/g)

7. Pseudo Forces — Non-Inertial Frames

In a non-inertial (accelerating) reference frame, Newton’s laws do not hold in their usual form. To apply Newton’s laws in such frames, we introduce a pseudo force (also called a fictitious force):

Fpseudo = −maframe
(applied on every object of mass m in the non-inertial frame, opposite to the acceleration of the frame)

The pseudo force is not a real force — it has no agent, no reaction pair, and exists only because of the observer’s accelerated frame of reference.

Motion in a Lift (Elevator)

A classic application of pseudo forces. Consider a person of mass m standing on a weighing scale inside a lift. The scale reads the normal force N (the apparent weight):

  • Lift at rest or moving with constant velocity: N = mg (true weight).
  • Lift accelerating upward (a): N = m(g + a). The person feels heavier.
  • Lift accelerating downward (a < g): N = m(g − a). The person feels lighter.
  • Lift in free fall (a = g): N = 0. The person experiences weightlessness.
  • Lift accelerating down with a > g: N becomes negative — the person would lift off the floor (physically impossible with just a floor scale; they’d need to be strapped).

Solved Examples

Example 1 — Atwood Machine

Two blocks of masses m1 = 5 kg and m2 = 3 kg are connected by a light inextensible string passing over a smooth, massless pulley. Find the acceleration of the system and the tension in the string. (Take g = 10 m/s²)

Solution: For the Atwood machine, the heavier block accelerates downward and the lighter block accelerates upward with the same magnitude a.

For m1 (taking downward as positive): m1g − T = m1a  … (i)
For m2 (taking upward as positive): T − m2g = m2a  … (ii)

Adding (i) and (ii):
(m1 − m2)g = (m1 + m2)a
a = (m1 − m2)g / (m1 + m2) = (5 − 3) × 10 / (5 + 3) = 20/8 = 2.5 m/s²

Substituting back into (ii):
T = m2(g + a) = 3 × (10 + 2.5) = 3 × 12.5 = 37.5 N

Example 2 — Friction on an Incline

A block of mass 10 kg is placed on an inclined plane of angle 30°. The coefficient of static friction is μs = 0.6 and kinetic friction is μk = 0.4. Determine whether the block slides and, if so, find the acceleration. (Take g = 10 m/s²)

Solution: First check if the block slides. The angle of repose α = tan−1(0.6) ≈ 31°.
Since θ = 30° < α = 31°, the block does not slide.

The friction force required to keep it stationary:
fs = mg sinθ = 10 × 10 × sin 30° = 50 N
Maximum static friction: fs,max = μs × mg cosθ = 0.6 × 10 × 10 × cos 30° = 0.6 × 86.6 = 52 N
Since 50 N < 52 N, static friction is sufficient. The block remains at rest with a = 0.

If the angle were increased to 37° (greater than α), the block would slide with:
a = g(sin 37° − μk cos 37°) = 10(0.6 − 0.4 × 0.8) = 10(0.6 − 0.32) = 2.8 m/s²

Example 3 — Banking of Roads

A car travels around a banked curve of radius 100 m. The road is banked at an angle of 30°. What is the ideal speed at which the car can negotiate the curve without any friction? (g = 10 m/s²)

Solution: For the ideal banking condition (no friction required):

tanθ = v² / rg
v² = rg tanθ = 100 × 10 × tan 30° = 1000 × (1/√3) ≈ 577.35
v = √577.35 ≈ 24.03 m/s ≈ 86.5 km/h

Key Formulas Summary

F = ma   |   p = mv   |   J = FΔt = Δp
fs ≤ μsN   |   fk = μkN   |   tanα = μs (angle of repose)
Fc = mv²/r   |   tanθ = v²/rg (banking)
Fpseudo = −maframe   |   Nlift = m(g ± a)
