Moving Charges & Magnetism

Grade XII — Chapter 03

1. Magnetic Force on a Moving Charge

Force on a Charge in a Magnetic Field

A charge q moving with velocity v in a magnetic field B experiences a force given by:

F = q(v × B)

Magnitude: F = qvB sinθ
where θ = angle between v and B

Key properties of the magnetic force:

  • The force is always perpendicular to both the velocity and the magnetic field.
  • The force does no work on the charge (since F ⊥ v), so it cannot change the kinetic energy or speed of the charge — only its direction.
  • If v is parallel to B (θ = 0° or 180°), the force is zero.
  • The force is maximum when v is perpendicular to B (θ = 90°).
  • A stationary charge (v = 0) experiences no magnetic force.

Lorentz Force

When both electric and magnetic fields are present, the total electromagnetic force on a charge is called the Lorentz force:

F = q(E + v × B)

The electric force qE acts along (or against) the electric field and can do work on the charge. The magnetic force q(v × B) acts perpendicular to the velocity and does no work. Together, they determine the trajectory of a charged particle in combined fields.

2. Motion of a Charged Particle in a Magnetic Field

Circular Motion (v perpendicular to B)

When a charged particle enters a uniform magnetic field with its velocity perpendicular to the field, the magnetic force provides the centripetal force for circular motion. The particle moves in a circle of radius r in the plane perpendicular to B.

qvB = mv² / r

Radius: r = mv / (qB)
Time period: T = 2πm / (qB)
Frequency: f = qB / (2πm)
Angular frequency: ω = qB / m (cyclotron frequency)

A remarkable feature is that the time period and frequency are independent of the speed of the particle and the radius of the orbit. This property is the basis of the cyclotron.

Helical Motion (v at angle to B)

When the velocity has components both parallel and perpendicular to B, the perpendicular component causes circular motion while the parallel component remains unchanged. The particle traces a helix (spiral path) along the direction of B.

v⊥ = v sinθ,   v∥ = v cosθ

Radius of helix: r = mv sinθ / (qB)
Pitch of helix: p = v∥ × T = 2πmv cosθ / (qB)

Velocity Selector

A velocity selector uses crossed electric and magnetic fields (perpendicular to each other and to the velocity of the particle) to select particles of a specific velocity. For the particle to pass undeflected:

qE = qvB
v = E / B

Particles with this specific velocity pass straight through, while faster or slower particles are deflected.

Solved Example 1

A proton (m = 1.67 × 10−27 kg, q = 1.6 × 10−19 C) moves with a speed of 106 m/s perpendicular to a magnetic field of 0.1 T. Find the radius of its circular orbit, the time period, and the kinetic energy.

Solution:
m = 1.67 × 10−27 kg, q = 1.6 × 10−19 C, v = 106 m/s, B = 0.1 T

Radius: r = mv/(qB) = (1.67 × 10−27)(106) / [(1.6 × 10−19)(0.1)]
r = 1.67 × 10−21 / 1.6 × 10−20 = 0.104 m ≈ 10.4 cm

Time period: T = 2πm/(qB) = 2π(1.67 × 10−27) / [(1.6 × 10−19)(0.1)]
T = 10.49 × 10−27 / 1.6 × 10−20 = 6.56 × 10−7 s ≈ 0.656 μs

Kinetic energy: KE = ½mv² = ½(1.67 × 10−27)(106)²
KE = 8.35 × 10−16 J ≈ 5.22 keV
(Note: KE remains constant since the magnetic force does no work.)

3. Force on a Current-Carrying Conductor

Force on a Straight Conductor

A current-carrying conductor placed in a magnetic field experiences a force because the magnetic field exerts a force on the moving charges (drift electrons) within the conductor. For a straight conductor of length l carrying current I in a uniform field B:

F = I(l × B)

Magnitude: F = BIl sinθ
where θ = angle between the current direction and B

The direction of force is given by Fleming's left-hand rule: point the index finger along the magnetic field, the middle finger along the current, and the thumb gives the direction of the force.

Torque on a Current Loop

A rectangular current loop of N turns, each of area A, carrying current I, placed in a uniform magnetic field B, experiences a torque:

τ = NIA × B = m × B

Magnitude: τ = NIAB sinθ
where θ = angle between the normal to the loop and B,
and m = NIA is the magnetic dipole moment

The torque is maximum when the plane of the loop is parallel to the field (θ = 90°) and zero when the plane is perpendicular to the field (θ = 0°, equilibrium position).

Magnetic Dipole Moment

m = NIA
SI unit: A m² (or equivalently, J/T)

The direction of the magnetic moment is along the normal to the loop, given by the right-hand thumb rule: curl the fingers in the direction of current flow, and the thumb points in the direction of m.

4. Biot-Savart Law

Statement

The Biot-Savart law gives the magnetic field dB produced at a point P by a small current element Idl of a conductor carrying current I:

dB = (μ0 / 4π) × (Idl × r̂) / r²

Magnitude: dB = (μ0 / 4π) × (I dl sinθ) / r²

where μ0 = 4π × 10−7 T m A−1 (permeability of free space)

Magnetic Field of a Long Straight Wire

For an infinitely long straight wire carrying current I, the magnetic field at a perpendicular distance r from the wire is:

B = μ0I / (2πr)

The field lines are concentric circles around the wire. The direction is given by the right-hand thumb rule: point the thumb along the current, and the curled fingers give the direction of B.

Magnetic Field at the Centre of a Circular Loop

For a circular loop of N turns, radius R, carrying current I, the field at the centre is:

B = μ0NI / (2R)

Magnetic Field on the Axis of a Circular Loop

B = μ0NIR² / [2(R² + x²)3/2]

At centre (x = 0): B = μ0NI / (2R)
For x >> R: B ≈ μ0NIR² / (2x³) = μ0m / (2πx³)

Magnetic Field of a Solenoid

A solenoid is a long coil of wire wound in a helix. For an ideal solenoid (length >> radius), the field inside is uniform and parallel to the axis:

Inside: B = μ0nI
where n = N/L = number of turns per unit length

Outside an ideal solenoid: B = 0
At the ends: B = μ0nI / 2

5. Ampere's Circuital Law

Statement

Ampere's circuital law states that the line integral of the magnetic field B around any closed loop (Amperian loop) is equal to μ0 times the total current enclosed by that loop.

∮ B · dl = μ0 Ienc

This law is the magnetic analogue of Gauss's law in electrostatics. Like Gauss's law, it is always true but is most useful when there is sufficient symmetry to simplify the line integral.

Application: Solenoid

Consider a rectangular Amperian loop with one side inside the solenoid (length l along the axis) and the other outside. The field outside is essentially zero for an ideal solenoid, so:

Bl = μ0(nl)I
B = μ0nI

Application: Toroid

A toroid is a solenoid bent into a closed ring shape. Using a circular Amperian loop of radius r passing through the interior of the toroid (where r is the distance from the centre of the toroid):

Inside the toroid: B(2πr) = μ0NI
B = μ0NI / (2πr)

Outside the toroid: B = 0
In the central cavity: B = 0

Unlike a solenoid, the field inside a toroid varies with the radius (it is not perfectly uniform) but is approximately uniform if the cross-section is small compared to the toroid radius.

Solved Example 2

A solenoid of length 50 cm has 500 turns and carries a current of 2 A. Find the magnetic field inside the solenoid. If this solenoid is bent into a toroid of mean radius 10 cm, find the field inside the toroid.

Solution:
Solenoid:
n = N/L = 500/0.50 = 1000 turns/m, I = 2 A
B = μ0nI = (4π × 10−7)(1000)(2)
B = 8π × 10−4 = 2.51 × 10−3 T ≈ 25.1 Gauss

Toroid:
N = 500, I = 2 A, r = 0.10 m
B = μ0NI / (2πr) = (4π × 10−7)(500)(2) / (2π × 0.10)
B = (4π × 10−7 × 1000) / (0.2π)
B = 4000 × 10−7 / 0.2 = 2.0 × 10−3 T = 20 Gauss

6. Force Between Parallel Current-Carrying Conductors

Derivation

Consider two long, straight, parallel conductors separated by a distance d, carrying currents I1 and I2 respectively. The magnetic field produced by conductor 1 at the location of conductor 2 is B1 = μ0I1/(2πd). This field exerts a force on conductor 2.

Force per unit length: F/l = μ0I1I2 / (2πd)
  • Parallel currents (same direction): The conductors attract each other.
  • Anti-parallel currents (opposite directions): The conductors repel each other.

Definition of the Ampere

The SI unit of current, the ampere, is defined using this force: One ampere is that steady current which, when flowing in each of two infinitely long, straight, parallel conductors placed 1 metre apart in vacuum, produces a force of 2 × 10−7 N per metre of length between them.

F/l = μ0I1I2 / (2πd)
For I1 = I2 = 1 A, d = 1 m:
F/l = (4π × 10−7)(1)(1) / (2π × 1) = 2 × 10−7 N/m

7. Moving Coil Galvanometer

Principle and Working

A moving coil galvanometer works on the principle that a current-carrying coil placed in a magnetic field experiences a torque. The coil is suspended between the poles of a strong permanent magnet (radial field design ensures the field is always parallel to the plane of the coil). A restoring torque is provided by a spring.

At equilibrium, the magnetic torque equals the restoring torque:

NIAB = kφ

where N = number of turns, A = area of coil, B = magnetic field,
k = torsional constant of spring, φ = deflection angle

φ = (NAB/k) × I   ⇒   φ ∝ I

Current Sensitivity

Current sensitivity = φ/I = NAB/k   (deflection per unit current)

Voltage Sensitivity

Voltage sensitivity = φ/V = NAB/(kRG)   (where RG = galvanometer resistance)

Conversion to Ammeter

To convert a galvanometer into an ammeter, a low resistance (shunt) S is connected in parallel with the galvanometer:

S = IgRG / (I − Ig)

where Ig = full-scale deflection current of galvanometer,
I = desired maximum current of ammeter

Conversion to Voltmeter

To convert a galvanometer into a voltmeter, a high resistance R is connected in series with the galvanometer:

R = V/Ig − RG

where V = desired maximum voltage reading
Solved Example 3

A galvanometer has a resistance of 50 Ω and gives full-scale deflection for a current of 1 mA. How can it be converted into (a) an ammeter of range 0–5 A, and (b) a voltmeter of range 0–10 V?

Solution:
RG = 50 Ω, Ig = 1 mA = 10−3 A

(a) Ammeter (range 0–5 A):
Shunt S = IgRG / (I − Ig)
S = (10−3)(50) / (5 − 10−3)
S = 0.05 / 4.999 ≈ 0.01 Ω
Connect S = 0.01 Ω in parallel with the galvanometer.

(b) Voltmeter (range 0–10 V):
R = V/Ig − RG
R = 10 / (10−3) − 50
R = 10000 − 50 = 9950 Ω
Connect R = 9950 Ω in series with the galvanometer.
