1. Biot-Savart Law

Fig 13.1

Fundamental Statement

The Biot-Savart law is the fundamental law of magnetostatics that gives the magnetic field produced by an infinitesimal current element at any point in space. It is the magnetic analogue of Coulomb's law in electrostatics. If a wire carries a steady current I, and dl is a tiny vector element of the wire pointing in the direction of current flow, then the magnetic field dB at a point P located at position vector r from the element is:

dB = (μ0 / 4π) × I(dl × r̂) / r²

In terms of the full position vector:
dB = (μ0 / 4π) × I(dl × r) / r³

Magnitude form:
dB = (μ0 / 4π) × (I dl sinθ) / r²

where:
μ0 = 4π × 10−7 T·m·A−1 (permeability of free space)
θ = angle between dl and position vector r
r = distance from current element to point P

Key Characteristics

  • The magnetic field dB is perpendicular to both the current element dl and the position vector r, as dictated by the cross product.
  • The field is directly proportional to the current I and the element length dl, and inversely proportional to the square of the distance r.
  • The field depends on the angle θ between the current direction and the line joining the element to the observation point. It is maximum when θ = 90° and zero when θ = 0° or 180°.
  • For a medium with relative permeability μr, replace μ0 with μ0μr.
  • For a complete conductor, the total field is the vector integral: B = (μ0I / 4π) ∫ (dl × r̂) / r².

Direction: Right-Hand Rule

Point the fingers of your right hand in the direction of current flow (dl), and curl them towards the position vector r. Your thumb points in the direction of dB. Equivalently, for a long straight wire, if you grip the wire with your right hand so that the thumb points along the current, your curled fingers give the direction of the circular magnetic field lines.

Comparison with Coulomb's Law

  • Both are inverse-square laws in terms of distance dependence.
  • Coulomb's law gives a scalar (charge) source, while Biot-Savart involves a vector (current element) source.
  • The electric field due to a charge is radial; the magnetic field due to a current element is perpendicular to both dl and r.
  • Both obey the superposition principle — the total field is the vector sum of individual contributions.

2. Magnetic Field Due to Straight Conductors

Fig 13.2

Infinite Straight Wire

Integrating the Biot-Savart law over an infinitely long straight wire carrying current I, we obtain the magnetic field at a perpendicular distance r from the wire. Consider an element dx at distance x from the foot of the perpendicular. The angle between dx and the position vector is (π − θ). After carrying out the integration from −∞ to +∞:

B = (μ0I) / (2πr)

The magnetic field lines form concentric circles around the wire. The magnitude decreases inversely with distance. All points equidistant from the wire experience the same field magnitude. The direction is given by the right-hand thumb rule: point your thumb along the current direction, and your fingers curl in the direction of the field.

Finite Straight Conductor

For a wire of finite length, the magnetic field at perpendicular distance r depends on the angles subtended by the wire endpoints at the observation point. If α and β are the angles between the perpendicular from the point to the wire and the lines joining the observation point to the two ends of the wire:

B = (μ0I) / (4πr) × (sinα + sinβ)

Special cases that appear frequently in JEE problems:

  • Infinite wire: α = β = 90°, so B = μ0I / (2πr).
  • Semi-infinite wire (one end at perpendicular foot): α = 90°, β = 0°, so B = μ0I / (4πr).
  • At the midpoint of a finite wire of length L: sinα = sinβ = L / (2√(r² + L²/4)), which gives B = (μ0IL) / (2πr√(4r² + L²)).

Superposition for Complex Geometries

For wires bent into shapes (squares, polygons, combinations of straight and curved segments), the total magnetic field at any point is the vector sum of contributions from each straight segment and each curved segment. Each segment is analysed individually using the appropriate formula, and the results are added vectorially. Symmetry arguments often simplify the calculation considerably.

Example 1 — Magnetic Field at the Centre of a Square Loop

A square loop of side a carries a current I. Find the magnetic field at the centre of the loop.

Solution:
The centre of the square is at perpendicular distance r = a/2 from each side.
Each side subtends equal angles at the centre: α = β = 45°.

Field due to one side:
B1 = (μ0I) / (4π × a/2) × (sin 45° + sin 45°)
B1 = (μ0I) / (2πa) × (2 × √2/2)
B1 = (μ0I√2) / (2πa)

All four sides contribute fields in the same direction (into or out of the plane), so the total field is:
Btotal = 4 × B1 = 4 × (μ0I√2) / (2πa)
Btotal = 2√2 μ0I / (πa)

3. Magnetic Field of a Circular Current Loop & Arc

Fig 13.3

Field at the Centre of a Circular Loop

A circular loop of radius R carrying current I produces a magnetic field at its centre that is directed perpendicular to the plane of the loop (determined by the right-hand rule). Using the Biot-Savart law, every element of the loop is at the same distance R from the centre, and dl is always perpendicular to r (so sinθ = 1). Integrating around the full loop:

Bcentre = (μ0I) / (2R)

For a coil of N turns:
Bcentre = (μ0NI) / (2R)

Field on the Axis of a Circular Loop

At a point P on the axis of a circular loop at distance x from the centre, the field components perpendicular to the axis cancel by symmetry (from diametrically opposite elements). Only the axial component survives:

Baxis = (μ0IR²) / [2(R² + x²)3/2]

For N turns:
Baxis = (μ0NIR²) / [2(R² + x²)3/2]

Special cases:
At centre (x = 0): B = (μ0NI) / (2R)
Far from loop (x >> R): B ≈ (μ0NIR²) / (2x³) — dipole-like fall-off

The far-field result (B ∝ 1/x³) shows that a current loop behaves like a magnetic dipole at large distances. This is analogous to how an electric dipole field falls off as 1/r³.

Magnetic Field at the Centre of a Current Arc

A curved wire segment (arc) of radius r subtending an angle α (in radians) at its centre carries current I. The field at the centre is simply the fraction α/(2π) of the full-loop field:

B = (μ0Iα) / (4πr)

Special cases:
Full circle (α = 2π): B = μ0I / (2r)
Semicircle (α = π): B = μ0I / (4r)
Quarter circle (α = π/2): B = μ0I / (8r)

JEE Tip: Straight segments of a wire that pass through the centre of the arc contribute zero field at the centre (since r is parallel to dl, making dl × r = 0). Only the curved portions contribute.

Combinations of Arcs and Straight Segments

Many JEE problems involve wires bent into shapes with both straight and curved portions (e.g., a semicircle connected by straight radial segments). The strategy is:

  • Identify each segment: straight radial parts contribute zero field at the centre.
  • For each arc segment, use B = μ0Iα / (4πr) and determine the direction.
  • Add the fields vectorially.

4. Solenoid & Toroid

Fig 13.4

Magnetic Field Inside a Long Solenoid

A solenoid is a cylindrical coil made by winding a large number of turns of insulated wire. When carrying current, it produces a nearly uniform magnetic field inside and a negligible field outside. A solenoid can be modelled as a stack of closely spaced circular loops, each contributing to the total field.

Inside (ideal solenoid, length >> radius):
B = μ0nI = (μ0NI) / L

Outside: B ≈ 0

At the ends (open solenoid):
Bend = (μ0nI) / 2 = Binside / 2

where n = N/L = number of turns per unit length,
N = total turns, L = length of solenoid
  • The field inside is uniform, parallel to the axis, and independent of the solenoid radius — it depends only on n and I.
  • Near the ends of a finite solenoid, the field is approximately half the interior value.
  • The field outside an ideal (infinite) solenoid is exactly zero. For real (finite) solenoids, it is very small.
  • A solenoid behaves like a bar magnet — one end acts as a magnetic north pole and the other as south.

Detailed Axis Field for Finite Solenoid

For a finite solenoid with N turns and length L, the field at a general point on the axis can be expressed in terms of the angles subtended by the solenoid ends:

B = (μ0IN) / (2L) × (cosα − cosβ)

where α and β are angles from the observation point to each end of the solenoid.
At the midpoint of a long solenoid: α ≈ 0°, β ≈ 180°,
so B ≈ μ0IN/L = μ0nI

Magnetic Field in a Toroid

A toroid is formed by bending a solenoid into a closed ring (doughnut shape). The magnetic field is confined entirely within the toroid — there is no field in the central cavity or outside the toroid. This can be shown rigorously using Ampere's law.

Inside the toroid at radius r from the centre:
B = (μ0NI) / (2πr)

In the central cavity: B = 0
Outside the toroid: B = 0
  • The field lines form concentric circles inside the toroid.
  • Unlike a solenoid, the toroidal field varies with radius (it is stronger near the inner edge).
  • If the cross-sectional radius is much smaller than the major radius, the field is approximately uniform across the cross-section.
  • A toroid is useful in transformers and inductors because it confines the magnetic flux completely.

5. Ampere's Circuital Law

Fig 13.5

Statement

Ampere's circuital law is one of the four Maxwell's equations and is the magnetic analogue of Gauss's law in electrostatics. It states that the line integral of the magnetic field B around any closed path (Amperian loop) equals μ0 times the total current threading through the surface bounded by that path:

∮ B · dl = μ0 Ienclosed

The law is always true for steady (time-independent) currents, but is most useful as a computational tool when the current distribution has high symmetry — cylindrical, planar, or toroidal — allowing the line integral to be evaluated easily.

Choosing the Amperian Loop

The key to applying Ampere's law is selecting an Amperian loop on which B is either constant and parallel to dl, or perpendicular to dl (contributing zero). Common choices:

  • Straight wire: Circular loop centred on the wire — B is constant and tangent to the circle.
  • Solenoid: Rectangular loop with one side inside and one outside — only the inside side contributes.
  • Toroid: Circular loop concentric with the toroid axis.
  • Infinite conducting sheet: Rectangular loop straddling the sheet.

Application: Long Straight Wire

For a long straight wire carrying current I, choose a circular Amperian loop of radius r centred on the wire. By cylindrical symmetry, B is constant in magnitude and tangent to the loop at every point:

∮ B · dl = B × 2πr = μ0I
B = (μ0I) / (2πr)

Application: Inside a Thick Wire

For a cylindrical conductor of radius R carrying uniformly distributed current I, at a point inside (r < R), only the fraction of current enclosed by the Amperian loop contributes:

Ienclosed = I × (r² / R²)
B × 2πr = μ0I(r² / R²)
B = (μ0Ir) / (2πR²)    (for r < R)

At the surface (r = R): B = μ0I / (2πR)
Outside (r > R): B = μ0I / (2πr)

The field increases linearly from zero at the centre to a maximum at the surface, then decreases as 1/r outside — a favourite JEE graph-based question.

Application: Solenoid (via Ampere's Law)

Rectangular Amperian loop (length l inside, one side outside):
B × l + 0 + 0 + 0 = μ0(nl)I
B = μ0nI

Application: Toroid (via Ampere's Law)

Circular Amperian loop at radius r inside toroid:
B × 2πr = μ0NI
B = (μ0NI) / (2πr)

6. Lorentz Force on Moving Charges

Fig 13.6

The Complete Lorentz Force

When a charged particle moves through a region containing both electric and magnetic fields, the total electromagnetic force on it is called the Lorentz force:

F = q(E + v × B)

Electric force: FE = qE (along E for +q)
Magnetic force: FB = q(v × B)
Magnitude of magnetic force: FB = qvB sinθ
where θ = angle between v and B

Properties of the Magnetic Force

  • The magnetic force is always perpendicular to the velocity, so it acts as a centripetal-type force that changes the direction of motion without changing the speed.
  • Since FB ⊥ v, the magnetic force does no work: W = ∫ F · ds = 0. The kinetic energy remains constant.
  • The force is maximum when v ⊥ B (θ = 90°): Fmax = qvB.
  • The force is zero when v ∥ B (θ = 0° or 180°).
  • A stationary charge (v = 0) experiences no magnetic force. Magnetism is inherently a phenomenon of moving charges.
  • For a positive charge, the force direction is given by the right-hand rule (point fingers along v, curl towards B, thumb gives F). For a negative charge, the force is reversed.

Velocity Selector (Crossed E and B Fields)

When uniform electric and magnetic fields are arranged perpendicular to each other and to the initial velocity of a charged particle, the electric and magnetic forces can balance at a specific velocity:

At balance: qE = qvB
Selected velocity: v = E / B

Only particles moving with exactly this velocity pass through undeflected. Slower particles are deflected toward the electric field direction, and faster particles are deflected by the dominant magnetic force. This arrangement is called a velocity selector and is a crucial component of mass spectrometers and Thomson's charge-to-mass ratio experiment.

Cyclotron

The cyclotron is a charged-particle accelerator that exploits the fact that the time period of circular motion in a magnetic field is independent of speed. A charged particle spirals outward in a magnetic field, gaining energy each time it crosses the gap between two D-shaped electrodes (dees) where an alternating electric field accelerates it.

Cyclotron frequency: fc = qB / (2πm)
Maximum kinetic energy: KEmax = q²B²R² / (2m)
where R = radius of the dees

Limitation: At relativistic speeds, the mass increases and the cyclotron frequency decreases, causing the particle to fall out of sync with the oscillating electric field. This limits the cyclotron to non-relativistic particles.

7. Motion of Charged Particles in a Magnetic Field

Fig 13.7

Case 1: v Parallel to B (Straight Line)

When the velocity is parallel (or antiparallel) to the magnetic field, θ = 0° (or 180°), so sinθ = 0 and the magnetic force is zero. The particle continues in a straight line with constant velocity, completely unaffected by the field.

F = qvB sin 0° = 0
Trajectory: straight line along B

Case 2: v Perpendicular to B (Circular Motion)

When the velocity is perpendicular to the magnetic field, the magnetic force provides the centripetal acceleration for uniform circular motion. The particle moves in a circle in the plane perpendicular to B:

Force balance: mv²/r = qvB

Radius of circular orbit: r = mv / (qB) = p / (qB)
Time period: T = 2πm / (qB)
Frequency: f = qB / (2πm)
Angular frequency (cyclotron frequency): ω = qB / m
  • Key insight: The time period T and frequency f are independent of speed and radius. All particles with the same q/m ratio revolve with the same period, regardless of their energy. This is the operating principle of the cyclotron.
  • The radius r is proportional to momentum (mv). Faster particles trace larger circles but take the same time to complete each revolution.
  • A positive charge circles anticlockwise when B points out of the page; a negative charge circles clockwise.
  • Kinetic energy is conserved: the magnetic force, being perpendicular to velocity, does no work.

Case 3: v at Angle to B (Helical Motion)

When the velocity makes an arbitrary angle with the magnetic field, resolve it into parallel and perpendicular components. The parallel component is unaffected (no force), and the perpendicular component undergoes circular motion. The combination produces a helical (corkscrew) trajectory:

Case 3: v at Angle θ to B (Helical Motion)

When the velocity makes an arbitrary angle θ with the magnetic field, resolve it into parallel and perpendicular components. The parallel component is unaffected (no force), and the perpendicular component undergoes circular motion. The combination produces a helical (corkscrew) trajectory:

v∥ = v cosθ   (constant, along B)
v⊥ = v sinθ   (produces circular motion)

Radius of helix: r = m(v sinθ) / (qB)
Period: T = 2πm / (qB)   (independent of θ)
Pitch (advance per revolution):
p = v∥ × T = (2πmv cosθ) / (qB)
  • The axis of the helix is parallel to B.
  • The radius depends only on the perpendicular component of velocity.
  • The pitch depends on the parallel component — as θ → 0°, the helix stretches into a straight line; as θ → 90°, it collapses into a circle.

Motion in a Non-Uniform Magnetic Field (Magnetic Bottle)

In a non-uniform magnetic field that is stronger at the ends and weaker in the middle (a magnetic bottle or mirror configuration), charged particles undergoing helical motion are reflected back and forth between the high-field regions. This is the principle behind plasma confinement and the trapping of charged particles in Earth's Van Allen radiation belts.

Example 2 — Helical Motion of an Electron

An electron (m = 9.11 × 10−31 kg, q = 1.6 × 10−19 C) enters a uniform magnetic field of 0.2 T at an angle of 30° to the field direction with a speed of 107 m/s. Find the radius of the helix, the pitch, and the time period.

Solution:

Perpendicular component of velocity:
v⊥ = v sin 30° = 107 × 0.5 = 5 × 106 m/s

Radius of helix:
r = mv⊥ / (qB)
r = (9.11 × 10−31 × 5 × 106) / (1.6 × 10−19 × 0.2)
r = 4.555 × 10−24 / 3.2 × 10−20
r = 1.42 × 10−4 m = 0.142 mm

Time period:
T = 2πm / (qB) = (2π × 9.11 × 10−31) / (1.6 × 10−19 × 0.2)
T = 1.79 × 10−10 s = 0.179 ns

Pitch:
v∥ = v cos 30° = 107 × (√3/2) = 8.66 × 106 m/s
p = v∥ × T = 8.66 × 106 × 1.79 × 10−10
p = 1.55 × 10−3 m = 1.55 mm

8. Force on Current-Carrying Conductors

Fig 13.8

Force on a Straight Conductor

A current-carrying conductor placed in an external magnetic field experiences a force. This is because the magnetic field exerts a force on each moving charge (drift electron) inside the conductor, and these microscopic forces add up to a macroscopic force on the wire. For a straight conductor of length l carrying current I in a uniform field B:

F = I(l × B)

Magnitude: F = BIl sinθ
where θ = angle between conductor direction and B
  • Maximum force when conductor ⊥ field (θ = 90°): F = BIl.
  • Zero force when conductor ∥ field (θ = 0°): F = 0.
  • The direction is given by the cross product, or equivalently, by Fleming's left-hand rule: index finger along B, middle finger along current (I), thumb gives force (F).

Force on a Curved Conductor

For an arbitrarily shaped conductor in a uniform magnetic field, the total force depends only on the straight-line displacement between the two endpoints of the conductor, not on the actual path:

F = I(Leff × B)
where Leff = vector from start to end of conductor

Important corollary: For a closed current loop in a uniform magnetic field, the net force is zero (since Leff = 0). However, the loop does experience a torque.

Torque on a Current Loop

A rectangular coil of N turns, area A, carrying current I in a uniform field B experiences a torque:

τ = NIA × B = m × B

Magnitude: τ = NIAB sinθ
where θ = angle between the area normal (n̂) and B,
and m = NIA is the magnetic dipole moment.
  • Maximum torque when loop plane is parallel to B (θ = 90°): τmax = NIAB.
  • Zero torque when loop plane is perpendicular to B (θ = 0°): stable equilibrium.
  • This result holds for any planar loop shape, not just rectangles. The torque depends only on the area and the number of turns.

9. Force Between Parallel Current-Carrying Conductors

Fig 13.9

Derivation

Consider two infinitely long, straight, parallel wires separated by distance d, carrying currents I1 and I2. Wire 1 creates a magnetic field at the location of wire 2:

B1 = (μ0I1) / (2πd)

This field is perpendicular to wire 2, so it exerts the maximum force on it. The force per unit length on wire 2 is:

F / l = I2 × B1 = (μ0I1I2) / (2πd)

By Newton's third law, the force on wire 1 due to wire 2 is equal in magnitude and opposite in direction.

Nature of the Force

  • Parallel currents (same direction): The wires attract each other. The field from each wire at the other wire's location creates a force directed towards the first wire.
  • Anti-parallel currents (opposite directions): The wires repel each other.
  • Memory aid: "Like currents attract, unlike currents repel" — opposite to the rule for charges!

Definition of the Ampere

The SI unit of current, the ampere, is defined using this force: One ampere is that steady current which, when maintained in each of two infinitely long straight parallel conductors of negligible cross-section placed 1 metre apart in vacuum, produces a force of exactly 2 × 10−7 N per metre of length between them.

Verification: For I1 = I2 = 1 A, d = 1 m:
F/l = (4π × 10−7 × 1 × 1) / (2π × 1) = 2 × 10−7 N/m ✓
Example 3 — Force Between Parallel Wires

Two long parallel wires are 5 cm apart and carry currents of 10 A and 20 A in opposite directions. Find the force per unit length between them and state whether it is attractive or repulsive.

Solution:
Given: d = 5 cm = 0.05 m, I1 = 10 A, I2 = 20 A

F/l = (μ0I1I2) / (2πd)
F/l = (4π × 10−7 × 10 × 20) / (2π × 0.05)
F/l = (8π × 10−5) / (0.1π)
F/l = 8 × 10−4 N/m = 0.8 mN/m

Since the currents are in opposite directions, the force is repulsive.

10. Magnetic Dipole Moment & Potential Energy

Fig 13.10

Magnetic Dipole Moment

Any closed current loop acts as a magnetic dipole. The magnetic dipole moment is a vector quantity that characterises the strength and orientation of the dipole:

m = NIA n̂

Magnitude: m = NIA
SI unit: A·m² (ampere-metre squared) or J/T (joule per tesla)

where N = number of turns, I = current, A = area of loop,
n̂ = unit normal to loop plane (right-hand rule)

The direction of m is perpendicular to the loop plane, given by the right-hand thumb rule: curl the fingers in the direction of current flow, and the thumb points along m. The magnetic moment is independent of the shape of the loop — it depends only on the enclosed area.

Torque on a Magnetic Dipole

A magnetic dipole placed in a uniform magnetic field experiences a torque that tries to align the dipole moment with the field:

τ = m × B
Magnitude: τ = mB sinθ
where θ = angle between m and B

Maximum torque: τmax = mB (when θ = 90°)
Zero torque: when θ = 0° (stable) or 180° (unstable)

Potential Energy of a Magnetic Dipole

The potential energy of a magnetic dipole in a uniform field depends on its orientation:

U = −m · B = −mB cosθ

Stable equilibrium (θ = 0°): Umin = −mB
Unstable equilibrium (θ = 180°): Umax = +mB
At θ = 90°: U = 0

Work to rotate from θ1 to θ2:
W = mB(cosθ1 − cosθ2)
  • The dipole naturally tends toward θ = 0° (minimum energy).
  • Work done by external agent to rotate from 0° to 180° is W = 2mB.
  • If released from any angle, the dipole oscillates about the equilibrium position (like a torsional pendulum in a magnetic field).

Magnetic Moment of a Rotating Charged Body

A uniformly charged body rotating about its axis creates current loops and hence possesses a magnetic dipole moment. For a uniformly charged disc of total charge Q, radius R, rotating with angular velocity ω:

m = QωR² / 4

For a uniformly charged sphere of charge Q, mass M:
Gyromagnetic ratio: m/L = Q / (2M)
where L = angular momentum of the body

The ratio of magnetic moment to angular momentum (gyromagnetic ratio) is a fundamental quantity that appears in atomic physics. For orbital electron motion: m/L = e/(2me), which is the classical gyromagnetic ratio.

11. Moving Coil Galvanometer

Fig 13.11

Principle

A moving coil galvanometer is based on the torque experienced by a current-carrying coil in a magnetic field. It is the fundamental instrument from which ammeters and voltmeters are derived. The key design feature is the use of a radial magnetic field (achieved with a cylindrical soft-iron core between curved pole pieces), which ensures that the plane of the coil is always parallel to the field, regardless of deflection. This makes sinθ = 1 at all positions, giving a linear relationship between deflection and current.

Construction

The galvanometer consists of a rectangular coil of N turns wound on a non-magnetic metallic frame, suspended in the radial field of a strong permanent magnet by a fine phosphor-bronze strip (which also serves as a current lead and provides restoring torque). A soft-iron cylinder placed inside the coil concentrates the magnetic field lines radially.

Working and Deflection Formula

When current I flows through the coil, the magnetic torque is balanced by the restoring torque of the suspension fibre at equilibrium:

Magnetic torque: τm = NIAB
Restoring torque: τr = kφ

At equilibrium: NIAB = kφ

Deflection: φ = (NAB / k) × I

where:
N = number of turns, A = area of coil, B = magnetic field,
k = torsional spring constant of suspension, φ = deflection angle

Since φ ∝ I, the galvanometer has a linear scale.

Sensitivity

Current sensitivity: SI = φ/I = NAB/k   (rad/A)

Voltage sensitivity: SV = φ/V = NAB/(kRG)   (rad/V)
where RG = coil resistance

To increase sensitivity: use more turns (N), larger area (A), stronger magnet (B), or softer suspension (smaller k). However, increasing N also increases RG, so voltage sensitivity may not improve with more turns.

Conversion to Ammeter

An ammeter measures current and must be connected in series with the circuit. To extend the range of a galvanometer, connect a low-resistance shunt S in parallel:

S = (Ig × RG) / (I − Ig) = RG / (n − 1)

where n = I/Ig = multiplication factor,
Ig = full-scale deflection current,
I = desired full-scale ammeter reading

The shunt carries most of the current (I − Ig), protecting the galvanometer. The effective resistance of the ammeter is very low: RA = RGS / (RG + S).

Conversion to Voltmeter

A voltmeter measures potential difference and must be connected in parallel with the circuit element. Connect a high resistance R in series with the galvanometer:

R = (V / Ig) − RG

where V = desired full-scale voltage reading

The series resistance limits the current to Ig when voltage V is applied. The effective resistance of the voltmeter is very high: RV = RG + R.

Example 4 — Galvanometer Conversion

A galvanometer has resistance 100 Ω and gives full-scale deflection for 2 mA. Design (a) an ammeter of range 0–10 A, and (b) a voltmeter of range 0–50 V.

Given: RG = 100 Ω, Ig = 2 mA = 2 × 10−3 A

(a) Ammeter (0–10 A):
n = I/Ig = 10 / 0.002 = 5000
S = RG/(n − 1) = 100/4999
S ≈ 0.02 Ω
Connect 0.02 Ω shunt in parallel with galvanometer.

(b) Voltmeter (0–50 V):
R = V/Ig − RG = 50/(2 × 10−3) − 100
R = 25000 − 100 = 24,900 Ω
Connect 24.9 kΩ resistor in series with galvanometer.

12. Magnetic Materials

Fig 13.12

Magnetisation and Magnetic Intensity

When a material is placed in an external magnetic field, the atomic magnetic moments respond, and the material becomes magnetised. Two key quantities describe this:

Magnetisation: M = net magnetic moment per unit volume (A/m)
Magnetic intensity: H = B/μ0 − M
Relation: B = μ0(H + M)

Magnetic susceptibility: χm = M/H (dimensionless)
M = χmH
B = μ0(1 + χm)H = μ0μrH
Relative permeability: μr = 1 + χm

Diamagnetic Materials

Diamagnetic materials have no permanent atomic magnetic dipole moment. When placed in an external field, the orbital motion of electrons is slightly modified, inducing tiny magnetic moments that oppose the applied field (a consequence of Lenz's law at the atomic level). They are weakly repelled by magnetic fields.

Susceptibility: χm < 0 (small and negative)
Typical range: χm ≈ −10−5 to −10−6
Relative permeability: μr < 1 (slightly less than 1)
  • All materials exhibit some diamagnetism, but it is usually masked by stronger para- or ferromagnetic effects.
  • Diamagnetism is independent of temperature (unlike paramagnetism).
  • In a non-uniform field, diamagnetic materials move from stronger to weaker field regions.
  • A diamagnetic rod placed in a uniform field aligns perpendicular to the field.
  • Examples: bismuth, copper, gold, silver, water, diamond, lead, mercury.
  • Superconductors are perfect diamagnets (χm = −1, complete flux expulsion: Meissner effect).

Paramagnetic Materials

Paramagnetic materials possess permanent atomic magnetic dipole moments (due to unpaired electrons), but these are randomly oriented due to thermal agitation in the absence of an external field. When a field is applied, the dipoles tend to align with it, producing a weak net magnetisation in the field direction.

Susceptibility: χm > 0 (small and positive)
Typical range: χm ≈ 10−5 to 10−3
Relative permeability: μr > 1 (slightly greater than 1)

Curie's Law: χm = C / T
where C = Curie constant, T = absolute temperature
  • Paramagnetic materials are weakly attracted towards regions of strong field.
  • Magnetisation decreases with increasing temperature (thermal energy disrupts alignment).
  • A paramagnetic rod placed in a uniform field aligns parallel to the field.
  • Examples: aluminium, manganese, chromium, sodium, oxygen, platinum, liquid oxygen.

Ferromagnetic Materials

Ferromagnetic materials have strong permanent atomic magnetic moments that exhibit spontaneous alignment within small regions called magnetic domains (typically 0.1–1 mm in size), even without an external field. The interaction responsible is the quantum-mechanical exchange interaction. In an external field, favourably oriented domains grow at the expense of others, producing very strong magnetisation.

Susceptibility: χm >> 1 (very large and positive)
Typical range: χm ≈ 10² to 105
Relative permeability: μr >> 1
  • Hysteresis: When a ferromagnetic material is subjected to a cyclic magnetic field, the B-H curve traces a loop. The area of this hysteresis loop represents the energy dissipated per cycle per unit volume.
  • Retentivity (Remanence): The magnetisation retained when the external field is reduced to zero. High retentivity is desired for permanent magnets.
  • Coercivity: The reverse field needed to demagnetise the material completely. High coercivity means the magnet is hard to demagnetise.
  • Saturation: At sufficiently strong fields, all domains align and magnetisation reaches a maximum.
  • Curie temperature (TC): Above this temperature, thermal energy overcomes the exchange interaction, domains break up, and ferromagnetic behaviour is lost — the material becomes paramagnetic. For iron: TC ≈ 1043 K.
  • Soft ferromagnets (soft iron): Low coercivity, narrow hysteresis loop. Used in transformer cores, electromagnets.
  • Hard ferromagnets (steel, Alnico): High coercivity, wide hysteresis loop. Used in permanent magnets.
  • Examples: iron, cobalt, nickel, gadolinium, and their alloys.

Comparison Table

Diamagnetic: χm < 0, μr < 1, weakly repelled, independent of T
Paramagnetic: χm > 0 (small), μr > 1, weakly attracted, χ ∝ 1/T
Ferromagnetic: χm >> 1, μr >> 1, strongly attracted, complex T-dependence

13. Earth's Magnetism

Fig 13.13

The Geomagnetic Field

The Earth possesses a magnetic field that resembles the field of a giant magnetic dipole located near its centre, tilted at about 11.3° from the rotation axis. This field is generated by convection currents in the molten iron-nickel outer core (the geodynamo mechanism). The field extends far into space as the magnetosphere, which deflects most of the charged particles from the solar wind, protecting life on Earth.

Magnetic and Geographic Poles

The Earth's magnetic south pole is located near the geographic north pole and vice versa. This is why the north-seeking pole of a compass needle (which is a magnetic north pole) points roughly toward geographic north — it is attracted to the Earth's magnetic south pole situated there.

Surface field strength: B ≈ 25–65 μT (0.25–0.65 gauss)
Weakest near equator (~25 μT), strongest near poles (~65 μT)

Elements of Earth's Magnetic Field

The geomagnetic field at any point on Earth's surface is completely described by three quantities called the magnetic elements:

  • Declination (δ): The angle between the geographic north and the horizontal component of Earth's magnetic field (magnetic north) at a place. It varies from place to place and changes slowly with time.
  • Angle of Dip / Inclination (I or δ): The angle that the total magnetic field makes with the horizontal plane. At the magnetic equator, dip = 0° (field is horizontal). At the magnetic poles, dip = 90° (field is vertical).
  • Horizontal Component (BH): The component of the total field in the horizontal plane.
BH = B cos I   (horizontal component)
BV = B sin I   (vertical component)
B = √(BH² + BV²)   (total field)
tan I = BV / BH

Neutral Points

When a bar magnet is placed on a table, the resultant of the magnet's field and the horizontal component of Earth's field creates neutral points where the net field is zero. For a magnet placed with its axis along the magnetic meridian:

  • Axial position (N-S axis): Neutral points on the equatorial line (perpendicular bisector). Here, the magnet's equatorial field equals BH and is opposite to it.
  • Equatorial position: Neutral points on the axial line. Here, the magnet's axial field equals BH and is opposite to it.

Applications and Significance

  • Navigation: Magnetic compasses utilise the horizontal component BH for directional guidance. The declination must be accounted for in precise navigation.
  • Magnetosphere: The Earth's magnetic field creates a protective shield (magnetosphere) that deflects solar wind particles and cosmic rays.
  • Van Allen Belts: Charged particles from the solar wind are trapped in helical paths along Earth's field lines, forming radiation belts.
  • Biological navigation: Many migratory birds, sea turtles, and other animals sense the geomagnetic field for orientation and long-distance navigation.
  • Palaeomagnetism: Rocks record the direction of Earth's field at the time of their formation, providing evidence for plate tectonics and magnetic field reversals.
