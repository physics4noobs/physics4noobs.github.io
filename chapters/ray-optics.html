1. Reflection of Light & Fermat's Principle

Fig 17.1

Laws of Reflection

When a ray of light strikes a smooth surface, it bounces back into the same medium. This phenomenon is called reflection. The two fundamental laws of reflection are:

  • The incident ray, the reflected ray, and the normal to the reflecting surface at the point of incidence all lie in the same plane.
  • The angle of incidence equals the angle of reflection: i = r.

Fermat's Principle: Light travels between two points along the path that requires the minimum time. This principle underlies both the laws of reflection and refraction.

These laws hold for all types of reflecting surfaces — plane, concave, or convex. At each point of a curved surface, the normal is drawn perpendicular to the tangent at that point.

$$i = r$$ $$\text{Mirror rotation: if mirror rotates by } \theta, \text{ reflected ray rotates by } 2\theta$$

Real and Virtual Images

A real image is formed when light rays actually pass through the image point — it can be projected on a screen. A virtual image is formed where reflected (or refracted) rays only appear to diverge from — it cannot be projected on a screen.

Plane Mirrors

A plane mirror forms a virtual, erect, laterally inverted image of the same size as the object. Key properties:

  • The image is formed as far behind the mirror as the object is in front: image distance = object distance.
  • The image is laterally inverted (left-right reversal).
  • Magnification m = +1 (same size, erect).
  • If the mirror rotates by angle θ, the reflected ray rotates by 2θ.
  • Minimum length of mirror to see full image of a person = half the person's height. The top edge of the mirror must be at the level halfway between the eyes and the top of the head.
  • Two plane mirrors inclined at angle θ produce n images where n = (360°/θ) − 1 (when 360/θ is even).
Example 1 — Plane Mirror and Person’s Full Image

A plane mirror of length L/2 is long enough for a person of height L to view their entire reflected image. Where must the top edge of the mirror be positioned?

Solution:
Let the person have eyes at height heye from the floor, top of head at height L, and feet at the floor. For the person to see their full image, the mirror must reflect rays from both the top of the head and the feet to the eyes.

The top edge of the mirror must be at the level halfway between the eyes and the top of the head:
Top edge height = heye + (L − heye)/2 = (L + heye)/2

The mirror length needed = (L − 0)/2 = L/2, confirming that half the person's height is sufficient regardless of their distance from the mirror.

2. Sign Convention & Spherical Mirrors

Fig 17.2

New Cartesian Sign Convention

All distances for spherical mirrors are measured from the pole (P) of the mirror along the principal axis. The convention is:

  • Distances measured in the direction of incident light (towards the mirror) are taken as negative.
  • Distances measured against the direction of incident light (behind the mirror) are taken as positive.
  • Heights above the principal axis are positive; heights below are negative.

With this convention: for a concave mirror, f and R are negative; for a convex mirror, f and R are positive. Object distance u is always negative for a real object (object is in front of the mirror, in the direction of incident light).

Key Terms for Spherical Mirrors

  • Centre of curvature (C): The centre of the sphere of which the mirror is a part.
  • Radius of curvature (R): The radius of that sphere.
  • Pole (P): The geometric centre of the mirror surface.
  • Principal axis: The line joining P and C.
  • Focus (F): The point where paraxial rays parallel to the principal axis converge (concave) or appear to diverge from (convex) after reflection.
$$f = \frac{R}{2}$$ $$\text{Concave: } f < 0, R < 0; \quad \text{Convex: } f > 0, R > 0$$ $$\text{Plane mirror: } f = \infty, \quad v = -u$$

Concave vs Convex Mirrors

A concave mirror is a converging mirror. It can form real or virtual images depending on the object position. Used in torches, headlights, shaving mirrors, solar furnaces, and satellite dishes.

A convex mirror is a diverging mirror. It always forms virtual, erect, and diminished images regardless of object position. Used as rear-view mirrors in vehicles because it provides a wider field of view.

3. Mirror Formula & Magnification

Fig 17.3

Mirror Formula

The relationship between object distance (u), image distance (v), and focal length (f) for a spherical mirror:

$$\frac{1}{v} + \frac{1}{u} = \frac{1}{f}$$ $$v = \text{image distance}, \quad u = \text{object distance}, \quad f = \frac{R}{2}$$

Magnification

$$m = \frac{h_i}{h_o} = -\frac{v}{u}$$ $$m = \frac{f - v}{f} = \frac{f}{f - u}$$ $$m > 0: \text{ erect}, \quad m < 0: \text{ inverted}$$ $$|m| > 1: \text{ magnified}, \quad |m| < 1: \text{ diminished}$$

Image Positions for Concave Mirror

  • Object at infinity → image at F, real, point-sized
  • Object beyond C → image between F and C, real, inverted, diminished
  • Object at C → image at C, real, inverted, same size
  • Object between C and F → image beyond C, real, inverted, enlarged
  • Object at F → image at infinity
  • Object between F and P → image behind mirror, virtual, erect, enlarged

For a convex mirror, the image is always virtual, erect, and diminished, located between P and F behind the mirror.

Example 2 — Concave Mirror Image Position

An object is placed 30 cm in front of a concave mirror of focal length 20 cm. Find the position, nature, and magnification of the image.

Solution:
Using sign convention: u = −30 cm, f = −20 cm

Mirror formula: 1/v + 1/u = 1/f
1/v + 1/(−30) = 1/(−20)
1/v = −1/20 + 1/30 = (−3 + 2)/60 = −1/60
v = −60 cm

The image is 60 cm in front of the mirror (same side as the object), so it is real.

Magnification: m = −v/u = −(−60)/(−30) = −2
The image is real, inverted, and magnified (2×).

4. Ray Diagrams for Spherical Mirrors

Fig 17.4

Three Key Rays for Mirror Diagrams

Any two of the following three standard rays are sufficient to locate an image in a spherical mirror:

  • Ray 1: A ray parallel to the principal axis reflects through the focus F (concave) or appears to come from F (convex).
  • Ray 2: A ray passing through (or directed towards) the focus F reflects parallel to the principal axis.
  • Ray 3: A ray directed towards the centre of curvature C strikes the mirror normally and reflects back along the same path.

The intersection of any two reflected rays gives the position of the real image. If the reflected rays diverge, they are extended backwards to find the virtual image behind the mirror.

Two Inclined Plane Mirrors

When two plane mirrors are inclined at angle θ to each other, a ray undergoes multiple reflections. The total deviation of the emergent ray depends on the geometry. For two reflections (one from each mirror), the net deviation is:

$$\text{Net deviation} = 180Â° - 2\theta$$ $$\text{Number of images} = \frac{360Â°}{\theta} - 1$$

This is why two mirrors at 90° produce 3 images, at 60° produce 5 images, and at 45° produce 7 images.

5. Refraction at Plane Surfaces

Fig 17.5

Snell's Law

When light passes from one transparent medium to another, it changes direction at the interface. This bending of light is called refraction. The refractive index of a medium is defined as:

$$\mu = \frac{c}{v}$$ $$n_1 \sin\theta_1 = n_2 \sin\theta_2$$ $${}^1n_2 = \frac{n_2}{n_1} = \frac{\sin\theta_1}{\sin\theta_2}$$

When light goes from a rarer medium (lower n) to a denser medium (higher n), it bends towards the normal (θ2 < θ1). When it goes from denser to rarer, it bends away from the normal (θ2 > θ1).

Real and Apparent Depth

When an object is submerged in a denser medium and viewed from a rarer medium (e.g., looking at a coin in water from air), the object appears closer to the surface than it actually is.

$$\text{Apparent depth} = \frac{\text{Real depth}}{n}$$ $$\text{Apparent shift} = \text{Real depth} \times \left(1 - \frac{1}{n}\right)$$ $$\text{Total shift} = \sum t_i\left(1 - \frac{1}{n_i}\right)$$
Example 3 — Snell’s Law Through Multiple Media

A layer of ice (μ = 1.33) lies on a glass plate (μ = 1.5). A ray of light makes an angle of incidence of 60° on the surface of ice. Find the angle between this ray and the normal inside the glass.

Solution:
Apply Snell's Law across the full path. At normal incidence, by Snell's Law applied consecutively:

nair sin 60° = nice sin r1 = nglass sin r2

At the air-ice interface: 1 × sin 60° = 1.33 × sin r1
sin r1 = sin 60° / 1.33 = 0.866 / 1.33 ≈ 0.651   → r1 ≈ 40.6°

At the ice-glass interface: nice sin r1 = nglass sin r2
sin r2 = (1.33 × 0.651) / 1.5 = 0.866 / 1.5 ≈ 0.577   → r2 ≈ 35.3°

Note: the intermediate medium (ice) drops out. Effectively: sin 60° / sin r2 = nglass/nair = 1.5, giving sin r2 = sin 60° / 1.5 = 0.577, so r2 ≈ 35.3°.

6. Total Internal Reflection

Fig 17.6

Critical Angle

When light travels from a denser medium to a rarer medium and the angle of incidence exceeds the critical angle θc, the light is completely reflected back into the denser medium. This is called total internal reflection (TIR).

The critical angle is the angle of incidence for which the refraction angle equals 90°.

$$\sin\theta_c = \frac{n_{\text{rarer}}}{n_{\text{denser}}}$$ $$\sin\theta_c = \frac{1}{n} \quad \text{(medium to air)}$$ $$\text{TIR occurs when: } i > \theta_c$$

Conditions for TIR

  • Light must travel from a denser medium to a rarer medium (n1 > n2).
  • The angle of incidence must be greater than the critical angle (θi > θc).

Applications of Total Internal Reflection

Optical Fibre: A thin glass or plastic core surrounded by cladding of lower refractive index. Light undergoes repeated TIR as it propagates along the fibre with negligible energy loss. Used in telecommunications and medical endoscopy.

Mirage: On hot days, air near the ground is hotter and less dense (lower n) than layers above. Light travelling downward is progressively refracted until TIR occurs, creating an inverted image that looks like a pool of water.

Diamond Brilliance: Diamond has n ≈ 2.42, giving θc ≈ 24.4°. Light entering a well-cut diamond undergoes multiple TIR before emerging, producing its characteristic brilliance.

Totally Reflecting Prisms: Right-angled isosceles prisms (45°-90°-45°) of glass (n = 1.5, θc ≈ 42°) are used in periscopes and binoculars for nearly 100% reflection efficiency.

Example 4 — Radius of Bright Circle in TIR

A point source S is placed at the bottom of a tank containing a liquid of refractive index μ up to depth h. Show that the radius of the bright circle visible from above is r = h / √(μ² − 1).

Solution:
Light rays from S that strike the liquid-air interface beyond the critical angle undergo TIR and cannot escape. Only rays within the critical angle cone emerge.

From the geometry: tan θc = r / h

Since sin θc = 1/μ, we have cos θc = √(1 − 1/μ²) = √(μ² − 1) / μ

tan θc = sin θc / cos θc = (1/μ) / (√(μ² − 1) / μ) = 1 / √(μ² − 1)

Therefore: r = h × tan θc = h / √(μ² − 1)

7. Refraction Through a Glass Slab

Fig 17.7

Key Properties

When a ray of light passes through a parallel-sided glass slab, it refracts at both surfaces. Since the surfaces are parallel, the angle of refraction at the first surface equals the angle of incidence at the second surface (by geometry), and the emergent ray is parallel to the incident ray. There is no angular deviation, only a lateral displacement.

$$e = i$$ $$d = t \times \frac{\sin(i - r)}{\cos r}$$ $$\text{For small angles: } d \approx t(i - r) = ti\left(1 - \frac{1}{n}\right)$$

Lateral displacement increases with angle of incidence, slab thickness, and refractive index. At normal incidence (i = 0), there is no lateral shift.

Effect on Converging Beams

When a glass slab is inserted in the path of a converging beam, it shifts the point of convergence away from the lens by a distance:

$$\text{Shift} = t\left(1 - \frac{1}{n}\right)$$
Example 5 — Lateral Displacement Through a Glass Plate

A narrow beam of light strikes a glass plate (μ = 1.60) at an angle of 53° to the normal. If the plate is 20 mm thick, find the lateral displacement of the beam.

Solution:
At the first surface, using Snell's Law:
sin 53° = 1.60 × sin r
sin r = 0.800 / 1.60 = 0.500   → r = 30°

Lateral displacement:
d = t × sin(i − r) / cos r
d = 20 × sin(53° − 30°) / cos 30°
d = 20 × sin 23° / cos 30°
d = 20 × 0.3907 / 0.8660
d ≈ 9.02 mm

8. Refraction Through a Prism

Fig 17.8

Deviation by a Prism

A prism is a transparent medium bounded by two plane refracting surfaces at an angle A (the refracting angle or apex angle). When a ray of light enters a prism, it refracts at the first surface, travels through the prism, and refracts again at the second surface. The angle between the incident ray and the emergent ray is called the angle of deviation (δ).

$$r_1 + r_2 = A$$ $$\delta = (i_1 + i_2) - A$$

Minimum Deviation

As i1 increases from a small value, the deviation first decreases, reaches a minimum δm, and then increases. At minimum deviation, the ray passes symmetrically through the prism:

  • i1 = i2 = i (angle of incidence = angle of emergence)
  • r1 = r2 = A/2 (the refracted ray inside the prism is parallel to the base)
$$\delta_m = 2i - A \quad \Rightarrow \quad i = \frac{A + \delta_m}{2}$$ $$n = \frac{\sin\left(\frac{A + \delta_m}{2}\right)}{\sin\left(\frac{A}{2}\right)}$$ $$\delta = (n - 1)A \quad \text{(thin prism)}$$

The same value of deviation is possible for two different angles of incidence (one less than, one greater than the angle at minimum deviation). At grazing incidence (i = 90°), the refraction angle at the first surface equals the critical angle.

9. Dispersion Through a Prism

Fig 17.9

Why Dispersion Occurs

The refractive index of a material depends on the wavelength of light. For most optical materials, μ decreases with increasing wavelength: μviolet > μyellow > μred. As a result, violet light is deviated more than red light by a prism. This splitting of white light into its constituent colours is called dispersion.

$$\delta_V = (\mu_V - 1)A$$ $$\delta_R = (\mu_R - 1)A$$ $$\phi = \delta_V - \delta_R = (\mu_V - \mu_R)A$$ $$\delta_Y = (\mu_Y - 1)A$$ $$\omega = \frac{\phi}{\delta_Y} = \frac{\mu_V - \mu_R}{\mu_Y - 1}$$

Rainbow Formation

Rainbows are formed by the dispersion and total internal reflection of sunlight in water droplets. A primary rainbow (one internal reflection per droplet) shows colours from violet (inner) to red (outer) at about 42° from the anti-solar point. A secondary rainbow (two internal reflections) appears at about 51° with reversed colour order and is fainter.

10. Apparent Shift & Real vs Apparent Depth

Fig 17.10

Object in a Denser Medium

When an object is located in a denser medium and viewed from a rarer medium (e.g., a fish at the bottom of a pool, viewed from air above), the object appears closer to the surface than it actually is. This upward shift is the apparent shift.

$$\text{Apparent depth} = \frac{\text{Real depth}}{n}$$ $$\text{Apparent shift} = \text{Real depth} \times \left(1 - \frac{1}{n}\right)$$ $$\text{Total shift} = \sum t_i\left(1 - \frac{1}{\mu_i}\right)$$ $$\text{Focus shift} = t\left(1 - \frac{1}{n}\right)$$

Observer in a Denser Medium

If the observer is also in a medium of refractive index μ0 and the object is in a medium of index μ, the apparent depth is:

$$\text{Apparent depth} = \text{real depth} \times \frac{\mu_0}{\mu}$$ $$\text{Apparent shift} = t\left(1 - \frac{\mu_0}{\mu}\right)$$
Example 6 — Apparent Depth Through Multiple Layers

A layer of ether (μ = 1.36) 2 cm thick floats on water (μ = 4/3) 4 cm deep. What is the apparent distance from the ether surface to the bottom of the water layer when viewed from directly above?

Solution:
Treat each layer separately. For normal incidence, apparent depth of each layer as viewed from air above:

Apparent depth of ether layer = 2 / 1.36 ≈ 1.47 cm
Apparent depth of water layer = 4 / (4/3) = 4 × 3/4 = 3.00 cm

Total apparent depth from ether surface to bottom = 1.47 + 3.00 = 4.47 cm

(The actual total depth is 6 cm, but it appears only 4.47 cm deep due to refraction in the two layers.)

11. Refraction at Spherical Surfaces

Fig 17.11

Formula for Refraction at a Single Spherical Surface

When light travels from a medium of refractive index μ1 to a medium of refractive index μ2 through a spherical interface of radius of curvature R, the relation between object distance u and image distance v is:

$$\frac{\mu_2}{v} - \frac{\mu_1}{u} = \frac{\mu_2 - \mu_1}{R}$$ $$\text{Plane surface: } \frac{\mu_2}{v} = \frac{\mu_1}{u}$$

Application: Glass Sphere

For a glass sphere of radius R and refractive index n, refraction occurs at two spherical surfaces. The second refraction is applied using the image from the first refraction as the new object. This sequential application is a common exam problem type.

12. Lenses — Convex and Concave

Fig 17.12

Lens Maker's Equation

The lens maker's equation relates the focal length of a thin lens to the refractive index of the lens material and the radii of curvature of its two surfaces:

$$\frac{1}{f} = \left(\frac{\mu}{\mu_0} - 1\right)\left(\frac{1}{R_1} - \frac{1}{R_2}\right)$$ $$\text{In air: } \frac{1}{f} = (n - 1)\left(\frac{1}{R_1} - \frac{1}{R_2}\right)$$

Convex (Converging) Lens

A convex lens has surfaces that bulge outward. It has a positive focal length and converges parallel rays to the focus.

  • Object beyond 2F → image between F and 2F, real, inverted, diminished
  • Object at 2F → image at 2F, real, inverted, same size
  • Object between 2F and F → image beyond 2F, real, inverted, enlarged
  • Object at F → image at infinity
  • Object between F and lens → image on same side as object, virtual, erect, enlarged

Concave (Diverging) Lens

A concave lens has surfaces that curve inward. It has a negative focal length. For any real object, it always forms a virtual, erect, and diminished image on the same side as the object. It cannot form a real image of a real object. Used as corrective lenses for myopia (short-sightedness).

Power of a Lens

$$P = \frac{1}{f} \quad \text{(f in metres, P in dioptres)}$$ $$\text{Convex: } P > 0, \quad \text{Concave: } P < 0$$

13. Lens Equation & Magnification

Fig 17.13

Thin Lens Formula

$$\frac{1}{v} - \frac{1}{u} = \frac{1}{f}$$

Magnification

$$m = \frac{h_i}{h_o} = \frac{v}{u}$$ $$m = \frac{f}{f + u} = \frac{v - f}{f}$$ $$m > 0: \text{ erect}, \quad m < 0: \text{ inverted}$$
Example 7 — Convex Lens Image Formation

A convex lens of focal length 15 cm forms an image of an object placed 25 cm from it. Find the image position, nature, and magnification.

Solution:
u = −25 cm, f = +15 cm

Using the thin lens formula: 1/v − 1/u = 1/f
1/v − 1/(−25) = 1/15
1/v + 1/25 = 1/15
1/v = 1/15 − 1/25 = (5 − 3)/75 = 2/75
v = +37.5 cm

Since v is positive, the image is on the opposite side of the lens — it is real.

Magnification: m = v/u = 37.5/(−25) = −1.5
The image is real, inverted, and magnified (1.5×).

Example 8 — Lens Maker’s Formula for Biconvex Lens

Find the focal length of a biconvex lens in air if the radii of its surfaces are 60 cm and 15 cm, and the refractive index of glass is 1.5.

Solution:
For a biconvex lens: R1 = +60 cm (first convex surface), R2 = −15 cm (second convex surface, centre on incidence side).

Lens maker's equation:
1/f = (n − 1)(1/R1 − 1/R2)
1/f = (1.5 − 1)(1/60 − 1/(−15))
1/f = 0.5 × (1/60 + 1/15)
1/f = 0.5 × (1/60 + 4/60)
1/f = 0.5 × 5/60 = 1/24
f = 24 cm

14. Displacement Method (Bessel’s Method)

Fig 17.14

Principle

Bessel's method is an experimental technique to measure the focal length of a converging lens accurately. The object and screen are fixed at a distance D apart (D > 4f). The lens is moved along the axis and there are exactly two positions of the lens that give a sharp image on the screen. Let L be the distance between these two lens positions.

$$f = \frac{D^2 - L^2}{4D}$$ $$D = \text{object-screen distance}, \quad L = \text{lens displacement}$$ $$D > 4f$$ $$O = \sqrt{I_1 \times I_2}$$

At the first position, the image is magnified (m1 > 1) and at the second position it is diminished (m2 < 1). The product of the two magnifications m1 × m2 = 1.

15. Combinations of Lenses

Fig 17.15

Lenses in Contact

When two or more thin lenses are placed in contact (negligible separation), their powers add directly:

$$\frac{1}{F} = \frac{1}{f_1} + \frac{1}{f_2} + \frac{1}{f_3} + \ldots$$ $$P = P_1 + P_2 + P_3 + \ldots$$

Lenses Separated by Distance d

$$\frac{1}{F} = \frac{1}{f_1} + \frac{1}{f_2} - \frac{d}{f_1 \times f_2}$$

Silvered Lens (Equivalent Mirror)

When one surface of a lens is silvered, the combination acts as an equivalent mirror. Light refracts through the lens, reflects from the silvered surface, and refracts through the lens again. The equivalent focal length is:

$$\frac{1}{F_{\text{eq}}} = \frac{2}{f_{\text{lens}}} + \frac{1}{f_{\text{mirror}}}$$ $$\text{Acts as a concave mirror}$$

16. Chromatic Aberration & Achromatic Combination

Fig 17.16

Chromatic Aberration

Since the refractive index of a lens material varies with wavelength (μV > μR), different colours of light are focused at different points by the same lens. This defect is called chromatic aberration. Violet light focuses closer to the lens (shorter focal length) and red light focuses farther away.

$$\mu_V > \mu_R \quad \Rightarrow \quad f_V < f_R$$ $$\omega = \frac{\mu_V - \mu_R}{\mu_Y - 1}$$

Achromatic Doublet

An achromatic doublet combines a converging lens (crown glass) and a diverging lens (flint glass) in contact. By choosing appropriate focal lengths, the chromatic aberrations of the two lenses cancel each other while still providing net converging power.

$$\frac{\omega_1}{f_1} + \frac{\omega_2}{f_2} = 0$$ $$\frac{1}{F} = \frac{1}{f_1} + \frac{1}{f_2}$$

17. Optical Instruments

Fig 17.17

The Human Eye

The human eye is a natural optical instrument with a variable-focal-length lens system. The near point (least distance of distinct vision) for a normal eye is D = 25 cm, and the far point is at infinity. The ability to adjust focal length is called accommodation.

Defects of Vision:

  • Myopia (short-sight): far point is finite. Image of distant objects forms before the retina. Corrected using a concave lens of power P = −1/(far point in metres).
  • Hypermetropia (long-sight): near point farther than 25 cm. Corrected using a convex lens.
  • Presbyopia: Age-related loss of accommodation. Corrected using bifocal lenses.
  • Astigmatism: Non-spherical cornea. Corrected using cylindrical lenses.

Simple Microscope (Magnifying Glass)

A convex lens of short focal length used to view small objects. The object is placed between F and the lens, forming a virtual, erect, enlarged image.

$$m = 1 + \frac{D}{f} \quad \text{(near point)}$$ $$m = \frac{D}{f} \quad \text{(at infinity)}$$

Compound Microscope

Consists of two convex lenses: the objective (very short fo, small aperture) near the object, and the eyepiece (short fe). The objective forms a real, magnified, inverted intermediate image which the eyepiece further magnifies as a simple microscope.

$$m = -\frac{L}{f_o} \times \left(1 + \frac{D}{f_e}\right) \quad \text{(near point)}$$ $$m = -\frac{L}{f_o} \times \frac{D}{f_e} \quad \text{(at } \infty\text{)}$$

Astronomical Telescope (Refracting)

Used to view distant objects. The objective has a large focal length fo and large aperture (to collect more light). The eyepiece has a short focal length fe. In normal adjustment, the final image is at infinity.

$$m = -\frac{f_o}{f_e} \quad \text{(normal adjustment)}$$ $$L = f_o + f_e$$ $$m = -\frac{f_o}{f_e}\left(1 + \frac{f_e}{D}\right) \quad \text{(near point)}$$

The image formed by an astronomical telescope is inverted. A terrestrial telescope uses an additional erecting lens to produce an erect final image.

Example 9 — Astronomical Telescope Magnification

The objective of an astronomical telescope has a focal length of 100 cm and the eyepiece has a focal length of 5 cm. Calculate the magnifying power and the length of the telescope in normal adjustment.

Solution:
fo = 100 cm, fe = 5 cm

Magnifying power (normal adjustment): m = −fo/fe = −100/5 = −20
The magnitude is 20, and the negative sign indicates an inverted image.

Length of telescope: L = fo + fe = 100 + 5 = 105 cm

18. Key Formulas Summary

Mirrors

$$i = r$$ $$f = \frac{R}{2}$$ $$\frac{1}{v} + \frac{1}{u} = \frac{1}{f}$$ $$m = -\frac{v}{u}$$

Refraction

$$n_1 \sin\theta_1 = n_2 \sin\theta_2$$ $$n = \frac{c}{v}$$ $$d_{\text{app}} = \frac{d_{\text{real}}}{n}$$ $$d = t\frac{\sin(i - r)}{\cos r}$$ $$\frac{\mu_2}{v} - \frac{\mu_1}{u} = \frac{\mu_2 - \mu_1}{R}$$

Total Internal Reflection

$$\sin\theta_c = \frac{n_{\text{rarer}}}{n_{\text{denser}}} = \frac{1}{n}$$ $$i > \theta_c \quad \text{(denser to rarer)}$$

Prism

$$r_1 + r_2 = A$$ $$\delta = i_1 + i_2 - A$$ $$n = \frac{\sin\left(\frac{A + \delta_m}{2}\right)}{\sin\left(\frac{A}{2}\right)}$$ $$\delta = (n - 1)A \quad \text{(thin prism)}$$ $$\omega = \frac{\mu_V - \mu_R}{\mu_Y - 1}$$ $$\phi = (\mu_V - \mu_R)A$$

Lenses

$$\frac{1}{f} = (n - 1)\left(\frac{1}{R_1} - \frac{1}{R_2}\right)$$ $$\frac{1}{v} - \frac{1}{u} = \frac{1}{f}$$ $$m = \frac{v}{u}$$ $$P = \frac{1}{f}$$ $$\frac{1}{F} = \frac{1}{f_1} + \frac{1}{f_2} + \ldots$$ $$\frac{1}{F} = \frac{1}{f_1} + \frac{1}{f_2} - \frac{d}{f_1 f_2}$$ $$\frac{1}{F_{\text{eq}}} = \frac{2}{f_{\text{lens}}} + \frac{1}{f_{\text{mirror}}}$$

Bessel's Method (Displacement Method)

$$f = \frac{D^2 - L^2}{4D}$$ $$O = \sqrt{I_1 \times I_2}$$

Optical Instruments

$$m = 1 + \frac{D}{f} \quad \text{or} \quad m = \frac{D}{f}$$ $$m = -\frac{L}{f_o} \times \frac{D}{f_e}$$ $$m = -\frac{f_o}{f_e}, \quad L = f_o + f_e$$ $$\frac{\omega_1}{f_1} + \frac{\omega_2}{f_2} = 0$$ $$D = 25 \text{ cm}$$
