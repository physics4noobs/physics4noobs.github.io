03 — Work, Power & Energy

Work Done by Forces Work-Energy Theorem Kinetic Energy Potential Energy Conservation of Energy Power Vertical Circle Collisions & Momentum Impulse Centre of Mass Key Formulas Solved Examples

1. Work Done by Forces

Fig 3.1

In physics, work is done when a force displaces a body in its direction of application. Work is a scalar quantity with the SI unit Joule (J). Unlike everyday usage, work has a precise mathematical definition that distinguishes between positive, negative, and zero work.

Work by a Constant Force

When a constant force F acts on a body and displaces it through a distance d, the work done is:

$$W = F \cdot d \cdot \cos\theta$$ $$W = \vec{F} \cdot \vec{d}$$

where θ is the angle between the force vector and the displacement vector.

  • Positive work (θ < 90°): Force has a component along displacement. Example: pulling a box forward.
  • Negative work (θ > 90°): Force has a component opposing displacement. Example: friction on a sliding body, or gravity on a body thrown upward.
  • Zero work (θ = 90°): Force is perpendicular to displacement. Example: centripetal force in circular motion, normal force on a body moving on a horizontal surface.

Work by a Variable Force

When the force varies with position, work is calculated using integration. The work done by a variable force F(x) in displacing the body from position x1 to x2 is:

$$W = \int_{x_1}^{x_2} F(x) \, dx$$ $$W = \int \vec{F} \cdot d\vec{r} = \int (F_x \, dx + F_y \, dy + F_z \, dz)$$

Work done by a spring: $W = -\frac{1}{2}k(x_2^2 - x_1^2)$. When stretching from natural length ($x_1 = 0$) to extension $x$: $W_{\text{spring}} = -\frac{1}{2}kx^2$ (negative, as the spring opposes the displacement). The external agent does $W_{\text{ext}} = +\frac{1}{2}kx^2$.

2. Work-Energy Theorem

Fig 3.2

The Work-Energy Theorem is one of the most powerful tools in mechanics. It states that the net work done by all forces (both conservative and non-conservative) on a body equals the change in its kinetic energy:

$$W_{\text{net}} = \Delta KE = KE_f - KE_i = \frac{1}{2}m v_f^2 - \frac{1}{2}m v_i^2$$

This theorem is valid in all inertial reference frames. It provides a scalar equation (no direction involved), making it extremely useful when force and acceleration analysis using Newton’s second law becomes complicated — for example, problems with curved paths, variable forces, or when only speeds (not directions) are required.

The net work includes work done by all forces: gravity, normal force, friction, applied force, spring force, etc. Alternatively, Wnet = Wgravity + Wfriction + Wnormal + Wapplied + Wspring + …

Application on Inclines with Friction

For a block moving up an incline of angle θ with friction μk, the work-energy theorem gives:

$$W_{\text{applied}} + W_{\text{gravity}} + W_{\text{friction}} = \Delta KE$$ $$Fs - mgs \sin\theta - \mu_k mgs \cos\theta = \frac{1}{2}m(v_f^2 - v_i^2)$$

3. Kinetic Energy

Fig 3.3

Kinetic energy is the energy a body possesses by virtue of its motion. For a body of mass m moving with speed v:

$$KE = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$$

Key properties of kinetic energy:

  • KE is always non-negative (KE ≥ 0). It is zero only when the body is at rest.
  • KE is a scalar quantity — it depends on speed, not direction.
  • KE is a frame-dependent quantity — different observers may measure different KE values for the same body.
  • KE is related to momentum: KE = p²/2m, where p = mv. This relation is useful in momentum-energy combined problems.
  • If two bodies have the same momentum, the lighter body has greater KE. If they have the same KE, the heavier body has greater momentum.

4. Potential Energy

Fig 3.4

Potential energy is the energy stored in a body by virtue of its position or configuration in a force field. It is defined only for conservative forces.

Gravitational Potential Energy

For a body of mass m at height h above a chosen reference level (where PE = 0):

$$PE = mgh \quad \text{(near Earth's surface)}$$ $$PE = -\frac{GMm}{r} \quad \text{(general)}$$

The choice of reference level is arbitrary — only the change in PE has physical significance.

Elastic Potential Energy

Energy stored in a spring (or any elastic body) when deformed from its natural length:

$$PE_{\text{spring}} = \frac{1}{2}kx^2$$

where k is the spring constant and x is the extension or compression from the natural length.

Conservative vs Non-Conservative Forces

A conservative force is one where the work done depends only on the initial and final positions, not the path taken. Equivalently, the work done in a closed path is zero. Examples: gravity, spring force, electrostatic force. These forces have an associated potential energy function.

A non-conservative force is path-dependent. Examples: friction, air resistance, viscous force. These forces dissipate mechanical energy into heat, sound, or deformation. No potential energy function exists for them.

Force-Potential Energy Relationship

$$F(x) = -\frac{dU}{dx}$$ $$\vec{F} = -\nabla U = -\left(\frac{\partial U}{\partial x} \hat{\mathbf{i}} + \frac{\partial U}{\partial y} \hat{\mathbf{j}} + \frac{\partial U}{\partial z} \hat{\mathbf{k}}\right)$$

Potential Energy Graphs and Equilibrium

Points where dU/dx = 0 correspond to equilibrium positions:

  • Stable equilibrium: U is a minimum (d²U/dx² > 0). The particle, if slightly displaced, returns to the equilibrium position. Example: a ball at the bottom of a bowl.
  • Unstable equilibrium: U is a maximum (d²U/dx² < 0). A slight displacement causes the particle to move away. Example: a ball balanced on top of a hill.
  • Neutral equilibrium: U is constant (d²U/dx² = 0). The particle remains in equilibrium wherever it is placed. Example: a ball on a flat surface.
Diagram: A potential energy U(x) graph showing stable equilibrium (valley), unstable equilibrium (peak), and neutral equilibrium (flat region). Mark the equilibrium points and indicate the direction of restoring force on either side of each point.

5. Conservation of Energy

Fig 3.5

Conservation of Mechanical Energy

If only conservative forces do work on a system, the total mechanical energy (KE + PE) remains constant:

$$KE_i + PE_i = KE_f + PE_f$$ $$\frac{1}{2}m v_i^2 + mgh_i = \frac{1}{2}m v_f^2 + mgh_f \quad \text{(gravity)}$$ $$\frac{1}{2}m v_i^2 + \frac{1}{2}k x_i^2 = \frac{1}{2}m v_f^2 + \frac{1}{2}k x_f^2 \quad \text{(spring)}$$

With Non-Conservative Forces Present

When non-conservative forces (like friction) are present, mechanical energy is not conserved, but total energy is. The work done by non-conservative forces equals the change in mechanical energy:

$$W_{nc} = \Delta KE + \Delta PE = (KE_f + PE_f) - (KE_i + PE_i)$$ $$W_{\text{friction}} = -f_k \times d \quad \text{(always negative)}$$

The energy balance: Initial mechanical energy + Work by non-conservative forces = Final mechanical energy. The “lost” mechanical energy is converted to other forms (heat, sound, deformation).

6. Power

Fig 3.6

Power is the rate at which work is done, or equivalently, the rate of energy transfer. The SI unit is Watt (W), where 1 W = 1 J/s.

$$P_{\text{avg}} = \frac{W}{t} = \frac{\Delta E}{\Delta t}$$ $$P = \frac{dW}{dt} = \vec{F} \cdot \vec{v} = Fv \cos\theta$$ $$1 \text{ hp} = 746 \text{ W} \quad | \quad 1 \text{ kWh} = 3.6 \times 10^6 \text{ J}$$

Power is maximum when force and velocity are parallel (θ = 0°), zero when perpendicular (θ = 90°), and negative when opposing motion (θ = 180°).

For a vehicle moving at constant velocity v against a resistive force f, the engine must supply power P = fv. The maximum speed of a vehicle with engine power P and resistive force f is vmax = P/f.

For a vehicle starting from rest with constant power P, the acceleration decreases with increasing velocity (since P = mav, we get a = P/mv). The velocity as a function of time for constant power is: v = √(2Pt/m).

7. Motion in a Vertical Circle

Fig 3.7

A particle moves in a complete vertical circle of radius r, attached by a string or rod. The tension in the string provides the centripetal force along with gravity.

Critical Speeds

$$v_{\text{top}} = \sqrt{gr}$$ $$v_{\text{bottom}} = \sqrt{5gr}$$

Tension Analysis

At the bottom of the circle:

$$T_{\text{bottom}} = mg + \frac{m v_{\text{bottom}}^2}{r}$$

At the top of the circle (both tension and weight point toward centre):

$$T_{\text{top}} = \frac{m v_{\text{top}}^2}{r} - mg$$

Conditions for Motion

  • Complete circle (v ≥ √(5gr) at bottom): Particle completes the entire circular path. Tension remains positive throughout.
  • Oscillatory motion (v < √(2gr) at bottom): Particle doesn’t reach the top. Oscillates like a pendulum.
  • Leaves circle (√(2gr) < v < √(5gr) at bottom): Particle leaves the path before completing it. String becomes slack when T = 0, then follows a parabolic path.

Minimum Height to Complete Loop

A block released from height h on a circular track of radius r. For the block to complete the loop:

$$h_{\min} = 2.5r = \frac{5r}{2}$$

8. Collisions & Momentum

Fig 3.8

A collision is a short-duration interaction between two or more bodies in which the impulsive forces are much larger than any external forces. During a collision, momentum is always conserved (assuming no external impulsive force). Energy conservation depends on the type of collision.

Conservation of Momentum

$$m_1 u_1 + m_2 u_2 = m_1 v_1 + m_2 v_2$$

Coefficient of Restitution

$$e = \frac{\text{velocity of separation}}{\text{velocity of approach}}$$ $$e = \frac{v_2 - v_1}{u_1 - u_2}$$ $$e = 1 \text{ (elastic)} \quad | \quad e = 0 \text{ (inelastic)} \quad | \quad 0 < e < 1$$

Types of Collisions

  • Perfectly Elastic (e = 1): Both momentum and kinetic energy are conserved. Example: collision between ideal billiard balls, atomic/molecular scale collisions.
  • Perfectly Inelastic (e = 0): Objects stick together after collision. Maximum kinetic energy is lost. Example: a bullet embedding in a block.
  • Inelastic (0 < e < 1): Momentum is conserved, some kinetic energy is lost. Most common type in real life.

1D Elastic Collision Formulas

$$v_1 = \frac{(m_1 - m_2)u_1 + 2m_2 u_2}{m_1 + m_2}$$ $$v_2 = \frac{(m_2 - m_1)u_2 + 2m_1 u_1}{m_1 + m_2}$$

Special cases:

  • Equal masses (m1 = m2): The bodies exchange velocities. v1 = u2, v2 = u1.
  • Target at rest (u2 = 0), m1 >> m2: The heavy body continues nearly unchanged; the light body flies off at approximately 2u1.
  • Target at rest (u2 = 0), m1 << m2: The light body bounces back with approximately the same speed; the heavy body barely moves.

Perfectly Inelastic Collision

$$v = \frac{m_1 u_1 + m_2 u_2}{m_1 + m_2}$$ $$\text{Loss in KE} = \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{m_1 m_2}{m_1 + m_2} \cdot (u_1 - u_2)^2$$

2D Oblique Collisions

In 2D collisions, momentum is conserved along both x and y axes independently. For an oblique elastic collision of equal masses with one initially at rest, the two bodies move at right angles to each other after the collision. The coefficient of restitution applies along the line of impact only.

Energy Loss Formula

$$\Delta KE = \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{m_1 m_2}{m_1 + m_2} \cdot (1 - e^2) \cdot (u_1 - u_2)^2$$

9. Impulse

Fig 3.9

Impulse is the change in momentum, calculated as the integral of force over time:

$$I = \Delta p = m(v_f - v_i)$$ $$I = \int F \, dt \quad \text{(variable force)}$$ $$F_{\text{avg}} = \frac{\Delta p}{\Delta t} = \frac{m(v_f - v_i)}{\Delta t}$$

The impulse-momentum theorem states that Favg · Δt = m(vf − vi). Impulse is a vector quantity with SI unit N·s (or equivalently kg·m/s).

A large force for a short time produces the same impulse as a small force for a long time. This principle is used in real-world applications: airbags increase the collision time, reducing the average force on passengers; a cricket player pulls hands back while catching to increase contact time and reduce the force felt.

10. Centre of Mass

Fig 3.10

Position of Centre of Mass

For a system of particles:

$$x_{\text{cm}} = \frac{\sum (m_i \cdot x_i)}{M_{\text{total}}}$$ $$y_{\text{cm}} = \frac{\sum (m_i \cdot y_i)}{M_{\text{total}}}$$ $$\vec{r}_{\text{cm}} = \frac{\sum (m_i \cdot \vec{r}_i)}{M_{\text{total}}}$$

Velocity and Acceleration of Centre of Mass

$$V_{\text{cm}} = \frac{\sum (m_i \cdot v_i)}{M_{\text{total}}}$$ $$a_{\text{cm}} = \frac{F_{\text{ext}}}{M_{\text{total}}}$$ $$M_{\text{total}} \cdot V_{\text{cm}} = P_{\text{total}}$$

The centre of mass accelerates as if all the mass were concentrated there and all external forces acted at that point.

Conservation of Momentum via Centre of Mass

If the total external force is zero (Fext = 0), then acm = 0, meaning Vcm = constant. The centre of mass moves with constant velocity (or remains at rest) regardless of internal interactions. This is the most general statement of conservation of momentum.

Key Formulas Summary

$$W = Fd \cos\theta \quad | \quad W = \int F \cdot dx$$ $$KE = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 \quad | \quad KE = \frac{p^2}{2m}$$ $$PE_{\text{grav}} = mgh \quad | \quad PE_{\text{spring}} = \frac{1}{2}kx^2$$ $$F = -\frac{dU}{dx} \quad | \quad W_{\text{net}} = \Delta KE$$ $$KE_i + PE_i = KE_f + PE_f$$ $$P = \frac{dW}{dt} = Fv \cos\theta \quad | \quad 1 \text{ hp} = 746 \text{ W}$$ $$v_{\text{top}} = \sqrt{gr} \quad | \quad v_{\text{bottom}} = \sqrt{5gr}$$ $$e = \frac{v_2 - v_1}{u_1 - u_2}$$ $$I = \Delta p = F_{\text{avg}} \Delta t \quad | \quad x_{\text{cm}} = \frac{\sum m_i x_i}{M_{\text{total}}}$$

Solved Examples

Example 1 — Work by Variable Force

A body of mass 2 kg is initially at rest on a smooth horizontal surface. A horizontal force F = (3 + 2x) N acts on it, where x is in metres. Find the speed of the body when it has been displaced 4 m from the initial position.

Solution: Using the work-energy theorem: Wnet = ΔKE.

W = ∫ F dx (from 0 to 4) = ∫ (3 + 2x) dx (from 0 to 4)
W = [3x + x²] from 0 to 4 = (12 + 16) − (0) = 28 J

Since the surface is smooth and force is horizontal, Wnet = 28 J.
½mv² − 0 = 28
½ × 2 × v² = 28
v² = 28
v = √28 = 2√7 ≈ 5.29 m/s

Example 2 — Conservation of Energy on an Incline

A block of mass 1 kg slides down a frictionless incline of height 5 m starting from rest. Find the speed at the bottom. If the horizontal surface at the bottom has a coefficient of kinetic friction μk = 0.2, how far does the block travel before stopping? (g = 10 m/s²)

Solution: On the frictionless incline, using conservation of energy:

mgh = ½mv²
v = √(2gh) = √(2 × 10 × 5) = √100 = 10 m/s

On the rough horizontal surface, applying the work-energy theorem:
Wfriction = ΔKE
−μkmg × d = 0 − ½mv²
d = v² / (2μkg) = 100 / (2 × 0.2 × 10) = 100/4 = 25 m

Example 3 — Perfectly Inelastic Collision (Ballistic Pendulum)

A bullet of mass 10 g moving at 400 m/s embeds itself in a wooden block of mass 4 kg suspended as a pendulum. Find the velocity after collision and the maximum height reached by the block.

Solution: Using conservation of momentum (perfectly inelastic):

mbub + mBuB = (mb + mB)v
0.01 × 400 + 4 × 0 = (0.01 + 4) × v
4 = 4.01v
v ≈ 1 m/s

Using energy conservation to find maximum height:
½(mb + mB)v² = (mb + mB)gh
h = v²/(2g) = 1/(2 × 10) = 0.05 m = 5 cm

Example 4 — Block Colliding with Spring

A 2 kg block moving at 6 m/s on a frictionless surface collides with a spring (k = 100 N/m). Find the maximum compression of the spring.

Solution: At maximum compression, the block momentarily stops (v = 0).

Using energy conservation:
½mv² = ½kx²
½ × 2 × 36 = ½ × 100 × x²
36 = 50x²
x = √(36/50) = 0.85 m

Example 5 — Coefficient of Restitution (Bouncing Ball)

A ball is dropped from height h1 = 5 m and bounces to height h2 = 3.2 m. Find the coefficient of restitution.

Solution: Velocity just before impact: u = √(2gh1) = √(2 × 10 × 5) = 10 m/s

Velocity just after impact: v = √(2gh2) = √(2 × 10 × 3.2) = 8 m/s

e = v/u = 8/10 = 0.8
Alternatively: e = √(h2/h1) = √(3.2/5) = 0.8

Example 6 — Explosion and Centre of Mass

A 10 kg object at rest explodes into two fragments: one of 4 kg moving at 15 m/s to the right. Find the velocity of the other fragment and the energy released.

Solution: Using conservation of momentum (initial momentum = 0):

0 = 4 × 15 + 6 × v2
v2 = −10 m/s (6 kg fragment moves at 10 m/s to the left)

Energy released:
ΔKE = ½ × 4 × 15² + ½ × 6 × 10² − 0
= 450 + 300 = 750 J (chemical energy converted to kinetic energy)

Example 7 — Vertical Circle

A block is released from rest at height h on the inside of a circular track of radius r. Find the minimum height h such that the block completes the loop.

Solution: For the block to complete the loop, it must have minimum speed at the top: vtop = √(gr)

Using energy conservation from release point to top of loop:
mgh = ½mvtop² + mg(2r)
gh = ½gr + 2gr
h = r/2 + 2r = 5r/2 = 2.5r

The block must be released from a minimum height h = 2.5r to just complete the loop.
