Simple Harmonic Motion

Chapter 09 — Grade XI • Oscillations

1. Oscillatory & Periodic Motion

A body is said to possess oscillatory motion if it moves back and forth repeatedly about a mean (equilibrium) position. Examples include a swinging pendulum and the vibratory motion of a mass attached to a spring. The mean position is defined as the position where the net force on the body is zero; any small displacement from it produces a restoring force directed back toward it.

A motion that repeats itself after equal intervals of time is called periodic motion. The smallest time interval after which the motion repeats is the time period T, and the number of repetitions per second is the frequency ν = 1/T (in Hz).

Key Distinction

  • Every oscillatory motion is periodic, but not every periodic motion is oscillatory.
  • A particle moving uniformly in a circle executes periodic motion (period = time for one revolution) but it is not oscillatory because it does not move back and forth about a mean position.
  • A mass on a spring oscillating about its equilibrium is both oscillatory and periodic.

For JEE, the most important type of oscillatory motion is Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM), in which the restoring force is directly proportional to the displacement from the mean position.

Time period T: smallest time after which motion repeats
Frequency: ν = 1/T   (Hz or cycles per second)

2. SHM Definition & Differential Equation

A system executes Simple Harmonic Motion if the magnitude of the restoring force is directly proportional to the displacement from the mean position and the force is always directed toward the mean position. Common examples include a simple pendulum (small angle), a vibrating tuning fork, and a loaded spring.

Consider a particle of mass m executing SHM along the x-axis with the mean position at the origin. If x(t) denotes the displacement from the mean position, the defining condition is:

Restoring force: F = −kx   (k = force constant / spring constant)
Newton’s second law: ma = −kx
Differential equation of SHM: d²x/dt² = −(k/m)x = −ω²x
where ω = √(k/m) is the angular frequency

The general solution of the SHM differential equation is:

x(t) = A sin(ωt + φ0)
Alternative form: x(t) = A cos(ωt + φ0)

Here A is the amplitude (maximum displacement), ω is the angular frequency, and φ0 is the initial phase constant. Any equation of the form d²y/dt² = −ω²y represents SHM, regardless of the variable y.

Important Notes

  • The key condition for SHM: the restoring force (or acceleration) must be proportional to displacement and directed opposite to it.
  • If displacement is given as y = a cos(ωt) + b sin(ωt), the motion is SHM with amplitude A = √(a² + b²) and tan(φ0) = a/b.

3. Amplitude, Time Period, Frequency & Phase

The key parameters that completely characterise SHM are:

  • Amplitude (A): The magnitude of the maximum displacement on either side of the mean position. From x(t) = A sin(ωt + φ0), since the maximum value of sin is 1, we have −A ≤ x ≤ A.
  • Time Period (T): The time taken to complete one full cycle. The function x(t) repeats after an interval of 2π/ω.
  • Frequency (ν): The number of oscillations completed per second, measured in Hertz (Hz).
  • Angular Frequency (ω): Equal to 2π times the frequency.
  • Phase: The state of the particle (position and direction of motion) at any instant, given by the argument of the sine function: phase = ωt + φ0.
  • Initial Phase (φ0): The phase constant at t = 0, determined entirely by the initial conditions.
T = 2π/ω = 2π√(m/k)
ν = ω/(2π) = (1/2π)√(k/m)
ω = 2πν = 2π/T = √(k/m)

Initial Conditions & Phasor Representation

At t = 0: x(0) = A sin(φ0) and v(0) = Aω cos(φ0). The initial phase φ0 is fixed by these two conditions.

  • If φ0 = π/2: x(0) = A and v(0) = 0 — particle starts from the right extreme. Equation: x(t) = A cos(ωt).
  • If φ0 = 0: x(0) = 0 and v(0) = Aω (positive) — particle starts from mean position moving in +x direction. Equation: x(t) = A sin(ωt).
  • If φ0 = π: x(0) = 0 and v(0) = −Aω — particle starts from mean position moving in −x direction. Equation: x(t) = −A sin(ωt).
  • If φ0 = −π/2: x(0) = −A — particle starts from the left extreme. Equation: x(t) = −A cos(ωt).

In the phasor representation, the SHM is visualised as the projection of uniform circular motion onto a diameter. A phasor of length A rotates at angular frequency ω, and its projection on the x-axis gives x(t) = A sin(ωt + φ0). This is extremely useful for superposition problems.

Isochronous property: The time period and frequency of SHM are independent of the amplitude. This is a defining characteristic of SHM.

4. Velocity & Acceleration in SHM

The velocity is obtained by differentiating the displacement equation, and the acceleration by differentiating the velocity (or double-differentiating displacement).

As functions of time:
v(t) = Aω cos(ωt + φ0)
a(t) = −Aω² sin(ωt + φ0)

As functions of position:
v(x) = ±ω√(A² − x²)
a(x) = −ω²x

Extreme Values

  • Maximum velocity: |vmax| = Aω (at the mean position, x = 0)
  • Minimum velocity: v = 0 (at the extreme positions, x = ±A)
  • Maximum acceleration: |amax| = Aω² (at the extremes, x = ±A)
  • Minimum acceleration: a = 0 (at the mean position, x = 0)
  • Maximum force: |Fmax| = mω²A = kA (at the extremes)

Phase Relationships

The velocity leads the displacement by π/2 (90°), and the acceleration leads the velocity by π/2 — equivalently, the acceleration is π (180°) out of phase with the displacement. These phase relationships are a hallmark of SHM.

Velocity–Displacement Relation

The ± sign in v(x) = ±ω√(A² − x²) indicates that at any position the particle passes with equal and opposite velocities (once going right, once going left). This relation can be rewritten as an ellipse: v²/(ωA)² + x²/A² = 1. The v–x graph is therefore an ellipse.

Solved Example 1

A 50 g mass vibrates in SHM at the end of a spring. The amplitude is 12 cm and the time period is 1.70 s. Find: (a) frequency, (b) spring constant, (c) maximum speed, (d) maximum acceleration, (e) speed when displacement is 6 cm.

(a) ν = 1/T = 1/1.70 = 0.588 Hz

(b) T = 2π√(m/k) ⇒ k = 4π²m/T² = 4π² × 0.05 / (1.70)² = 0.68 N/m

(c) vmax = ωA = (2π/T) × A = (2π/1.70) × 0.12 = 0.44 m/s

(d) amax = ω²A = (4π²/T²) × A = (4π²/(1.70)²) × 0.12 = 1.63 m/s²

(e) v(x) = ω√(A² − x²) = (2π/1.70) × √(0.12² − 0.06²) = (3.696) × √(0.0108) = 0.38 m/s

5. Energy in SHM

A particle executing SHM possesses both kinetic energy (due to velocity) and potential energy (due to displacement from the mean position). These two forms of energy interconvert continuously during the oscillation, but their sum — the total mechanical energy — remains constant (provided there is no dissipation).

KE = ½mv² = ½mω²(A² − x²) = ½k(A² − x²)
PE = ½kx²   (taking PE = 0 at mean position)
In general: PE = U(0) + ½kx²
TE = KE + PE = ½kA² = ½mω²A² = constant

Key Energy Facts

  • At mean position (x = 0): KE is maximum = ½kA², PE is zero (if reference is mean position).
  • At extreme positions (x = ±A): PE is maximum = ½kA², KE is zero.
  • At x = ±A/√2: KE = PE, and each equals half the total energy.
  • At x = A/2: KE:PE = 3:1. At x = A√3/2: KE:PE = 1:3.
  • The average KE over one full cycle equals the average PE: ⟨KE⟩ = ⟨PE⟩ = ½Etotal.
  • Total energy is proportional to A²: TE ∝ A².
  • Both KE and PE oscillate with angular frequency 2ω (i.e., frequency 2ν), which is twice the SHM frequency.

Energy as a Function of Time

KE = ½mω²A² cos²(ωt + φ0) and PE = ½mω²A² sin²(ωt + φ0). These oscillate with angular frequency 2ω, hence the frequency of KE (or PE) oscillation is 2f (twice the SHM frequency). This is an important JEE fact.

Solved Example 2

A 0.5 kg block attached to a spring of spring constant k = 200 N/m oscillates with an amplitude of 0.1 m. Find the total energy and the maximum speed of the block.

Total energy: E = ½kA² = ½ × 200 × (0.1)² = 1 J

At mean position, all energy is kinetic:
½mv²max = E
vmax = √(2E/m) = √(2 × 1 / 0.5) = √4 = 2 m/s

Alternatively: vmax = Aω = A√(k/m) = 0.1 × √(200/0.5) = 0.1 × 20 = 2 m/s

6. Analysis of Forces in SHM

To identify whether a given physical system executes SHM, follow these systematic steps:

  • Step 1: Displace the particle from the mean (equilibrium) position by a distance x along the line of expected SHM.
  • Step 2: Analyse the forces on the particle and show that (a) the net force is proportional to x, and (b) the force is directed toward the mean position (restoring nature).
  • Step 3: Find the proportionality constant k from the expression for the magnitude of force: F = kx.
  • Step 4: Find the time period using T = 2π√(m/k).
General SHM condition: Fnet = −kx   (restoring, proportional to displacement)
Time period: T = 2π√(m/k)   |   ω = √(k/m)

Liquid Oscillating in a U-Tube

A liquid column of total length 2l in a U-tube of uniform cross-section A is displaced by a distance x from equilibrium. The extra height of liquid on one side is 2x (since liquid drops by x on one side and rises by x on the other). The restoring force is:

F = −(2x)ρgA   ⇒   k = 2Aρg
T = 2π√(l/g)   (equivalent to a simple pendulum of length l)

Cylinder Floating in a Liquid

A cylinder of mass m, density d, length L, and cross-section A floating in a liquid of density ρ with immersed length l at equilibrium. At equilibrium: Ld = lρ (flotation condition). If the cylinder is pushed down by x, the extra buoyant force is Aρgx, which is the restoring force.

F = −Aρgx   ⇒   k = Aρg
T = 2π√(m/(Aρg)) = 2π√(l/g)   (depends only on immersed length)

Ball in a Smooth Bowl

A particle sliding inside a smooth hemispherical bowl of radius R executes SHM (for small displacements) with ω = √(g/R). This is equivalent to a simple pendulum of length R.

Gas in a Piston (Isothermal Process)

A cylindrical piston of mass M slides smoothly inside a long cylinder closed at one end, enclosing gas at pressure P and the piston is at distance h from the closed end. For an isothermal process (diathermic piston), if the piston is displaced by x, the restoring force can be shown to be proportional to x (for small displacements).

T = 2π√(Mh/(PA))

Block on an Oscillating Platform

For a block on a piston executing vertical SHM: the block separates from the piston when the normal reaction becomes zero, i.e., when ω²A ≥ g. For the block to maintain contact at all times, we need ω²A ≤ g.

7. Simple Pendulum

A simple pendulum is an idealised body consisting of a point mass (bob) suspended by a light, inextensible string of length l from a fixed support. The forces on the bob are its weight mg (vertically downward) and tension T (along the string toward the support). The component mg cosθ balances the tension, while the component mg sinθ acts as the tangential restoring force.

For small angular displacements (θ << 1 radian): sinθ ≈ θ, so the restoring force becomes F = mgθ = mg(x/l), which is proportional to displacement. This gives SHM with:

Force constant: k = mg/l
T = 2π√(l/g)   |   ν = (1/2π)√(g/l)
ω = √(g/l)
Maximum tension in string (at lowest point): Tmax = mg(1 + θm²)

Key Properties

  • The time period is independent of the mass of the bob and the amplitude (for small angles) — this is the property of isochronism.
  • The time period depends only on the length l and the acceleration due to gravity g.
  • The equivalent length l is measured from the point of suspension to the centre of mass of the bob.

Effect of Acceleration

  • Pendulum in a lift accelerating upward with acceleration a: T = 2π√(l/(g+a)). The period decreases.
  • Pendulum in a lift accelerating downward with acceleration a: T = 2π√(l/(g−a)). The period increases.
  • In free fall (geff = 0): the pendulum does not oscillate, T → ∞.
  • Pendulum in a car on a circular track (speed v, radius R): geff = (g² + v4/R²)1/4... more precisely, T = 2π√(l / (g² + v4/R²)1/2).

Seconds Pendulum

A seconds pendulum has T = 2 s, giving l = g/π² ≈ 0.994 m on Earth’s surface (taking g = 9.8 m/s²).

Effect of Medium Density

If the pendulum oscillates in a medium of density σ and the bob has density ρ, the effective g is replaced by g(1 − σ/ρ), giving a longer period: T = 2π√(l / (g(1 − σ/ρ))).

Solved Example 3

A simple pendulum has a time period of 2 s on the surface of the Earth. What will be its time period on a planet where g is 4 times that on Earth?

T = 2π√(l/g)
T is proportional to 1/√g (for the same length l)
T′/T = √(g/g′) = √(g/4g) = √(1/4) = 1/2
T′ = T/2 = 2/2 = 1 s

8. Spring-Mass Systems

The spring-mass system is the simplest physical realisation of SHM. Both horizontal and vertical spring-mass systems yield the same time period formula.

(a) Horizontal Spring

A block of mass m on a smooth horizontal surface is connected to a spring of force constant k, with the other end fixed. When displaced by distance x from the natural length position, the spring exerts a restoring force F = −kx. The block executes SHM with the natural length position as the mean position.

(b) Vertical Spring

A spring suspended vertically carries a block of mass m at its free end. At equilibrium, the spring elongates by d such that kd = mg. If further displaced by x from equilibrium, the net restoring force is F = k(d + x) − mg = kx (since kd = mg). The block executes SHM about the equilibrium position (not the natural length position). Gravity only determines the equilibrium position but does not affect the time period.

Horizontal & Vertical: T = 2π√(m/k)   |   ω = √(k/m)
Equilibrium elongation (vertical): d = mg/k

Cutting a Spring

The force constant of a spring is inversely proportional to its natural length: k × l = constant. If a spring of constant k and length l is cut into parts of lengths l1 and l2, then k1 = kl/l1 and k2 = kl/l2. Each shorter piece has a higher force constant.

Spring with Mass

If the spring itself has mass Ms (not massless), the effective mass for the oscillation becomes m + Ms/3 (for a spring with uniform mass distribution), and the time period is T = 2π√((m + Ms/3)/k).

Rolling Body on a Spring

A solid cylinder of mass m attached to a spring (constant k) rolling without slipping on a horizontal surface has both translational and rotational KE. For a solid cylinder (Icm = mR²/2):

T = 2π√(3m/(2k))

9. Combinations of Springs

Springs in Series

When two springs of force constants k₁ and k₂ are connected end-to-end (in series), both springs experience the same force, but the total extension is the sum of individual extensions. The effective spring constant is:

Series: 1/keff = 1/k₁ + 1/k₂  ⇒  keff = k₁k₂/(k₁ + k₂)
For n springs in series: 1/keff = 1/k₁ + 1/k₂ + … + 1/kn

Springs in Parallel

When two springs of force constants k₁ and k₂ are connected side-by-side (in parallel), both springs have the same extension, but the total restoring force is the sum of individual forces. Two springs attached to a block from opposite sides also act as a parallel combination.

Parallel: keff = k₁ + k₂
For n springs in parallel: keff = k₁ + k₂ + … + kn
Time period: T = 2π√(m/keff)

Two-Body Oscillation (Reduced Mass)

When a spring connects two blocks of masses m₁ and m₂ (both free to move), the system oscillates with:

T = 2π√(μ/k)
Reduced mass: μ = m₁m₂/(m₁ + m₂)

Important Results

  • In series combination, the weaker spring (smaller k) dominates, and keff is less than the smallest individual constant.
  • In parallel combination, keff is greater than the largest individual constant.
  • If T₁ and T₂ are time periods with springs separately, then for parallel combination: 1/T² = 1/T₁² + 1/T₂².
  • If a spring of constant k is cut into n equal parts, each part has constant nk. If those n parts are connected in parallel, the effective constant is n²k.
  • For two springs attached from opposite sides of a mass on a smooth incline, keff = k₁ + k₂ (parallel), and the incline angle does not affect the time period.

10. Physical Pendulum & Torsional Pendulum

Physical (Compound) Pendulum

A physical pendulum is any rigid body suspended from a fixed point O, free to oscillate in a vertical plane about a horizontal axis through O. Let C be the centre of gravity at distance d from O. When displaced by a small angle θ, the weight creates a restoring torque: τ = −mgd sinθ ≈ −mgdθ (for small θ).

T = 2π√(I / mgd)
where I = moment of inertia about the axis through O
Using parallel axis theorem: I = Icm + md²
Equivalent simple pendulum length: Leq = I/(md) = (k²/d) + d
(k = radius of gyration about centre of mass)

Centre of Oscillation & Kater’s Pendulum

The centre of oscillation is a point at distance Leq from the pivot, on the opposite side of the centre of mass. If the pendulum is instead suspended from the centre of oscillation, the time period remains the same. This is the principle behind Kater’s reversible pendulum, used to measure g accurately.

Examples of Physical Pendulums

  • Simple pendulum is a special case where all mass is at a point: I = ml², d = l, giving T = 2π√(l/g).
  • Uniform rod of length l suspended from one end: I = ml²/3, d = l/2, so T = 2π√(2l/(3g)).
  • Metre stick pivoted at the 75 cm mark: d = |75 − 50| = 25 cm = 0.25 m, I = ml²/12 + md², T = 2π√((l² + 12d²)/(12gd)).

Torsional Pendulum

A rigid body (e.g., a disc) suspended by a wire attached to its centre. When rotated in the horizontal plane, the twisted wire exerts a restoring torque proportional to the angular displacement: τ = −Cθ, where C is the torsional constant of the wire. This is angular SHM.

Torsional pendulum: T = 2π√(I/C)
where I = moment of inertia about the axis of the wire, C = torsional constant

Comparison: Linear SHM vs Angular SHM

  • Linear: F = −kx   ↔   Angular: τ = −Cθ
  • Linear: T = 2π√(m/k)   ↔   Angular: T = 2π√(I/C)
  • Linear: x(t) = A sin(ωt + φ)   ↔   Angular: θ(t) = θmax sin(ωt + φ)
  • Linear: v(x) = ±ω√(A² − x²)   ↔   Angular: Ω(θ) = ±ω√(θmax² − θ²)
  • Linear: a(x) = −ω²x   ↔   Angular: α(θ) = −ω²θ

11. Superposition of SHMs

When two or more SHMs act on a particle simultaneously, the resultant displacement is the vector sum (algebraic sum for same-line motions) of individual displacements.

Same Frequency, Different Phase (Along the Same Line)

If x₁ = A₁ sin(ωt + φ₁) and x₂ = A₂ sin(ωt + φ₂), the resultant is also SHM with the same frequency. The resultant amplitude is:

AR = √(A₁² + A₂² + 2A₁A₂ cosδ)
tanφ = (A₂ sinδ) / (A₁ + A₂ cosδ)
where δ = φ₂ − φ₁ is the phase difference
  • δ = 0 (in phase): AR = A₁ + A₂ (constructive superposition).
  • δ = π (out of phase): AR = |A₁ − A₂| (destructive superposition).
  • If two SHMs have the same amplitude A and same frequency but phase difference π, they cancel completely.

Special Case: x₁ = A sin(ωt) and x₂ = A cos(ωt)

Resultant: x = A√2 sin(ωt + π/4). The resultant amplitude is A√2, the time period is unchanged, and the initial phase is π/4.

Perpendicular SHMs & Lissajous Figures

When two SHMs are perpendicular to each other, the resulting path traced by the particle depends on the amplitudes, frequencies, and phase difference. If x = A sin(ωt) and y = B sin(ωt + δ), the path depends on δ:

  • δ = 0 or π: straight line (y = ±(B/A)x)
  • δ = π/2: ellipse; if A = B, a circle (x² + y² = A²)
  • Other values of δ: general ellipse

When the two frequencies are different, the Lissajous figures become more complex and their shape depends on the frequency ratio. These patterns are used in oscilloscopes to compare frequencies.

Beats from Superposition

Superposition of two SHMs of slightly different frequencies produces beats. For instance, x = a cos(2.1t) cos(50t) represents a modulated oscillation with constituent frequencies ω₁ = 52.1 rad/s and ω₂ = 47.9 rad/s; the beat frequency = |ω₁ − ω₂|/(2π).

12. Damped & Forced Oscillations

Damped Oscillations

In real systems, dissipative forces (friction, air resistance, viscosity) act on the oscillator, causing the amplitude to decrease over time. If the damping force is proportional to velocity (Fd = −bv), the equation of motion becomes:

m(d²x/dt²) + b(dx/dt) + kx = 0
Solution: x(t) = A₀ e−(b/2m)t sin(ω′t + φ)
Damped angular frequency: ω′ = √(ω₀² − (b/2m)²)
where ω₀ = √(k/m) is the natural frequency

The amplitude decays exponentially with the damping factor b/2m. The oscillation frequency ω′ is slightly less than the natural frequency ω₀.

Types of Damping

  • Underdamped (b/2m < ω₀): the system oscillates with decreasing amplitude. This is the most common case.
  • Critically damped (b/2m = ω₀): the system returns to equilibrium without oscillating, in the shortest possible time.
  • Overdamped (b/2m > ω₀): the system returns to equilibrium without oscillating, but more slowly than critically damped.

Quality Factor

Q = ω₀ / (2b/m) = ω₀ × m / (2b)
Higher Q ⇒ sharper resonance, less damping

Forced Oscillations & Resonance

When an external periodic force F = F₀ cos(ωdt) drives a damped oscillator, the system eventually oscillates at the driving frequency ωd (not its natural frequency) with a steady amplitude that depends on how close ωd is to the natural frequency ω₀.

Resonance occurs when ωd ≈ ω₀, at which the amplitude of oscillation becomes maximum. At resonance, maximum energy transfer occurs from the driving force to the system.

  • The sharpness of the resonance peak depends on the damping: less damping leads to a sharper, taller peak.
  • Examples of resonance: soldiers breaking step on a bridge (to avoid resonance), tuning a radio (matching circuit frequency to broadcast frequency), musical instruments, Tacoma Narrows Bridge collapse.

Resonance Condition

At resonance: ωd = ω₀ = √(k/m)

In JEE, damped and forced oscillations are mostly conceptual; focus on understanding the resonance condition, energy considerations, and the three damping regimes.
