Simple Harmonic Motion

Chapter 09 — Grade XI • Oscillations

1. Definition & Characteristics

Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM) is a special type of periodic oscillatory motion in which the restoring force is directly proportional to the displacement from the mean position and is always directed towards it. This is the most fundamental and widely studied type of oscillation in physics.

The defining condition for SHM is that the restoring force is F = −kx, where k is the force constant (or spring constant) and x is the displacement from equilibrium. Applying Newton’s second law, F = ma, this gives a = −(k/m)x = −ω²x, where ω = √(k/m) is the angular frequency.

Key Parameters

  • Amplitude (A): the maximum displacement from the mean position.
  • Angular frequency (ω): ω = 2π/T = 2πf, measured in rad/s.
  • Time period (T): the time for one complete oscillation.
  • Frequency (f): the number of oscillations per second, f = 1/T, in Hz.
  • Phase (φ): determines the initial state of the oscillation at t = 0.
F = −kx   |   a = −ω²x
ω = √(k/m) = 2π/T = 2πf

2. Equation of Motion

The general solution to the SHM equation a = −ω²x is a sinusoidal function. The displacement, velocity, and acceleration as functions of time are:

x = A sin(ωt + φ)   (displacement)
v = dx/dt = Aω cos(ωt + φ)   (velocity)
a = d²x/dt² = −Aω² sin(ωt + φ)   (acceleration)

Here A is the amplitude, ω is the angular frequency, and φ is the initial phase. The velocity is maximum at the mean position (|vmax| = Aω) and zero at the extremes. The acceleration is maximum at the extremes (|amax| = Aω²) and zero at the mean position. At any displacement x, the velocity can be expressed as v = ω√(A² − x²).

Phase Relationships

The velocity leads the displacement by π/2 (90°), and the acceleration leads the velocity by π/2 — equivalently, the acceleration is π (180°) out of phase with the displacement. These phase relationships are a hallmark of SHM.

3. Energy in SHM

A particle executing SHM possesses both kinetic energy and potential energy that interconvert during the oscillation. At any displacement x from the mean position:

KE = ½mω²(A² − x²) = ½k(A² − x²)
PE = ½mω²x² = ½kx²
TE = KE + PE = ½mω²A² = ½kA² = constant

The total energy is constant throughout the motion and is proportional to the square of the amplitude. At the mean position (x = 0), KE is maximum and PE is zero. At the extreme positions (x = ±A), PE is maximum and KE is zero. The average KE equals the average PE, each being half the total energy: ⟨KE⟩ = ⟨PE⟩ = ½Etotal.

Solved Example 1

A 0.5 kg block attached to a spring of spring constant k = 200 N/m oscillates with an amplitude of 0.1 m. Find the total energy and the maximum speed of the block.

Total energy: E = ½kA² = ½ × 200 × (0.1)² = 1 J
At mean position, all energy is kinetic:
½mv²max = E
vmax = √(2E/m) = √(2 × 1 / 0.5) = √4 = 2 m/s

Alternatively: vmax = Aω = A√(k/m) = 0.1 × √(200/0.5) = 0.1 × 20 = 2 m/s

4. Spring-Mass System

The spring-mass system is the simplest physical realisation of SHM. A block of mass m attached to a spring of spring constant k, displaced from its natural length and released, executes SHM with:

T = 2π√(m/k)   |   f = (1/2π)√(k/m)

Springs in Series and Parallel

When springs are combined in parallel (both attached to the same block), the effective spring constant is keff = k₁ + k₂. When in series (end-to-end), the effective constant follows 1/keff = 1/k₁ + 1/k₂.

Parallel: keff = k₁ + k₂
Series: 1/keff = 1/k₁ + 1/k₂ ⇒ keff = k₁k₂/(k₁ + k₂)

Two-Body Oscillation (Reduced Mass)

When a spring connects two blocks of masses m₁ and m₂ (both free to move), the system oscillates with T = 2π√(μ/k), where μ = m₁m₂/(m₁ + m₂) is the reduced mass.

5. Simple Pendulum

A simple pendulum consists of a point mass (bob) suspended by a massless, inextensible string of length l from a fixed support. For small angular displacements (θ << 1 radian), the restoring torque is approximately proportional to θ, and the pendulum executes SHM with:

T = 2π√(l/g)
f = (1/2π)√(g/l)

Important Points

  • The time period is independent of the mass of the bob and the amplitude (for small oscillations).
  • The equivalent length l is measured from the point of suspension to the centre of mass of the bob.
  • A seconds pendulum has T = 2 s, giving l = g/π² ≈ 0.994 m on Earth’s surface.
  • If the pendulum oscillates in a medium of density σ and the bob has density ρ, the effective g is replaced by g(1 − σ/ρ), giving a longer period.
  • In an accelerating lift (upward acceleration a): T = 2π√(l/(g+a)); in a freely falling lift: T → ∞ (no oscillation).
Solved Example 2

A simple pendulum has a time period of 2 s on the surface of the Earth. What will be its time period on a planet where g is 4 times that on Earth?

T = 2π√(l/g)
T is proportional to 1/√g (for the same length l)
T′/T = √(g/g′) = √(g/4g) = √(1/4) = 1/2
T′ = T/2 = 2/2 = 1 s

6. Physical (Compound) Pendulum

A physical pendulum is any rigid body that can oscillate about a horizontal axis that does not pass through its centre of mass. If the body of mass m is pivoted at a point O at a distance l from its centre of mass G, and I is the moment of inertia about the pivot, then for small oscillations:

T = 2π√(I / mgl)

Using the parallel axis theorem, I = Icm + ml², so the period becomes T = 2π√((Icm + ml²) / mgl). The equivalent simple pendulum length is Leq = I / ml = (k²/l) + l, where k is the radius of gyration about the centre of mass.

Centre of Oscillation

The centre of oscillation is a point at distance Leq from the pivot, on the opposite side of the centre of mass. If the pendulum is instead suspended from the centre of oscillation, the time period remains the same — this is the principle behind Kater’s reversible pendulum used to measure g accurately.

7. Damped & Forced Oscillations

Damped Oscillations

In real systems, dissipative forces (friction, air resistance) act on the oscillator, causing the amplitude to decrease over time. If the damping force is proportional to the velocity (Fd = −bv), the equation of motion becomes:

m(d²x/dt²) + b(dx/dt) + kx = 0
x(t) = A₀ e−(b/2m)t sin(ω′t + φ)
ω′ = √(ω₀² − (b/2m)²)   (damped angular frequency)

The amplitude decays exponentially with the damping factor b/2m. The oscillation frequency ω′ is slightly less than the natural frequency ω₀ = √(k/m). If b/2m = ω₀, the system is critically damped and returns to equilibrium without oscillating. If b/2m > ω₀, the system is overdamped.

Forced Oscillations & Resonance

When an external periodic force F = F₀ cos(ωdt) drives a damped oscillator, the system eventually oscillates at the driving frequency ωd with a steady amplitude that depends on how close ωd is to the natural frequency ω₀.

Resonance occurs when ωd ≈ ω₀, at which the amplitude of oscillation becomes maximum. The sharpness of the resonance peak depends on the damping: less damping leads to a sharper, taller peak. Resonance has important applications (musical instruments, radio tuning) and can be destructive (bridge collapse due to wind or marching soldiers).

8. Superposition of SHM

Same Frequency, Different Phase

When two SHMs of the same frequency but different amplitudes and phases are superposed along the same direction, the resultant is also SHM with the same frequency. If x₁ = A₁ sin(ωt) and x₂ = A₂ sin(ωt + δ), the resultant amplitude is:

A = √(A₁² + A₂² + 2A₁A₂ cosδ)
tanφ = (A₂ sinδ) / (A₁ + A₂ cosδ)

For δ = 0 (in phase): A = A₁ + A₂ (constructive). For δ = π (out of phase): A = |A₁ − A₂| (destructive).

Perpendicular SHM & Lissajous Figures

When two SHMs are perpendicular to each other, the resulting path traced by the particle is called a Lissajous figure. If x = A sin(ωt) and y = B sin(ωt + δ), the path depends on the phase difference δ:

  • δ = 0 or π: straight line (y = ±(B/A)x)
  • δ = π/2: ellipse; if A = B, a circle
  • Other values of δ: general ellipse

When the two frequencies are different, the Lissajous figures become more complex and their shape depends on the frequency ratio. These patterns are used in oscilloscopes to compare frequencies.

Solved Example 3

Two SHMs of equal amplitude A and same frequency are superposed along the same line with a phase difference of 120°. Find the amplitude of the resultant SHM.

AR = √(A² + A² + 2A² cos 120°)
cos 120° = −1/2
AR = √(A² + A² + 2A²(−1/2))
AR = √(A² + A² − A²)
AR = √(A²) = A

The resultant amplitude equals A, the same as each individual SHM.

Key Formulas Summary

x = A sin(ωt + φ)   |   ω = 2π/T = 2πf
v = Aω cos(ωt + φ)   |   a = −Aω² sin(ωt + φ)
Tspring = 2π√(m/k)   |   Tpendulum = 2π√(l/g)
E = ½kA² = ½mω²A²
KE = ½k(A² − x²)   |   PE = ½kx²
