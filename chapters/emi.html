04 — Electromagnetic Induction

Magnetic Flux Faraday's Law Lenz's Law Motional EMF Induced Charge Heat Dissipation Self-Inductance Inductor Combinations RL Circuit: Growth RL Circuit: Decay LC Circuit Mutual Inductance AC Generator Induced Electric Field Eddy Currents Key Formulas

1. Magnetic Flux

Fig 14.1

Definition

Magnetic flux through a surface is a measure of the total magnetic field passing through that surface. For a uniform magnetic field B passing through a flat surface of area A:

$$\Phi_B = \mathbf{B} \cdot \mathbf{A} = BA\cos\theta$$ $$\text{SI unit: Weber (Wb) = TÂ·m}^2$$

Key points about magnetic flux:

  • ΦB is maximum when B is perpendicular to the surface (θ = 0°): Φ = BA.
  • ΦB is zero when B is parallel to the surface (θ = 90°).
  • Magnetic flux can be positive, negative, or zero depending on the orientation.
  • For a non-uniform field or curved surface: Φ = ∫ B · dA.
  • Flux through any closed surface is always zero — there are no magnetic monopoles.

Flux Linkage

For a coil of N turns, the total flux linkage is N times the flux through one turn:

$$\text{Flux linkage} = N\Phi = NBA\cos\theta$$

2. Faraday's Law of Electromagnetic Induction

Fig 14.2

Faraday's Experiments

Michael Faraday discovered that a changing magnetic flux through a circuit induces an electromotive force (EMF) in the circuit. He demonstrated this through several experiments:

  • Moving a magnet towards or away from a coil produces a current in the coil.
  • Moving a coil towards or away from a stationary magnet also produces a current.
  • Changing the current in one coil (primary) induces a current in a nearby coil (secondary).
  • The induced EMF exists only as long as the flux is changing.

Statement of Faraday's Law

The magnitude of the induced EMF in a circuit is equal to the rate of change of magnetic flux linkage through the circuit. For a coil of N turns:

$$\varepsilon = -\frac{d(N\Phi)}{dt} = -N\frac{d\Phi}{dt}$$ $$\varepsilon = -N\frac{d(BA\cos\theta)}{dt}$$

The negative sign indicates that the induced EMF opposes the change in flux (Lenz's law). The flux can change due to a change in B, A, θ, or any combination of these.

Ways to Change Magnetic Flux

  • Change the magnitude of the magnetic field B.
  • Change the area A of the coil within the field.
  • Change the angle θ between the field and the area vector (rotation).
  • Move the coil into or out of the field region.

3. Lenz's Law

Fig 14.3

Statement

Lenz's law states that the direction of the induced current (or EMF) is such that it opposes the change in magnetic flux that caused it. This is a consequence of the law of conservation of energy.

Applications of Lenz's law:

  • If the magnetic flux through a coil is increasing, the induced current flows in a direction that creates a magnetic field opposing the increase (opposing the external field).
  • If the flux is decreasing, the induced current creates a field that supports the external field (opposing the decrease).
  • When a magnet's north pole approaches a coil, the face of the coil facing the magnet becomes a north pole (to repel the approaching magnet).
  • When a north pole recedes from a coil, the face becomes a south pole (to attract and oppose the recession).

Energy Conservation

Lenz's law is a manifestation of the conservation of energy. If the induced current aided the change in flux instead of opposing it, the current would further increase the flux, which would induce more current, creating a runaway process that generates energy from nothing — violating conservation of energy. The opposing nature ensures that external work must be done to maintain the change, and this work is converted into electrical energy.

4. Motional EMF

Fig 14.4

EMF in a Straight Conductor

When a straight conductor of length l moves with velocity v perpendicular to a uniform magnetic field B, an EMF is induced across its ends. The free electrons in the conductor experience a magnetic force (F = qvB), causing charge separation until the electric force balances the magnetic force.

$$\varepsilon = Blv$$ $$B = \text{magnetic field}, \quad l = \text{length}, \quad v = \text{velocity} \perp \text{ to } l \text{ and } B$$

Sliding Rod on Rails

A conducting rod slides with velocity v on two parallel rails connected by a resistance R, in a uniform magnetic field B perpendicular to the plane of the rails. The induced EMF drives a current through the circuit:

$$\varepsilon = Blv$$ $$I = \frac{Blv}{R}$$ $$F = BIl = \frac{B^2 l^2 v}{R}$$ $$P = \frac{B^2 l^2 v^2}{R} = Fv$$

Rotating Rod

A conducting rod of length l rotates about one end with angular velocity ω in a uniform magnetic field B perpendicular to the plane of rotation. Different parts of the rod have different velocities. The EMF is found by integration:

$$\varepsilon = \frac{1}{2}Bl^2\omega$$
Example 1 — Rotating Rod in Earth's Field

A metallic rod of length 1 m is rotated about one of its ends in a horizontal plane with an angular velocity of 10 rad/s. If the horizontal component of the Earth's magnetic field is 3 × 10−5 T, find the EMF induced across the ends of the rod.

Solution:
l = 1 m, ω = 10 rad/s, B = 3 × 10−5 T

$$\varepsilon = \frac{1}{2}(3 \times 10^{-5})(1)^2(10) = \frac{1}{2} \times 3 \times 10^{-4} = 1.5 \times 10^{-4} \text{ V}$$

5. Induced Charge Flow

Fig 14.5

Concept

When the magnetic flux through a circuit of resistance R changes, a current flows and a total charge is displaced. A key result is that the total charge is independent of how fast the flux changes — it depends only on the total change in flux and the resistance.

$$q = \int i \, dt = \frac{1}{R}\int \frac{d\Phi}{dt} dt = \frac{|\Delta\Phi|}{R}$$ $$q = \frac{N|\Delta\Phi|}{R} \quad \text{(N-turn coil)}$$

Physical Insight

Because q = |ΔΦ|/R does not involve time:

  • A fast flux change gives a large current over a short duration.
  • A slow flux change gives a small current over a long duration.
  • In both cases the product (current × time) — the total charge — is the same.

This principle is used in the ballistic galvanometer, which measures the total charge (and hence the flux change) by the first deflection of its needle.

Example 2 — Charge Through a Loop Pulled Out of a Field

A circular loop of radius 10 cm and resistance 5 Ω is pulled completely out of a uniform magnetic field of 1.2 T. Find the total charge that flows through the loop.

Solution:
Initial flux: Φ1 = BA = 1.2 × π(0.1)² = 0.012π Wb
Final flux: Φ2 = 0 Wb
|ΔΦ| = 0.012π Wb

q = |ΔΦ| / R = (0.012π) / 5
q = 0.0024π ≈ 7.54 × 10−3 C ≈ 7.54 mC

Note: the answer is independent of how quickly the loop was pulled out.

6. Heat Dissipation in Induced Currents

Fig 14.6

Concept

When an induced current flows through a resistive circuit, Joule heating occurs. The total heat generated depends on the resistance and the nature of the current variation. Two important cases arise:

Heat When Current is Constant

If the induced current i is held constant (e.g., by an external mechanism) while the flux changes by ΔΦ:

$$H = i|\Delta\Phi|$$

Heat from a Known Flux Change (General Case)

When a flux change ΔΦ occurs in a single-turn circuit of resistance R:

$$H = \frac{(\Delta\Phi)^2}{R}$$

Like the induced charge, this is also independent of how fast the flux changes:

  • Fast change → large current, short time → same heat.
  • Slow change → small current, long time → same heat.

Energy Stored Then Dissipated

When an inductor carrying current I0 is short-circuited, all magnetic energy stored is dissipated as heat in the resistance of the circuit:

$$H = U = \frac{1}{2}LI_0^2$$

7. Self-Inductance

Fig 14.7

Concept

When the current in a coil changes, the magnetic flux through the coil itself changes, inducing an EMF in the same coil. This phenomenon is called self-induction, and the induced EMF is called a back-EMF. The self-inductance L of a coil relates the flux linkage to the current:

$$N\Phi = LI$$ $$\varepsilon = -L\frac{dI}{dt}$$ $$\text{SI unit: Henry (H) = VÂ·s/A = Wb/A}$$

Inductance of a Solenoid

For a solenoid of N turns, length l, and cross-sectional area A:

$$B = \mu_0 n I = \mu_0 \frac{N}{l}I$$ $$L = \frac{\mu_0 N^2 A}{l} = \mu_0 n^2 A l$$ $$L = \frac{\mu N^2 A}{l} = \frac{\mu_0 \mu_r N^2 A}{l} \quad \text{(with core)}$$

Energy Stored in an Inductor

An inductor stores energy in its magnetic field. The energy stored when a current I flows through an inductance L is:

$$U = \frac{1}{2}LI^2$$

The energy density (energy per unit volume) in the magnetic field is:

$$u = \frac{B^2}{2\mu_0}$$
Example 3 — Solenoid Self-Inductance and Energy

A solenoid of length 40 cm, radius 2 cm, and 1000 turns carries a current of 5 A. Find the self-inductance and the energy stored.

Solution:
l = 0.40 m, r = 0.02 m, N = 1000, I = 5 A
$$A = \pi r^2 = \pi(0.02)^2 = 4\pi \times 10^{-4} \text{ m}^2$$ $$L = \frac{\mu_0 N^2 A}{l} = \frac{(4\pi \times 10^{-7})(10^6)(4\pi \times 10^{-4})}{0.40} = 1.58 \times 10^{-3} \text{ H}$$ $$U = \frac{1}{2}LI^2 = \frac{1}{2}(1.58 \times 10^{-3})(25) = 19.7 \text{ mJ}$$

8. Combination of Inductors

Fig 14.8

Series Combination

When inductors are connected in series (assuming no mutual coupling between them), the same current flows through each. The total back-EMF is the sum of individual back-EMFs, so the equivalent inductance is:

$$L_{\text{series}} = L_1 + L_2 + L_3 + \ldots$$

Series connection increases the total inductance. This is analogous to resistors in series.

Parallel Combination

When inductors are connected in parallel, the same voltage appears across each. The total current is the sum of individual currents, giving:

$$\frac{1}{L_{\text{parallel}}} = \frac{1}{L_1} + \frac{1}{L_2} + \frac{1}{L_3} + \ldots$$

Parallel connection decreases the total inductance. For n identical inductors of value L in parallel, Leq = L/n.

Note on Mutual Coupling

If two coils in series are placed close together so that their magnetic fields interact (mutual inductance M between them), the equivalent inductance becomes:

$$L_{\text{eq}} = L_1 + L_2 \pm 2M$$ $$(+) \text{ series-aiding, } (-) \text{ series-opposing}$$

9. RL Circuit — Growth of Current

Fig 14.9

Circuit Analysis

When a battery of EMF ε is suddenly connected to a series RL circuit (switch closed at t = 0), the inductance opposes any instantaneous change in current. Applying Kirchhoff's voltage law:

$$\varepsilon - iR - L\frac{di}{dt} = 0$$

Solving this differential equation with initial condition i(0) = 0:

$$i(t) = \frac{\varepsilon}{R}(1 - e^{-Rt/L}) = I_0(1 - e^{-t/\tau})$$ $$I_0 = \frac{\varepsilon}{R}, \quad \tau = \frac{L}{R}$$

Key Time Points

  • At t = 0: i = 0 (current cannot change instantaneously through an inductor).
  • At $t = \tau$: $i \approx 0.632 I_0$ (63.2% of steady-state).
  • At $t = 5\tau$: $i \approx 0.993 I_0$ (effectively steady state).
  • As $t \to \infty$: $i \to \varepsilon/R$ (inductor acts as short circuit).

Self-Induced EMF During Growth

The back-EMF of the inductor decreases exponentially as the current builds up:

$$\varepsilon_L = L\frac{di}{dt} = \varepsilon e^{-t/\tau}$$
Example 4 — Current Growth in RL Circuit

An RL circuit has ε = 12 V, R = 4 Ω, and L = 2 H. Find (a) the time constant, (b) the steady-state current, and (c) the current at t = τ.

Solution:
(a) τ = L/R = 2/4 = 0.5 s

(b) Steady-state current: I0 = ε/R = 12/4 = 3 A

(c) At t = τ:
i(τ) = 3(1 − e−1) = 3(1 − 0.368) = 3 × 0.632 = 1.90 A

10. RL Circuit — Decay of Current

Fig 14.10

Circuit Analysis

When the battery is suddenly disconnected from a steady-state RL circuit (with initial current I0), the inductor now acts as the source of EMF, driving the current through the circuit. Applying KVL:

$$-L\frac{di}{dt} - iR = 0$$

Solving with i(0) = I0:

$$i(t) = I_0 e^{-Rt/L} = I_0 e^{-t/\tau}$$ $$\tau = \frac{L}{R}$$

Key Features of Decay

  • At t = 0: i = I0 (current cannot drop to zero instantaneously).
  • At $t = \tau$: $i \approx 0.368 I_0$ (37% of initial value remains).
  • Energy stored in inductor $(\frac{1}{2}LI_0^2)$ is entirely dissipated as heat in R.
  • A larger L/R ratio means a slower decay (current persists longer).
Example 5 — Current Decay in RL Circuit

In an RL circuit with L = 3 H and R = 10 Ω, the steady-state current is 5 A. If the battery is suddenly disconnected (and the circuit remains closed), find the current after 0.1 s and the heat dissipated in total.

Solution:
τ = L/R = 3/10 = 0.3 s

Current at t = 0.1 s:
i(0.1) = 5 × e−0.1/0.3 = 5 × e−1/3
i(0.1) = 5 × 0.717 = 3.59 A

Total heat dissipated (all magnetic energy):
$$H = \frac{1}{2}LI_0^2 = \frac{1}{2} \times 3 \times 25 = 37.5 \text{ J}$$

11. LC Circuit — Oscillatory Behaviour

Fig 14.11

Analogy with SHM

An LC circuit (inductor L connected to capacitor C with no resistance) exhibits electromagnetic oscillations analogous to simple harmonic motion. The analogy is:

  • Charge q on capacitor ↔ displacement x
  • Current i = dq/dt ↔ velocity v = dx/dt
  • Inductance L ↔ mass m
  • 1/C ↔ spring constant k
  • Electric energy ½q²/C ↔ potential energy ½kx²
  • Magnetic energy ½Li² ↔ kinetic energy ½mv²

Differential Equation and Solution

Applying KVL to the LC loop:

$$L\frac{d^2q}{dt^2} + \frac{q}{C} = 0$$ $$q(t) = q_0\cos(\omega t + \phi), \quad \omega = \frac{1}{\sqrt{LC}}$$ $$i(t) = -\omega q_0\sin(\omega t + \phi)$$ $$I_0 = \omega q_0 = \frac{q_0}{\sqrt{LC}}$$ $$f = \frac{1}{2\pi\sqrt{LC}}$$

Energy Oscillation

In an ideal (lossless) LC circuit, total energy is conserved and oscillates between the electric field of the capacitor and the magnetic field of the inductor:

$$U_C = \frac{q^2}{2C} = \frac{q_0^2\cos^2(\omega t)}{2C}$$ $$U_L = \frac{1}{2}Li^2 = \frac{q_0^2\sin^2(\omega t)}{2C}$$ $$U_{\text{total}} = \frac{q_0^2}{2C} = \text{constant}$$

When the capacitor is fully charged, i = 0 and all energy is electric. When the capacitor is fully discharged, i is maximum and all energy is magnetic.

Example 6 — LC Oscillation Frequency

An LC circuit has L = 1 H and C = 1 μF. The capacitor is initially charged to q0 = 100 μC and the circuit is closed. Find the angular frequency, the oscillation frequency, and the maximum current.

Solution:
$$\omega = \frac{1}{\sqrt{LC}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 \times 10^{-6}}} = 1000 \text{ rad/s}$$ $$f = \frac{\omega}{2\pi} = \frac{1000}{2\pi} \approx 159 \text{ Hz}$$ $$I_0 = \omega q_0 = 1000 \times 100 \times 10^{-6} = 0.1 \text{ A}$$

12. Mutual Inductance

Fig 14.12

Concept

When the current in one coil (primary) changes, it changes the magnetic flux through a nearby coil (secondary), inducing an EMF in the secondary coil. This phenomenon is called mutual induction. The mutual inductance M between two coils relates the flux linkage in one coil due to the current in the other:

$$N_2\Phi_2 = MI_1$$ $$\varepsilon_2 = -M\frac{dI_1}{dt}$$ $$\text{SI unit: Henry (H)}$$

Mutual inductance is a reciprocal property: M12 = M21 = M. The value of M depends on the geometry of the coils, their relative orientation, and the medium between them.

Mutual Inductance of Two Coaxial Solenoids

For two coaxial solenoids, one inside the other (the inner solenoid of N1 turns, area A, length l; the outer solenoid of N2 turns, same length l):

$$M = \frac{\mu_0 N_1 N_2 A}{l}$$

Coefficient of Coupling

The coefficient of coupling k describes how effectively the two coils are magnetically linked:

$$M = k\sqrt{L_1 L_2}$$ $$0 \le k \le 1$$ $$k = 1 \text{ (perfect coupling)}, \quad k = 0 \text{ (no coupling)}$$

13. AC Generator (Induced EMF in Rotating Coil)

Fig 14.13

Principle

An AC generator (alternator) works on the principle of electromagnetic induction. A coil is rotated in a uniform magnetic field, causing a continuously changing magnetic flux through the coil, which induces an alternating EMF.

Construction and Working

The main components are: a rectangular coil (armature), a strong permanent magnet or electromagnet (field magnet), slip rings, and carbon brushes. As the coil rotates with angular velocity ω, the angle between the magnetic field and the area vector changes continuously.

Expression for Induced EMF

If the coil has N turns, area A, and rotates with angular velocity ω in a field B, the flux at time t is:

$$\Phi = NBA\cos(\omega t)$$ $$\varepsilon = -\frac{d\Phi}{dt} = NBA\omega\sin(\omega t)$$ $$\varepsilon = \varepsilon_0\sin(\omega t), \quad \varepsilon_0 = NBA\omega$$

The EMF varies sinusoidally with time. One complete rotation produces one full cycle of alternating EMF. The frequency of the AC produced equals the rotational frequency of the coil: f = ω/(2π).

Example 7 — AC Generator Output

An AC generator has a coil of 200 turns, each of area 50 cm². The coil rotates at 3000 rpm in a magnetic field of 0.2 T. Find the peak EMF and the RMS EMF generated.

Solution:
N = 200, A = 50 × 10−4 m² = 5 × 10−3 m², B = 0.2 T
Rotational speed = 3000 rpm = 3000/60 = 50 rps
$$\omega = 2\pi f = 100\pi \text{ rad/s}$$ $$\varepsilon_0 = NBA\omega = 200 \times 0.2 \times 5 \times 10^{-3} \times 100\pi = 20\pi \approx 62.83 \text{ V}$$ $$\varepsilon_{\text{rms}} = \frac{\varepsilon_0}{\sqrt{2}} = \frac{62.83}{1.414} \approx 44.43 \text{ V}$$

14. Induced Electric Field

Fig 14.14

Faraday's Law in Integral Form

A changing magnetic field does not just induce EMF in a conducting loop — it creates a real electric field in space, whether or not a conductor is present. This induced electric field is described by the integral form of Faraday's law:

$$\oint \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = -\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt}$$

The line integral of the induced electric field around any closed path equals the negative rate of change of magnetic flux through the area enclosed by that path.

Induced Field in Cylindrical Symmetry

For a cylindrical region of radius R in which a uniform magnetic field B changes at rate dB/dt, consider a circular path of radius r centred on the axis:

$$\text{Inside } (r < R): \quad E = \frac{r}{2}\left|\frac{dB}{dt}\right|$$ $$\text{Outside } (r > R): \quad E = \frac{R^2}{2r}\left|\frac{dB}{dt}\right|$$

Inside the region, E increases linearly with r. Outside, E falls off as 1/r.

Non-Conservative Nature

Unlike the electrostatic field, the induced electric field is non-conservative:

  • Its field lines form closed loops (no beginning or end — no associated scalar potential).
  • Work around closed path is non-zero: $W = q\oint \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = -q\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt}$.
  • The concept of potential difference between two points is path-dependent.
  • This non-conservative field is responsible for energy transfer in transformers.
Example 8 — Induced Electric Field Inside a Solenoid

A long solenoid of radius R = 0.2 m has its magnetic field increasing at dB/dt = 2 T/s. Find the induced electric field at a point (a) 0.1 m from the axis (inside) and (b) 0.4 m from the axis (outside).

Solution:
(a) Inside $(r = 0.1 \text{ m} < R = 0.2 \text{ m})$: $$E = \frac{0.1}{2} \times 2 = 0.1 \text{ V/m}$$ (b) Outside $(r = 0.4 \text{ m} > R = 0.2 \text{ m})$: $$E = \frac{(0.2)^2}{2 \times 0.4} \times 2 = 0.1 \text{ V/m}$$

15. Eddy Currents

Fig 14.15

Formation

When a bulk conductor (not a thin wire) is subjected to a changing magnetic flux, induced currents flow in closed loops within the body of the conductor. These circulating currents are called eddy currents (or Foucault currents). They flow in planes perpendicular to the magnetic field and follow Lenz's law.

Applications of Eddy Currents

  • Electromagnetic braking: A metallic disc rotating in a magnetic field experiences eddy currents that oppose its motion, providing a smooth braking force. Used in trains and roller coasters.
  • Induction furnace: A metallic body placed in a rapidly changing magnetic field heats up due to the Joule heating effect of eddy currents. Used for melting metals.
  • Electromagnetic damping: Eddy currents damp the oscillations of a galvanometer coil quickly (dead-beat galvanometer).
  • Induction cooktop: Alternating magnetic fields induce eddy currents in the base of a metallic cooking vessel, heating it directly.
  • Electric power meters: The aluminium disc in energy meters rotates due to eddy currents.
  • Metal detectors: Changes in eddy current patterns indicate the presence of metallic objects.

Minimization of Eddy Currents

In many devices (like transformer cores), eddy currents are undesirable as they waste energy as heat. They are minimized by:

  • Lamination: The core is made of thin, insulated sheets (laminations) stacked together. This breaks the eddy current loops into smaller loops, greatly reducing their magnitude.
  • Using materials with high resistivity for the core.
  • Using ferrite cores (ceramic magnetic materials with very high resistivity) at high frequencies.
