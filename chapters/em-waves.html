1. Displacement Current

Fig 16.1

The Problem with Ampere's Law

Ampere's circuital law in its original form states that the line integral of the magnetic field B around a closed loop equals μ0 times the conduction current I enclosed by the loop. However, James Clerk Maxwell identified a fundamental inconsistency: consider a parallel plate capacitor being charged by a current. If we draw an Amperian loop around the wire, the enclosed conduction current is I. But if we draw a surface that passes between the capacitor plates (where no charge flows), the enclosed conduction current is zero. This contradicts the uniqueness of the result, since both surfaces are bounded by the same loop.

Maxwell's Correction

Maxwell resolved this paradox by introducing the concept of displacement current. Between the capacitor plates, although no charges flow, the electric field is changing with time. Maxwell postulated that a changing electric field produces the same magnetic effects as a conduction current. He defined the displacement current as:

Id = ε0 dΦE/dt

where ΦE is the electric flux through the surface. The displacement current is not a current in the conventional sense (no charges flow); rather, it is a current-equivalent that arises from a time-varying electric field. Between the capacitor plates, the displacement current exactly equals the conduction current flowing in the connecting wires.

Ampere-Maxwell Law

With the addition of the displacement current, Ampere's law is modified to the Ampere-Maxwell law:

∮ B · dl = μ0(Ic + Id) = μ0 Ic + μ0 ε0 dΦE/dt

This modified law is consistent for all surfaces bounded by a given Amperian loop. The total current (conduction + displacement) passing through any surface bounded by the loop is always the same. This was one of Maxwell's greatest contributions to physics, as it led directly to the prediction of electromagnetic waves.

  • Displacement current exists wherever the electric field is changing with time, even in empty space.
  • In a region of space with no conduction current, a time-varying electric field still produces a magnetic field.
  • The converse is also true: a time-varying magnetic field produces an electric field (Faraday's law).
  • These two effects together sustain electromagnetic waves that propagate through free space.
  • Inside a capacitor: Ic = 0 but Id ≠ 0. Outside the capacitor plates: Ic ≠ 0 but Id = 0.

2. Maxwell's Equations

Fig 16.2

Maxwell unified electricity, magnetism, and optics into a single theoretical framework through four fundamental equations. These equations, taken together, completely describe all classical electromagnetic phenomena.

Gauss's Law for Electricity

The total electric flux through any closed surface is equal to 1/ε0 times the total charge enclosed within that surface.

∮ E · dA = Qenclosed / ε0

This law implies that electric field lines originate from positive charges and terminate on negative charges. Electric charges are the sources and sinks of electric field lines.

Gauss's Law for Magnetism

The total magnetic flux through any closed surface is always zero.

∮ B · dA = 0

This equation reflects the fact that magnetic monopoles do not exist. Magnetic field lines always form closed loops — they have no beginning or end. Every magnetic north pole is always accompanied by a south pole.

Faraday's Law of Electromagnetic Induction

A time-varying magnetic flux through a surface bounded by a closed loop induces an electromotive force (and hence an electric field) around that loop.

∮ E · dl = − dΦB/dt

The negative sign embodies Lenz's law: the induced EMF opposes the change in magnetic flux. This equation shows that a changing magnetic field creates an electric field, even in empty space.

Ampere-Maxwell Law

The line integral of the magnetic field around a closed loop equals μ0 times the sum of the conduction current and the displacement current passing through any surface bounded by the loop.

∮ B · dl = μ0 Ic + μ0 ε0 dΦE/dt

This equation shows that magnetic fields are produced both by conduction currents and by time-varying electric fields. Together, Faraday's law and the Ampere-Maxwell law establish the beautiful symmetry: a changing E creates B, and a changing B creates E. This mutual generation is the mechanism that sustains electromagnetic waves.

  • Maxwell's equations are the foundation of all classical electrodynamics, optics, and electric circuits.
  • In free space (no charges, no currents), the equations predict transverse waves travelling at speed c = 1/√(μ0ε0).
  • Maxwell calculated this speed and found it to be approximately 3 × 108 m/s, which matched the known speed of light — leading him to conclude that light is an electromagnetic wave.

3. Electromagnetic Wave Fundamentals

Fig 16.3

What Are EM Waves?

Electromagnetic waves are self-sustaining oscillations of electric and magnetic field vectors that propagate through space without requiring a material medium. The source of EM waves is an oscillating electric charge, which produces time-varying electric and magnetic fields that radiate outward. Both fields oscillate in phase with each other, maintaining a constant amplitude ratio.

Key Characteristics

  • EM waves are transverse waves: the electric field E, magnetic field B, and direction of propagation form a mutually perpendicular right-handed triad.
  • The cross product E × B points in the direction of energy flow (propagation direction).
  • In vacuum, c = 3 × 108 m/s is constant for all frequencies — independent of the source or observer's motion.
  • The ratio of electric to magnetic field amplitudes is always equal to c in vacuum: E0/B0 = c.
  • EM waves require no material medium; they propagate through vacuum.
Speed of light: c = 1 / √(μ0ε0) ≈ 3 × 108 m/s

Field amplitude ratio: E0 / B0 = c

Angular frequency: ω = 2πν

Wave number: k = 2π / λ

Wave speed: c = νλ = ω / k

Mathematical Form

For a plane wave propagating along the +x direction with E along y and B along z:

Ey = E0 sin(kx − ωt)
Bz = B0 sin(kx − ωt)

where B0 = E0 / c

The electric and magnetic fields are always in phase — they reach their maxima and minima simultaneously at any given point.

4. Electromagnetic Wave Properties

Fig 16.4

Nature of EM Waves

Electromagnetic waves are transverse waves consisting of oscillating electric and magnetic fields that are mutually perpendicular to each other and to the direction of propagation. Unlike mechanical waves (sound, water waves), EM waves do not require a material medium — they can propagate through vacuum.

Key Characteristics

  • Transverse nature: The electric field E, the magnetic field B, and the direction of propagation v are mutually perpendicular: E ⊥ B ⊥ v.
  • Self-sustaining: A time-varying electric field generates a magnetic field (Ampere-Maxwell law), and a time-varying magnetic field generates an electric field (Faraday's law). Once initiated, the wave sustains itself without any external agency.
  • Speed in vacuum: All electromagnetic waves travel at the same speed in vacuum, regardless of frequency or wavelength.
  • No charge or mass: EM waves carry no charge. They carry energy and momentum.
  • Direction of propagation: Given by the cross product E × B.
Speed of EM waves in vacuum: c = 1 / √(μ0 ε0) ≈ 3 × 108 m/s

Relation between E and B amplitudes: E0 / B0 = c

At any instant: E / B = c

Fundamental wave relation: c = f λ

Mathematical Representation

For a plane electromagnetic wave travelling in the +x direction with the electric field oscillating along y and the magnetic field along z:

Ey = E0 sin(kx − ωt)
Bz = B0 sin(kx − ωt)

where k = 2π/λ (wave number), ω = 2πf (angular frequency)
E0 = c B0

The electric and magnetic fields oscillate in phase — they reach their maxima and minima simultaneously at any given point in space.

Speed in a Medium

In a material medium with relative permittivity εr and relative permeability μr, the speed of EM waves is:

v = 1 / √(μ ε) = c / √(μr εr)

Refractive index: n = c / v = √(μr εr)
Example 1 — Finding B-field from E-field expression

An EM wave has its electric field given by E = E0 sin(kx − ωt) j (along y-direction). Write the expression for the magnetic field.

Solution:
Direction of propagation is +x. The B-field must be perpendicular to both E and the propagation direction.

Since E is along y and propagation is along x:
E × B must point along +x, so B must be along +z.

Amplitude: B0 = E0 / c

Phase and frequency match E, so:
B = (E0/c) sin(kx − ωt) k (along z)

5. Wave Parameters in Different Media

Fig 16.5

Speed in a Medium

When an EM wave enters a material medium, its speed changes depending on the electric permittivity (ε) and magnetic permeability (μ) of that medium. The refractive index relates the vacuum speed to the medium speed and is always greater than or equal to 1 for physical materials.

Speed in medium: cM = 1 / √(εμ)

Refractive index: nM = c / cM = √(εrμr)

Speed from relative values: cM = c / √(εrμr)

Wavelength in medium: λM = λ0 / nM

Frequency in medium: νM = ν (unchanged across media)

Important Notes

  • Frequency remains constant when a wave crosses from one medium to another; only speed and wavelength change.
  • Refractive index is typically greater than 1 for materials, meaning the wave slows down.
  • For non-magnetic materials (μr ≈ 1), the refractive index simplifies to n ≈ √εr.
  • Permittivity and permeability are material-specific properties that determine how the medium interacts with EM fields.
Example 2 — Wavelength change on entering a medium

An EM wave has a wavelength of 0.67 m in vacuum. It enters a medium with refractive index n = 1.5. Find the wavelength in the medium and the speed of the wave in the medium.

Solution:
Given: λ0 = 0.67 m, n = 1.5

Wavelength in medium:
λM = λ0 / n = 0.67 / 1.5 = 0.447 m

Speed in medium:
cM = c / n = (3 × 108) / 1.5 = 2 × 108 m/s

Frequency is unchanged: ν = c / λ0 = (3 × 108) / 0.67 ≈ 4.48 × 108 Hz

6. Energy & Momentum of EM Waves

Fig 16.6

Energy Density

Electromagnetic waves carry energy. The energy is stored equally in the oscillating electric and magnetic fields. The energy density (energy per unit volume) at any point is:

Energy density in E field: uE = ½ ε0 E²
Energy density in B field: uB = B² / (2μ0)
Total energy density: u = uE + uB = ε0 E² = B² / μ0

Average energy density: uavg = (1/4)ε0E0² = (1/4μ0)B0²

Since uE = uB at every instant, the electric and magnetic fields contribute equally to the total energy of an EM wave.

Intensity

The intensity of an EM wave is the average energy crossing a unit area perpendicular to the direction of propagation per unit time. It equals the average energy density multiplied by the speed of the wave.

Intensity: I = ½ ε0 c E0²

Also: I = c <u> = ½ ε0 c E0² = E0² / (2μ0c) = c B0² / (2μ0)

Using Erms: I = ε0 c Erms²

Poynting vector magnitude: S = (E0B0) / (2μ0)

Momentum & Radiation Pressure

Electromagnetic waves carry momentum as well as energy. When an EM wave is absorbed by a surface, it transfers momentum to the surface, exerting a force (and hence a pressure) called radiation pressure. The momentum carried by an EM wave of energy U is:

Momentum: p = U / c   (total absorption)
Momentum: p = 2U / c   (total reflection)

Radiation pressure (absorption): P = I / c
Radiation pressure (reflection): P = 2I / c

The factor of 2 for reflection arises because the incoming wave has momentum U/c in one direction, and the reflected wave has momentum U/c in the opposite direction, so the total momentum change is 2U/c.

  • Radiation pressure is very small for ordinary light sources but can be significant for intense laser beams or in astrophysical contexts (e.g., radiation pressure from stars affects interstellar dust).
  • The concept of radiation pressure provides direct evidence that EM waves carry momentum.
Example 3 — Intensity and electric field amplitude of a laser

A laser beam has a cross-sectional area of 2 mm² and a power of 1 mW. Calculate: (a) the intensity of the beam, (b) the amplitude of the electric field, and (c) the radiation pressure on a perfectly reflecting surface.

Solution:
Power P = 1 mW = 1 × 10−3 W, Area A = 2 mm² = 2 × 10−6 m²

(a) Intensity:
I = P / A = (1 × 10−3) / (2 × 10−6) = 500 W/m²

(b) Amplitude of electric field:
I = ½ ε0 c E0²
E0² = 2I / (ε0 c) = 2 × 500 / (8.85 × 10−12 × 3 × 108)
E0² = 1000 / (2.655 × 10−3) = 3.77 × 105
E0 ≈ 614 V/m

(c) Radiation pressure (perfectly reflecting surface):
Prad = 2I / c = 2 × 500 / (3 × 108) = 3.33 × 10−6 Pa

Example 4 — Peak electric field and wave parameters

The electric field in a plane electromagnetic wave is given by E = 300 sin(5 × 106 t − 1.67 × 10−2 x) V/m. Find the amplitude of the magnetic field, the frequency, the wavelength, and the speed of the wave.

Solution:
Comparing with E = E0 sin(ωt − kx):
E0 = 300 V/m, ω = 5 × 106 rad/s, k = 1.67 × 10−2 rad/m

Amplitude of magnetic field:
B0 = E0 / c = 300 / (3 × 108) = 10−6 T = 1 μT

Frequency:
f = ω / (2π) = 5 × 106 / (2π) ≈ 7.96 × 105 Hz ≈ 796 kHz

Wavelength:
λ = 2π / k = 2π / (1.67 × 10−2) ≈ 376 m

Speed:
v = ω / k = 5 × 106 / (1.67 × 10−2) ≈ 3 × 108 m/s = c

7. Electromagnetic Spectrum

Fig 16.7

The electromagnetic spectrum is the entire range of electromagnetic radiation, classified by frequency (or equivalently, wavelength). All EM waves travel at the same speed in vacuum (c = 3 × 108 m/s) but differ in frequency and wavelength. The spectrum is continuous — there are no sharp boundaries between regions. The classification is based on the method of production and detection.

Radio Waves

Frequency: 3 × 103 Hz to ~109 Hz.
Wavelength: > 0.3 m (can be kilometres long).
Source: Oscillating circuits (LC oscillators), antennas.
Uses: AM/FM radio broadcasting, television signals, radar, cellular communication.
Detection: Antennas, tuned receivers.

Microwaves

Frequency: 109 Hz to 1012 Hz (1 GHz to 1 THz).
Wavelength: 0.3 m to 0.3 mm.
Source: Klystrons, magnetrons, Gunn diodes.
Uses: Microwave ovens (2.45 GHz resonance of water molecules), satellite communication, Wi-Fi, Bluetooth, RADAR.
Detection: Point-contact diodes, bolometers.

Infrared Radiation

Frequency: 1012 Hz to 4 × 1014 Hz.
Wavelength: 0.3 mm to 750 nm.
Source: Hot bodies, sun, incandescent lamps, LED devices.
Uses: Night vision equipment, TV remote controls, physiotherapy, greenhouse heating, thermal imaging.
Detection: Thermopiles, bolometers, infrared photographic film.

Visible Light

Frequency: 4 × 1014 Hz to 7.5 × 1014 Hz.
Wavelength: 750 nm (red) to 400 nm (violet).
Source: Sun, stars, lamps, lasers, LEDs.
Uses: Vision, photography, illumination, optical communication (fibre optics).
Detection: Human eye, photocells, photographic film.

Ultraviolet Radiation

Frequency: 8 × 1014 Hz to 1017 Hz.
Wavelength: 400 nm to 1 nm.
Source: Sun, mercury vapour lamps, very hot bodies (T > 3000 K).
Uses: Sterilization, LASIK eye surgery, water purification, detection of forged documents, fluorescence studies.
Detection: Photocells, UV sensitive photographic film.

X-Rays

Frequency: 1017 Hz to 1019 Hz.
Wavelength: 10 nm to 10−3 nm.
Source: Bombardment of high-energy electrons on heavy metal targets (Coolidge tube), synchrotron radiation.
Uses: Medical diagnostics (bone fractures), CT scans, crystallography (Bragg diffraction), airport security screening, industrial quality control.
Detection: Photographic plates, Geiger-Muller counters, fluorescent screens.

Gamma Rays

Frequency: > 1019 Hz.
Wavelength: < 10−3 nm (less than 10−12 m).
Source: Nuclear reactions (radioactive decay), cosmic events, matter-antimatter annihilation.
Uses: Cancer treatment (radiotherapy), food irradiation, sterilization of medical equipment, study of nuclear structure.
Detection: Geiger-Muller counters, scintillation detectors.

Key points for the exam:

  • The order of increasing frequency: Radio < Microwave < Infrared < Visible < Ultraviolet < X-rays < Gamma rays.
  • The order of increasing wavelength is the reverse of the above.
  • All EM waves travel at speed c in vacuum. In a medium, the speed is less than c.
  • The photon energy E = hf = hc/λ increases with frequency. Hence, gamma rays are the most energetic and radio waves are the least energetic.
  • Photon energy: E = hf where h = 6.63 × 10−34 J·s (Planck's constant).

8. Magnetic Field Lines and Gauss's Law for Magnetism

Fig 16.8

Properties of Magnetic Field Lines

Magnetic field lines are a visual representation of the magnetic field in space. They have several distinguishing properties that set them apart from electric field lines. Most importantly, magnetic field lines always form closed loops — they have no starting or ending point. This is a direct consequence of the non-existence of magnetic monopoles.

  • Magnetic field lines form continuous closed loops with no beginning or end.
  • The density of field lines at a point represents the strength of the magnetic field at that point.
  • Magnetic field lines never cross or intersect each other.
  • Inside a bar magnet, field lines run from the south pole to the north pole. Outside, they run from north to south.
  • The direction at any point is the direction in which a north pole would tend to move (or the direction a compass needle would point).

Gauss's Law for Magnetism

The total magnetic flux through any closed surface is always zero. This is a formal statement of the fact that magnetic monopoles do not exist:

∮ B · ds = 0

This means: No net magnetic flux can leave any closed surface.
Every field line that enters a closed surface must also exit it.

Field of a Bar Magnet

At large distances r from the centre of a bar magnet with magnetic moment m, the field can be approximated as that of a magnetic dipole:

On axis (axial point): B = (μ0 / 4π) × (2m / r³)

On equatorial line: B = (μ0 / 4π) × (m / r³)

Inside a long solenoid: B = μ0nI   (n = turns per unit length)
  • No isolated magnetic poles exist — you cannot create a magnetic monopole by breaking a magnet; each half becomes a complete dipole.
  • Axial field is twice the equatorial field at the same distance from the dipole centre.
  • Magnetic field falls off as 1/r³ at large distances from a dipole.

9. Magnetic Moment and Bar Magnet Properties

Fig 16.9

Magnetic Moment of a Bar Magnet

The magnetic moment of a bar magnet is a vector quantity that characterises the strength and orientation of the magnet. It is defined as the product of the pole strength and the magnetic length (the effective separation between the poles):

Magnetic moment: M = m × 2l
  where m = pole strength (A·m), 2l = magnetic length (m)

Pole strength: m = M / (2l)

Relation between lengths: Magnetic length / Geometric length ≈ 0.84

Axial field (far): B = (μ0 / 4π)(2M / r³)

Equatorial field (far): B = (μ0 / 4π)(M / r³)

Key Properties

  • The SI unit of magnetic moment is A·m² (ampere metre squared), also expressed as J/T.
  • Magnetic moment is a vector quantity directed from the south pole to the north pole of the magnet.
  • A bar magnet produces the same external field as an equivalent current-carrying solenoid at large distances.
  • The effective magnetic length (2l) is about 84% of the geometric (physical) length of the magnet.
  • The far-field behavior depends only on the total dipole moment, not on individual pole strengths or the separation independently.
Example 5 — Magnetic moment and field of a bar magnet

A bar magnet has a magnetic length of 4 cm and a pole strength of 10 A·m. Find: (a) the magnetic moment, and (b) the magnetic field at a point 20 cm from the centre on the axial line.

Solution:
Given: m = 10 A·m, 2l = 0.04 m, r = 0.20 m

(a) Magnetic moment:
M = m × 2l = 10 × 0.04 = 0.4 A·m²

(b) Axial magnetic field:
B = (μ0 / 4π) × (2M / r³)
B = 10−7 × (2 × 0.4) / (0.20)³
B = 10−7 × 0.8 / (8 × 10−3)
B = 10−7 × 100 = 10−5 T = 0.1 G

10. Magnetic Dipole in a Uniform Field

Fig 16.10

Torque on a Magnetic Dipole

When a magnetic dipole (bar magnet) is placed in a uniform external magnetic field, the field exerts equal and opposite forces on the two poles. These forces constitute a couple that produces a torque on the dipole, tending to align it with the field. The torque depends on the angle θ between the magnetic moment vector and the field direction:

Torque: τ = M × B = MB sinθ

Potential energy: U = −M·B = −MB cosθ

Equilibrium positions:
  θ = 0° (stable, U = −MB, minimum energy)
  θ = 180° (unstable, U = +MB, maximum energy)

Oscillation of a Magnetic Dipole

When a magnetic dipole is displaced slightly from its stable equilibrium position (θ = 0) and released, it undergoes simple harmonic motion. For small angles, sinθ ≈ θ, and the restoring torque becomes proportional to the angular displacement:

Equation of motion: d²θ/dt² = −(MB/I)θ

Angular frequency: ω = √(MB/I)

Time period: T = 2π√(I/(MB))

where I = moment of inertia of the magnet about its pivot axis
  • The period of oscillation increases with the moment of inertia (heavier or longer magnets oscillate more slowly).
  • The period decreases in a stronger magnetic field (the dipole oscillates more rapidly).
  • This principle is used in vibration magnetometers to compare magnetic moments or measure field strengths.

11. Earth's Magnetic Field and Elements

Fig 16.11

Origin of Earth's Magnetic Field

Earth behaves like a giant magnetic dipole. Its magnetic field is believed to arise from convective motion of molten iron and nickel in the Earth's outer core (the dynamo effect). This sets up circulating electric currents that generate a dipole-like magnetic field. The magnetic north pole of Earth is located near the geographic south pole, and vice versa.

Elements of Earth's Magnetic Field

Three quantities (called magnetic elements) completely describe Earth's field at any location:

  • Declination (θ): The angle between the geographic meridian (true north) and the magnetic meridian at that location. It tells us how much a compass deviates from true geographic north.
  • Inclination (Angle of Dip, δ): The angle that the total magnetic field vector makes with the horizontal plane. At the magnetic equator, δ = 0°. At the magnetic poles, δ = 90°.
  • Horizontal Component (H): The component of Earth's total field in the horizontal plane, pointing toward the magnetic north.
Total field: B = √(H² + Z²)

Vertical component: Z = B sinδ
Horizontal component: H = B cosδ

Angle of dip: tanδ = Z / H

Earth's field magnitude ≈ 3 × 10−5 T to 6 × 10−5 T (varies by location)

Important Points

  • The magnetic equator is the line on Earth's surface where the dip angle is zero.
  • The geographic and magnetic poles do not coincide; the angular difference is the declination.
  • Earth's magnetic field reverses direction on geological timescales (polarity reversals).
  • At the geographic poles, the dip angle is not exactly 90° because the magnetic poles are not at the geographic poles.

12. Magnetization and Magnetic Intensity

Fig 16.12

Three Key Magnetic Quantities

When a material is placed in a magnetic field, the material's response must be accounted for. Three related but distinct magnetic quantities describe this:

  • Magnetization M: The net magnetic moment per unit volume of the material, measured in A/m. It represents how strongly the material has been magnetized by the external field.
  • Magnetic Intensity H: A field quantity that represents the applied (external) field, independent of the material's response. It equals B/μ0 − M.
  • Magnetic Induction B: The total field inside the material, including both the external field and the field due to the magnetized material itself.
Magnetization: M = mnet / V   (net magnetic moment / volume)

Relation: B = μ0(H + M)

In vacuum (M = 0): B = μ0H

Magnetic susceptibility: χ = M / H   (dimensionless)

Relative permeability: μr = 1 + χ

Field in material: B = μ0μrH = μH

Significance of Susceptibility

  • Susceptibility χ measures how easily a material magnetizes in an applied field.
  • Positive χ: material is attracted to field (paramagnetic or ferromagnetic).
  • Negative χ: material is repelled by field (diamagnetic).
  • For linear materials, M is proportional to H: M = χH.
  • The permeability μ = μ0μr = μ0(1 + χ) relates B and H inside the material.

13. Classification of Magnetic Materials

Fig 16.13

Based on their response to an external magnetic field, materials are classified into three broad categories: diamagnetic, paramagnetic, and ferromagnetic.

Diamagnetic Materials

Diamagnetic materials are weakly repelled by magnetic fields. When placed in an external field, they develop a small magnetization opposite to the field direction. This arises from the orbital motion of electrons being slightly altered by the applied field.

  • Susceptibility: χ is small and negative (approximately −10−5 to 0).
  • Relative permeability: μr slightly less than 1.
  • Magnetic field lines are pushed out of a diamagnetic material.
  • Examples: Bismuth (Bi), Copper (Cu), Water (H2O), Gold (Au), NaCl.
  • Superconductors are perfect diamagnets: χ = −1, μr = 0. They expel all magnetic flux (Meissner effect).
  • Diamagnetism is temperature-independent.

Paramagnetic Materials

Paramagnetic materials are weakly attracted by magnetic fields. They have atoms or molecules with permanent magnetic dipole moments (due to unpaired electrons). In the absence of a field, thermal agitation randomizes these dipole orientations. An applied field partially aligns the dipoles, producing a small net magnetization in the field direction.

  • Susceptibility: χ is small and positive (approximately 10−5 to 10−3).
  • Relative permeability: μr slightly greater than 1.
  • Magnetization decreases with temperature (Curie's law: χ = C/T, where C is the Curie constant).
  • Examples: Aluminium (Al), Platinum (Pt), Oxygen (O2), Sodium (Na), Manganese (Mn).

Ferromagnetic Materials

Ferromagnetic materials are strongly attracted by magnetic fields and can retain their magnetism after the external field is removed. They exhibit magnetic domains — regions where atomic magnetic moments are aligned. An applied field causes domain walls to move and domains aligned with the field to grow.

  • Susceptibility: χ is very large and positive (102 to 105 or more).
  • Relative permeability: μr much greater than 1 (can be thousands).
  • Above the Curie temperature, ferromagnets become paramagnetic (domain structure destroyed).
  • Examples: Iron (Fe), Nickel (Ni), Cobalt (Co), and their alloys (alnico, permalloy).
  • Ferromagnetism is responsible for permanent magnets and electromagnets.
Curie's law (paramagnetism): χ = C / T
  C = Curie constant (material-specific), T = temperature in kelvin

Curie-Weiss law (ferromagnetism, above TC): χ = C / (T − TC)
  TC = Curie temperature (material-specific)

14. Hysteresis in Ferromagnetic Materials

Fig 16.14

The B-H Curve and Hysteresis Loop

When a ferromagnetic material is subjected to a cyclic variation of the applied magnetic intensity H (from a large positive value to a large negative value and back), the magnetic induction B in the material does not retrace the same path. Instead, B lags behind H. This phenomenon is called hysteresis (from the Greek word for "lagging behind"). The closed curve traced on a B-H graph during one complete cycle is called the hysteresis loop.

Key Parameters from the Hysteresis Loop

  • Saturation Magnetization: The maximum value of B reached when all domains are aligned with the applied field.
  • Retentivity (Remanence, Br): The value of B remaining in the material when H is reduced back to zero. It measures the material's ability to retain magnetism.
  • Coercivity (Hc): The reverse field required to reduce B back to zero. It measures the material's resistance to demagnetization.
  • Energy dissipation: The area enclosed by the B-H loop equals the energy dissipated as heat per unit volume per cycle. A wider loop means greater energy loss per cycle.
Energy dissipated per cycle per unit volume = Area of B−H loop

Retentivity Br: value of B when H = 0 (after saturation)

Coercivity Hc: magnitude of H when B = 0 (after retentivity)

Relative permeability at operating point: μr = B / (μ0H)

Hard vs. Soft Magnetic Materials

The shape of the B-H loop determines the practical application of the material:

  • Hard ferromagnets (wide loop, high retentivity, high coercivity): Used for permanent magnets. Examples: steel, alnico, samarium-cobalt, neodymium-iron-boron. Once magnetized, they are difficult to demagnetize.
  • Soft ferromagnets (narrow loop, low retentivity, low coercivity): Used for transformer cores, electromagnet cores, and motor cores where the field must be switched rapidly. Examples: soft iron, silicon steel, permalloy. They are easy to magnetize and demagnetize.
  • Heating a ferromagnet above its Curie temperature destroys ferromagnetism permanently (until cooled again).

15. Permanent Magnets and Electromagnets

Fig 16.15

Permanent Magnets

Permanent magnets are made from hard ferromagnetic materials that retain their magnetization indefinitely after the magnetizing field is removed. Their key properties are high retentivity (so they retain strong magnetism) and high coercivity (so they resist demagnetization by stray fields, vibrations, or moderate heating).

  • Materials: Steel, alnico (Al, Ni, Co alloy), samarium-cobalt (SmCo5), neodymium-iron-boron (NdFeB — the strongest common permanent magnet material).
  • Applications: Loudspeakers, electric motors, generators, MRI machines, refrigerator magnets, hard disk drives.
  • Permanent magnets can be demagnetized by heating above the Curie temperature, mechanical shock, or exposure to a strong opposing field.

Electromagnets

Electromagnets are made from soft ferromagnetic materials (cores) wound with current-carrying coils. They can produce very strong magnetic fields when current flows, and they lose their magnetism when the current is switched off. The key requirements are low retentivity (so the magnet turns off cleanly) and low coercivity (so it can be easily magnetized and demagnetized).

Field inside solenoid electromagnet: B = μ0μrnI
  n = turns per unit length, I = current, μr = relative permeability of core

Choosing core: low hysteresis loss (narrow B−H loop) is essential
for AC applications (transformers, motors) to minimize energy loss.
  • Materials: Soft iron, silicon steel, permalloy, ferrites (for high-frequency applications).
  • Applications: Electric bells, relays, motors, transformers, cranes (for lifting scrap iron), MRI (superconducting electromagnets).
  • The strength of an electromagnet can be controlled by adjusting the current.

Comparison: Permanent Magnets vs. Electromagnets

  • Permanent magnet: No power required; always "on"; cannot be switched off easily; field strength fixed by material and geometry.
  • Electromagnet: Requires continuous power; field can be switched on/off and varied; field strength controllable; typically much stronger fields achievable with superconducting coils.

Key Formulas Summary

Electromagnetic Waves

Speed in vacuum: c = 1 / √(μ0ε0) = 3 × 108 m/s

Field ratio: E0 / B0 = c

Wave number: k = 2π / λ    Angular frequency: ω = 2πν

Wave speed: c = νλ = ω / k

Speed in medium: cM = c / √(εrμr)    Refractive index: n = c / cM = √(εrμr)

Wavelength in medium: λM = λ0 / n

Energy and Intensity

Energy density: u = ε0E² = B² / μ0    (uE = uB always)

Average energy density: uavg = (1/4)ε0E0² = B0² / (4μ0)

Intensity: I = (1/2)ε0cE0² = cB0² / (2μ0) = E0B0 / (2μ0)

Radiation momentum (absorbed): p = U/c    Radiation pressure (absorbed): P = I/c

Radiation momentum (reflected): p = 2U/c    Radiation pressure (reflected): P = 2I/c

Displacement Current

Displacement current: Id = ε0(dΦE/dt)

Ampere-Maxwell law: ∮B·dl = μ0(Ic + Id) = μ0Ic + μ0ε0(dΦE/dt)

Magnetism and Dipole

Magnetic moment: M = m × 2l   (pole strength × magnetic length)

Dipole fields: Baxial = (μ0/4π)(2M/r³)    Bequatorial = (μ0/4π)(M/r³)

Torque on dipole: τ = MB sinθ

Potential energy: U = −MB cosθ

Period of oscillation: T = 2π√(I/(MB))   (I = moment of inertia)

Earth's Magnetic Field

Total field: B = √(H² + Z²)

Angle of dip: tanδ = Z/H

Components: H = B cosδ    Z = B sinδ

Magnetization and Materials

Magnetic induction: B = μ0(H + M)

Susceptibility: χ = M/H    Relative permeability: μr = 1 + χ

Curie's law (paramagnetism): χ = C/T

Diamagnetic: χ < 0    Paramagnetic: 0 < χ < 1    Ferromagnetic: χ ≫ 1

Solenoid magnetic moment: M = NIA   (N = total turns, I = current, A = area)
