1. Pressure in a Stationary Liquid

Pressure is defined as the normal force acting per unit area of a surface. It is a scalar quantity (though force is a vector, pressure acts equally in all directions at a point in a static fluid).

P = F / A

The SI unit of pressure is the Pascal (Pa), where 1 Pa = 1 N/m². Other common units include atmosphere (atm), bar, and mm of Hg (torr).

Key Pressure Definitions

  • Atmospheric Pressure: The pressure exerted by the Earth’s atmosphere at sea level. 1 atm = 1.013 × 105 Pa = 760 mm Hg.
  • Gauge Pressure: The pressure measured above atmospheric pressure. Pgauge = P − Patm.
  • Absolute Pressure: The total pressure at a point, including atmospheric pressure. Pabs = Patm + Pgauge.

2. Thrust on a Surface

Thrust is the total force exerted by a fluid on a surface. For uniform pressure on a horizontal surface (e.g., the bottom of a tank), thrust is simply the pressure multiplied by the area. For non-uniform pressure (e.g., on a vertical wall), we must integrate the pressure over the entire surface.

Thrust on Horizontal Surfaces

F = PA

For a horizontal surface at depth h, the pressure is P = ρgh (assuming gauge pressure), so the thrust is F = ρghA.

Thrust on Vertical Walls

For a vertical wall submerged in a liquid, the pressure increases linearly with depth. Consider a small area dA at depth y. The pressure at that depth is P(y) = ρgy, and the thrust on the small area is dF = P(y) dA = ρgy dA. Integrating over the entire wall of height h:

F = ∫₀h ρgy dA = ρg × h² / 2

The point of application of this thrust (center of pressure) is located at a depth of 2h/3 below the free surface, not at the centroid (which would be at h/2).

Example: Tank Thrust

A vertical rectangular tank wall is 4 m wide and 5 m deep, filled with water (ρ = 1000 kg/m³). Calculate the total thrust on the wall and the depth of its center of pressure.

Solution:
Total thrust: F = ρg h² × width / 2
F = 1000 × 9.8 × 25 × 4 / 2
F = 1000 × 9.8 × 50
F = 490,000 N = 490 kN

Depth of center of pressure: d = 2h/3 = 2 × 5 / 3 = 3.33 m below surface

3. Hydraulic Press & Pascal’s Law

Pascal’s Law states that a change in pressure applied to an enclosed fluid is transmitted undiminished to every portion of the fluid and to the walls of the containing vessel. This principle is the foundation of hydraulic systems.

Hydraulic Lift / Press

In a hydraulic system, a small force applied on a small-area piston produces a large force on a large-area piston. Since pressure is transmitted equally:

F₁ / A₁ = F₂ / A₂

F₂ = F₁ × (A₂ / A₁)

This is the principle behind hydraulic brakes, hydraulic lifts, and hydraulic presses. The mechanical advantage is the ratio A₂/A₁. Although the force is amplified, the work done on both sides is the same (conservation of energy): F₁d₁ = F₂d₂.

Example 1: Hydraulic Lift

In a hydraulic lift, the area of the small piston is 10 cm² and the area of the large piston is 1000 cm². If a force of 50 N is applied on the small piston, what is the maximum weight that can be lifted by the large piston?

Solution:
By Pascal’s Law: F₁/A₁ = F₂/A₂

F₂ = F₁ × (A₂ / A₁)
F₂ = 50 × (1000 / 10)
F₂ = 50 × 100
F₂ = 5000 N

The large piston can lift a maximum weight of 5000 N (approximately 510 kg).

4. Archimedes’ Principle & Buoyancy

Archimedes’ Principle states that when a body is partially or wholly immersed in a fluid, it experiences an upward buoyant force equal to the weight of the fluid displaced by the body.

Fb = ρfluid × Vdisplaced × g

Floating and Sinking Conditions

  • If ρbody < ρfluid: Body floats (partially submerged). The fraction submerged equals ρbody/ρfluid.
  • If ρbody = ρfluid: Body is in neutral equilibrium (floats fully submerged at any depth).
  • If ρbody > ρfluid: Body sinks.

Apparent Weight

The apparent weight of a body immersed in a fluid is less than its true weight by the amount of the buoyant force:

Wapparent = W − Fb = (ρbody − ρfluid)Vg

5. Floating Bodies

For a floating body in equilibrium, the buoyant force equals the weight of the body. If the body has total volume V and density ρbody, and the fluid has density ρfluid, the fraction of the body that is submerged is determined by the balance of forces.

Floating Condition

Vsubmerged × ρfluid = V × ρbody

Fraction submerged = ρbody / ρfluid
Fraction above surface = 1 − ρbody / ρfluid

Two Immiscible Liquids

When a body floats on two immiscible liquids (e.g., oil and water), the buoyancy from both liquids must be considered:

V₁ × ρ₁ + V₂ × ρ₂ = V × ρbody

where V₁ and V₂ are the volumes immersed in liquids 1 and 2 respectively.

Hydrometer

A hydrometer is a floating instrument used to measure the density or relative density of a liquid. It sinks deeper in less dense liquids and floats higher in denser liquids, with a calibrated scale indicating the density.

Example: Sphere in Two Liquids

A wooden sphere of volume 0.1 m³ and density 600 kg/m³ is floating on water (ρ = 1000 kg/m³). What is the volume of the sphere that is submerged?

Solution:
Using the floating condition:
Vsubmerged × ρwater = V × ρsphere
Vsubmerged × 1000 = 0.1 × 600
Vsubmerged = 60 / 1000 = 0.06 m³

Fraction submerged = 0.06 / 0.1 = 0.6 or 60%
Fraction above water = 1 − 0.6 = 0.4 or 40%

6. Equation of Continuity

For a steady, incompressible fluid flow through a tube of varying cross-section, the mass flow rate must remain constant. Since the fluid is incompressible (ρ = constant), the volume flow rate is conserved:

A₁v₁ = A₂v₂ = Q (constant)

This means that where the tube narrows (smaller A), the fluid speeds up (larger v), and vice versa. This is why water from a garden hose moves faster when you partially cover the opening with your thumb.

Streamline vs. Turbulent Flow

  • Streamline (Laminar) Flow: Fluid particles follow smooth, well-defined paths (streamlines). Adjacent layers slide smoothly past each other. Occurs at low velocities.
  • Turbulent Flow: Fluid particles move in irregular, chaotic paths with eddies and vortices. Occurs at high velocities.

7. Bernoulli’s Theorem

Bernoulli’s theorem is a statement of conservation of energy for a flowing fluid. For an ideal (incompressible, non-viscous) fluid in steady flow, the sum of pressure energy, kinetic energy, and potential energy per unit volume remains constant along a streamline:

P + ½ρv² + ρgh = constant

This equation shows that where the fluid velocity increases (as in a constriction), the pressure decreases, and vice versa. This is the principle behind many applications in fluid dynamics.

Applications of Bernoulli's Theorem

  • Venturi Meter: A device to measure fluid flow rate. The pressure drop across a constriction is related to the velocity (and hence flow rate) by Bernoulli’s equation.
  • Lift on an Airfoil: Air moves faster over the curved upper surface of an airplane wing than the flat lower surface, creating lower pressure above and higher pressure below — resulting in an upward lift force.
  • Magnus Effect: A spinning ball moving through air experiences a lateral force because the spin creates a pressure difference on opposite sides of the ball, causing it to curve.
  • Atomiser / Spray Gun: High-speed air flowing over a tube reduces pressure at the top, drawing liquid up and atomising it into a fine spray.
Example 2: Bernoulli's Equation

Water flows through a horizontal pipe of varying cross-section. At a point where the cross-sectional area is 40 cm², the velocity is 2 m/s and the pressure is 3 × 104 Pa. Find the pressure at another point where the area is 20 cm². (Density of water = 1000 kg/m³)

Solution:
Step 1: Find v₂ using the equation of continuity:
A₁v₁ = A₂v₂
40 × 2 = 20 × v₂
v₂ = 4 m/s

Step 2: Apply Bernoulli’s equation (horizontal pipe, so h₁ = h₂):
P₁ + ½ρv₁² = P₂ + ½ρv₂²
P₂ = P₁ + ½ρ(v₁² − v₂²)
P₂ = 3 × 104 + ½ × 1000 × (4 − 16)
P₂ = 30,000 + 500 × (−12)
P₂ = 30,000 − 6,000
P₂ = 24,000 Pa = 2.4 × 104 Pa

The pressure at the narrower section is 2.4 × 104 Pa, confirming that where velocity increases, pressure decreases.

8. Venturi Meter

A Venturi meter is a device used to measure the flow rate of a fluid in a pipe. It consists of a constriction (narrow tube) connected to the main pipe. By measuring the pressure difference between the main pipe and the constriction, we can determine the flow velocity and hence the volume flow rate using Bernoulli's equation.

Venturi Meter with Same Liquid Manometer

When the manometer fluid is the same as the flowing liquid, the pressure difference ΔP is directly related to the height difference Δh in the manometer:

Q = A₁ A₂ √(2gΔh / (A₁² − A₂²))

where A₁ is the area of the main pipe, A₂ is the area of the constriction, and Δh is the height difference in the manometer.

Venturi Meter with Different Manometer Liquid

If the manometer fluid has a different density ρm than the flowing liquid with density ρ:

Q = A₁ A₂ √(2gΔhρm / (ρ(A₁² − A₂²)))

9. Pitot Tube

A Pitot tube is a simple device used to measure the velocity of a fluid (liquid or gas). It consists of a tube bent at right angles, with one end pointing upstream into the flow. The fluid decelerates at the stagnation point (nose of the tube), and the pressure increase is related to the velocity by Bernoulli's equation.

Pitot Tube for Liquids

For a liquid flowing with velocity V, using Bernoulli's equation between the stagnation point and the free stream:

V = √(2gh)

where h is the height difference in the manometer connected to the Pitot tube.

Pitot Tube for Gases

For gases (where density is much lower), the pressure difference must account for the gas density ρ and the manometer liquid density ρm:

V = √(2ghρm / ρ)
Example: Pitot Tube in Water

A Pitot tube is inserted into a water pipe. The manometer shows a height difference of 0.5 m. What is the velocity of water in the pipe?

Solution:
Using the Pitot tube formula for liquids:
V = √(2gh)
V = √(2 × 9.8 × 0.5)
V = √(9.8)
V = 3.13 m/s

10. Velocity of Efflux (Torricelli’s Theorem)

When a liquid flows out of a hole in the bottom or side of a container, the velocity of efflux (the speed at which it leaves) can be found using Bernoulli's equation. Torricelli’s theorem states that the velocity of efflux through a small hole is the same as the velocity that a body would acquire if falling freely from a height h equal to the depth of the hole.

Efflux Velocity

V = √(2gh)

where h is the height of the liquid column above the hole.

Horizontal Range of Efflux

When liquid effluxes horizontally from a hole at height h below the surface, it travels as a projectile. The horizontal range R (for a hole at height h below the surface of a container of height H) is:

R = 2√(h(H − h))

The range is maximum when h = H/2, giving Rmax = H.

Reaction Force

The jet of liquid exiting creates a reaction force on the container. If the liquid exits with velocity V from an orifice of area a, and the container has liquid density ρ:

F = 2ρagh

Time to Empty

For a container of cross-sectional area A with a hole of area a at the bottom, the time to completely empty a liquid column of height H is:

t = (A / a) √(2H / g)

Sealed Container with Pressure

If the container is sealed and has pressure P₀ inside (with external pressure Patm):

V = √(2gh + 2(P₀ − Patm) / ρ)
Example: Tank Efflux

A water tank of height 10 m and cross-sectional area 2 m² has a hole of area 0.01 m² at the bottom. How long will it take to empty the tank?

Solution:
Using the time to empty formula:
t = (A / a) √(2H / g)
t = (2 / 0.01) × √(2 × 10 / 9.8)
t = 200 × √(20 / 9.8)
t = 200 × √(2.04)
t = 200 × 1.43
t â‰ˆ 286 seconds â‰ˆ 4.77 minutes

11. Surface Tension

Surface tension is the property of a liquid surface that makes it behave like a stretched elastic membrane. It arises because molecules at the surface experience a net inward force (they have neighbours only on one side), minimising the surface area.

T = F / l

where T is the surface tension (N/m) and F is the force acting along a length l of an imaginary line on the surface. Surface tension can also be defined as the surface energy per unit area (J/m²).

Factors Affecting Surface Tension

  • Surface tension decreases with increasing temperature.
  • Adding detergents or soaps reduces the surface tension of water.
  • Different liquids have different surface tensions; mercury has much higher surface tension than water.

12. Angle of Contact

The angle of contact (or contact angle) θ is the angle between the liquid surface and the solid surface at the three-phase contact line (where solid, liquid, and gas meet). It depends on the relative strengths of adhesive forces (between liquid and solid) and cohesive forces (within the liquid).

Types of Contact Angles

  • Acute angle (θ < 90Â°): Adhesion > cohesion. Example: water on glass. The meniscus is concave, and the liquid "wets" the surface.
  • Obtuse angle (θ > 90Â°): Cohesion > adhesion. Example: mercury on glass. The meniscus is convex, and the liquid does not wet the surface.
  • Right angle (θ = 90Â°): Adhesion = cohesion. The meniscus is flat.
Example: Contact Angle

Water on clean glass has a contact angle of approximately 0Â°, while mercury on glass has a contact angle of approximately 140Â°. This explains why water spreads on glass while mercury forms beads.

13. Excess Pressure

Excess pressure is the additional pressure inside a curved interface due to surface tension. The pressure difference across a curved interface is given by the Young-Laplace equation, which depends on the surface tension and the radius of curvature.

Excess Pressure in a Liquid Drop

For a spherical liquid drop of radius R suspended in air, the excess pressure inside the drop over the outside pressure is:

ΔP = 2T / R

Excess Pressure in a Soap Bubble

A soap bubble has two surfaces (inner and outer), each contributing to the excess pressure:

ΔP = 4T / R

Air Bubble in a Liquid

For an air bubble of radius R in a liquid at depth h below the surface:

Pbubble = Patm + ρgh + 2T / R

Double Bubble

When two soap bubbles of radii r₁ and r₂ merge into a single bubble, the radius of the resulting bubble is related by:

r = (r₁ r₂) / (r₂ − r₁)
Example: Soap Bubble Pressure

A soap bubble of radius 2 cm is formed in air. The surface tension of soap solution is 0.025 N/m. Calculate the excess pressure inside the bubble.

Solution:
For a soap bubble (two surfaces):
ΔP = 4T / R
ΔP = 4 × 0.025 / 0.02
ΔP = 0.1 / 0.02
ΔP = 5 Pa

14. Capillarity

Capillarity is the rise or fall of a liquid in a narrow tube (capillary tube). It occurs due to the competition between adhesive forces (liquid-tube wall) and cohesive forces (liquid-liquid). The height to which the liquid rises (or falls) in a capillary tube is given by:

h = (2T cos θ) / (r ρ g)

where T is surface tension, θ is the contact angle, r is the radius of the capillary, ρ is the liquid density, and g is the acceleration due to gravity.

Rise vs. Fall

  • If θ < 90Â° (e.g., water in glass): cos θ > 0, and the liquid rises in the tube (capillary rise).
  • If θ > 90Â° (e.g., mercury in glass): cos θ < 0, and the liquid falls in the tube (capillary depression).

Insufficient Capillary Length

If the capillary tube is not tall enough for the full rise, the liquid will rise as much as it can, and the meniscus will adjust its radius to balance the forces. The contact angle remains the same, but the effective height is limited by the tube length.

Example: Capillary Rise in Glass

Water rises in a glass capillary tube of radius 0.5 mm to a height of 30 mm. Calculate the surface tension of water. (Assume cos θ = 1 for water on glass, ρ = 1000 kg/m³, g = 9.8 m/s²)

Solution:
Using h = (2T cos θ) / (r ρ g):
0.03 = (2 × T × 1) / (0.0005 × 1000 × 9.8)
0.03 = 2T / 4.9
T = 0.03 × 4.9 / 2
T = 0.147 / 2
T â‰ˆ 0.073 N/m

15. Viscosity

Viscosity is the property of a fluid that opposes the relative motion between adjacent fluid layers. It is the internal friction of fluids. Newton’s law of viscosity states that the viscous force between two parallel layers is:

F = η A (dv/dy)

where η is the coefficient of dynamic viscosity (SI unit: Pa·s or N·s/m²), A is the area of the layers, and dv/dy is the velocity gradient perpendicular to the flow direction. Fluids that obey this linear relationship are called Newtonian fluids.

Kinematic Viscosity

The kinematic viscosity is defined as ν = η / ρ, where ρ is the fluid density. Its SI unit is m²/s.

16. Reynolds Number

The Reynolds number (Re) is a dimensionless quantity that predicts whether fluid flow will be laminar or turbulent. It is the ratio of inertial forces to viscous forces:

Re = (ρ V D) / η

where ρ is the fluid density, V is the flow velocity, D is the characteristic length (e.g., pipe diameter), and η is the dynamic viscosity.

Flow Classification

  • Re < 2000: Laminar flow — smooth, orderly flow with layers sliding past each other.
  • 2000 ≤ Re ≤ 4000: Transition region — flow can be either laminar or turbulent.
  • Re > 4000: Turbulent flow — chaotic, irregular flow with eddies and vortices.

Critical Velocity

The critical velocity is the velocity at which the flow transitions from laminar to turbulent. For flow in a pipe, the critical Reynolds number is approximately 2000, so:

Vcritical = (2000 η) / (ρ D)
Example: Reynolds Number in a Pipe

Water with density 1000 kg/m³ and viscosity 0.001 Pa·s flows through a pipe of diameter 0.01 m at velocity 2 m/s. Calculate the Reynolds number and determine if the flow is laminar or turbulent.

Solution:
Re = (ρ V D) / η
Re = (1000 × 2 × 0.01) / 0.001
Re = 20 / 0.001
Re = 20,000

Since Re = 20,000 > 4000, the flow is turbulent.

17. Poiseuille’s Formula

Poiseuille’s equation describes the volume flow rate of a viscous fluid in laminar flow through a horizontal cylindrical pipe. It shows that flow rate is extremely sensitive to the pipe radius, varying with the fourth power of the radius.

Q = (π ΔP R4) / (8 η L)

where ΔP is the pressure difference between the ends of the pipe, R is the radius, η is the dynamic viscosity, and L is the length of the pipe.

Fluid Resistance

The resistance to flow can be defined as:

Rflow = ΔP / Q = (8 η L) / (π R4)

Series and Parallel Combinations

  • Series: Rtotal = R₁ + R₂ + ... (resistances add)
  • Parallel: 1/Rtotal = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + ... (reciprocals add)
Example: Three Capillaries in Series

Three identical capillary tubes, each with radius 1 mm and length 10 cm, are connected in series. A fluid with viscosity 0.01 Pa·s is flowing through them under a pressure difference of 1000 Pa. Calculate the volume flow rate.

Solution:
For one capillary:
R = (8 η L) / (π R4)
R = (8 × 0.01 × 0.1) / (π × (0.001)4)
R = 0.008 / (π × 10−12)
R â‰ˆ 2.55 × 109 Pa·s/m³

For three in series:
Rtotal = 3R = 7.65 × 109 Pa·s/m³

Q = ΔP / Rtotal
Q = 1000 / (7.65 × 109)
Q â‰ˆ 1.31 × 10−7 m³/s

18. Stokes’ Law & Terminal Velocity

When a small sphere moves through a viscous fluid at low speed (low Reynolds number), the viscous drag force acting on it is given by Stokes’ Law:

F = 6πηrv

where r is the radius of the sphere, v is its velocity, and η is the dynamic viscosity. This is valid only for laminar flow (Re < 1).

Terminal Velocity

When a sphere falls through a viscous fluid, it accelerates initially. As its velocity increases, the viscous drag increases until it balances the gravitational force minus the buoyant force. At this point, the sphere falls with a constant velocity called the terminal velocity:

vt = (2r²(ρsphere − ρfluid)g) / (9η)

Combining Drops

When two identical liquid drops combine to form a single drop, the volume doubles but the surface area decreases. The terminal velocity of the combined drop is related to the terminal velocity of a single drop by:

vt,combined = vt,single × 22/3
Example 3: Terminal Velocity

A steel ball of radius 2 mm and density 8000 kg/m³ is dropped in a container of glycerine (density = 1300 kg/m³, viscosity η = 0.83 Pa·s). Find the terminal velocity of the ball.

Solution:
Using the terminal velocity formula:
vt = (2r²(ρs − ρf)g) / (9η)

Given: r = 2 mm = 2 × 10−3 m, ρs = 8000 kg/m³, ρf = 1300 kg/m³, η = 0.83 Pa·s, g = 9.8 m/s²

vt = (2 × (2 × 10−3)² × (8000 − 1300) × 9.8) / (9 × 0.83)
vt = (2 × 4 × 10−6 × 6700 × 9.8) / 7.47
vt = (525.28 × 10−3) / 7.47
vt â‰ˆ 0.070 m/s â‰ˆ 7.0 cm/s

19. Rotating Fluids

When a fluid container rotates about a vertical axis with constant angular velocity ω, the liquid surface forms a paraboloid. This topic explores the pressure distribution and surface shape in rotating fluids, important in applications like centrifuges and spinning containers.

Pressure Distribution in Rotating Fluids

In a rotating reference frame, the fluid experiences a centrifugal force. The pressure at a point at distance r from the axis and depth z below the surface is:

P = P0 + ½ρω²r² + ρgz

where P0 is the pressure at the axis and surface level.

Free Surface Shape

The free surface of a rotating fluid forms a paraboloid of revolution. At a distance r from the rotation axis, the surface height y above the minimum point (at the axis) is:

y = (ω² r²) / (2g)

This parabolic shape arises because at the free surface, the pressure is constant, and the effect of centrifugal acceleration must be balanced by the change in gravitational potential energy.

Applications

  • Centrifuge: Used to separate particles of different densities by rotating them at high angular velocities.
  • Spinning bucket: The water surface forms a paraboloid when a bucket of water is spun rapidly.
  • Rotating machinery: Understanding pressure distribution in rotating fluids is important for pump and turbine design.
Example: Rotating Water Container

A cylindrical container of water rotates about its vertical axis with an angular velocity of 10 rad/s. At the center (r = 0), the water depth is 0.5 m. Calculate the height of the water surface at a radial distance of 0.2 m from the axis.

Solution:
The free surface height relative to the lowest point is:
y = (ω² r²) / (2g)
y = (10² × 0.2²) / (2 × 9.8)
y = (100 × 0.04) / 19.6
y = 4 / 19.6
y â‰ˆ 0.204 m

So the water surface rises by about 0.2 m from the center to a radius of 0.2 m.

A Pitot tube is inserted into a water pipe. The manometer shows a height difference of 0.5 m. What is the velocity of water in the pipe?

Solution:
Using the Pitot tube formula for liquids:
V = √(2gh)
V = √(2 × 9.8 × 0.5)
V = √(9.8)

Example 7: Floating Body

A wooden sphere of volume 0.1 m³ and density 600 kg/m³ is floating on water (ρ = 1000 kg/m³). What is the volume of the sphere that is submerged?

Solution:
Using the floating condition:
Vsubmerged × ρwater = V × ρsphere
Vsubmerged × 1000 = 0.1 × 600
Vsubmerged = 0.06 m³

Fraction submerged = 0.06 / 0.1 = 60%

Example 8: Soap Bubble Excess Pressure

A soap bubble of radius 2 cm is formed in air. The surface tension of soap solution is 0.025 N/m. Calculate the excess pressure inside the bubble.

Solution:
For a soap bubble (two surfaces):
ΔP = 4T / R
ΔP = 4 × 0.025 / 0.02
ΔP = 0.1 / 0.02 = 5 Pa

Example 9: Tank Thrust

A vertical rectangular tank wall is 4 m wide and 5 m deep, filled with water (ρ = 1000 kg/m³). Calculate the total thrust on the wall and the depth of its center of pressure.

Solution:
Total thrust: F = ρg h² × width / 2
F = 1000 × 9.8 × 25 × 4 / 2
F = 490,000 N = 490 kN

Depth of center of pressure: d = 2h/3 = 2 × 5 / 3 â‰ˆ 3.33 m below surface

Example 10: Rotating Fluid Container

A cylindrical container of water rotates about its vertical axis with an angular velocity of 10 rad/s. At the center (r = 0), the water depth is 0.5 m. Calculate the height of the water surface at a radial distance of 0.2 m from the axis.

Solution:
The free surface height relative to the lowest point is:
y = (ω² r²) / (2g)
y = (10² × 0.2²) / (2 × 9.8)
y = (100 × 0.04) / 19.6
y = 4 / 19.6 â‰ˆ 0.204 m

The water surface rises by about 0.2 m from the center.
