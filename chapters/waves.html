1. Basic Ideas of Wave Motion

Fig 10.1

Wave motion is the process by which a disturbance (energy) is transferred from one point to another through a medium, while the particles of the medium oscillate about their mean (equilibrium) positions. The key insight is that energy is transported without net transfer of matter. When a stone is dropped into a pool of water, the disturbance spreads outward as a circular wave, setting neighbouring particles into motion. Each particle oscillates over a short path about its initial position — the medium as a whole does not travel in the direction of the wave.

Mechanical vs. Electromagnetic Waves

  • Mechanical waves (elastic waves): require a material medium for propagation. The medium must possess inertia and elasticity. Examples include sound waves, water waves, waves on a string, and seismic waves.
  • Electromagnetic waves: do not require any medium and can travel through vacuum. Examples include light, radio waves, microwaves, and X-rays. They consist of oscillating electric and magnetic fields.

Wave Speed vs. Particle Speed

It is essential to distinguish between two very different velocities associated with a wave:

  • Wave speed (phase speed): the speed with which the disturbance (or a particular phase, such as a crest) propagates through the medium. It depends on the properties of the medium (elasticity, density) and not on the source.
  • Particle speed: the speed at which the individual particles of the medium vibrate about their equilibrium positions. It depends on the amplitude and frequency of the wave.
$$v = f\lambda \quad | \quad f = \frac{1}{T}$$

The wave speed is completely determined by the medium, while the frequency is determined by the source. When a wave passes from one medium to another, its frequency remains the same but its wavelength and speed change.

2. Transverse and Longitudinal Waves

Fig 10.2

(a) Transverse Waves

In transverse waves, the particles of the medium oscillate perpendicular to the direction in which the wave travels. The disturbance propagates in the form of crests (upward peaks) and troughs (downward peaks).

Transverse waves require a shearing force in the medium. Hence, transverse mechanical waves can propagate only in media that support shearing stress — primarily solids and the surface of liquids. They cannot propagate through bulk liquids or gases (though electromagnetic waves, being non-mechanical, can be transverse in any medium or vacuum).

(b) Longitudinal Waves

In longitudinal waves, the particles oscillate parallel (along) the direction of wave propagation. The disturbance travels as alternating compressions (regions where particles crowd together, pressure is higher) and rarefactions (regions where particles spread apart, pressure is lower).

Longitudinal waves do not require shearing stress and hence can travel in any elastic medium — solids, liquids, and gases. Sound waves in air are the most common example of longitudinal waves.

Speed Formulas for Different Media

$$v_{\text{string}} = \sqrt{\frac{T}{\mu}}$$ $$\text{(T = tension, } \mu \text{ = linear mass density)}$$ $$v_{\text{liquid}} = \sqrt{\frac{B}{\rho}}$$ $$\text{(B = Bulk modulus, } \rho \text{ = density)}$$ $$v_{\text{solid}} = \sqrt{\frac{Y}{\rho}}$$ $$\text{(Y = Young's modulus, } \rho \text{ = density)}$$

Speed of Sound in a Gas — Newton’s Formula & Laplace Correction

Newton initially assumed that sound propagation in a gas is an isothermal process, giving v = √(P/ρ). This yielded a value of about 280 m/s for air at NTP, which was significantly lower than the experimentally observed value of ~332 m/s.

Laplace corrected this by recognising that the compressions and rarefactions in a sound wave occur so rapidly that the process is actually adiabatic, not isothermal. The corrected formula is:

$$v = \sqrt{\frac{\gamma P}{\rho}} \quad \text{(Laplace correction)}$$ $$v = \sqrt{\frac{\gamma RT}{M}}$$ $$\text{where } \gamma = C_P/C_V, M = \text{ molar mass}, T = \text{ absolute temperature}$$

For air (γ = 1.4) at NTP, this gives v ≈ 331.6 m/s, which matches experimental values.

Effect of Temperature on Speed of Sound

Since v ∝ √T (at constant composition), the speed of sound increases with temperature:

$$v_T = v_0 \sqrt{\frac{T}{T_0}}$$ $$v \approx (331 + 0.6t) \text{ m/s} \quad (t \text{ in }Â°C\text{, near room temperature})$$

3. Wave Properties

Fig 10.3

Every wave is characterised by a set of fundamental properties that describe its spatial and temporal behaviour:

Wavelength (λ)

The wavelength is the distance between two consecutive points in the same phase — for example, the distance between two successive crests (transverse wave) or two successive compressions (longitudinal wave). It is measured in metres.

Frequency (f) and Time Period (T)

The frequency f is the number of complete oscillations per second (in Hz). The time period T is the time for one complete oscillation: T = 1/f. In a transverse wave, frequency is the number of crests (or troughs) passing a point per second. In a longitudinal wave, it is the number of compressions (or rarefactions) passing a point per second.

Amplitude (A)

The amplitude is the maximum displacement of a particle from its equilibrium position. It determines the energy and intensity of the wave.

Wave Number (k) and Angular Frequency (ω)

$$k = \frac{2\pi}{\lambda} \quad \text{(unit: rad/m)}$$ $$\omega = 2\pi f = \frac{2\pi}{T} \quad \text{(unit: rad/s)}$$ $$v = f\lambda = \frac{\omega}{k}$$

Phase

When a wave passes through a medium, all particles oscillate with the same frequency, but they reach corresponding positions in their oscillation at different instants. The quantity that describes where a particle is in its oscillation cycle is the phase.

  • Particles in same phase: separated by distances 0, λ, 2λ, 3λ, … (phase difference = 0, 2π, 4π, …). They have the same displacement and velocity at all times.
  • Particles in opposite phase: separated by distances λ/2, 3λ/2, 5λ/2, … (phase difference = π, 3π, 5π, …). Their displacements are equal in magnitude but opposite in direction.

Intensity and Amplitude

The intensity of a wave is proportional to the square of the amplitude: I ∝ A². This is a fundamental result that applies to all types of waves.

4. Wave Equation

Fig 10.4

A sinusoidal progressive wave travelling in the positive x-direction is described by:

$$y(x, t) = A \sin(kx - \omega t + \phi_0)$$

Here y is the displacement of the medium particle at position x and time t, A is the amplitude, k = 2π/λ is the angular wave number, ω = 2πf is the angular frequency, and φ₀ is the initial phase (depends on initial conditions). The full argument φ = kx − ωt + φ₀ is called the phase angle.

For a wave travelling in the negative x-direction, the equation becomes:

$$y(x, t) = A \sin(kx + \omega t + \phi_0)$$

Phase Difference and Path Difference

The phase difference between two particles located at positions x₁ and x₂ is related to the path difference Δx = x₁ − x₂ by:

$$\Delta\phi = \frac{2\pi}{\lambda} \times \Delta x = k \times \Delta x$$
  • Same phase: Δφ = 0, 2π, 4π, … ⇒ Δx = 0, λ, 2λ, …
  • Opposite phase: Δφ = π, 3π, 5π, … ⇒ Δx = λ/2, 3λ/2, …

Particle Velocity and Acceleration

The transverse velocity of a particle (at fixed x) is obtained by differentiating y with respect to t:

vp = ∂y/∂t = −ωA cos(kx − ωt + φ₀)
Maximum particle speed: vp(max) = ωA = 2πfA

Particle acceleration: ap = ∂²y/∂t² = −ω²A sin(kx − ωt + φ₀) = −ω²y

Note the important relation between particle velocity and wave velocity: vp = −v × (∂y/∂x), where v = ω/k is the wave speed. The particle velocity and wave velocity are fundamentally different quantities.

Differential Wave Equation

The general wave equation satisfied by any progressive wave y = f(x ± vt) is:

∂²y/∂t² = v² × ∂²y/∂x²

Any function y = f(ax ± bt), where a and b are positive constants, represents a wave if y is finite for all x and t. The wave speed is b/a, the ‘−’ sign indicates a wave travelling in the +x direction, and the ‘+’ sign indicates a wave travelling in the −x direction.

Example 1 — Forming a Transverse Wave Equation

A transverse wave of wavelength 50 cm is travelling towards +x direction along a string whose linear density is 0.05 g/cm. The tension in the string is 450 N. At t = 0, the particle at x = 0 is passing through its mean position with an upward velocity. The amplitude of the wave is 2.5 cm. Form the equation of the wave.

Step 1: Wave number k = 2π/λ = 2π/0.5 = 4π rad/m

Step 2: Wave speed v = √(T/μ) = √(450 / 0.005) = 300 m/s
(Here μ = 0.05 g/cm = 0.005 kg/m)

Step 3: ω = v × k = 300 × 4π = 1200π rad/s

Step 4: At x = 0, t = 0: y(0,0) = 0
A sin(φ₀) = 0 ⇒ φ₀ = 0 or π

Step 5: vp at (0,0) > 0 (upward velocity)
−ωA cos(φ₀) > 0 ⇒ cos(φ₀) < 0 ⇒ φ₀ = π

Step 6: The wave equation is:
y = 2.5 sin(4πx − 1200πt + π) cm

5. Intensity and Energy of Waves

Fig 10.5

In a travelling wave, energy is transferred through the medium in the direction of wave propagation. The intensity of a wave is defined as the energy transferred per unit time per unit area perpendicular to the direction of wave motion (i.e., power per unit area).

Intensity Formula

I = 2π² v ρ f² A² = ½ρω²A²v
where v = wave speed, ρ = density of medium, f = frequency, A = amplitude

Key dependences: I ∝ A² and I ∝ f². The intensity is proportional to the square of both the amplitude and the frequency.

Inverse Square Law for Spherical Waves

For a point source emitting waves uniformly in all directions (isotropic source), the energy spreads over a sphere of radius r. The intensity at distance r from the source is:

I = P / (4πr²)
⇒ I ∝ 1/r²   and   A ∝ 1/r   (point source)

For a line source (cylindrical wave), I ∝ 1/r and A ∝ 1/√r.

Sound Intensity Level (Decibels)

The human ear perceives loudness on a logarithmic scale. The sound intensity level in decibels (dB) is defined as:

β = 10 log₁₀(I/I₀)   dB
where I₀ = 10−12 W/m² (threshold of hearing)
  • Threshold of hearing: 0 dB (I = 10−12 W/m²)
  • Threshold of pain: ~120 dB (I ≈ 1 W/m²)
  • An increase of 10 dB corresponds to a 10-fold increase in intensity

6. Principle of Superposition and Interference

Fig 10.6

The principle of superposition states that when two or more waves overlap in a region, the resultant displacement at any point is the algebraic sum of the displacements due to each individual wave:

y = y₁ + y₂ + y₃ + …

This principle is valid for all types of waves (mechanical and electromagnetic). After the waves pass through each other, they continue with their original properties (amplitude, frequency, wavelength) unchanged — they do not alter each other permanently.

Interference of Waves

When two coherent waves (same frequency, constant phase difference) superpose, the nature of interference at a point depends on the path difference (Δx) or phase difference (Δφ) between the two waves:

Constructive Interference

The waves add up to give maximum amplitude when they arrive in phase:

Path difference: Δx = nλ   (n = 0, 1, 2, …)
Phase difference: Δφ = 2nπ
Resultant amplitude: Amax = A₁ + A₂
Imax = (A₁ + A₂)²

Destructive Interference

The waves cancel to give minimum amplitude when they arrive in anti-phase:

Path difference: Δx = (2n + 1)λ/2   (n = 0, 1, 2, …)
Phase difference: Δφ = (2n + 1)π
Resultant amplitude: Amin = |A₁ − A₂|
Imin = (A₁ − A₂)²

For two waves of equal amplitude (A₁ = A₂ = A), constructive interference gives Imax = 4A² and destructive interference gives Imin = 0 (complete cancellation).

Two Important Superposition Phenomena

  • Stationary (standing) waves: formed when two waves of the same frequency travel in opposite directions.
  • Beats: formed when two waves of slightly different frequencies travel in the same direction.

7. Standing (Stationary) Waves

Fig 10.7

Standing waves are formed when two identical progressive waves travelling in opposite directions superpose. Consider two waves: y₁ = A sin(kx − ωt) and y₂ = A sin(kx + ωt). By the superposition principle, the resultant is:

y(x, t) = 2A sin(kx) cos(ωt)   (standing wave equation)

This is not a travelling wave — it does not have the form f(x ± vt). Instead, it describes an oscillation in which the amplitude depends on position x. The particle at location x performs SHM with angular frequency ω and amplitude |2A sin(kx)|.

Nodes

Points where the amplitude is permanently zero (particles never move):

sin(kx) = 0  ⇒  x = 0, λ/2, λ, 3λ/2, …
i.e., x = nλ/2   (n = 0, 1, 2, …)

Antinodes

Points where the amplitude is maximum (= 2A):

|sin(kx)| = 1  ⇒  x = λ/4, 3λ/4, 5λ/4, …
i.e., x = (2n + 1)λ/4

Key Properties of Standing Waves

  • Nodes and antinodes are alternately spaced, separated by λ/4.
  • The distance between two consecutive nodes (or antinodes) is λ/2.
  • No energy is transferred from one point to another — nodes are permanently at rest and cannot transmit energy.
  • All particles between two consecutive nodes oscillate in phase with each other (they all reach their maximum displacement simultaneously).
  • Particles on opposite sides of a node oscillate in opposite phase.
  • The segment between two consecutive nodes is called a loop.

8. Vibrations on Strings and in Air Columns

Fig 10.8

(a) Stretched String Fixed at Both Ends

A string of length L fixed at both ends supports standing waves only at specific resonant frequencies. Both fixed ends must be nodes. Since the distance between consecutive nodes is λ/2, the condition is:

L = nλ/2  ⇒  λn = 2L/n

fn = nv/2L = (n/2L)√(T/μ)   (n = 1, 2, 3, …)
  • n = 1: fundamental frequency (1st harmonic), f₁ = v/2L
  • n = 2: 2nd harmonic (1st overtone), f₂ = 2v/2L
  • n = 3: 3rd harmonic (2nd overtone), f₃ = 3v/2L
  • All harmonics are present.

Sonometer Law (Law of Vibrating Strings)

From f = (1/2L)√(T/μ), we derive three laws for the fundamental frequency:

  • Law of length: f ∝ 1/L (at constant T and μ)
  • Law of tension: f ∝ √T (at constant L and μ)
  • Law of mass: f ∝ 1/√μ (at constant L and T)

(b) Open Pipe (Open at Both Ends)

Both open ends are displacement antinodes (particles at the open end oscillate freely). The condition for resonance is:

L = nλ/2  ⇒  fn = nv/2L   (n = 1, 2, 3, …)
All harmonics are present.

(c) Closed Pipe (Closed at One End)

The closed end is a node and the open end is an antinode. The distance between a node and the nearest antinode is λ/4, so:

L = (2n − 1)λ/4  ⇒  fn = (2n − 1)v/4L   (n = 1, 2, 3, …)
Only odd harmonics (1st, 3rd, 5th, …) are present.

The fundamental frequency of a closed pipe (v/4L) is half that of an open pipe of the same length (v/2L). This also means the quality of sound from an open pipe is richer, since it contains all harmonics.

End Correction

In practice, the antinode forms slightly beyond the open end of the pipe. The end correction e ≈ 0.6R (where R is the radius of the pipe) must be added to the effective length:

  • Closed pipe: effective length = L + e
  • Open pipe: effective length = L + 2e

Resonance Tube Experiment

A resonance tube is a closed pipe whose length can be varied by changing the water level. When the air column length matches a resonant condition for a given tuning fork frequency, a loud sound (resonance) is heard. The first resonance occurs at L₁ ≈ λ/4, the second at L₂ ≈ 3λ/4, giving λ = 2(L₂ − L₁) and v = fλ.

Example 2 — String Resonant Frequencies & Overtones

A steel wire 0.5 m long has a mass of 5 g and is stretched with a force of 400 N. (a) What is the fundamental frequency? (b) Can the wire vibrate at 1100 Hz? (c) What is the highest overtone audible to a person who can hear up to 10,000 Hz?

(a) Linear mass density μ = 5 × 10−3 / 0.5 = 0.01 kg/m
f₁ = (1/2L)√(T/μ) = (1/(2 × 0.5))√(400/0.01)
f₁ = 1 × √(40000) = 1 × 200 = 200 Hz

(b) For the wire to vibrate at 1100 Hz, we need 1100 = n × 200, i.e., n = 5.5. Since n must be a positive integer, the wire cannot vibrate at 1100 Hz.

(c) 10000 = n × 200 ⇒ n = 50. So the 50th harmonic (= 49th overtone) at 10,000 Hz is the highest audible frequency. The answer is the 49th overtone.

9. Beats

Fig 10.9

Beats are produced when two waves of slightly different frequencies travel in the same direction and superpose at a point. The resultant sound has an amplitude that varies periodically — alternately waxing (getting louder) and waning (getting softer).

Mathematical Analysis

Consider two waves of equal amplitude A and frequencies f₁ and f₂ arriving at a point: y₁ = A sin(2πf₁t) and y₂ = A sin(2πf₂t). Using superposition:

y = y₁ + y₂ = 2A cos[π(f₁ − f₂)t] × sin[π(f₁ + f₂)t]

This represents a wave of frequency (f₁ + f₂)/2 with a slowly varying amplitude envelope 2A cos[π(f₁ − f₂)t].

Beat Frequency

fbeat = |f₁ − f₂|
  • Loud sounds (maximum amplitude = 2A) occur when cos[π(f₁ − f₂)t] = ±1, i.e., at intervals of 1/|f₁ − f₂|.
  • Weak sounds (zero amplitude) occur when cos[π(f₁ − f₂)t] = 0, also at the same interval spacing.
  • The ear can typically distinguish beats when fbeat < 6–7 Hz; above this, the fluctuations merge into a rough, continuous sound.

Determining Unknown Frequency (Loading/Filing Method)

If a tuning fork of known frequency f produces n beats per second with an unknown fork, the unknown frequency is f ± n. To decide the sign:

  • If loading wax on the unknown fork (which decreases its frequency) causes the beat frequency to decrease, then the unknown frequency was f + n (higher).
  • If loading causes the beat frequency to increase, then the unknown frequency was f − n (lower).
  • Conversely, filing (sharpening) the prongs increases the frequency of the fork.
Example 3 — Resonance in Air Columns with Beats

A closed air column 32 cm long is in resonance with a tuning fork. Another open air column of length 66 cm is in resonance with a second tuning fork. If the two forks produce 8 beats per second when sounded together, find the speed of sound in air and the frequencies of the two forks.

For the closed pipe: f₁ = v/(4 × 0.32) = v/1.28
For the open pipe: f₂ = v/(2 × 0.66) = v/1.32

Since f₁ > f₂, we have f₁ − f₂ = 8
v/1.28 − v/1.32 = 8
v(1.32 − 1.28)/(1.28 × 1.32) = 8
v × 0.04/1.6896 = 8
v = 8 × 1.6896/0.04 = 337.92 m/s ≈ 338 m/s

f₁ = 337.92/1.28 ≈ 264 Hz
f₂ = 337.92/1.32 ≈ 256 Hz

Verification: 264 − 256 = 8 beats/s ✓

10. Doppler Effect

Fig 10.10

The Doppler effect is the change in the observed frequency (or wavelength) of a wave when there is relative motion between the source and the observer. If both are stationary, the observer hears the actual frequency f. If either or both are moving, the apparent frequency f′ differs from f.

General Formula

f′ = f × (v ± vo) / (v ∓ vs)

where v = speed of sound, vo = speed of observer, vs = speed of source.

Sign Convention

Take the direction from source to observer as positive. Then:

  • vo is positive when the observer moves towards the source (numerator: v + vo).
  • vo is negative when the observer moves away from the source (numerator: v − vo).
  • vs is positive when the source moves towards the observer (denominator: v − vs).
  • vs is negative when the source moves away from the observer (denominator: v + vs).

Special Case 1: Source Moving, Observer Stationary

Source approaching: f′ = f × v / (v − vs)   (frequency increases)
Source receding: f′ = f × v / (v + vs)   (frequency decreases)

When the source moves towards the observer, the wavefronts are compressed (shorter wavelength) ahead of the source and expanded (longer wavelength) behind it.

Special Case 2: Observer Moving, Source Stationary

Observer approaching: f′ = f × (v + vo) / v   (frequency increases)
Observer receding: f′ = f × (v − vo) / v   (frequency decreases)

When the observer moves towards the source, they intercept more wavefronts per unit time. Importantly, the wavelength remains unchanged in this case (only the rate of interception changes).

Special Case 3: Both Moving

The general formula f′ = f(v ± vo)/(v ∓ vs) handles all cases. Use the upper signs (+vo, −vs) when source and observer approach each other, and the lower signs (−vo, +vs) when they recede from each other.

Effect of Medium (Wind) Velocity

If the medium is moving with velocity Vm (positive in the source-to-observer direction), the formula becomes:

f′ = f × (v + Vm − Vo) / (v + Vm − Vs)

Applications of the Doppler Effect

  • Police radar and speed guns: use the Doppler effect with electromagnetic waves to measure vehicle speeds.
  • Medical ultrasound (Doppler echocardiography): measures blood flow velocity by detecting frequency shifts in reflected ultrasound.
  • Astronomical redshift/blueshift: reveals whether stars and galaxies are moving towards (blueshift) or away from (redshift) the Earth.
  • SONAR systems: detect underwater objects and measure their speeds using reflected sound waves.
Example 4 — Doppler Effect with a Moving Train

A train moves towards a stationary observer at 30 m/s, sounding a horn of frequency 500 Hz. The speed of sound in air is 340 m/s. What frequency does the observer hear (a) as the train approaches, and (b) after the train passes?

(a) Approaching: vo = 0, vs = 30 m/s (towards observer)
f′ = f × v / (v − vs) = 500 × 340 / (340 − 30)
f′ = 500 × 340/310 ≈ 548.4 Hz

(b) Receding: vs = 30 m/s (away from observer)
f′ = f × v / (v + vs) = 500 × 340 / (340 + 30)
f′ = 500 × 340/370 ≈ 459.5 Hz

The observer hears a higher pitch (548.4 Hz) as the train approaches and a lower pitch (459.5 Hz) as it recedes. This is the classic demonstration of the Doppler effect.
