Waves

Chapter 10 — Grade XI • Wave Motion

1. Types of Waves

A wave is a disturbance that transfers energy from one point to another without the net transport of matter. Waves are classified in several ways based on their nature and behaviour.

Transverse vs. Longitudinal

  • Transverse waves: the particles of the medium oscillate perpendicular to the direction of wave propagation. Examples: waves on a string, electromagnetic waves.
  • Longitudinal waves: the particles oscillate along (parallel to) the direction of wave propagation, creating compressions and rarefactions. Examples: sound waves in air, pressure waves in fluids.

Mechanical vs. Electromagnetic

  • Mechanical waves: require a material medium for propagation (sound, water waves, seismic waves).
  • Electromagnetic waves: do not require a medium and can travel through vacuum (light, radio waves, X-rays).

Progressive vs. Stationary

  • Progressive (travelling) waves: transport energy continuously from one region to another. Every particle has the same amplitude.
  • Stationary (standing) waves: formed by the superposition of two identical progressive waves travelling in opposite directions. They do not transport energy. Particles at different positions have different amplitudes.

2. Wave Equation

A sinusoidal progressive wave travelling in the positive x-direction is described by:

y = A sin(ωt − kx)

Here y is the displacement of the medium particle at position x and time t, A is the amplitude, ω = 2πf = 2π/T is the angular frequency, and k = 2π/λ is the wave number (angular wave number). For a wave travelling in the negative x-direction, the equation becomes y = A sin(ωt + kx).

Wave Speed (Phase Velocity)

The speed at which a particular phase of the wave (e.g., a crest) moves is the phase velocity:

v = fλ = ω/k = λ/T

The wave speed depends on the properties of the medium, not on the frequency or amplitude of the wave (for non-dispersive media).

3. Speed of Waves in Different Media

Transverse Waves in a Stretched String

The speed of a transverse wave on a string depends on the tension T in the string and its linear mass density μ (mass per unit length):

v = √(T/μ)

A tighter string (higher T) or a lighter string (lower μ) produces a faster wave. This is why tuning a guitar string by tightening it raises the pitch.

Longitudinal Waves (Sound) in a Gas

Newton initially derived the speed of sound in air assuming isothermal conditions, giving v = √(P/ρ), but this underestimated the observed speed. Laplace corrected this by noting that sound propagation is an adiabatic process:

v = √(γP/ρ)   (Laplace correction)
Using PV = nRT: v = √(γRT/M)

Effect of Temperature

Since v ∝ √T (for an ideal gas at constant composition), the speed of sound increases with temperature. At temperature T (in Kelvin):

vT = v₀ √(T/T₀)
Approximately: v ≈ (331 + 0.6t) m/s   (t in °C, near room temperature)

4. Superposition Principle

The principle of superposition states that when two or more waves overlap in a region, the resultant displacement at any point is the algebraic sum of the displacements due to each wave individually: y = y₁ + y₂ + y₃ + …

Interference

When two coherent waves (same frequency, constant phase difference) superpose, they produce an interference pattern. The nature of interference at a point depends on the path difference (Δx) between the two waves:

  • Constructive interference: path difference = nλ (n = 0, 1, 2, …). The waves add up to maximum amplitude.
  • Destructive interference: path difference = (n + ½)λ. The waves cancel, giving minimum (or zero) amplitude.
Constructive: Δx = nλ   (phase diff = 2nπ)
Destructive: Δx = (2n+1)λ/2   (phase diff = (2n+1)π)
Imax = (A₁ + A₂)²   |   Imin = (A₁ − A₂)²

5. Standing Waves

Standing waves are formed when two identical progressive waves travelling in opposite directions superpose. The resulting wave does not propagate — it oscillates in place with fixed points of zero displacement (nodes) and maximum displacement (antinodes).

y = 2A sin(kx) cos(ωt)   (standing wave equation)
Nodes at: x = nλ/2   (n = 0, 1, 2, …)
Antinodes at: x = (2n+1)λ/4

Vibrations of a Stretched String (Fixed at Both Ends)

A string of length L fixed at both ends supports standing waves only at specific resonant frequencies. The ends must be nodes. The allowed wavelengths and frequencies are:

λn = 2L/n   |   fn = nv/2L = (n/2L)√(T/μ)
n = 1, 2, 3, … (1st harmonic = fundamental, 2nd harmonic = 1st overtone, …)

Organ Pipes

For a pipe open at both ends, both ends are displacement antinodes, and all harmonics are present:

Open pipe: fn = nv/2L   (n = 1, 2, 3, … — all harmonics)

For a pipe closed at one end, the closed end is a node and the open end is an antinode. Only odd harmonics are present:

Closed pipe: fn = nv/4L   (n = 1, 3, 5, … — odd harmonics only)

The fundamental frequency of a closed pipe is half that of an open pipe of the same length.

Solved Example 1

A string of length 1 m is fixed at both ends. The speed of transverse waves on the string is 400 m/s. Find the first three resonant frequencies.

fn = nv / 2L
f₁ = 1 × 400 / (2 × 1) = 200 Hz   (fundamental)
f₂ = 2 × 400 / (2 × 1) = 400 Hz   (2nd harmonic)
f₃ = 3 × 400 / (2 × 1) = 600 Hz   (3rd harmonic)

6. Beats

Beats are a phenomenon that occurs when two waves of slightly different frequencies superpose. The resultant sound has an amplitude that varies periodically — alternately waxing (getting louder) and waning (getting softer). The beat frequency equals the absolute difference between the two frequencies:

fbeat = |f₁ − f₂|

If two tuning forks of frequencies 256 Hz and 260 Hz are sounded together, we hear 4 beats per second. The ear can typically detect beats when fbeat < 6–7 Hz; above this, the fluctuations merge into a rough, continuous sound.

Practical Uses of Beats

  • Tuning musical instruments: a musician adjusts one string until the beat frequency with a reference tone becomes zero.
  • Determining an unknown frequency: if loading wax on a tuning fork decreases its frequency and the beat frequency decreases, the unknown fork was originally of higher frequency.
Solved Example 2

Two tuning forks A and B produce 5 beats per second. Fork A has a frequency of 512 Hz. When fork B is loaded with a small piece of wax, the beat frequency becomes 3 beats per second. Find the original frequency of fork B.

fbeat = |fA − fB| = 5
So fB = 512 + 5 = 517 Hz or fB = 512 − 5 = 507 Hz.

Loading wax on B decreases its frequency. If fB = 517 Hz, after loading it decreases towards 512, so beats should decrease — and they do (from 5 to 3). This is consistent.

If fB = 507 Hz, decreasing it further moves it further from 512, so beats should increase — contradicting the observation.

Therefore, fB = 517 Hz.

7. Doppler Effect

The Doppler effect is the change in the observed frequency (or wavelength) of a wave when there is relative motion between the source and the observer. For sound waves in a medium where the wave speed is v:

f′ = f × (v ± vo) / (v ∓ vs)

Sign Convention

Take the direction from source to observer as positive. Then:

  • vo is positive when the observer moves towards the source (numerator: v + vo).
  • vo is negative when the observer moves away from the source (numerator: v − vo).
  • vs is positive when the source moves towards the observer (denominator: v − vs).
  • vs is negative when the source moves away from the observer (denominator: v + vs).

Special Cases

Source Moving, Observer Stationary

When only the source moves towards a stationary observer: f′ = f × v / (v − vs). The observed frequency increases (pitch rises) as the source approaches. When the source recedes: f′ = f × v / (v + vs).

Observer Moving, Source Stationary

When only the observer moves towards a stationary source: f′ = f × (v + vo) / v. The observed frequency increases. When the observer recedes: f′ = f × (v − vo) / v.

Both Moving

The general formula f′ = f (v ± vo) / (v ∓ vs) handles all cases. The upper signs apply when source and observer approach each other; the lower signs when they recede from each other.

Applications

  • Police radar and speed guns use the Doppler effect with electromagnetic waves.
  • Medical ultrasound (Doppler echocardiography) measures blood flow velocity.
  • Astronomical redshift/blueshift reveals whether stars and galaxies are moving towards or away from Earth.
  • SONAR systems detect underwater objects using reflected sound waves.
Solved Example 3

A train moves towards a stationary observer at 30 m/s, sounding a horn of frequency 500 Hz. The speed of sound in air is 340 m/s. What frequency does the observer hear? What frequency does the observer hear after the train passes?

Approaching: vo = 0, vs = 30 m/s (towards observer)
f′ = f × v / (v − vs) = 500 × 340 / (340 − 30)
f′ = 500 × 340 / 310 ≈ 548.4 Hz

Receding: vs = 30 m/s (away from observer)
f′ = f × v / (v + vs) = 500 × 340 / (340 + 30)
f′ = 500 × 340 / 370 ≈ 459.5 Hz

The observer hears a higher pitch (548.4 Hz) as the train approaches and a lower pitch (459.5 Hz) as it recedes.

Key Formulas Summary

y = A sin(ωt − kx)   |   v = fλ = ω/k
vstring = √(T/μ)   |   vsound = √(γP/ρ) = √(γRT/M)
fn = nv/2L (string, open pipe)   |   fn = nv/4L (closed pipe, odd n)
fbeat = |f₁ − f₂|
f′ = f(v ± vo) / (v ∓ vs)   (Doppler effect)
