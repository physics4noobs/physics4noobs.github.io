1. Classification of Metals, Conductors & Semiconductors

Fig 14.1 — Classification based on conductivity

On the Basis of Conductivity

On the basis of their electrical conductivity (σ) or resistivity (ρ = 1/σ), solids are broadly classified into three categories. This classification forms the foundation of understanding semiconductor devices.

(i) Metals (Conductors)

Metals possess very low resistivity (or high conductivity). They have a large number of free electrons available for conduction. Examples include copper, aluminium, silver, and gold.

$$\rho \sim 10^{-2} \text{ to } 10^{-8} \; \Omega \cdot \text{m}$$ $$\sigma \sim 10^{2} \text{ to } 10^{8} \; \text{S m}^{-1}$$

(ii) Semiconductors

Semiconductors have resistivity or conductivity intermediate to metals and insulators. Their conductivity can be dramatically altered by adding small amounts of impurities (doping), changing temperature, or exposing them to light. Silicon (Si) and Germanium (Ge) are the most commonly used elemental semiconductors. Compound semiconductors include GaAs, CdS, CdSe, and InP.

$$\rho \sim 10^{-5} \text{ to } 10^{6} \; \Omega \cdot \text{m}$$ $$\sigma \sim 10^{-6} \text{ to } 10^{5} \; \text{S m}^{-1}$$

(iii) Insulators

Insulators have high resistivity (or low conductivity). The electrons in insulators are tightly bound to their atoms and cannot contribute to conduction under normal conditions. Examples include rubber, glass, wood, and most plastics.

$$\rho \sim 10^{11} \text{ to } 10^{19} \; \Omega \cdot \text{m}$$ $$\sigma \sim 10^{-19} \text{ to } 10^{-11} \; \text{S m}^{-1}$$

Important: The distinction between metals, semiconductors and insulators is not just about resistivity values. The fundamental difference lies in the behaviour of their energy bands, which we discuss next.

2. Energy Bands in Solids

Fig 14.2 — Energy band diagrams of metals, insulators and semiconductors

Origin of Energy Bands

In an isolated atom, electrons occupy discrete energy levels. When atoms come together to form a crystalline solid, the outer electron orbits overlap significantly. Due to the Pauli exclusion principle, each discrete energy level splits into a band of closely spaced energy levels. The two most important bands are:

Valence Band: The energy band that includes the energy levels of valence electrons. At absolute zero, this band is completely filled with electrons.

Conduction Band: The energy band above the valence band. At absolute zero, this band is completely empty. Electrons in this band are free to move and can contribute to electrical conduction.

Energy Band Gap (Eg)

The gap between the top of the valence band and the bottom of the conduction band is called the energy band gap (or forbidden gap). No electron can have energy in this gap. The magnitude of Eg determines the electrical behaviour of the material.

Case I: Metals (Eg ≈ 0)

In metals, either the conduction band is partially filled or the valence and conduction bands overlap. This makes a large number of electrons available for conduction. No external energy is needed to push electrons from the valence band to the conduction band.

Case II: Insulators (Eg > 3 eV)

In insulators, a large band gap (Eg > 3 eV) exists between the valence and conduction bands. The conduction band is completely empty and no electrons can be excited across this wide gap by thermal excitation at room temperature. Hence, no electrical conduction is possible.

Case III: Semiconductors (Eg < 3 eV)

In semiconductors, the band gap is finite but small (Eg < 3 eV). At absolute zero, the semiconductor behaves like an insulator. But at room temperature, thermal energy is sufficient to excite some electrons from the valence band to the conduction band, enabling a small amount of conduction.

$$E_g(\text{Si}) = 1.1 \text{ eV}, \quad E_g(\text{Ge}) = 0.67 \text{ eV}$$ $$E_g(\text{C, diamond}) = 5.4 \text{ eV (insulator)}$$ $$E_g(\text{Sn}) \approx 0 \text{ eV (metal)}$$

JEE/NEET Tip: C, Si, Ge, and Sn are all Group IV elements with 4 valence electrons. The energy gap decreases as we go down the group: C > Si > Ge > Sn. This is why carbon (diamond) is an insulator, Si and Ge are semiconductors, and Sn is a metal.

3. Intrinsic Semiconductors

Fig 14.3 — Covalent bonding in Si/Ge crystal

Crystal Structure

Silicon and Germanium have a diamond-like crystal structure. Each atom is surrounded by four nearest neighbours, and each atom shares one of its four valence electrons with each neighbour, forming covalent bonds. At low temperatures, all bonds are intact and no free electrons are available for conduction.

Electron-Hole Pair Generation

As temperature increases, thermal energy breaks some covalent bonds, freeing electrons into the conduction band. Each freed electron leaves behind a vacancy called a hole. This hole behaves as an apparent free particle with effective positive charge (+q). The process of creating a free electron and a hole together is called electron-hole pair (EHP) generation.

Intrinsic Carrier Concentration

In a pure (intrinsic) semiconductor, the number of free electrons (ne) is always equal to the number of holes (nh). This common value is called the intrinsic carrier concentration (ni).

$$n_e = n_h = n_i$$ $$n_i(\text{Si at 300 K}) \approx 1.5 \times 10^{16} \text{ m}^{-3}$$ $$n_i(\text{Ge at 300 K}) \approx 2.4 \times 10^{19} \text{ m}^{-3}$$

Current in Intrinsic Semiconductors

In an intrinsic semiconductor, both electrons and holes contribute to current. Under an applied electric field, free electrons move towards the positive terminal while holes move towards the negative terminal. The total current is the sum of the electron current and the hole current.

$$I = I_e + I_h$$ $$\sigma = n_i e (\mu_e + \mu_h)$$

Here \(\mu_e\) and \(\mu_h\) are the electron and hole mobilities respectively. For silicon, \(\mu_e \approx 0.135\) m2V-1s-1 and \(\mu_h \approx 0.048\) m2V-1s-1.

Temperature Dependence

Unlike metals, the conductivity of semiconductors increases with temperature because more electron-hole pairs are generated. This is why semiconductors have a negative temperature coefficient of resistance. This property makes them useful as thermistors (temperature sensors).

4. Extrinsic Semiconductors (Doping)

Fig 14.4 — n-type and p-type doping

What is Doping?

The conductivity of intrinsic semiconductors at room temperature is too low for practical applications. To increase conductivity, small amounts of impurity atoms (a few parts per million) are deliberately added to the pure semiconductor. This process is called doping, and the resulting semiconductor is called an extrinsic or doped semiconductor.

There are two types of dopants for tetravalent Si or Ge:

(i) n-type Semiconductor (Donor Doping)

When a pentavalent impurity (valency 5) such as Arsenic (As), Antimony (Sb), or Phosphorus (P) is added to Si or Ge, four of its five electrons form covalent bonds with the neighbouring Si atoms. The fifth electron remains very weakly bound and is easily set free at room temperature, contributing to conduction. The ionisation energy required is very small (~0.05 eV for Si, ~0.01 eV for Ge).

Since the pentavalent atom donates one extra electron, it is called a donor impurity. In n-type semiconductors, electrons are the majority carriers and holes are the minority carriers.

$$n_e \gg n_h$$ $$n_e \approx N_D \quad \text{(donor concentration)}$$

(ii) p-type Semiconductor (Acceptor Doping)

When a trivalent impurity (valency 3) such as Boron (B), Aluminium (Al), or Indium (In) is added to Si or Ge, it can form covalent bonds with only three neighbouring atoms. The fourth bond has a vacancy — a hole. This hole is available for conduction. The trivalent atom effectively accepts an electron to complete its bonding, and is called an acceptor impurity.

In p-type semiconductors, holes are the majority carriers and electrons are the minority carriers.

$$n_h \gg n_e$$ $$n_h \approx N_A \quad \text{(acceptor concentration)}$$

Mass Action Law

A fundamental result in semiconductor physics: the product of the number of electrons and holes in a semiconductor in thermal equilibrium is constant, regardless of the amount of doping. This is known as the mass action law.

$$n_e \cdot n_h = n_i^2$$

JEE/NEET Tip: This is a very frequently tested formula. If you know ne (or nh) and ni, you can calculate the other. For example, in an n-type semiconductor: \(n_h = n_i^2 / n_e \approx n_i^2 / N_D\).

Important Points about Doped Semiconductors

Charge neutrality: A doped semiconductor remains electrically neutral overall. The additional charge carriers are balanced by the ionised dopant atoms (fixed in the lattice). In n-type: fixed positive donor ions balance the extra electrons. In p-type: fixed negative acceptor ions balance the extra holes.

5. p-n Junction

Fig 14.5 — p-n junction formation and depletion region

Formation of p-n Junction

A p-n junction is formed by joining a p-type semiconductor with an n-type semiconductor. In practice, this is achieved by doping different regions of the same semiconductor wafer with different impurities. You cannot simply press two separate slabs together — the junction requires atomic-level continuity.

Diffusion and Drift

When a p-n junction is first formed, two important processes occur simultaneously:

Diffusion: Due to the concentration gradient, holes diffuse from p-side to n-side and electrons diffuse from n-side to p-side. This diffusion creates a diffusion current across the junction.

Drift: As electrons leave the n-side, they leave behind positively ionised donor atoms. Similarly, holes leaving the p-side expose negatively ionised acceptor atoms. These fixed ions create an electric field directed from n-side to p-side. This field pushes electrons back to n-side and holes back to p-side, creating a drift current that opposes the diffusion current.

Depletion Region

The region near the junction that is depleted of free charge carriers (both electrons and holes) is called the depletion region (or space-charge region). It consists of immobile ionised donor and acceptor atoms. The width of the depletion region is typically 0.1 to 1 μm.

At equilibrium, the diffusion current equals the drift current, and the net current across the junction is zero.

Barrier Potential (V0)

The potential difference developed across the depletion region due to the space charge is called the barrier potential (or built-in potential). It prevents further net diffusion of majority carriers.

$$V_0 \approx 0.7 \text{ V for Si}$$ $$V_0 \approx 0.3 \text{ V for Ge}$$

Important: The depletion region extends more into the lightly doped side. If the n-side is lightly doped compared to the p-side, the depletion width on the n-side will be larger.

6. Semiconductor Diode & V-I Characteristics

Fig 14.6 — Diode symbol and V-I characteristics

What is a Semiconductor Diode?

A semiconductor diode is a p-n junction with metallic contacts at both ends for applying external voltage. It is a two-terminal device. The p-side terminal is called the anode and the n-side terminal is called the cathode. In the circuit symbol, the arrowhead points from p to n (direction of conventional current under forward bias).

Forward Bias

When the p-side is connected to the positive terminal and the n-side to the negative terminal of a battery, the diode is said to be forward biased. The applied voltage opposes the barrier potential, reducing it to (V0 − V). This narrows the depletion region and allows majority carriers to cross the junction, producing a significant current.

The current first increases very slowly until the applied voltage crosses a certain value called the threshold voltage (or cut-in voltage or knee voltage). Beyond this voltage, the current increases exponentially.

$$V_{\text{threshold}} \approx 0.7 \text{ V (Si)}, \quad \approx 0.3 \text{ V (Ge)}$$ $$\text{Effective barrier} = V_0 - V$$

Reverse Bias

When the n-side is connected to the positive terminal and the p-side to the negative terminal, the diode is reverse biased. The applied voltage adds to the barrier potential, increasing it to (V0 + V). The depletion region widens and the diffusion current drops to nearly zero.

A very small current still flows due to the drift of minority carriers across the junction. This is called the reverse saturation current (of the order of μA). It remains nearly constant regardless of the applied reverse voltage.

Breakdown Voltage

At very high reverse bias (V = Vbr), the reverse current increases sharply. This is called breakdown. There are two mechanisms:

Zener breakdown: Occurs in heavily doped junctions with thin depletion regions (Vbr < 5V). The strong electric field directly strips electrons from covalent bonds.

Avalanche breakdown: Occurs in lightly doped junctions with wide depletion regions (Vbr > 5V). Minority carriers gain enough kinetic energy to knock out electrons by collision, causing a chain reaction (avalanche).

Diode Current Equation (Shockley Equation)

The current through a p-n junction diode as a function of applied voltage is given by:

$$I = I_0 \left[\exp\left(\frac{eV}{k_B T}\right) - 1\right]$$ $$I_0 = \text{reverse saturation current}$$ $$e = 1.6 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}, \quad k_B = 1.38 \times 10^{-23} \text{ J/K}$$

Forward bias (V > 0): The exponential term dominates, and current grows exponentially with voltage. At room temperature (300 K), \(k_BT/e \approx 0.026\) V, so even a small forward voltage produces a large current.

Reverse bias (V < 0): The exponential term becomes negligible, and \(I \approx -I_0\). The current saturates at the small reverse saturation value.

JEE/NEET Tip: This equation explains why the V-I curve is exponential in forward bias and nearly flat in reverse bias. The thermal voltage \(k_BT/e \approx 26\) mV at 300 K is a frequently used value in numericals.

Dynamic Resistance

$$r_d = \frac{\Delta V}{\Delta I}$$

The dynamic (or AC) resistance is the ratio of a small change in voltage to the corresponding small change in current. In forward bias, rd is very small (a few ohms). In reverse bias, rd is very large (MΩ).

7. Application of Junction Diode as a Rectifier

Fig 14.7 — Half-wave and Full-wave rectifier circuits

Half-Wave Rectifier

A half-wave rectifier uses a single diode in series with a load resistor. During the positive half-cycle of the AC input, the diode is forward biased and conducts. During the negative half-cycle, the diode is reverse biased and does not conduct. Thus, only one half of the input AC waveform appears across the load.

$$\text{Output frequency} = f_{\text{input}}$$ $$V_{\text{dc}} = \frac{V_0}{\pi}$$ $$I_{\text{dc}} = \frac{I_0}{\pi}$$

Full-Wave Rectifier

A full-wave rectifier uses two diodes with a centre-tap transformer (or four diodes in a bridge configuration). Both halves of the AC cycle are rectified — each diode conducts during alternate half-cycles. The output is a pulsating DC with twice the frequency of the input.

$$\text{Output frequency} = 2f_{\text{input}}$$ $$V_{\text{dc}} = \frac{2V_0}{\pi}$$ $$I_{\text{dc}} = \frac{2I_0}{\pi}$$

Filtering

The pulsating output of a rectifier is smoothed using a filter capacitor connected in parallel with the load. When the rectified voltage rises, the capacitor charges. When it falls, the capacitor discharges through the load, maintaining a relatively steady voltage. The larger the product RC (time constant), the smoother the output.

JEE/NEET Tip: Half-wave rectifier output frequency = input frequency. Full-wave rectifier output frequency = 2 × input frequency. If input is 50 Hz, half-wave output is 50 Hz and full-wave output is 100 Hz. This is a very commonly asked question!

8. Special Purpose Diodes

Fig 14.8 — Zener diode, LED, Photodiode, Solar cell

Zener Diode

A Zener diode is a heavily doped p-n junction designed to operate in the reverse breakdown region. Unlike ordinary diodes, the breakdown is not destructive — it is reversible. In the breakdown region, the voltage across the Zener remains nearly constant (VZ) even as the current changes. This makes it ideal as a voltage regulator.

$$V_{\text{out}} = V_Z = \text{constant (in breakdown region)}$$ $$I_S = \frac{V_{\text{in}} - V_Z}{R_S}$$ $$I_Z = I_S - I_L$$

Here RS is the series resistance, IS is the total current, IL is the load current, and IZ is the current through the Zener diode.

Light Emitting Diode (LED)

An LED is a forward-biased p-n junction that emits light when electrons recombine with holes. The energy released during recombination is emitted as photons. The colour of light depends on the band gap of the semiconductor material used.

$$E = h\nu = \frac{hc}{\lambda}$$

LEDs are made from compound semiconductors like GaAs (infrared), GaAsP (red, yellow), GaP (green), and GaN/InGaN (blue, white). They have advantages over incandescent bulbs: low power consumption, long life, fast switching, and monochromatic light output.

Photodiode

A photodiode is a p-n junction operated under reverse bias. When light of appropriate frequency falls on the junction, it generates electron-hole pairs, increasing the reverse current. The photocurrent is proportional to the intensity of incident light. Photodiodes are used in light detection, optical communication, and as light sensors.

Solar Cell

A solar cell is essentially a large-area p-n junction (photodiode) that operates without external bias. When sunlight falls on the junction, electron-hole pairs are generated. The built-in electric field separates these carriers, creating a voltage (open-circuit voltage ~0.5–1.0 V) and driving current through an external circuit. Materials used include Si, GaAs, and CdTe.

Important distinction: A photodiode is used in reverse bias as a detector (photocurrent ∝ light intensity). A solar cell operates at zero bias as an energy source (converts solar energy to electrical energy).

9. Bipolar Junction Transistor (BJT)

Fig 14.9 — npn and pnp transistor diagrams

Structure

A transistor is a three-terminal device formed by sandwiching a thin layer of one type of semiconductor between two layers of the other type. There are two types: npn (thin p-layer between two n-layers) and pnp (thin n-layer between two p-layers). The three terminals are called the Emitter (E), Base (B), and Collector (C).

The emitter is heavily doped and emits charge carriers. The base is very thin and lightly doped. The collector is moderately doped and physically larger than the emitter to collect the majority of carriers passing through the base.

Transistor Biasing (Active Mode)

For a transistor to work as an amplifier, the emitter-base junction must be forward biased and the collector-base junction must be reverse biased. In an npn transistor in active mode:

Electrons are injected from the emitter into the base. Since the base is thin and lightly doped, most electrons (about 95–99%) diffuse through the base and are swept into the collector by the reverse-biased collector junction. Only a small fraction recombine with holes in the base.

$$I_E = I_B + I_C$$ $$\text{Current gain (common emitter): } \beta = \frac{I_C}{I_B}$$ $$\text{Current gain (common base): } \alpha = \frac{I_C}{I_E}$$ $$\beta = \frac{\alpha}{1 - \alpha}, \quad \alpha = \frac{\beta}{1 + \beta}$$

Typical values: α = 0.95 to 0.99, β = 20 to 500.

Transistor as an Amplifier (CE Configuration)

In common emitter (CE) configuration, the input signal is applied between base and emitter, and the output is taken between collector and emitter. A small change in base current (ΔIB) causes a large change in collector current (ΔIC = β × ΔIB).

$$A_V = \frac{\Delta V_{\text{out}}}{\Delta V_{\text{in}}} = -\beta \frac{R_C}{R_{\text{in}}}$$ $$\text{Power gain} = \beta^2 \times \frac{R_C}{R_{\text{in}}}$$

The negative sign indicates a 180° phase shift between input and output. CE amplifier provides both current gain and voltage gain.

Transistor as a Switch

When the base current is zero (or below threshold), the transistor is in cut-off mode: IC ≈ 0 and the transistor acts as an open switch. When the base current is large enough to drive the transistor into saturation, VCE ≈ 0 and the transistor acts as a closed switch. This is the basis of digital electronics.

Transistor as an Oscillator

An oscillator produces continuous, undamped electrical oscillations (AC signal) without any external AC input. It converts DC energy into AC energy. The key principle is positive feedback — a portion of the output signal is fed back to the input in phase, sustaining the oscillations.

For sustained oscillations, the circuit must satisfy Barkhausen's condition:

$$|A_V \cdot \beta_{\text{feedback}}| = 1$$ $$\text{Total phase shift around the loop} = 0^\circ \text{ (or } 360^\circ\text{)}$$

Here AV is the voltage gain of the amplifier and βfeedback is the feedback fraction. If the loop gain is exactly 1 and the feedback is in phase, oscillations are sustained at a specific frequency determined by the LC or RC components in the feedback network.

The frequency of oscillation for an LC oscillator is:

$$f = \frac{1}{2\pi\sqrt{LC}}$$

Key difference: An amplifier requires an external AC input and produces a larger AC output. An oscillator needs no external AC input — it generates AC from DC using positive feedback.

10. Logic Gates

Fig 14.10 — Logic gate symbols and truth tables

Digital Electronics Basics

In digital electronics, signals have only two values: HIGH (1) and LOW (0). Logic gates are the fundamental building blocks of digital circuits. They perform Boolean operations on one or more binary inputs and produce a single binary output.

OR Gate

Output is 1 if any input is 1. Boolean expression: Y = A + B.

A = 0, B = 0 → Y = 0
A = 0, B = 1 → Y = 1
A = 1, B = 0 → Y = 1
A = 1, B = 1 → Y = 1

AND Gate

Output is 1 only if all inputs are 1. Boolean expression: Y = A · B.

A = 0, B = 0 → Y = 0
A = 0, B = 1 → Y = 0
A = 1, B = 0 → Y = 0
A = 1, B = 1 → Y = 1

NOT Gate (Inverter)

Output is the complement of the input. Boolean expression: Y = Ā (A bar).

A = 0 → Y = 1
A = 1 → Y = 0

NAND Gate (Universal Gate)

Output is 0 only when all inputs are 1. It is the complement of AND. Boolean expression: Y = A · B. NAND is called a universal gate because any other gate can be constructed using only NAND gates.

A = 0, B = 0 → Y = 1
A = 0, B = 1 → Y = 1
A = 1, B = 0 → Y = 1
A = 1, B = 1 → Y = 0

NOR Gate (Universal Gate)

Output is 1 only when all inputs are 0. It is the complement of OR. Boolean expression: Y = A + B. NOR is also a universal gate.

A = 0, B = 0 → Y = 1
A = 0, B = 1 → Y = 0
A = 1, B = 0 → Y = 0
A = 1, B = 1 → Y = 0

JEE/NEET Tip: NAND and NOR are universal gates — any Boolean function can be implemented using only NAND gates or only NOR gates. Questions often ask you to identify the gate from its truth table, or to construct one gate using others (e.g., make NOT from NAND).

De Morgan's Theorems

De Morgan's theorems are essential tools for simplifying Boolean expressions and converting between gate types. They are frequently tested in both JEE Main and NEET.

Theorem 1: \(\overline{A \cdot B} = \bar{A} + \bar{B}\)

The complement of AND equals OR of the complements.
(NAND = OR with inverted inputs)

Theorem 2: \(\overline{A + B} = \bar{A} \cdot \bar{B}\)

The complement of OR equals AND of the complements.
(NOR = AND with inverted inputs)

Application: Using De Morgan's theorems, you can convert any Boolean expression into one that uses only NAND gates or only NOR gates. This is why NAND and NOR are called universal gates.

Example: To implement Y = A + B using only NAND gates:
Step 1: Apply De Morgan's: \(A + B = \overline{\bar{A} \cdot \bar{B}}\)
Step 2: This requires inverting A and B (using NAND as NOT), then NANDing the results — three NAND gates total.

11. Key Formulas at a Glance

Carrier Concentrations

$$n_e \cdot n_h = n_i^2 \quad \text{(Mass Action Law)}$$ $$\text{Intrinsic: } n_e = n_h = n_i$$ $$\text{n-type: } n_e \approx N_D, \quad n_h = \frac{n_i^2}{N_D}$$ $$\text{p-type: } n_h \approx N_A, \quad n_e = \frac{n_i^2}{N_A}$$

Conductivity

$$\sigma = e(n_e \mu_e + n_h \mu_h)$$ $$\rho = \frac{1}{\sigma}$$

Diode and Rectifier

$$I = I_0 \left[\exp\left(\frac{eV}{k_B T}\right) - 1\right]$$ $$V_{\text{threshold}} \approx 0.7 \text{ V (Si)}, \quad 0.3 \text{ V (Ge)}$$ $$r_d = \frac{\Delta V}{\Delta I} \quad \text{(dynamic resistance)}$$ $$\text{Half-wave: } V_{\text{dc}} = \frac{V_0}{\pi}, \quad f_{\text{out}} = f_{\text{in}}$$ $$\text{Full-wave: } V_{\text{dc}} = \frac{2V_0}{\pi}, \quad f_{\text{out}} = 2f_{\text{in}}$$

Transistor

$$I_E = I_B + I_C$$ $$\alpha = \frac{I_C}{I_E}, \quad \beta = \frac{I_C}{I_B}$$ $$\beta = \frac{\alpha}{1 - \alpha}, \quad \alpha = \frac{\beta}{1 + \beta}$$ $$A_V = -\beta \frac{R_C}{R_{\text{in}}}$$

Oscillator

$$\text{Barkhausen condition: } |A_V \cdot \beta_{\text{feedback}}| = 1$$ $$f = \frac{1}{2\pi\sqrt{LC}} \quad \text{(LC oscillator frequency)}$$

Logic Gates (De Morgan's Theorems)

$$\overline{A \cdot B} = \bar{A} + \bar{B}$$ $$\overline{A + B} = \bar{A} \cdot \bar{B}$$

Zener Diode (Voltage Regulator)

$$V_{\text{out}} = V_Z$$ $$I_S = \frac{V_{\text{in}} - V_Z}{R_S}, \quad I_Z = I_S - I_L$$

JEE & NEET Corner

Frequently Tested Topics

Note: Semiconductors have been removed from the JEE Advanced syllabus (confirmed for 2025 and 2026). They remain in JEE Main only with a weightage of approximately 4.8% (1–2 questions per session).

JEE Main: Energy band gap classification, mass action law numerical problems, V-I characteristics of diode, Zener diode as voltage regulator, transistor amplifier (CE) gain calculations, logic gate truth tables and combinations, rectifier output frequency.

NEET: Classification of conductors/semiconductors/insulators, intrinsic vs extrinsic semiconductors, n-type vs p-type identification, p-n junction working, forward and reverse bias, diode applications (rectifier, LED, photodiode, solar cell), basic logic gates.

Common Traps & Tricks

1. A doped semiconductor is electrically neutral, not charged. Students often confuse “having extra electrons” in n-type with “being negatively charged” — the extra electrons come with positively ionised donor atoms.

2. In a p-n junction at equilibrium, diffusion current = drift current, NOT zero current individually. The net current is zero.

3. You cannot make a p-n junction by pressing two slabs together. The junction requires atomic-level crystal continuity.

4. Photodiode works in reverse bias. Solar cell works at zero bias. Both use the photovoltaic effect but in different modes.

5. In half-wave rectification, output frequency = input frequency. In full-wave rectification, output frequency = 2 × input frequency.

6. Energy gap order for Group IV: Eg(C) > Eg(Si) > Eg(Ge) > Eg(Sn).

7. For a transistor in active mode: E-B junction is forward biased, C-B junction is reverse biased. In saturation: both junctions forward biased. In cut-off: both junctions reverse biased.

Important Numerical Types

Type 1: Given ni and doping concentration ND (or NA), find minority carrier concentration using ne · nh = ni2.

Type 2: Transistor current relations — given α find β (or vice versa), or given IB and β find IC and IE.

Type 3: Zener diode regulator — calculate series resistance current, Zener current, and load current.

Type 4: CE amplifier voltage gain calculation given β, RC, and Rin.
