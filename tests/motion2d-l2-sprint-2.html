Motion in 2D Level 2 — Sprint Test 2

Grade XI • 10 Questions • 30 Minutes
30:00
Question 1
A projectile is aimed at a mark on a horizontal plane through the point of projection and falls 6 m short when its elevation is 30° but overshoots the mark by 9 m when its elevation is 45°. The angle of elevation of projectile to hit the target on the horizontal plane is:
A. sin−1[(3√3 + 4)/5]
B. cos−1[(3√3 + 4)/5]
C. (1/2)cos−1[(3√3 + 4)/10]
D. (1/2)sin−1[(3√3 + 4)/10]
Solution: Let the target distance be R. Range formula: R = u²sin2θ/g. At 30°: u²sin60°/g = R − 6, so u²√3/(2g) = R − 6. At 45°: u²sin90°/g = R + 9, so u²/g = R + 9. Dividing: √3/2 = (R − 6)/(R + 9). Solving: R√3 + 9√3 = 2R − 12, R(2 − √3) = 9√3 + 12, R = (9√3 + 12)/(2 − √3). Then u²/g = R + 9. For the required angle θ: sin2θ = Rg/u² = R/(R+9) = (3√3 + 4)/10. So θ = (1/2)sin−1[(3√3 + 4)/10].
Question 2
If the vertical retardation produced by air resistance of projectile is constant and equal to one-tenth of acceleration due to gravity: (g = 10 m/s²). The time to reach maximum height:
A. Increases by 9 percent
B. Decreases by 9 percent
C. Increases by 11 percent
D. Decreases by 11 percent
Solution: Without air resistance: effective upward deceleration = g = 10 m/s². Time to max height t1 = uy/g. With air resistance adding a retardation of g/10: effective deceleration = g + g/10 = 11g/10. New time t2 = uy/(11g/10) = 10uy/(11g) = (10/11)t1. Change = (t1 − t2)/t1 = 1/11 ≈ 9.09%. Time decreases by approximately 9 percent.
Question 3
The horizontal range and maximum height attained by a projectile are R and H respectively. If a constant horizontal acceleration a = g/4 is imparted to the projectile due to wind, then its horizontal range and maximum height will be:
A. (R + H), H/2
B. (R + H/2), 2H
C. (R + 2H), H
D. (R + H), H
Solution: The vertical motion is unchanged, so maximum height remains H. Time of flight T = 2u sinθ/g is also unchanged. The horizontal displacement gets an extra contribution from the wind acceleration: Δx = (1/2)(g/4)T² = (g/8)(2u sinθ/g)² = u²sin²θ/(2g) = 2H · (1/2) = H. So new horizontal range = R + H, and height = H.
Question 4
A body of mass m thrown horizontally with velocity v, from the top of tower of height h touches the level ground at distance of 250 metre from the foot of the tower. A body of mass 2m thrown horizontally with velocity v/2 from the top of tower of height 4h will touch the level ground at a distance x from the foot of tower. The value of x is:
A. 250 m
B. 500 m
C. 125 m
D. 250√2 m
Solution: Horizontal range for projectile thrown horizontally: x = v√(2h/g). Mass is irrelevant. Case 1: x1 = v√(2h/g) = 250 m. Case 2: x2 = (v/2)√(2·4h/g) = (v/2) · 2√(2h/g) = v√(2h/g) = 250 m. So x = 250 m.
Question 5
An airplane, diving at an angle of 53.0° with the vertical releases a projectile at an altitude of 730 m. The projectile hits the ground 5.00 s after being released. What is the speed of the aircraft?
A. 282 ms−1
B. 202 ms−1
C. 182 ms−1
D. 102 ms−1
Solution: The plane dives at 53° with vertical, so angle with horizontal = 37°. Let speed = u. Vertical component (downward) = u cos53° = 0.6u. Horizontal component = u sin53° = 0.8u. Vertical displacement: 730 = 0.6u · 5 + (1/2)(10)(25). 730 = 3u + 125. 3u = 605. u = 201.67 ≈ 202 ms−1.
Question 6
A particle is projected with a certain velocity at an angle α above the horizontal from the foot of an inclined plane of inclination 30°. If the particle strikes the plane normally then α is equal to:
A. 30° + tan−1(√3/2)
B. 45°
C. 60°
D. 30° + tan−1(2√3)
Solution: For the projectile to strike the inclined plane normally, the velocity at the point of impact must be perpendicular to the plane (i.e., directed along the normal to the incline, which is at 30° + 90° from horizontal = making angle 60° below the incline direction). At impact, horizontal velocity component vx = u cosα (unchanged) and vertical component vy = u sinα − gT (downward). For normal impact on a 30° incline: tan(30°) = vx/vy at impact (vx/vy = tan30° = 1/√3). Working through the geometry gives α = 30° + tan−1(√3/2).
Question 7
For a particle moving along circular path, the radial acceleration ar is proportional to time t. If at is the tangential acceleration, then which of the following will be independent of time t?
A. at
B. ar · at
C. ar/at
D. ar(at)2
Solution: ar = v²/r ∝ t, so v² ∝ t, meaning v ∝ t1/2. Tangential acceleration at = dv/dt ∝ t−1/2. So at ∝ t−1/2 (not constant). ar · at ∝ t · t−1/2 = t1/2 (not constant). ar/at ∝ t/t−1/2 = t3/2 (not constant). ar(at)2 ∝ t · t−1 = t0 = constant. So D is independent of time.
Question 8
A very broad elevator is going up vertically with a constant acceleration of 2 m/s². At the instant when its velocity is 4 m/s a ball is projected from the floor of the lift with a speed of 4 m/s relative to the floor at an elevation of 30°. The time taken by the ball to return to the floor is: (g = 10 m/s²)
A. 0.5 s
B. 0.33 s
C. 0.25 s
D. 1 s
Solution: In the non-inertial frame of the elevator, a pseudo-force acts downward giving effective g′ = g + a = 10 + 2 = 12 m/s². The ball is projected at 4 m/s at 30° relative to the floor. Vertical component (relative to lift) = 4 sin30° = 2 m/s. Time to return to floor: T = 2uy/g′ = 2(2)/12 = 4/12 = 1/3 ≈ 0.33 s.
Question 9
A ball is projected from a high tower with speed 20 m/s at an angle 30° with horizontal x-axis. The x-coordinate of the ball at the instant when the velocity of the ball becomes perpendicular to the velocity of projection will be (take point of projection as origin):
A. 40√3 m
B. 40 m
C. 20√3 m
D. 20 m
Solution: Initial velocity: ux = 20cos30° = 10√3 m/s, uy = 20sin30° = 10 m/s. When velocity is perpendicular to initial velocity, their dot product = 0. vxux + vyuy = 0. Since vx = ux = 10√3: (10√3)(10√3) + vy(10) = 0. 300 + 10vy = 0, vy = −30 m/s. Time: vy = uy − gt ⇒ −30 = 10 − 10t ⇒ t = 4 s. x = uxt = 10√3 × 4 = 40√3 m.
Question 10
With what minimum speed must a particle be projected from origin so that it is able to pass through a given point (30 m, 40 m)? (g = 10 m/s²)
A. 60 m/s
B. 30 m/s
C. 50 m/s
D. 40 m/s
Solution: The trajectory equation is y = x tanθ − gx²/(2u²cos²θ) = x tanθ − gx²(1 + tan²θ)/(2u²). For the point (30, 40): 40 = 30T − (10 · 900)(1 + T²)/(2u²) where T = tanθ. 40 = 30T − 4500(1 + T²)/u². For minimum u, differentiate with respect to T and set to zero: dT/du = 0. This gives the condition. Rearranging: u² = 4500(1 + T²)/(30T − 40). Minimising: d/dT[(1 + T²)/(30T − 40)] = 0 gives T = (1 + √(1 + (30/40)² …)). Solving the standard minimum-speed formula umin² = g(y + √(x² + y²)) = 10(40 + √(900 + 1600)) = 10(40 + 50) = 900. So umin = 30 m/s.
