What is Coulomb's Law?
Coulomb's Law describes the electrostatic force between two point charges. The force is directly proportional to the product of the magnitudes of the charges and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them. Mathematically: F = kq₁q₂/r², where k = 9 × 10&sup9; N·m²/C² is Coulomb's constant. The force is attractive for opposite charges and repulsive for like charges.
What happens when you double the distance?
Since the force follows an inverse-square law, doubling the distance reduces the force to one-quarter of its original value. If r → 2r, then F → F/4. This is a hallmark of inverse-square laws and can be verified directly in this simulation by dragging Q2 to double the separation distance.
How does a dielectric affect the force?
A dielectric medium reduces the electrostatic force between charges. The force becomes F = kq₁q₂/(κr²), where κ (kappa) is the dielectric constant of the medium. For vacuum κ = 1 (no reduction), for water κ ≈ 80 (force reduced by 80×), and for glass κ ≈ 5. This is why charged particles interact much more weakly in water than in air.
What's the difference between attraction and repulsion?
Like charges repel (both positive or both negative) and opposite charges attract (one positive, one negative). The magnitude of the force is the same in both cases for the same charge values and distance — only the direction differs. By Newton's Third Law, both charges experience equal and opposite forces regardless of whether the interaction is attractive or repulsive.
How is Coulomb's Law similar to Newton's Gravitation?
Both follow an inverse-square law: the force is proportional to the product of two quantities (charges or masses) and inversely proportional to the square of the distance. However, gravity is always attractive, while Coulomb's force can be attractive or repulsive. Also, the electrostatic force is enormously stronger — about 10³&sup6; times stronger than gravity for elementary particles — which is why electrostatics dominates at atomic scales while gravity dominates at astronomical scales.
